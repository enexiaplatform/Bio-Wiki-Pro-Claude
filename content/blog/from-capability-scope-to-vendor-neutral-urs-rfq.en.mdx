# From capability scope to a vendor-neutral URS and RFQ

A procurement list names things. A user requirements specification explains what a
system must enable, under which conditions, with what evidence and across which
lifecycle. An RFQ then asks suppliers to respond to that controlled need on a
comparable basis.

```text
quality decision and intended use
→ method and workload requirements
→ functional, performance and lifecycle requirements
→ vendor-neutral URS
→ RFQ instructions and bid boundary
→ compliance matrix, clarifications and technical recommendation
```

Atlas can compile and trace the planning basis. It does not approve a URS, select a
supplier, perform detailed engineering or replace procurement, validation, IT, EHS
and QA governance.

## Define the intended use before writing requirements

State:

- quality decisions and methods supported;
- products, matrices, markets and site boundaries;
- sample volume, peaks, shifts and turnaround target;
- users, skills and operating environment;
- required configurations, accessories and interfaces;
- data created, reviewed, transferred and retained;
- cleaning, decontamination and waste expectations;
- availability, redundancy and recovery objective;
- qualification, verification or validation boundary;
- forecast horizon and expansion trigger;
- assumptions, exclusions and approval roles.

If the intended use is broad, the URS will become generic. If it names a preferred
product too early, alternatives may be excluded without a quality or operational
rationale.

## Write requirements that can be answered and verified

Each requirement should carry:

- unique identifier;
- requirement statement;
- source or rationale;
- criticality and decision impact;
- acceptance or verification approach;
- responsible reviewer;
- supplier response and evidence reference;
- deviation, clarification or change status.

A useful construction is:

> The system shall [perform or provide a defined capability] under [stated operating
> condition] to support [intended decision], verified by [evidence or test].

Avoid unqualified words such as “robust,” “fast,” “future-proof,” “GMP compliant” or
“21 CFR Part 11 compliant.” Ask for the specific function, control, record or service
that the project needs and define how it will be assessed.

## Build the URS in traceable sections

### Scope and interfaces

Define supplied system boundary, exclusions, responsibilities, installation location,
adjacent systems, utilities, network, data systems and owner-furnished items.

### Functional and method requirements

Capture sample formats, method steps, controls, operating modes, workflows,
calculations, result outputs and exception handling without prescribing a vendor's
implementation unless the constraint is justified.

### Performance and capacity

State throughput unit, routine and peak load, cycle or run duration, usable geometry,
concurrency, operating calendar, turnaround dependency and future expansion. Do not
substitute nominal brochure capacity for the method-derived need.

### Quality, data and records

Define user access, audit trail, review, electronic signatures where applicable,
time synchronization, metadata, backup, restore, retention, export, interfaces and
data ownership. Requirements must follow the site's intended use and data-governance
assessment.

### Facility, utility and EHS

State point-of-use conditions, environmental envelope, heat/exhaust/drainage,
materials of construction, cleaning agents, contamination control, biosafety,
ergonomics, noise, waste and service access.

### Reliability and continuity

Define availability target, critical failure modes, alarm and notification behavior,
backup strategy, spares, service response, recovery, obsolescence and cybersecurity
support expectations.

### Delivery and lifecycle services

Separate design documents, factory/site testing, delivery, installation,
commissioning, calibration, qualification support, training, manuals, software,
warranty, maintenance, change notification and end-of-life support.

## Keep requirements solution-neutral where competition matters

Specify the outcome and constraint unless a particular design is required by:

- approved method or registered condition;
- site standard or validated interface;
- utility or facility limitation;
- safety or contamination-control decision;
- data architecture;
- qualified installed base or continuity strategy;
- controlled risk assessment.

Record that rationale. “Same brand as before” is a preference until the compatibility,
training, data, service, spare-parts or validation benefit is demonstrated.

## Separate the URS from the RFQ commercial wrapper

The RFQ should provide all bidders the same:

- issued URS and revision;
- quantity, options and delivery locations;
- response format and deadline;
- compliance-matrix template;
- required technical documents and samples/demonstrations;
- installation, qualification and training boundary;
- pricing schedule and currency/tax/freight instructions;
- warranty, service, consumables and lifecycle-cost schedule;
- assumptions and exclusions template;
- clarification process and contact governance;
- evaluation criteria and decision timetable.

Commercial terms, quality agreements and technical requirements are related but not
interchangeable documents. FDA's official [Quality Agreements guidance](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/contract-manufacturing-arrangements-drugs-quality-agreements-guidance-industry)
also distinguishes quality responsibilities from general commercial terms in the
contract-manufacturing context.

## Force bid comparability with a compliance matrix

For every requirement, require one controlled response:

- complies as offered;
- complies with stated option;
- partially complies;
- does not comply;
- clarification required.

Each response should cite a proposal page, datasheet, drawing, test or written
commitment. A blank cell is not compliance. Marketing text is not evidence of a
critical function.

Classify deviations by effect on:

- method or result integrity;
- capacity and turnaround;
- contamination control or safety;
- data integrity and cybersecurity;
- qualification and validation;
- utilities, space and installation;
- continuity, service and lifecycle cost;
- schedule and commercial boundary.

## Evaluate total fit, not feature count

A technical recommendation should reconcile:

1. mandatory requirement compliance;
2. critical deviations and proposed mitigations;
3. method and sample demonstration evidence;
4. usable capacity under the planned configuration;
5. installation and utility fit;
6. data and integration fit;
7. qualification and training evidence;
8. service, spares and recovery capability;
9. implementation schedule and project dependencies;
10. purchase, installed and lifecycle cost;
11. supplier assumptions, exclusions and change exposure;
12. reviewer conflicts, approvals and residual risks.

Scoring can support review, but a weighted total must not allow a critical unmet
requirement to disappear behind many low-value features.

## Control clarification and change

Use one question log with owner, issue date, bidder response, evidence, decision and
affected requirement. Reissue changed requirements to all relevant bidders under the
procurement process. Preserve the bid basis used for the recommendation.

After selection, trace approved deviations and clarifications into design review,
contract scope, acceptance testing, installation, qualification, procedures,
training and change control. The final delivered configuration must not drift away
from the evaluated offer.

EU GMP Annex 15 treats approved user requirements as a reference throughout the
validation lifecycle. The current official entry is available through the European
Commission's [EudraLex Volume 4 GMP guidelines](https://health.ec.europa.eu/medicinal-products/eudralex/eudralex-volume-4_en).

## Evidence package before issue

Before releasing an RFQ, collect:

- approved intended use and scope;
- method/application and workload basis;
- capacity, turnaround and resilience scenarios;
- facility, utility, data and EHS constraints;
- requirement identifiers, rationales and criticality;
- verification and acceptance strategy;
- project schedule and interface responsibilities;
- pricing and lifecycle-cost response structure;
- compliance-matrix and clarification templates;
- evaluation criteria, reviewers and approval route;
- confidentiality and information-sharing controls;
- assumptions, exclusions and unresolved decisions.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas should not label a package RFQ-ready when:

- the intended use or method boundary is unresolved;
- quantities are not linked to workload and resilience;
- requirements name a supplier without a controlled rationale;
- critical requirements have no verification approach;
- data, utilities, installation or lifecycle services are omitted;
- bidders can price different supply boundaries without reconciliation;
- compliance claims need no evidence reference;
- mandatory deviations can be hidden by weighted scoring;
- clarifications and configuration changes are not controlled;
- QA, users, engineering, IT/EHS, validation and procurement ownership is unclear.
