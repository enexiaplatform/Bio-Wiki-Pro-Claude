# Microbiology media and reagent readiness planning

Inventory on hand is not inventory ready for use. A test can start only when the
correct material, lot, status, quantity, storage condition and remaining use period
are available for the approved method.

```text
scheduled physical executions
→ method BOM and control demand
→ purchase or preparation lot
→ receipt, quarantine and release
→ storage, allocation and in-use status
→ consumption, reconciliation and replenishment
```

Atlas can compile readiness and supply demand. It does not release materials, assign
expiry, approve supplier certificates, establish reduced testing or substitute one
material for another.

## Build the method-material contract

For every material capture:

- controlled name, code, grade and formulation;
- method step and intended function;
- supplier/manufacturer and catalog configuration;
- purchased, prepared in-house or aliquoted status;
- lot and sub-lot relationship;
- quantity and unit per execution;
- controls, qualification, repeat and investigation demand;
- receipt, release, preparation and in-use lead time;
- storage and transport condition;
- expiry, retest, prepared or in-use dating rule;
- minimum remaining life at test start or completion;
- approved alternate and change-control status;
- evidence owner and reviewer.

Preserve method-specific roles. The same diluent or medium name may require different
preparation, controls or release status in different applications.

## Separate physical stock from usable stock

Use explicit statuses:

- ordered;
- received awaiting inspection;
- quarantined;
- sampling or testing in progress;
- released for defined use;
- restricted or conditionally released;
- allocated/reserved;
- opened or prepared;
- near expiry;
- expired, rejected or under investigation;
- consumed or disposed.

Only material with the correct approved status and scope should satisfy a readiness
check.

## Model lot-release lead time

Include:

- supplier and shipment lead time;
- receipt and condition review;
- certificate and document review;
- sampling or local testing;
- media growth promotion or other performance checks;
- reference-culture and inoculum readiness;
- incubation and reading time;
- technical review and release;
- discrepancy, failed-control and replacement scenarios.

USP's official [FAQs for microbial enumeration and specified-organism
tests](https://www.usp.org/frequently-asked-questions/microbial-enumeration-nonsterile-products-tests-specified-microorganisms)
state for the applications discussed that growth promotion is checked for each new
batch of medium. The site's current method must define the exact property, organism,
condition and release requirement.

## Build a readiness calendar

For each scheduled test or campaign, confirm:

- required quantity including controls;
- acceptable lots and material status;
- release completion before test start;
- remaining expiry/in-use window through completion;
- storage and transfer capacity;
- equipment and analyst availability for preparation/QC;
- conflicts with other planned work;
- reserve for invalid, investigation or urgent demand;
- approved alternate availability.

This calendar links inventory to execution. A monthly stock balance cannot reveal
that a new media lot will still be incubating when a launch-suitability campaign is
due to start.

## Reconcile expiry with demand

For each lot calculate:

- quantity on hand and usable quantity;
- committed/reserved quantity;
- forecast consumption before expiry;
- minimum pack or preparation batch;
- remaining shelf life at receipt;
- open-container or prepared-material use period;
- FEFO sequence and exception;
- likely excess, expiry or disposal;
- stockout exposure if replenishment is delayed.

Do not increase safety stock without checking whether it can be released, stored and
consumed before expiry.

## Control preparation and in-use states

For prepared or aliquoted material record:

- parent lot and genealogy;
- preparation formula and actual quantity;
- water, supplements and neutralizers;
- equipment and container;
- sterilization or filtration step;
- preparation, release and expiry times;
- storage and warming/conditioning;
- number and identity of sub-units;
- issued, returned and discarded quantities;
- visual or performance observations;
- affected methods and investigation link.

The FDA's non-binding [microbiological laboratory inspection guide](https://www.fda.gov/inspections-compliance-enforcement-and-criminal-investigations/inspection-guides/microbiological-pharmaceutical-quality-control-labs-793)
notes that media preparation, sterilization and storage can affect growth performance.

## Plan storage by condition and status

Calculate positions for:

- ambient, refrigerated and frozen materials;
- quarantine and released stock;
- prepared media and in-use materials;
- controlled cultures and supplements;
- light-, humidity- or access-sensitive items;
- investigation and retained lots;
- rejected/expired material awaiting disposition;
- temperature-monitoring devices and secondary containment.

Include shelf/rack geometry, segregation, alarms, door-opening practices, backup and
transfer route. Nominal refrigerator volume does not equal usable, status-controlled
capacity.

## Define stockout and shortage decisions

When readiness is threatened, record:

- affected methods, samples and due dates;
- quantity and time gap;
- approved alternate lot, material or supplier;
- comparability, verification or change requirement;
- outsourcing or rescheduling option;
- sample hold and product-release impact;
- allocation priority and decision owner;
- client/QA/procurement communication;
- recovery and CAPA evidence.

Atlas must not silently substitute a material or assume a supplier certificate closes
the gap.

## Evidence package before inventory approval

Collect:

1. method-level material and control BOM;
2. supplier, configuration and approved-alternate register;
3. receipt, quarantine and release workflow;
4. lot-QC and release lead time;
5. expiry, in-use and minimum-life rules;
6. test and material readiness calendar;
7. storage geometry, segregation and monitoring;
8. preparation and sub-lot genealogy;
9. safety-stock, reorder and FEFO basis;
10. stockout, shortage and change pathway;
11. investigation and surge allowances;
12. actual use, expiry, loss, delay and stockout history.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas should not label materials ready when:

- on-hand stock is not separated by status;
- method configuration or application is missing;
- media/reagent release lead time is omitted;
- expiry is checked only at receipt;
- prepared and in-use dating are not controlled;
- reserved and available quantities are merged;
- storage status and geometry are assumed;
- safety stock will expire before forecast use;
- alternates lack controlled approval evidence;
- actual use, waste and delay cannot be calibrated.
