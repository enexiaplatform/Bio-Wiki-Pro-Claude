# The Annual Product Review: what it's actually for

The Annual Product Review (APR) — the Product Quality Review (PQR) in EU GMP — has a
reputation as a box-ticking report someone compiles once a year. Used well, it is
the opposite: the one scheduled point where you step back and look across **all** of
a product's data to ask a simple question — **is this process still in control, and
do its specifications still fit?**

## What it pulls together

An APR/PQR gathers a year of data for a product into one view, typically including:

- Batch results and **in-process / release trends**
- **Deviations, OOS/OOT, and complaints**, and their CAPAs
- **Change controls** made during the year
- **Stability** data and any adverse trends
- Returns, recalls, and rejected batches
- Starting-material and supplier performance

## What it's for

Pulling the data together is the easy part. The value is in the **analysis**:

- **Is the process still in a state of control?** Look at the trends, not just
  whether each batch passed.
- **Are the specifications still appropriate?** A year of data may show a limit is
  too tight, too loose, or that a new trend has emerged.
- **Are there recurring issues** the day-to-day system has been closing one by one
  without anyone connecting the dots?
- **Do any changes or trends warrant action** — a revalidation, a tighter control, a
  CAPA?

## The common failure

The common failure is treating the APR as a retrospective formality — compiling the
numbers without drawing conclusions. An inspector reads an APR to see whether you
**looked** at your product holistically and **acted** on what you saw. A report with
data but no analysis or actions is a finding waiting to happen.
