# Which water grade does your test need?

Water is the silent variable behind a surprising number of QC results and audit
findings. Use too low a grade on a product-contact step and you risk
contamination; reach for the most controlled grade everywhere and you waste a
scarce resource. The choice is not arbitrary — it follows from what the water
touches.

## The grades, by use

- **Water for Injection (WFI)** — the most controlled compendial grade. Required
  for parenteral products and the final rinse of product-contact surfaces for
  injectables. Endotoxin is limited (not more than 0.25 EU/mL), with tight
  conductivity, TOC, and microbial control.
- **Purified Water (PW)** — the standard grade for non-sterile manufacture,
  cleaning, and as feedwater. Conductivity, TOC, and microbial limits apply;
  there is no compendial endotoxin limit.
- **Reagent-grade water (Type I/II/III)** — laboratory water for analysis. Type I
  (about 18.2 MΩ·cm, very low TOC) is for HPLC, trace, and molecular work; lower
  types cover general rinsing.

## Decision shortcuts

- Parenteral / injectable product-contact or final rinse → **WFI**
- Non-sterile product or cleaning final rinse → **Purified Water**
- HPLC, trace analysis, or molecular biology → **reagent Type I**
- Sterile non-injectable (ophthalmic, inhalation) → start at **Purified Water**,
  but the route and pharmacopeia may require WFI — confirm against your registered
  specification

## Common mistakes

- Using Purified Water on an injectable product-contact step (step up to WFI)
- Ignoring the endotoxin limit where the route demands it
- Treating "reagent grade" as interchangeable with compendial process water — they
  are controlled to different things
- Drawing water from an uncontrolled or unqualified source for a product- or
  sample-contact step
