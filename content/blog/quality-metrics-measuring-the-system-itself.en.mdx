# Quality metrics: measuring the system, not just the product

QC measures products all day — assay, purity, counts. But how do you measure the
**quality system itself**? Is your QMS healthy, or quietly drowning? **Quality metrics**
are how you find out: a small set of indicators that turn the day-to-day churn of
deviations, CAPAs, and changes into a picture of whether the system is in control.

## The metrics that signal health

Regulators and mature companies watch a recurring set, often including:

- **Right-first-time / batch rejection rate** — how often production gets it right
- **Deviation and CAPA cycle time** — how long issues stay open
- **CAPA effectiveness** — and the rate of **recurring** deviations (the real test)
- **Complaint and OOS rates and trends**
- **Overdue records** — change controls, CAPAs, training, periodic reviews
- **On-time stability and APR completion**

The numbers matter less than their **direction**: a rising backlog of overdue CAPAs or a
climbing recurrence rate is a warning long before it becomes a crisis.

## Leading vs lagging indicators

The most useful metrics are **leading** — they predict trouble. A growing CAPA backlog
or slipping training compliance predicts future failures. **Lagging** indicators (a
batch rejection, a recall) tell you about a problem that already happened. A good metrics
program weights toward the leading ones so you can act early.

## The gaming trap

The danger with any metric is that **what you measure, you change** — sometimes the
wrong way. If "number of deviations" becomes a target to minimise, people stop **raising**
deviations, and the metric improves while quality gets worse. A healthy program treats a
**rise** in deviations raised as potentially good (people are looking) and watches the
ones that matter — repeat issues, overdue items, effectiveness — rather than rewarding a
suspiciously quiet record.

## Where it feeds

Quality metrics feed **management review**, where leadership is expected to look at the
state of the system and act — resourcing, escalation, improvement. Metrics without a
management review that acts on them are just numbers in a slide.
