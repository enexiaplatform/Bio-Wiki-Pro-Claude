# The CTD and eCTD: how a regulatory dossier is structured

A marketing application is a large, multidisciplinary package — quality,
non-clinical, and clinical evidence that a medicine is safe, effective, and made to
a consistent standard. The **Common Technical Document (CTD)**, defined in ICH M4,
is the agreed structure that lets one dossier be assembled once and reviewed across
regions.

## The five modules

- **Module 1 — Regional administrative information.** Region-specific: application
  forms, prescribing information, and labelling. This is the only module that is
  **not** harmonised — it differs between FDA, EMA, and other agencies.
- **Module 2 — Summaries and overviews.** The quality overall summary plus the
  non-clinical and clinical overviews and summaries. Reviewers often start here.
- **Module 3 — Quality.** The CMC (chemistry, manufacturing, and controls) data:
  drug substance, drug product, specifications, stability, and the manufacturing
  process.
- **Module 4 — Non-clinical study reports.** Pharmacology, pharmacokinetics, and
  toxicology.
- **Module 5 — Clinical study reports.** The clinical trial data supporting safety
  and efficacy.

A useful mental model: Module 1 is regional, Module 2 summarises, and Modules 3–5
hold the underlying quality, non-clinical, and clinical evidence.

## What the eCTD adds

The **eCTD (electronic CTD)** is the electronic format and lifecycle standard for
submitting the CTD. Beyond a folder of PDFs, it provides:

- A defined **XML backbone** and folder structure agencies can validate.
- **Lifecycle operations** — new, replace, append, delete — so a submission builds
  on the last one rather than resubmitting everything.
- **Hyperlinking and bookmarking** so reviewers can navigate a very large dossier.

Most major agencies now require eCTD for new applications; the structure is the
CTD, the format and lifecycle handling are the eCTD's job.

## Why structure is a regulatory skill

A technically excellent dossier still gets refused or delayed if it is
mis-structured: documents in the wrong module, broken lifecycle, or a Module 1 that
does not meet regional requirements. Knowing the CTD map — and keeping the eCTD
lifecycle clean across variations — is core regulatory craft.
