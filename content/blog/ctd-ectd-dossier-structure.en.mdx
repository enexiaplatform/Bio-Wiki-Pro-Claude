# The CTD and eCTD: how a regulatory dossier is structured

A marketing application is not a pile of study reports. It is a structured
argument that a medicine is safe, effective, and consistently manufactured.

The **Common Technical Document (CTD)** is the map for that argument. It tells a
regulatory team where administrative information, summaries, quality data,
non-clinical reports, and clinical evidence belong. The **electronic Common
Technical Document (eCTD)** is the electronic submission standard that packages
the CTD into a validated, lifecycle-managed format for agencies.

The difference is simple:

- **CTD** is the structure of the dossier.
- **eCTD** is the electronic format and lifecycle mechanism used to submit and
  maintain that dossier.

Regulatory affairs teams need both. A technically strong document in the wrong
module delays review. A correctly written dossier with a broken eCTD lifecycle
can fail technical validation before a reviewer ever reaches the science.

## Why the CTD exists

Before the CTD, companies had to reorganise the same scientific evidence into
different dossier formats for different regions. ICH M4 harmonised the
organisation of the Common Technical Document so that the technical content for
human medicines could be presented in a common structure across major ICH
regions.

That harmonisation does not mean every regional requirement is identical. It
means the shared technical body of the submission follows a common map.

The practical benefit is huge:

- Quality, non-clinical, and clinical teams can write toward one dossier
  structure.
- Reviewers know where to find evidence.
- Global regulatory teams can reuse a shared core dossier.
- Regional teams can adapt Module 1 and local requirements without rebuilding
  the entire application.
- Lifecycle submissions can point to the exact section being changed.

The CTD turns a large development program into a navigable regulatory file.

## The five modules

The CTD has five modules. A useful mental model is:

- Module 1 is regional.
- Module 2 summarises.
- Modules 3, 4, and 5 hold the detailed evidence.

## Module 1: regional administrative information

Module 1 is not harmonised. It is defined by each region.

This is where a US, EU, UK, Japan, Canada, Australia, or other regional dossier
begins to diverge. Module 1 commonly includes:

- application forms;
- cover letters;
- administrative information;
- product information or prescribing information;
- labeling and packaging information;
- patent/exclusivity or legal-basis information where applicable;
- user-fee information;
- environmental assessment or regional declarations;
- pharmacovigilance or risk-management documents;
- pediatric information or waivers/deferrals where applicable;
- regional electronic forms and validation requirements.

Module 1 is where "same product, same science" becomes market-specific
regulatory execution. That is why global dossier reuse always needs local RA
review.

## Module 2: summaries and overviews

Module 2 is the reviewer's guided tour through the evidence.

It includes:

- **2.3 Quality Overall Summary (QOS)**;
- non-clinical overview;
- clinical overview;
- non-clinical written and tabulated summaries;
- clinical summary.

Module 2 should not be a copy-paste collage. It should explain the story. The
QOS should connect manufacturing process, control strategy, specifications,
stability, impurities, analytical methods, and product understanding. The
clinical and non-clinical overviews should help reviewers understand the
benefit-risk logic, not simply list studies.

For regulatory writers and CMC leads, Module 2 is where weak dossier thinking is
often exposed. If the summaries cannot clearly explain why the evidence supports
approval, the detailed reports underneath will feel harder to review.

## Module 3: quality

Module 3 is the CMC heart of the dossier.

It covers the drug substance, drug product, manufacturing process, controls,
specifications, analytical procedures, validation, reference standards, container
closure, stability, facilities or equipment information where applicable, and
regional quality information.

For many RA and CMC teams, Module 3 is the most operationally sensitive module
because it becomes the bridge between the approved dossier and the factory.
After approval, changes to manufacturing sites, process parameters, equipment,
raw materials, analytical methods, specifications, container closure, or shelf
life may require supplements or variations depending on what is registered and
how the change is classified.

A strong Module 3 does not merely describe how the product is made. It explains
how quality is controlled.

## Module 4: non-clinical study reports

Module 4 contains the detailed non-clinical study reports.

This may include pharmacology, pharmacokinetics, toxicology, genotoxicity,
carcinogenicity, reproductive toxicity, local tolerance, safety pharmacology,
and other studies depending on the product and development program.

Module 4 is not usually where a manufacturing site spends its day-to-day energy,
but it matters to the overall benefit-risk argument. Gaps or inconsistencies in
non-clinical evidence can affect clinical justification, labeling, risk
management, and regulatory questions.

## Module 5: clinical study reports

Module 5 contains the detailed clinical evidence.

It includes clinical study reports and related information supporting efficacy,
safety, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, bioavailability, bioequivalence,
population analyses, and other clinical topics depending on the application.

For generics, biosimilars, new indications, new formulations, or 505(b)(2)-type
strategies, Module 5 can vary heavily by legal basis and reliance strategy. The
same CTD module exists, but the evidence package is not the same for every
product.

## How the modules work together

The modules should not feel like five unrelated folders.

A reviewer may start in Module 2, then jump to Module 3 for a specification,
Module 5 for clinical relevance, Module 1 for labeling, and back to Module 3 for
stability or method validation.

Good dossiers support that movement. They use consistent terminology, traceable
document titles, working links, correct cross-references, coherent tables, and
aligned claims. If Module 2 says an impurity is controlled by a validated HPLC
method, Module 3 should contain the method, validation summary, specification,
and batch data that support the claim.

The dossier should read like one product story, not five departmental exports.

## What the eCTD adds

The eCTD allows the CTD to be submitted electronically in a standardised
technical format. FDA describes eCTD as the standard format for submitting
applications, amendments, supplements, and reports to CDER and CBER. EMA's
eSubmission resources describe eCTD as enabling electronic submission of the CTD
from applicant to regulator.

An eCTD submission generally includes:

- a defined folder/module structure;
- XML backbone files;
- regional Module 1 structure;
- document metadata;
- leaf titles and file references;
- checksums and validation information;
- PDF requirements;
- lifecycle operations;
- submission sequence numbers.

This is why eCTD publishing is not just "make PDFs and upload them." It is
controlled regulatory data management.

## Sequence and lifecycle

One of the most important eCTD concepts is the **sequence**.

A sequence is a submitted package in the lifecycle of an application. The first
marketing application might be sequence `0000`. Later responses, amendments,
supplements, variations, annual reports, safety updates, or other submissions
become later sequences.

The application lifecycle is cumulative. Reviewers and agency systems use the
sequence history to understand what is current, what was replaced, what was
deleted, and what was newly submitted.

Common lifecycle operations include:

- **new**: add a document for the first time;
- **replace**: replace an existing document with a new version;
- **delete**: remove a document from the current view where allowed;
- **append**: add material to existing content where supported by the rules.

Lifecycle mistakes can create serious confusion. If a document is submitted as
"new" when it should replace an earlier document, both may remain visible. If an
obsolete method, label, or response is not correctly replaced, the current view
may show conflicting information.

The eCTD lifecycle is the official memory of the application.

## What can go wrong in publishing

Technical validation failures are not glamorous, but they can delay a submission
at the worst possible time.

Common eCTD problems include:

- broken hyperlinks or bookmarks;
- wrong module or section placement;
- incorrect leaf titles;
- duplicate documents;
- PDF files that are not text-searchable where required;
- invalid file names or paths;
- missing forms or cover letters;
- incorrect sequence number;
- wrong lifecycle operation;
- replacing the wrong document;
- missing regional metadata;
- scanned pages with poor quality;
- unresolved comments or tracked changes;
- inconsistent document titles between the cover letter, table of contents, and
  eCTD leaf.

Many of these problems are avoidable if regulatory operations, writers, CMC,
clinical, QA, and publishing teams do not wait until the final day to align.

## CTD structure shapes lifecycle control

The CTD is not only for initial approval. It affects the entire product
lifecycle.

After approval, the dossier becomes the baseline for change control. When a site
wants to change a process parameter, supplier, analytical method, equipment
train, specification, shelf life, container closure, or manufacturing site,
regulatory affairs asks:

- Is this element described in the approved dossier?
- Is it an established condition or binding commitment?
- Which CTD section is affected?
- Is the change regional or global?
- What evidence is needed to support the change?
- Which supplement, variation, notification, or annual report category applies?
- Can implementation occur before approval, after notification, or only after
  formal approval?

This is why CTD knowledge matters outside the submissions team. QA, validation,
manufacturing, QC, supply chain, and technical transfer teams all create changes
that may touch the dossier.

## How to review a dossier section

When reviewing a CTD section, ask practical questions:

1. Is this document in the correct module and section?
2. Does the title match the content and the eCTD leaf title?
3. Does it support the claim made in Module 2?
4. Are cross-references accurate?
5. Are tables, figures, appendices, and study/report IDs consistent?
6. Are regional statements placed in the right region-specific section?
7. Does the document accidentally create a commitment the site cannot maintain?
8. Does it match current process, method, specification, or label reality?
9. Is the PDF technically acceptable for submission?
10. If this is a lifecycle submission, does it replace or update the correct
    prior document?

This is the quiet craft of regulatory operations. It prevents scientific work
from being hidden behind preventable structure errors.

## Common mistakes

CTD and eCTD mistakes often come from treating structure as clerical work.

- Module 2 summaries do not match the detailed evidence in Modules 3-5.
- Module 1 is reused from another region without local review.
- CMC documents are written as site files rather than regulatory arguments.
- Specifications, methods, batch data, and stability commitments are
  inconsistent across sections.
- Hyperlinks point to old or draft document names.
- A late change updates the PDF but not the summary, cover letter, or lifecycle
  operation.
- Publishing finds validation errors after management believes the submission is
  ready.
- Post-approval changes are assessed without checking the approved CTD section.

The fix is not more administrative checking at the end. The fix is building
dossier structure into the work from the beginning.

## Practical CTD/eCTD checklist

Use this checklist before a major submission:

1. Confirm the application type, region, and procedure.
2. Build the CTD table of contents early, not after writing.
3. Assign each document to a module and section owner.
4. Confirm regional Module 1 requirements separately for each market.
5. Align Module 2 summaries with the detailed evidence.
6. Check Module 3 consistency across process, controls, specifications, methods,
   validation, and stability.
7. Verify document titles, version numbers, hyperlinks, bookmarks, and
   cross-references.
8. Confirm PDF technical requirements before publishing.
9. Review eCTD lifecycle operations for every replacement or update.
10. Run technical validation with enough time to correct findings.
11. Keep a submission issue log and decision record.
12. After submission, preserve the final sequence package and correspondence.

## Key takeaway

The CTD gives the dossier its scientific architecture. The eCTD gives that
architecture an electronic lifecycle regulators can receive, validate, review,
and maintain.

Good regulatory teams do not treat the CTD as a filing cabinet or eCTD as a
last-minute publishing chore. They use the structure to make the product story
clear, keep regional differences controlled, and protect the approved dossier
through the product lifecycle.
