# FDA Inspection Readiness: A QC Team's Checklist

FDA inspection readiness is not the panic project that starts when the lobby
calls. It is the normal state of a GMP quality system: records are complete,
data are attributable, investigations are moving, CAPA is effective, and the team
can explain what the evidence means.

For QC teams, readiness is especially visible. Inspectors often ask for raw data,
audit trails, OOS investigations, method validation, stability records,
calibration files, analyst training, laboratory controls, and evidence that
results were reviewed before release decisions.

## What FDA is trying to assess

FDA inspections are not document collection exercises. The investigator is
evaluating whether the facility is operating in compliance with applicable law,
CGMP regulations, applications, and commitments.

For a QC laboratory, that usually means:

- Are test methods suitable, validated, verified, transferred, and controlled?
- Are instruments qualified, calibrated, maintained, and used within their
  intended state?
- Are data complete, original, accurate, available, and protected from
  inappropriate change?
- Are OOS/OOT results investigated before batch disposition?
- Are deviations, CAPA, and change controls scientifically justified and closed
  on time?
- Are analysts trained and qualified for the work they perform?
- Are records retrievable quickly and consistently?

The fastest way to look prepared is to operate that way before anyone arrives.

## Daily readiness controls

Inspection readiness should be reviewed through routine quality metrics, not only
through pre-inspection checklists.

Keep these under control:

- Overdue investigations, CAPA, change controls, and effectiveness checks.
- Unreviewed chromatographic data, audit trails, and electronic records.
- Open instrument qualification, calibration, maintenance, or service events.
- SOPs due for periodic review.
- Training gaps for analysts, reviewers, and SMEs.
- Repeated deviations or lab events with weak root cause.
- Stability pulls, CoA generation, and release testing backlogs.
- Archived data that require vendor help or special access to retrieve.

If a document cannot be produced during normal operations, it will not magically
become inspection-ready during an FDA visit.

## The front room / back room model

Most inspection programs use a two-room operating model:

- **Front room:** hosts the investigator, receives requests, coordinates answers,
  and manages subject-matter expert discussions.
- **Back room:** retrieves records, reviews them for completeness and relevance,
  prepares SMEs, logs requests, and tracks follow-up actions.

The back room should not "sanitize" records or hide relevant information. Its
role is to ensure the response is complete, accurate, and responsive to the
request. Giving the wrong version, an incomplete package, or irrelevant extra
material creates avoidable confusion.

## Request control

Every inspection request should be logged. A useful request log captures:

- request number and exact wording;
- date and time received;
- requester and owner;
- due time or priority;
- document title, number, version, and batch or sample reference;
- who reviewed the response before delivery;
- time delivered;
- open follow-up or clarification.

This log protects consistency. If the investigator asks for a related record two
days later, the team can see exactly what was already provided.

## Document review before delivery

Before giving a record to the investigator, quickly check:

- Is it the correct record, product, batch, sample, method, version, and date?
- Is it complete, including attachments, raw data, calculations, approvals, and
  referenced forms?
- Are signatures, timestamps, audit-trail reviews, and review dates present?
- Are corrections made according to GDP expectations?
- Are there open deviations, OOS results, invalidated runs, or unexplained
  comments that need context?
- Does the record answer the request without adding unrelated material?

If a gap is real, do not try to fix it silently. Escalate, explain the current
state accurately, and document remediation through the quality system.

## Preparing SMEs

A subject-matter expert should be coached on process, not scripted on answers.
Good preparation includes:

- Review the request and the exact records being discussed.
- Explain only what you know from approved procedures and evidence.
- Do not guess, speculate, or fill silence with extra information.
- If you need to check a record, say so.
- Answer the question asked, then stop.
- Be honest about known gaps and the approved remediation plan.

Credibility matters. A calm SME who says "I need to verify that record" is safer
than a confident guess.

## QC areas inspectors often probe

For QC and microbiology, be ready to explain:

- OOS and OOT investigations, including invalidated results.
- Audit-trail review scope, frequency, and exception handling.
- Chromatography integration and reprocessing controls.
- Reference-standard traceability, storage, and use.
- Method validation, verification, transfer, and system suitability.
- Instrument qualification, calibration, maintenance, and access control.
- Data backup, archive retrieval, and complete-copy capability.
- Microbial methods, media growth promotion, organism identification, and trend
  review.
- Stability program execution and missed-pull handling.
- Analyst training, qualification, and periodic requalification.

The strongest teams can connect each answer to evidence without hunting for it.

## If FDA issues a Form 483

FDA Form 483 observations are inspectional observations, not the final agency
compliance determination. They still deserve a disciplined response.

A strong response should:

- Restate each observation accurately.
- Acknowledge the issue where the observation is valid.
- Describe immediate corrections already taken.
- Explain the root-cause investigation plan.
- Define CAPA, owners, and due dates.
- Assess product, patient, data, and batch impact.
- Include objective evidence where actions are complete.
- Avoid vague promises such as "we will retrain personnel" without system-level
  correction.

FDA notes that responses submitted within the expected timeframe can be reviewed
before further enforcement decisions. Speed matters, but quality matters more.

## Common readiness failures

Most poor inspections are not caused by one missing binder. They come from system
signals that were visible earlier:

- investigations closed with weak root cause;
- CAPA effectiveness checks that only confirm a procedure was updated;
- audit trails reviewed after the fact;
- shared logins or uncontrolled administrator access;
- repeated OOS invalidations without a method or training fix;
- calibration events with unclear product impact;
- SOPs that do not match actual lab practice;
- SMEs who cannot explain the record they approved.

Inspection readiness is therefore a quality-system health measure, not a
presentation exercise.
