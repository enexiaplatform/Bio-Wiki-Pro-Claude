# Cleanroom classification under Annex 1 (2022)

Cleanroom classification is often treated as a room label: Grade A, B, C, or D.
Under EU GMP Annex 1 (2022), that label only matters if it is connected to the
operation performed in the area, the contamination control strategy (CCS), and the
monitoring evidence that shows the state remains controlled.

The core question is not "what grade is this room?" It is: **does the classified
environment protect the sterile product at the point of highest contamination
risk?**

## Grade A-D in plain language

Annex 1 uses the cleanroom grade to express how much contamination control is
needed for the activity.

| Grade | Typical meaning | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A | Highest-risk local zone where sterile product, sterile components, or critical surfaces are exposed | Filling point, aseptic connection, stopper bowl, open vial path, critical RABS/isolator zone |
| B | Background for Grade A in aseptic processing when the A zone is not fully isolated | Cleanroom surrounding an open Grade A filling operation |
| C | Controlled area for less critical sterile preparation steps or support operations | Solution preparation before sterile filtration, component preparation, some transfer steps |
| D | Basic classified support area | Earlier-stage preparation, washing, staging, lower-risk support activities |

This table is a starting point, not a design rule. Barrier technology, process
closure, intervention frequency, material transfer, and product exposure time can
change the risk profile.

## Classification is not the same as monitoring

Two related activities are easy to confuse:

1. **Classification or qualification** proves that the cleanroom or clean zone can
   meet the required airborne particle state under defined conditions.
2. **Routine monitoring** provides evidence that the area remains controlled during
   manufacturing and that trends are understood.

Classification is usually periodic and formal. Monitoring is continual or routine,
depending on grade and risk. A room can pass classification and still have a weak
control program if its routine monitoring does not detect drift, intervention risk,
or recurring microbial patterns.

## At rest vs in operation

Annex 1 expects cleanrooms to be considered in two states:

- **At rest:** equipment installed and operating, but no personnel performing the
  process.
- **In operation:** personnel, equipment, and process activities running in the
  normal or simulated operating state.

The in-operation state matters most for aseptic processing because people,
interventions, material movement, and equipment motion are often the real
contamination risks. A clean at-rest room does not prove the process remains clean
while operators are making aseptic connections or clearing stoppages.

## Particle limits are only part of the story

Annex 1 classification tables focus on airborne particle concentrations, but a
sterile control strategy needs more than particle counts. For release and
inspection readiness, connect classification to:

- viable air, surface, and personnel monitoring;
- airflow visualization and first-air protection;
- pressure cascades and airlock behavior;
- HEPA integrity, airflow velocity where relevant, and recovery testing;
- intervention design and operator qualification;
- cleaning and disinfection effectiveness;
- material and component transfer controls;
- contamination trends and investigation history.

If these controls are disconnected, the grade becomes a badge rather than evidence.

## How CCS changes the conversation

The 2022 Annex 1 revision makes the CCS the organizing logic for sterile
manufacturing. Cleanroom classification should therefore be justified inside the
CCS, not buried in an HVAC qualification file.

A useful CCS narrative answers:

- What contamination risks exist at each process step?
- Which areas require Grade A, B, C, or D and why?
- Where is product, sterile component, or sterile product-contact surface exposed?
- Which operations generate particles or microbial risk?
- What monitoring sites prove the controls are working?
- What trends would trigger escalation before an action-limit failure?
- How do changes to rooms, equipment, process, cleaning, or personnel feed back into
  classification and monitoring?

The best version of this is a living map: process step -> contamination risk ->
area grade -> engineering control -> monitoring evidence -> escalation rule.

## Common inspection gaps

Auditors rarely stop at "show me the classification report." Expect them to probe
whether the grade is meaningful in operation.

Common gaps include:

- Grade A/B monitoring locations chosen for convenience rather than risk.
- Classification reports not linked to the current equipment layout.
- Smoke studies that do not match routine interventions.
- Requalification not triggered after HVAC, room, equipment, or process changes.
- At-rest data used to defend in-operation contamination risk.
- Pressure cascade alarms reviewed as utilities data rather than contamination
  control data.
- Microbial trends discussed only after action-limit failures.
- CCS documents listing cleanroom grades without explaining why they are adequate.

## A practical review checklist

Use this when reviewing a cleanroom or preparing for an Annex 1 walk-through:

- Confirm each Grade A-D area has a documented process purpose.
- Map product exposure and sterile surface exposure points.
- Check whether the classification state reflects actual equipment and layout.
- Compare monitoring sites with the current process risk map.
- Review Grade A/B intervention records against EM and particle trends.
- Confirm HVAC qualification, HEPA integrity, pressure cascade, airflow
  visualization, and recovery evidence are current.
- Verify change controls assessed impact on classification and monitoring.
- Confirm the CCS explains the grade decisions in operational language.

## Bottom line

Cleanroom classification is not a one-time certificate. It is part of the sterile
manufacturing control system. Annex 1 expects the grade, the process, the CCS, and
the monitoring data to tell one consistent story: the product is protected where it
is most vulnerable, and the site can prove that protection remains effective.
