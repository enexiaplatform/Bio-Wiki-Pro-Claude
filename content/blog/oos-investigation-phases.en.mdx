# OOS Investigations: Phase I, Phase II, and the Barr Lesson

Out-of-specification (OOS) results are where laboratory science, batch
disposition, and data integrity collide.

FDA's OOS guidance applies to test results that fall outside established
specifications or acceptance criteria, including in-process laboratory tests. 21
CFR 211.192 also requires any unexplained discrepancy or failure of a batch or
component to meet specifications to be thoroughly investigated, whether or not
the batch has already been distributed.

The practical mindset is simple: treat the OOS as real until objective evidence
shows otherwise.

## What counts as OOS?

An OOS result is not limited to final release assay failure. It can include:

- assay, impurity, dissolution, content uniformity, water, residual solvent, or
  other chemistry results outside specification,
- in-process laboratory tests outside established limits,
- stability results outside approved acceptance criteria,
- compendial test failures,
- validated method acceptance failures where the reported sample result is
  outside the defined criterion.

OOS is different from OOT (out-of-trend), but the two can interact. An OOT may
not fail a specification, yet it can still trigger investigation if the trend is
scientifically meaningful.

## Phase I: the immediate laboratory assessment

Phase I asks whether there is an obvious, scientifically supported laboratory
error. It should begin promptly, while the analyst's memory and lab evidence are
still fresh.

The analyst and supervisor should review:

- sample identity, preparation, dilution, and transfer steps,
- calculations, transcription, and unit conversions,
- standards, reagents, columns, media, and solution expiry,
- instrument status, calibration, maintenance, and run logs,
- system suitability and method acceptance criteria,
- chromatograms, spectra, raw data, audit trail, and integration events,
- analyst observations recorded at the time of testing,
- environmental or equipment conditions that could affect the result.

The goal is not to brainstorm excuses. The goal is to find an assignable cause
supported by evidence.

## When can the original result be invalidated?

An OOS result may be invalidated only when the investigation identifies a
scientifically sound cause that shows the result does not reflect the sample or
batch.

Stronger examples:

- wrong standard concentration confirmed by preparation record and independent
  calculation,
- instrument malfunction documented by logs and affecting the run,
- sample preparation error witnessed or proven from contemporaneous records,
- integration or processing error corrected according to a justified, approved
  method rule,
- system suitability failure that invalidates the run under predefined criteria.

Weak examples:

- "analyst error" with no objective evidence,
- "sample probably degraded" with no supporting handling record,
- "retest passed",
- "historically this batch family is fine",
- "supervisor judgment."

If the cause is not proven, the original result remains part of the batch record.

## Phase II: the full-scale investigation

If Phase I does not identify a confirmed laboratory error, the investigation must
expand beyond the lab.

Phase II should examine:

- manufacturing records and process parameters,
- raw material, component, and supplier history,
- equipment cleaning, setup, hold time, and maintenance,
- deviations, alarms, interventions, and yield discrepancies,
- previous batch history and trend data,
- stability or retain sample information where relevant,
- other lots, strengths, methods, or products that could share the same cause,
- potential patient, product, and compliance impact.

This is where the question changes from "did the lab make a mistake?" to "what
does this result tell us about the batch and the process?"

## Hypothesis testing is not testing into compliance

Additional laboratory work may be appropriate, but it must be planned.

A good hypothesis-testing plan defines:

1. the hypothesis being tested,
2. the scientific reason for the test,
3. the number of tests or preparations,
4. the acceptance logic,
5. how all results will be evaluated,
6. who approves the plan before testing starts.

Testing should not continue until a passing result appears. That is the core
Barr lesson: repeated testing without a predefined scientific plan can turn the
investigation into a search for a compliant number.

## Retesting, resampling, and averaging

These three terms are often confused.

| Action | Meaning | Key control |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Retest | Test a new preparation from the original sample or retained sample | Predefined number of replicates and scientific rationale |
| Resample | Collect a new sample from the batch or lot | Justified only when the original sample may not represent the batch |
| Averaging | Combine results mathematically | Appropriate only when the method and specification support averaging |

Retesting does not automatically erase the first OOS. Resampling is not a way to
avoid an inconvenient original sample. Averaging is not acceptable when it hides
unexplained variability or when the specification applies to individual results.

All valid results need to be considered in the final evaluation.

## Final evaluation and batch disposition

The final investigation report should answer:

- Was the original OOS confirmed, invalidated, or unresolved?
- What evidence supports that conclusion?
- Were all original data, audit trails, calculations, and repeat tests reviewed?
- Did the investigation extend to manufacturing when needed?
- Were related batches, products, or stability lots assessed?
- Is there a product quality or patient risk?
- Is CAPA required?
- What result is reportable, and why?
- Who approved the disposition?

The batch disposition decision should not be a separate administrative step
detached from the science. It should flow from the evidence.

## Common inspection traps

- Starting retests before the Phase I assessment is complete.
- Invalidating OOS results based on vague analyst error.
- Missing audit-trail review for changed integration, repeated injections, or
  deleted sequences.
- Failing to investigate manufacturing when lab error is not proven.
- Using a passing retest as the only reason to release the batch.
- Averaging valid OOS and passing results without scientific justification.
- Not assessing other batches that may share the same root cause.
- Closing the investigation without CAPA when the cause is systemic.

Most OOS findings are not about the existence of one bad number. They are about
the quality of the decision made after the bad number appeared.
