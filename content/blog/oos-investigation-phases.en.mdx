# OOS Investigations: Phase I, Phase II, and the Barr lesson

Out-of-Specification (OOS) results are where laboratory data integrity is tested
hardest. The FDA's OOS guidance — rooted in the 1993 *United States v. Barr
Laboratories* decision — sets clear expectations.

## Phase I — the laboratory investigation

Before anything else, determine whether the result is a genuine lab error. The
analyst and supervisor review:

- Calculations and transcription.
- Instrument performance and system suitability.
- Standards, reagents, and sample preparation.

A lab error must be **assignable with objective evidence** — "the analyst was
new" is not a root cause.

## Phase II — the full-scale investigation

If no lab error is confirmed, the OOS is potentially real. A Phase II
investigation extends to manufacturing: batch record review, process review, and
a documented hypothesis-driven testing plan.

## You can't retest into compliance

The Barr lesson is blunt: you cannot discard an OOS simply because a retest
passes. Retesting and resampling require a scientifically sound, pre-approved
rationale — not repetition until you get the answer you want. Averaging away an
OOS, or testing into compliance, is a classic warning-letter finding.

> Treat every OOS as real until objective evidence proves otherwise. That mindset
> keeps both your product and your data integrity defensible.
