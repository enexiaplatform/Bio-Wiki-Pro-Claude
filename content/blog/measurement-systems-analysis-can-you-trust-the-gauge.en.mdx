# Measurement systems analysis: can you trust the gauge?

Here is a question that should come before any trend chart: **how much of the variation
you are seeing is the product, and how much is the measurement?** If a chunk of your
"process variation" is actually measurement noise, you will chase ghosts — investigating
shifts that are really just your gauge. Measurement systems analysis (MSA) is how you
find out.

## The measurement is part of the variation

Every result is the true value plus measurement error. A measurement system has its own
variation, and if it is large relative to the thing you are measuring, the data becomes
hard to trust. MSA quantifies that measurement variation so you know whether your gauge
is sharp enough for the decision you are making.

## Gage R&R: repeatability and reproducibility

The workhorse study is **Gage R&R**, which splits measurement variation into two parts:

- **Repeatability** — the variation when the **same** person measures the **same** item
  repeatedly with the **same** equipment (the instrument's own scatter)
- **Reproducibility** — the variation **between** different operators (or instruments)
  measuring the same items (the human/setup contribution)

Together they tell you how much of your observed variation is the measurement system
rather than the product. If that share is large, you cannot reliably tell good from bad,
or detect a real shift.

## Why it underpins trending and specifications

MSA is the quiet foundation under SPC and trending. A control chart only separates signal
from noise if the **measurement noise** is small enough to begin with. And a
specification is only meaningful if you can measure against it precisely enough to decide
conformance. A capable process measured by an incapable gauge can look out of control —
or, worse, a drifting process can hide inside measurement noise.
