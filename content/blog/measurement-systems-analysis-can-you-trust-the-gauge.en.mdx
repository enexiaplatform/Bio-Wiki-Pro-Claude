# Measurement systems analysis: can you trust the gauge?

Before you trend a process, calculate capability, open an OOT investigation, or
argue that a shift is real, ask one uncomfortable question:

**How much of the variation you are seeing is the product or process, and how much
is the measurement system?**

If a large share of the variation is measurement noise, the chart is not telling
you the truth you think it is telling. You may chase false signals, miss real
drift, reject good material, accept bad material, or write CAPAs for problems that
belong to the gauge, method, analyst, fixture, software, or sample-preparation
step.

Measurement systems analysis, or MSA, is the discipline that answers whether the
measurement system is good enough for the decision being made.

## The measurement system is bigger than the instrument

In everyday language, people say "the gauge" or "the instrument." In MSA, the
measurement system includes everything that can affect the result:

- instrument or apparatus;
- analyst or operator;
- sample preparation;
- fixture, probe, column, consumable, or reagent;
- method instructions and calculations;
- environmental conditions;
- software settings and integration rules;
- reference standards or controls;
- sampling location and timing;
- unit-to-unit or sample homogeneity.

That broader view matters. A balance may be calibrated, but the weighing process
can still be poor because static charge, sample handling, minimum weight, or
analyst technique creates variation. An HPLC system may pass calibration and system
suitability, but integration rules or sample preparation may still create
measurement inconsistency. A visual inspection method may fail because inspectors
interpret defects differently, not because the lamp is wrong.

## Why MSA belongs before SPC and capability

SPC and capability analysis assume that the data are a useful signal from the
process. MSA checks whether that assumption is true.

If the measurement system is noisy, three bad things happen:

- **False alarms** - normal measurement scatter looks like process movement.
- **Missed signals** - real process drift is hidden inside measurement noise.
- **Bad capability estimates** - Cp, Cpk, Pp, and Ppk include measurement variation,
  so the process may look worse or better than it is.

This is why MSA should come before a serious trend program. A control chart can
only separate signal from noise if the measurement system is not the dominant
source of noise.

FDA's process validation guidance emphasizes understanding sources of variation
and using data during continued process verification. MSA is one of the practical
ways to avoid treating measurement noise as process knowledge.

## Repeatability and reproducibility

The classic variable-data study is **Gage R&R**, short for repeatability and
reproducibility.

- **Repeatability** is variation when the same operator measures the same item
  repeatedly with the same equipment under the same conditions. It is often called
  equipment variation, but it can include short-term method and setup noise.
- **Reproducibility** is variation between operators, instruments, sites, shifts, or
  other conditions. It captures whether different people or setups get the same
  answer on the same items.

Together, repeatability and reproducibility estimate how much of the observed
variation comes from the measurement system rather than from the parts, samples, or
batches being measured.

In analytical method language, this overlaps with precision. ICH Q2(R2) describes
precision at levels such as repeatability, intermediate precision, and
reproducibility. MSA is not a replacement for analytical method validation, but the
logic is similar: the method must be precise enough for its intended use.

## A practical Gage R&R design

A basic crossed Gage R&R study often uses:

- 10 representative parts, units, samples, or standards covering the expected range;
- 2 or 3 operators;
- 2 or 3 repeated measurements per operator per item;
- randomized measurement order;
- the same procedure and conditions used in routine work;
- analysis by ANOVA or an appropriate variance-component method.

The study should include real variation. If all items are nearly identical, the
study may falsely make the measurement system look terrible because there is little
part-to-part variation to compare against. If the items are unrealistically spread
out, the system may look better than it will near the actual decision point.

Choose samples that represent the range where decisions are made, especially near
specification limits, alert/action limits, or trend thresholds.

## What %GRR means

One common output is **%GRR**, the proportion of variation due to the measurement
system. It can be expressed relative to total process variation or relative to the
tolerance.

A common rule of thumb is:

| %GRR | Typical interpretation |
| --- | --- |
| Less than 10% | Measurement system is generally acceptable |
| 10-30% | May be acceptable depending on risk, cost, use, and available alternatives |
| Greater than 30% | Measurement system usually needs improvement before relying on the data |

These thresholds are not universal law. A safety-critical sterility-related
decision may need a stronger system than a low-risk screening measurement. A
development study may tolerate more noise than a release test. A destructive or
biological assay may require a more nuanced justification than a simple dimensional
gauge.

The key is to connect %GRR to the intended decision. If the measurement system
cannot reliably distinguish pass from fail, the data are not fit for release,
investigation, or process-control decisions.

## ndc: can the system see categories?

Another useful output is **ndc**, the number of distinct categories the measurement
system can distinguish across the observed part-to-part variation.

Low ndc means the system cannot separate items into many meaningful levels. In a
trend or capability program, low ndc is a warning that the chart may be built on
rounded, coarse, or noisy data. You may see flat lines, sudden jumps, or artificial
patterns that reflect the measurement system rather than the process.

For SPC, a higher ndc is usually needed because you are not only asking "pass or
fail?" You are asking whether the process is drifting before it fails.

## Attribute measurement systems need a different study

Not every measurement is a number. Some systems classify results:

- pass/fail visual inspection;
- defect present/absent;
- colony morphology category;
- color match;
- particle accept/reject;
- label inspection decision;
- manual review of chromatographic integration;
- investigation coding or root-cause category.

For attribute systems, MSA asks whether appraisers agree with themselves, agree
with each other, and agree with a known reference or expert standard. The output
may include within-appraiser agreement, between-appraiser agreement, false
acceptance, false rejection, or kappa-type statistics.

Attribute MSA is especially valuable when procedures say "obvious," "clear,"
"acceptable," or "major defect" but people interpret those words differently.

## MSA does not replace calibration or validation

Calibration, qualification, validation, system suitability, and MSA answer
different questions.

- **Calibration** asks whether an instrument reads correctly against a reference
  standard.
- **Qualification** asks whether the instrument or equipment is suitable for its
  intended use.
- **Method validation** asks whether the analytical procedure performs for its
  intended purpose.
- **System suitability** asks whether the system is performing for a specific run.
- **MSA** asks whether the whole measurement process has enough discrimination and
  consistency for the decision.

You can have a calibrated instrument and a poor measurement system. You can have a
validated method and still see poor reproducibility after transfer if analysts,
sample prep, or fixtures differ. You can pass system suitability and still have an
attribute inspection system where inspectors disagree.

## When to run MSA

Run or revisit MSA when:

- launching a new critical measurement;
- using data for SPC, CPV, capability, or process-control decisions;
- setting or tightening specifications, alert limits, or action limits;
- transferring a method, process, instrument, or site;
- changing operators, fixtures, software, sample preparation, or integration rules;
- investigating repeated OOS/OOT or borderline results;
- qualifying an automated inspection or classification system;
- introducing a new measurement technology;
- seeing unexpected variation that may be method- or analyst-driven;
- defending a control strategy in an audit or regulatory submission.

MSA is not only for automotive-style gauges. The same thinking applies to laboratory
assays, microbiology reads, environmental monitoring classifications, visual
inspection, dissolution apparatus checks, and process sensors.

## Diagnosing the failure

The value of MSA is not just the final number. It tells you where to improve.

If **repeatability** dominates, look at:

- instrument resolution or noise;
- fixture or probe contact;
- sample stability;
- environmental conditions;
- preparation technique;
- method robustness;
- calibration or maintenance;
- software/integration variability.

If **reproducibility** dominates, look at:

- operator training;
- ambiguous procedure language;
- different setup practices;
- site or shift differences;
- subjective judgement;
- inconsistent sample handling;
- uncontrolled timing or environmental conditions.

If **part-to-part variation** is too low for the study, redesign the study with
more representative samples. A bad study design can make a usable system look
unusable, or hide a weak system.

## What to do when MSA fails

Do not simply file the study and keep trending.

Possible actions include:

- clarify the procedure;
- train or qualify operators;
- improve fixtures, tooling, or sample presentation;
- increase instrument resolution;
- control temperature, humidity, vibration, or lighting;
- standardize sample preparation timing;
- revise integration or classification rules;
- use averaging or replicate strategy where scientifically justified;
- change the method or technology;
- widen or re-evaluate decision rules only if scientifically and regulatory
  justified;
- rerun MSA after changes to prove the fix worked.

If the data have already been used for release, validation, or investigations, a
quality assessment may be needed to determine whether past decisions remain
defensible.

## Common weak spots

MSA programs often fail in predictable ways:

- The study uses easy samples instead of samples near decision limits.
- Operators know the expected answers, biasing the result.
- The measurement order is not randomized.
- Repeat measurements are taken from memory rather than independently.
- The study ignores sample instability or destructive testing.
- Attribute studies have no agreed reference standard.
- %GRR is reported without explaining whether it is against tolerance or total
  variation.
- The conclusion says "acceptable" without linking to intended use.
- SPC is launched before the measurement system is shown fit for trending.
- A failed MSA leads only to retraining, even when the fixture or method is the real
  cause.

The best MSA reports are not long. They are clear about the decision, study design,
results, interpretation, and improvement actions.

## Review checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing a measurement system:

- What decision will this measurement support?
- Is the measurement variable or attribute?
- Are samples representative of routine work and decision limits?
- Are operators, instruments, sites, shifts, and conditions representative?
- Was the order randomized and were repeats independent?
- Are repeatability and reproducibility separated?
- Is %GRR calculated against the right denominator?
- Is ndc adequate for trending or capability use?
- For attribute systems, is there an agreed reference or expert standard?
- Are calibration, qualification, method validation, and system suitability current
  where relevant?
- If MSA failed or was marginal, were corrective actions implemented and verified?
- Does the conclusion say whether the system is fit for the intended decision?

## Key takeaway

MSA is the reality check under trending. Before treating data as process truth,
prove the measurement system can see the difference you care about. Otherwise the
organization may be investigating the gauge, rewarding noise, or missing the real
signal hiding underneath it.
