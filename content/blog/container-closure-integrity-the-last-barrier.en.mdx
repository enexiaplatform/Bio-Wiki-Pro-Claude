# Container closure integrity: the last barrier to a sterile product

You can validate the fill, qualify the operators, and pass every sterility test —
and still ship a non-sterile product if the **container cannot keep it sterile** for
its whole shelf life. Container closure integrity (CCI) is the last barrier in the
sterility assurance story, and Annex 1 (2022) treats it as a formal part of that
story, not an afterthought.

## What CCI is testing

CCI asks a simple question with serious consequences: **can a microorganism (or a
loss of the protective headspace) get into the container?** A leak that lets
microbes in defeats everything upstream. So CCI is verified across the product
lifecycle — during development, validation, and on stability — not just once.

## Deterministic vs probabilistic methods

CCI methods fall into two families, and the trend is clear:

- **Probabilistic** methods — like the traditional **microbial immersion** and
  **dye-ingress** tests — rely on chance: whether an organism or dye happens to
  migrate through a leak during the test. They are variable and hard to quantify.
- **Deterministic** methods — like **vacuum/pressure decay, helium leak,
  high-voltage leak detection, and headspace analysis** — measure a physical
  property directly, give quantitative results, and are more sensitive and
  repeatable.

Regulators increasingly prefer **deterministic** methods, and the field has moved
away from dye ingress as a primary release tool toward validated physical methods.

## Why it pairs with the whole CCS

CCI does not stand alone — it sits inside the contamination control strategy
alongside the sterilisation/aseptic process and environmental control. A robust
container/closure system, a validated sealing process, and a sensitive CCI method
together make the claim that the product **stays** sterile credible.
