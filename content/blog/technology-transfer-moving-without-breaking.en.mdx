# Technology transfer: moving a process without breaking it

Sooner or later a product moves — to a new site, a CMO, or a scaled-up line. On
paper a technology transfer is a project; in reality it is a **stress test of how
well you actually understand your process.** A process that runs fine in one pair of
hands, on one set of equipment, can behave very differently somewhere else if the
understanding behind it never transferred with it.

## What actually gets transferred

A good tech transfer moves more than a batch record:

- The **process and method** themselves, with their parameters and ranges
- The **knowledge** — why the parameters are what they are, what is critical, where
  the process is sensitive (this is the part most often lost)
- The **control strategy** — specifications, in-process controls, and the analytical
  methods
- The **validation/qualification status** of equipment and methods at the receiving
  site

## The gap assessment is the heart of it

The most valuable early step is a **gap assessment**: compare the sending and
receiving sites — equipment, scale, utilities, analysts, environment — and identify
where they differ. Each difference is a risk that the process may not behave the same
way, and it tells you what to verify before you rely on the transfer.

## Method transfer is its own discipline

Transferring an **analytical method** is a transfer in miniature, with defined
options — comparative testing (both labs run the method), co-validation, or a
documented justification for transfer waiver. The goal is the same: prove the
receiving lab gets equivalent, reliable results before it starts releasing product.

## Why knowledge beats paperwork

The transfers that fail usually had complete documentation and incomplete
understanding. The records said *what* to do; nobody captured *why*, so the receiving
site could not troubleshoot the first time the process drifted. Transferring the
know-how — the rationale, the failure modes, the tribal knowledge — is what makes a
transfer stick.
