# Technology transfer: moving a process without breaking it

Sooner or later a product moves. It may move from development to commercial
manufacturing, from one site to another, from an originator to a CMO, from a pilot
line to a scaled-up line, from one QC lab to another, or from an old facility to a
new one.

On a project plan, this is "technology transfer." In real life, it is a stress
test of how well the organization understands the product and process.

A weak transfer moves documents. A strong transfer moves knowledge.

That difference matters. A batch record can tell the receiving site what to do.
It may not explain why the process is sensitive to a mixing order, a hold time, a
raw material property, a column lot, an equipment geometry, a cleaning step, or a
sampling location. The transfer fails when the receiving site follows the words
but does not inherit the understanding.

## What technology transfer is actually transferring

WHO describes technology transfer as a documented, planned process between a
sending unit and a receiving unit, performed by trained and knowledgeable people
inside a quality system. That is a useful starting point because it keeps the
focus on planning, people, knowledge, and quality oversight, not only files.

A good transfer moves:

| Transfer object | What the receiving site needs |
| --- | --- |
| Product knowledge | QTPP, CQAs, formulation rationale, stability risks, impurity risks, microbial risks, device or pack risks. |
| Process knowledge | Unit operations, CPPs, CMAs, scale-up history, known sensitivities, normal variability, failure modes. |
| Control strategy | Specifications, IPCs, sampling points, hold times, monitoring, alarms, release tests, stability commitments. |
| Analytical methods | Methods, validation status, transfer strategy, system suitability, critical reagents, reference standards. |
| Equipment and facility fit | Equipment equivalence, utilities, room classification, cleaning, containment, automation, data systems. |
| Quality system controls | Deviations, change control, CAPA, training, documentation, data integrity, supplier and quality agreements. |
| Regulatory commitments | Approved dossier, established conditions, variations, post-approval commitments, market-specific constraints. |
| Tacit know-how | Practical experience, troubleshooting history, edge cases, "do not do this" lessons, and operator technique. |

If the transfer package only contains SOPs and executed batch records, it is
probably missing the part that prevents avoidable surprises.

## Sending unit and receiving unit

The sending unit owns the process knowledge. The receiving unit must prove it can
reproduce the process and control strategy under its own equipment, people,
systems, utilities, suppliers, and quality procedures.

Both sides have responsibilities.

The sending unit should provide:

- development and validation history;
- CQA, CPP, and material-attribute rationale;
- process description and batch record logic;
- process performance history and trend data;
- known failure modes and troubleshooting experience;
- analytical methods and method lifecycle information;
- reference standards and critical reagents;
- cleaning, hold-time, stability, packaging, and shipping knowledge;
- prior deviations, CAPA, complaints, and recurring weak points;
- regulatory commitments and dossier constraints.

The receiving unit should demonstrate:

- equipment and utility suitability;
- facility and environmental fit;
- trained personnel and supervision;
- approved local procedures;
- data systems and data-integrity controls;
- supplier and material readiness;
- analytical laboratory readiness;
- calibration, maintenance, and qualification status;
- change-control and deviation pathways;
- ability to execute and monitor the process reproducibly.

Technology transfer is not a one-way download. It is a controlled collaboration.

## The transfer package

The transfer package should let a new site understand the process without relying
on memory or informal coaching.

A practical package includes:

- product overview and quality target;
- approved and proposed manufacturing process;
- process flow diagram and critical steps;
- list of CQAs, CPPs, CMAs, and IPCs;
- development reports and scale-up rationale;
- process validation and PPQ history;
- CPV and APR/PQR trend summaries;
- batch records, master formulas, sampling plans, and yield expectations;
- raw material, excipient, API, component, supplier, and CoA requirements;
- equipment train and proposed equivalence assessment;
- cleaning validation and hold-time information;
- environmental, utility, and contamination-control requirements;
- analytical methods, validation reports, method transfer plans, and reference
  standard controls;
- stability data and ongoing stability commitments;
- packaging, labeling, serialization, and shipping requirements;
- quality agreement and responsibility matrix;
- regulatory filing status, approved variations, and market-specific constraints;
- risk assessment, gap assessment, and transfer protocol.

This is where [Quality by Design](/blog/quality-by-design-building-quality-in)
becomes practical. If the CQA/CPP/control strategy logic is weak, the receiving
site receives a recipe instead of a process.

## The gap assessment is the heart of transfer

The gap assessment compares the sending site and receiving site. It is the moment
where assumptions become visible.

A useful gap assessment covers:

- equipment type, model, working volume, geometry, mixing, heat transfer, hold-up
  volume, automation, and control loops;
- utilities such as water, steam, gases, HVAC, compressed air, and environmental
  controls;
- raw material suppliers, grades, particle size, bioburden, impurity profiles, or
  excipient functionality;
- process scale, batch size, line speed, filling speed, filtration area, or
  lyophilizer loading pattern;
- cleaning agents, cleaning equipment, dirty and clean hold times;
- analytical instruments, columns, reagents, standards, software, and sample
  handling;
- warehouse, cold chain, shipping lane, and distribution differences;
- data systems, audit trails, review workflows, and electronic signatures;
- local SOPs, training, deviation, CAPA, and change-control practices;
- regulatory commitments and market approvals.

Each gap should have a risk statement and a verification plan. "Different mixer"
is not yet a risk assessment. "Different impeller geometry may change shear and
dissolution endpoint, affecting particle size distribution and assay uniformity"
is closer to useful.

## Receiving-site readiness

A transfer should not begin commercial execution until the receiving site is
ready. Readiness is more than scheduling a batch.

Check:

- facility qualification and line clearance;
- equipment qualification and calibration;
- utility qualification and monitoring status;
- material and supplier qualification;
- approved master batch record or local equivalent;
- trained operators, analysts, QA reviewers, engineers, and supervisors;
- analytical method transfer or verification complete;
- reference standards, columns, media, reagents, filters, and critical consumables
  available;
- cleaning validation or cleaning verification strategy approved;
- environmental monitoring, contamination-control, or aseptic process controls
  defined where relevant;
- deviation and escalation plan agreed;
- batch disposition and regulatory release pathway understood.

The best readiness reviews are cross-functional. Manufacturing, QC, QA,
validation, engineering, regulatory, supply chain, and the sending unit should all
have a voice.

## The transfer protocol

The protocol turns the transfer plan into executable evidence.

A strong protocol defines:

- scope: product, process, site, line, scale, batch size, markets, and methods;
- roles: sending unit, receiving unit, QA, QC, validation, engineering, regulatory;
- prerequisites and readiness criteria;
- transfer batches: engineering, demonstration, exhibit, PPQ, or validation
  batches as applicable;
- sampling plan and extra characterization where needed;
- process parameters, IPCs, CPP monitoring, and alarm handling;
- acceptance criteria for yield, quality attributes, impurities, microbial
  control, hold times, cleaning, and analytical comparability;
- deviation handling and stop/go criteria;
- documentation and data review expectations;
- regulatory or quality approvals required before routine production;
- final report content and knowledge handover.

The protocol should be written so a failed batch teaches something. If a transfer
batch only has pass/fail release criteria, it may miss the learning needed to make
the next batch better.

## Engineering batches, PPQ, and commercial confidence

Not every transfer has the same batch strategy. The number and type of transfer
batches depend on product risk, process complexity, prior knowledge, site
differences, scale change, and regulatory commitments.

Common stages include:

| Stage | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Engineering or trial batch | Learn how the process behaves at the receiving site without relying on it for commercial supply. |
| Demonstration or transfer batch | Show the receiving site can execute the process and collect enhanced data. |
| PPQ or validation batch | Confirm the commercial process performs reproducibly under the approved control strategy. |
| Continued process verification | Monitor routine batches to confirm the transferred state remains controlled. |

This connects directly to [Process validation: the three stages](/blog/process-validation-three-stages)
and the [Process Validation workflow](/workflows/process-validation). Transfer is
not separate from validation. It is one of the moments where validation knowledge
is tested under new conditions.

## Analytical method transfer can make or break the project

Process transfer often stalls because method transfer was treated as a side task.

If the receiving lab cannot produce equivalent and reliable data, it cannot
release the product confidently, investigate deviations, trend CPV, or prove
comparability. The manufacturing process may be fine while the transfer still
fails because the method is not yet under control.

Analytical transfer should define:

- method purpose and reportable result;
- transfer strategy: comparative testing, co-validation, revalidation,
  verification, or justified waiver;
- sample set and number of lots;
- acceptance criteria and statistical approach;
- system suitability and control sample behavior;
- instrument, column, reagent, standard, software, and analyst differences;
- sample preparation, stability, shipping, and storage conditions;
- handling of bias, variability, invalid runs, and outliers;
- documentation and approval before GMP use.

For deeper method logic, use [Analytical Method Transfer](/library/analytical-method-transfer)
and [Analytical method validation: what ICH Q2 actually asks](/blog/analytical-method-validation-what-q2-asks).

## Comparability is not sameness by assertion

A transfer asks whether the receiving site can produce product of comparable
quality using the approved or proposed control strategy.

Comparability may include:

- release results and in-process controls;
- impurity and degradation profiles;
- dissolution, particle size, potency, or biological activity;
- microbial, endotoxin, or sterility-assurance controls;
- yield, cycle time, hold time, and process capability;
- stability data or stability commitments;
- additional characterization for biologics or complex products;
- trend comparison to historical sending-site data.

For biologics, comparability can be especially sensitive because small process
differences may shift glycosylation, charge variants, aggregation, host-cell
protein profile, potency, or other quality attributes. For sterile products,
equipment, environment, interventions, filtration, and container closure controls
can matter as much as the formula.

The conclusion should not be "the batch passed." The conclusion should explain
why the evidence supports routine manufacture at the receiving site.

## Regulatory and post-approval implications

A site transfer can be a regulatory change, not only an internal project.

The regulatory assessment should ask:

- Is the receiving site already approved for the relevant market and product?
- Does the transfer change manufacturing site, equipment, process, batch size,
  controls, specifications, analytical method, packaging, or supplier?
- Are established conditions or dossier commitments affected?
- Is prior approval, notification, variation, or annual report required?
- Is a post-approval change management protocol or comparable mechanism available?
- Are validation, stability, comparability, and inspection readiness sufficient
  for the filing route?
- Are markets different in their expectations?

ICH Q12 is relevant because it frames lifecycle management of post-approval CMC
changes. It does not make changes automatic. It helps teams define what is
reportable, what is managed under the PQS, and what evidence supports the change.

## What weak transfers look like

Failed transfers tend to rhyme.

Common failure modes include:

- transfer package contains documents but not rationale;
- gap assessment lists differences without risk or verification plan;
- receiving site readiness is assumed because the line is available;
- analytical method transfer starts too late;
- engineering batches are treated as commercial proof without enough data;
- equipment "equivalence" ignores geometry, control loops, or scale effects;
- raw material differences are underestimated;
- cleaning and hold-time controls are copied without local fit;
- deviations during transfer are normalized instead of investigated;
- regulatory assessment begins after batches are made;
- sending unit leaves before troubleshooting knowledge is absorbed;
- final report says "accepted" but does not update CPV or control strategy.

The underlying pattern is almost always the same: the project moved activity
faster than knowledge.

## Audit-ready transfer evidence

An auditor or reviewer should be able to follow the transfer story:

1. Why is the transfer happening?
2. What exactly is being transferred?
3. What product and process knowledge supports the transfer?
4. What is different between sending and receiving sites?
5. Which differences matter to CQAs, CPPs, methods, and control strategy?
6. What did the protocol require?
7. What batches, samples, and studies were executed?
8. What deviations occurred, and what was learned?
9. Were analytical methods transferred before relying on results?
10. Did the receiving site demonstrate readiness and capability?
11. Was regulatory impact assessed before implementation?
12. How will routine performance be monitored after transfer?

If the answer is scattered across emails, meeting notes, and tribal memory, the
transfer is not yet clean.

## A practical transfer checklist

Before approving a transfer, confirm:

- transfer charter and scope are approved;
- sending and receiving unit responsibilities are defined;
- quality agreement or responsibility matrix covers complaints, deviations, CAPA,
  change control, release, records, recalls, and regulatory communication;
- product knowledge package is complete;
- CQA/CPP/CMA rationale is documented;
- sending vs receiving gap assessment is complete;
- equipment, utilities, cleaning, and facility readiness are verified;
- analytical method transfer plan is approved and on schedule;
- critical materials, suppliers, standards, and reagents are available;
- training includes process rationale, not just batch record steps;
- transfer protocol includes enhanced sampling and clear acceptance criteria;
- deviations and failed criteria have stop/go rules;
- regulatory impact assessment is approved;
- final report will feed CPV, APR/PQR, change control, and lifecycle knowledge.

## Key takeaway

Technology transfer is not the movement of a recipe. It is the movement of a
controlled state.

The receiving site must inherit the product knowledge, process sensitivity,
control strategy, analytical capability, regulatory constraints, and practical
know-how needed to reproduce quality. Documents are necessary, but they are not
enough. The transfer succeeds when the receiving site can explain, execute,
monitor, investigate, and improve the process without leaning on the sending site
for every abnormal signal.
