# Change control: the system that protects your validated state

Change control is the quality system's way of asking one disciplined question:

**If we change this, what else could move?**

That question sounds cautious. It is actually what allows improvement. Without
change control, a site either freezes in place or changes by surprise. With change
control, the site can improve equipment, methods, suppliers, software, processes,
documents, packaging, and facilities without quietly breaking the validated state.

The validated state is not a certificate on a shelf. It is the continuing truth
that a process, method, system, utility, or piece of equipment remains fit for its
intended GMP use. FDA's process validation lifecycle language makes the same point:
after qualification, the process must remain in a state of control during routine
commercial manufacture. Change control is one of the systems that keeps that claim
true.

## Every change is a hypothesis

A proposed change is a hypothesis that something can be altered without creating
unacceptable risk.

Examples:

- "This alternate supplier is equivalent."
- "This software patch does not affect data integrity or calculations."
- "This column brand performs the same in the validated method."
- "This gasket material does not introduce extractables risk."
- "This new cleaning sequence removes residue at least as well."
- "This equipment move does not affect qualification."
- "This process parameter adjustment stays inside the proven control strategy."

Change control tests that hypothesis before implementation. It defines what must
be checked, who must approve, what evidence is needed, what can be implemented
when, and how success will be verified.

If the hypothesis is vague, the change record will be weak. A good change request
states the current state, proposed state, reason, affected products or systems, and
the boundary of the change in precise language.

## Planned change is not a deviation

Change control is for planned, evaluated changes. A deviation is an unplanned
departure from approved requirements.

If the site already made the change without approval, do not pretend it is a
normal change request. Open a deviation, assess product and system impact, and then
use change control to bring the future state under control if the change will be
kept.

This distinction matters because retrospective paperwork does not protect the
validated state. It only documents that control was lost.

## The impact assessment is the heart

The impact assessment is not a checkbox table. It is the scientific and regulatory
argument that decides what work the change triggers.

A strong assessment asks about at least these areas:

| Impact area | Core question |
| --- | --- |
| Product quality | Could identity, strength, purity, potency, safety, sterility, or performance change? |
| Process control | Could a CPP, IPC, yield, hold time, cleaning step, or operating range shift? |
| Validated or qualified state | Does this affect process validation, equipment qualification, utilities, methods, cleaning, CSV, or shipping qualification? |
| Stability | Could shelf life, storage, packaging, degradation, leachables, moisture, or microbiology change? |
| Regulatory filing | Does the change touch the dossier, marketing authorization, established conditions, label, site, supplier, method, specification, or commitment? |
| Data integrity | Could records, audit trails, calculations, interfaces, access, backup, or report generation change? |
| Supplier/material control | Does the change affect supplier qualification, quality agreements, material specs, CoA reliance, or incoming inspection? |
| Training and procedures | Which SOPs, batch records, forms, specs, methods, or job aids must be updated before use? |
| Batch disposition | Which batches are made before, during, or after implementation, and how are they released? |

The output should be a set of required actions, not just a risk rating. If a change
affects a validated method, the action may be method verification, partial
revalidation, bridging, or system suitability update. If it affects a registered
process, regulatory assessment may be needed before implementation. If it affects a
computerized system, validation and data-integrity review may be required.

## Risk scales the work

ICH Q9(R1) frames quality risk management as assessment, control, communication,
and review of risks to product quality across the lifecycle. That thinking belongs
inside change control.

Not every change needs a formal risk workshop. A low-risk typo correction in an
SOP can be handled lightly. A new sterile filling component supplier, a WFI system
modification, a process parameter change, or a LIMS upgrade needs deeper review.

Risk-based does not mean casual. It means the amount of evidence matches the
possible impact.

Useful risk questions include:

- What can go wrong because of this change?
- How could the failure reach product or data?
- What existing controls would detect it?
- What prior knowledge supports equivalence?
- What uncertainty remains?
- What evidence must be generated before implementation?
- What monitoring confirms the change worked after implementation?

The risk assessment should be living enough to update if implementation reveals
new information.

## Q10 makes change management a system

ICH Q10 describes change management as a core element of the pharmaceutical quality
system. It is not meant to be an isolated form owned only by QA.

Good change control depends on:

- knowledge management;
- quality risk management;
- process performance and product quality monitoring;
- CAPA;
- management review;
- supplier oversight;
- validation and qualification lifecycle management;
- regulatory intelligence.

Those systems feed each other. A CAPA may create a change. A trend may justify a
process improvement. A supplier issue may require material change control. A change
may trigger stability, validation, training, and regulatory actions. Management
review should see whether the change system is timely, effective, and not creating
hidden backlog risk.

## Q12 connects internal change to regulatory change

Many changes have two lives.

Inside the company, the change is managed through the PQS. Outside the company, it
may be a post-approval CMC change that requires notification, supplement,
variation, or prior approval depending on the market.

ICH Q12 gives a framework for more predictable post-approval change management. A
central idea is the **established condition**: information considered necessary to
assure product quality and therefore binding in the approved application. Changing
an established condition normally requires regulatory reporting according to the
approved category.

This matters for everyday change control. The impact assessment should ask:

- Is the changed element in the dossier or marketing authorization?
- Is it an established condition or approved commitment?
- Which markets are affected?
- Can the site implement immediately, after notification, after a waiting period,
  or only after approval?
- Are batches made during the transition controlled by market authorization status?

The answer should come before implementation, not after the change is already in
commercial use.

## What good actions look like

The best change records define actions that are specific enough to close
objectively.

Weak action:

> Update documents and train users.

Stronger action:

> Revise SOP QC-014, HPLC method AMV-022, and the batch release checklist; train
> analysts A/B/C before first use; verify first three routine runs meet resolution,
> %RSD, and bracketing criteria; QA review before routine release.

Weak action:

> Assess validation impact.

Stronger action:

> Perform partial requalification of the relocated incubator: IQ location check,
> alarm verification, temperature mapping over 72 hours, and review of affected
> stability samples before release from hold.

Good actions have owners, due dates, prerequisites, acceptance criteria, and
evidence. They also state which actions must be complete before implementation and
which can be completed after implementation under controlled commitment.

## Implementation is a controlled window

Many change failures happen during the handoff from approval to execution.

The record should define:

- implementation date or window;
- affected products, lots, systems, documents, and markets;
- old-state versus new-state segregation;
- inventory disposition;
- batch record or ERP status changes;
- training completion before use;
- validation or qualification prerequisites;
- rollback or contingency plan where needed;
- communication to QC, QA release, warehouse, engineering, regulatory, and
  suppliers.

For global products, implementation may differ by market. A change approved in one
region may still be pending in another. Batch release and distribution controls
must prevent the wrong product state from entering the wrong market.

## Closure is not filing the form

A change should close only when the required work is complete and reviewed.

Before closure, verify:

- all required actions are done or formally transferred to a controlled commitment;
- validation, qualification, method, stability, supplier, or CSV evidence is
  approved;
- documents and training are effective;
- regulatory filings or approvals are complete where required;
- affected batches are dispositioned correctly;
- deviations during implementation are assessed;
- the change achieved its intended outcome;
- any required effectiveness check is scheduled or complete.

Closing a change while validation remains open is one of the fastest ways to show
that the system values closure metrics over control.

## What inspectors notice

Inspectors often challenge change control because it reveals whether the quality
system actually governs operations.

Common findings include:

- changes implemented before QA approval;
- "no impact" assessments with no rationale;
- no regulatory assessment for post-approval products;
- validation or qualification actions not completed before use;
- document changes not aligned across SOPs, methods, batch records, and specs;
- supplier or material changes treated as purchasing decisions only;
- computerized-system changes missing data-integrity review;
- recurring deviations showing that a previous change was ineffective;
- old and new process states mixed in inventory or batch release;
- overdue changes with no risk-based escalation;
- closure before effectiveness verification.

The pattern behind most findings is simple: the change record did not connect the
technical change to the systems it could affect.

## Change control examples

| Change | Main risks | Likely actions |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Alternate API supplier | impurity profile, supply qualification, regulatory filing | supplier qualification, comparability, CoA/spec review, regulatory assessment |
| HPLC column change | specificity, retention, system suitability, trend continuity | method verification/bridging, SST criteria review, analyst training |
| Equipment relocation | utilities, calibration, qualification, environmental conditions | move protocol, IQ/OQ/PQ impact, calibration check, product hold assessment |
| Cleaning agent change | residue removal, compatibility, microbial control | cleaning validation impact, rinse method suitability, material compatibility |
| LIMS upgrade | calculations, audit trail, interfaces, reports, access | CSV assessment, regression testing, data migration, user training |
| Packaging component change | CCI, extractables/leachables, stability, labeling | supplier qualification, component testing, stability, regulatory review |

The exact action set depends on product and site risk, but the logic should be
visible.

## Review checklist

Use this checklist before approving a change:

- Is the current state and proposed state precise?
- Is the reason for change clear?
- Are affected products, systems, sites, markets, documents, and batches listed?
- Has product-quality impact been assessed?
- Has validation or qualification impact been assessed?
- Has stability impact been assessed?
- Has regulatory filing impact been assessed against the dossier?
- Has data-integrity impact been assessed where records or systems change?
- Are supplier/material impacts covered?
- Are required actions specific, owned, dated, and acceptance-based?
- Are prerequisites separated from post-implementation commitments?
- Is implementation timing controlled?
- Is batch release or inventory segregation needed?
- Is effectiveness verification required?
- Are overdue or failed actions escalated?

If the assessment cannot answer these questions, the change is not ready.

## Key takeaway

Change control is not the enemy of improvement. It is what lets improvement happen
without losing the validated state.

The strongest change systems do four things well: define the change precisely,
assess impact honestly, complete prerequisites before implementation, and verify
that the new state works. When those pieces are missing, the change may still get
done, but the quality system no longer knows what state it is in.
