# Change control: the system that protects your validated state

You spent months qualifying equipment, validating processes, and proving methods.
Change control is the system that keeps all of that **true** as the world changes
around it. Every proposed change — a new supplier, a process tweak, a software
update, a different site — is a hypothesis that **something could shift**, and
change control is how you test that hypothesis before the change reaches product.

## The impact assessment is the heart of it

The single most important step is the **impact assessment**: working out what a
change could affect before you make it.

- **Product quality** — could this change a CQA?
- **The validated / qualified state** — does it invalidate a qualification or
  validation?
- **Stability** — could it affect shelf life?
- **The regulatory filing** — does it need a variation or notification?

The output of that assessment is a list of actions the change triggers — validation,
documentation, training, regulatory filings — that must be done **before** the change
is closed.

## Risk scales the rigour

Not every change needs the same depth. Quality risk management (ICH Q9) lets you
classify changes — minor, major, critical — so a like-for-like consumable swap is
handled lightly while a new manufacturing site gets full scrutiny. ICH Q12 adds the
idea of **established conditions**: the parts of the dossier that are binding (and so
need a variation) versus what you can manage under your own quality system.

## The two failures inspectors look for

- **Implementing before approval** — the change was made, then the paperwork caught
  up. This breaks the whole control.
- **Closing before the actions are done** — the change is marked complete while the
  validation it triggered is still open.

Get those two right and change control does its job: continual improvement without
quietly breaking the validated state.
