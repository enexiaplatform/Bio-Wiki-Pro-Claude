# From method BOM to resilient QC consumable supply

A monthly consumables forecast answers only one question:

> How much material is expected to be consumed by routine work?

A laboratory supply plan must answer several more:

- what exact approved item supports each method step;
- how much demand becomes purchasing demand after controls, repeats and waste;
- when replenishment must be triggered;
- how much protected stock is justified;
- whether stock can be used before expiry;
- whether available storage preserves the required condition;
- what happens when the primary supplier, lot or route is unavailable.

These questions connect the **Quality Method Graph**, method bill of materials,
workload forecast, OPEX, storage concept, supplier qualification and business
continuity. Treating them as a purchasing spreadsheet breaks that chain.

## Net method demand is the starting point

For every in-house method execution, identify the consumable units created by:

- sample and dilution preparation;
- media, diluents, neutralizers and rinsing fluids;
- plates, membranes, funnels, canisters and test cartridges;
- positive, negative and method controls;
- reference cultures, standards and critical reagents;
- sterile or single-use accessories;
- cleaning, decontamination and waste handling;
- data-system or instrument-specific consumable kits.

The method BOM should retain the product, method, execution frequency, quantity per
execution, unit, control condition and evidence status. A category such as
“microbiology consumables” is too broad for supply planning.

## Separate net, gross and purchased quantities

These quantities are not interchangeable.

| Quantity | Meaning | Typical source |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Net demand | Material consumed by the planned method executions | Method BOM × workload |
| Gross demand | Net demand plus justified controls, repeats, handling loss and waste | Observed usage or explicit allowance |
| Purchased quantity | Order quantity after pack-size, MOQ and order-cycle rounding | Supplier and procurement data |
| Usable inventory | Released, in-date stock available under the correct storage condition | Inventory and quality status |

If a method needs 90 units per month, a ten-percent waste allowance produces 99
units of gross demand. It does **not** prove that ordering 99 units is possible or
appropriate. The item may be sold in packs of 50, require receipt testing, have a
minimum order of ten packs or expire before the excess can be consumed.

## Build the waste allowance from causes

A single percentage is useful for concept planning, but the controlled model should
decompose it into observable causes:

- method controls and blanks;
- invalid runs and justified repeats;
- setup or priming loss;
- breakage, leakage or compromised sterility;
- partial packs that cannot be retained;
- expired or temperature-excursion stock;
- growth-promotion or receipt-testing consumption;
- training and analyst qualification;
- investigation and method-lifecycle work.

Do not hide routine controls inside “waste.” Controls are part of the approved
method demand. Waste should represent loss or additional use beyond the planned
execution structure.

## Reorder point protects replenishment time

A simple concept formula is:

```text
Reorder point = average gross daily demand × replenishment lead time
```

Lead time must cover the complete replenishment path, not only the supplier's
shipping promise:

```text
internal approval
→ purchase-order release
→ supplier preparation
→ transport and customs
→ goods receipt
→ quarantine or receipt testing
→ quality release
→ internal delivery to the laboratory
```

Use observed end-to-end lead time where possible. For critical imported reagents,
supplier variability, customs delay and quality-release time can dominate the
nominal transport duration.

## Safety stock protects uncertainty

Safety stock is distinct from lead-time demand. A simple early-stage expression is:

```text
Safety stock = average gross daily demand × protected demand days
```

This is transparent, but it is not statistically calibrated. A mature model should
consider:

- demand variation and peak production campaigns;
- lead-time variation;
- required service level or acceptable stockout risk;
- criticality of the affected test and product decision;
- alternate qualified sources;
- substitution or method-change burden;
- expiry and storage constraints;
- cost of stockout versus cost of excess and expiry.

Adding more stock is not automatically more resilient. Excess stock can create an
expiry, obsolescence, storage or cash constraint.

## Target stock is not an order quantity

For a simple continuous-review concept:

```text
Target stock = reorder point + safety stock
```

This expresses the inventory position the planning model aims to protect. The
purchase order still requires:

- current on-hand and on-order inventory;
- open allocations or reservations;
- pack-size rounding;
- minimum order quantity;
- maximum storage capacity;
- lot-size and lot-segregation rules;
- expiry-at-receipt requirements;
- purchasing calendar and supplier order windows.

Atlas therefore reports target stock as a planning quantity, never as an automatic
purchase instruction.

## Shelf life constrains usable coverage

Inventory days are meaningless if the material expires before use. For each item,
record:

- manufacturer shelf life;
- minimum remaining shelf life at receipt;
- opened-container or prepared-use period;
- retest or requalification rule where applicable;
- storage temperature, light, humidity and segregation requirements;
- lot release or growth-promotion duration;
- expected monthly consumption by lot;
- FEFO execution and expiry-monitoring ownership.

A useful control compares **days of usable supply** with **days to expiry**. If
target stock exceeds the quantity likely to be consumed within usable life, the
solution may be smaller and more frequent orders, supplier-held stock, split
deliveries or an alternate pack size—not a larger store.

## Storage capacity is part of the supply model

Translate target stock into physical storage demand using:

- pack dimensions and secondary packaging;
- refrigerated, frozen, ambient, flammable or controlled-area conditions;
- quarantine, released, rejected and returned status segregation;
- lot segregation and FEFO access;
- monitoring and alarm requirements;
- maximum shelf loading and ergonomic handling;
- emergency power or alternate conditioned storage.

The refrigerator allowance in a laboratory concept cannot be finalized from sample
volume alone if critical media and reagents also compete for qualified usable space.

## Criticality should change the continuity strategy

Classify items by the operational consequence of shortage.

| Class | Example consequence | Continuity response |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Critical | Release or contamination-control decision stops; no rapid substitute | Protected stock, alternate source or tested recovery plan |
| Important | Work can continue briefly or be rescheduled | Monitored stock and defined escalation trigger |
| Routine | Readily available standard item with low switching burden | Normal replenishment control |

Criticality is not price. A low-cost membrane, neutralizer or culture can stop a
high-value release workflow.

## Alternate suppliers are not automatically interchangeable

An alternate source may change:

- material specification or composition;
- sterility assurance or packaging configuration;
- method suitability or recovery performance;
- instrument compatibility;
- growth-promotion or incoming-control requirements;
- validated method, registration or SOP references;
- data integrity or software dependencies.

Record whether the alternate is **qualified and deployable now**, qualified but
change-controlled, or merely a market candidate. Supplier count alone does not
prove resilience.

## Link supply risks to method and product decisions

The strongest supply register traces each item through:

```text
item
→ method step
→ method
→ product/material/monitoring program
→ quality decision
→ required turnaround
→ shortage consequence
```

This allows procurement and QA to prioritize a low-cost item that supports a
critical release method over a more expensive but non-critical category.

## Model future demand and step-change triggers

Future supply demand should not be only a uniform growth multiplier. Include events
such as:

- product launch or market expansion;
- new sampling points or EM locations;
- insourcing a previously outsourced method;
- method transfer or validation campaigns;
- revised control strategy or sampling frequency;
- supplier discontinuation or specification change;
- rapid-method adoption;
- new instrument platform with proprietary consumables.

Create measurable triggers for changing order policy, storage capacity, supplier
strategy or pack size.

## Evidence required before budget or procurement approval

For every material item, obtain:

1. approved specification and item identifier;
2. method/BOM linkage and quantity basis;
3. qualified supplier and quality status;
4. pack size, MOQ and commercial unit;
5. observed end-to-end lead-time history;
6. shelf life and minimum expiry at receipt;
7. storage and transport requirements;
8. receipt testing or release duration;
9. usage, waste, repeat and expiry history;
10. alternate-source status and change-control burden;
11. current inventory, open orders and reservations;
12. responsible owner and review cadence.

## What Atlas should block when evidence is missing

Atlas may calculate an indicative run rate, reorder point and target stock while
clearly exposing assumptions. It should block controlled use when:

- method BOM items are generic or unapproved;
- pack size and MOQ are unknown;
- lead time is a guess rather than an observed or supplier-confirmed basis;
- shelf life or storage condition is missing;
- receipt-release duration is excluded;
- critical items have no qualified continuity path;
- target stock exceeds qualified storage capacity;
- OPEX is presented without waste and purchasing-unit reconciliation.

## Practical review checklist

- Does every critical method have a complete consumable BOM?
- Are net demand, gross demand and purchased quantity kept separate?
- Are controls part of method demand rather than hidden as waste?
- Does lead time include internal and quality-release steps?
- Is safety stock justified against demand and supply uncertainty?
- Can target stock be consumed before expiry?
- Is qualified storage sufficient for the target stock?
- Are alternate sources actually deployable?
- Does future demand include launches, transfers and insourcing events?
- Are stockout, expiry and supplier-change outcomes fed back into Atlas calibration?
