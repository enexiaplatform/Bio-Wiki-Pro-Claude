# What Is a Certificate of Analysis (CoA)?

A **Certificate of Analysis (CoA)** is the batch-specific document that reports
the quality tests performed on a material, component, intermediate, or finished
product. It normally lists the specification, method reference, actual result,
and conclusion for each required test.

In plain language, a CoA is the quality "passport" for a batch. It tells the next
person in the supply chain what was tested, what was found, and whether the batch
conforms to the approved requirements. But in GMP work, a CoA should never be read
as a standalone promise. It is only credible when the laboratory records,
validated methods, calibrated instruments, qualified analysts, and data integrity
controls behind it are also credible.

## What a good CoA contains

A useful CoA gives enough information for QA, QC, manufacturing, or a customer to
trace the result back to the batch and to understand the release decision.

At minimum, expect to see:

- material or product name;
- grade, code, or catalog number where relevant;
- batch, lot, or serial number;
- manufacturer or supplier name and site;
- manufacturing date, expiry date, or retest date where applicable;
- each required test or quality attribute;
- the approved specification or acceptance criterion;
- the actual result, including units;
- the analytical method, compendial reference, or internal method number;
- pass/fail or conforming/nonconforming conclusion;
- authorized signature, role, and date;
- any remarks, limitations, or special storage conditions.

For pharma materials, the strongest CoAs do not merely say "passes." They show the
actual value where a numerical result exists. "Complies" may be acceptable for
some qualitative attributes, but it is weak for an assay, impurity, endotoxin,
bioburden, moisture, or other result where the number matters for trending,
release, and future investigations.

## Why the CoA matters in GMP

The CoA connects three things that must stay aligned:

1. **The specification** - what the material or product is required to meet.
2. **The result** - what the test data actually showed for this batch.
3. **The disposition** - whether the batch can be accepted, rejected, released,
   quarantined, or investigated.

If any of those three pieces do not match, the CoA loses value. A result can be
inside specification but still suspicious if it conflicts with trends, audit-trail
evidence, method suitability, sample history, or a known deviation. A result can
also be correctly reported but tied to the wrong batch, wrong method version, or
wrong specification. That is why CoA review is part of quality judgment, not just
document collection.

For incoming materials, the CoA supports supplier control. It helps the receiving
site decide whether a shipment can move from quarantine into approved status. For
finished products, the CoA becomes part of the release package that shows the
batch met approved requirements before distribution.

## A supplier CoA is not a free pass

One common mistake is treating a supplier CoA as if it removes the receiving
company's testing and verification responsibilities. It does not.

Under U.S. drug CGMP expectations, components, containers, and closures are
withheld from use until they are sampled, tested, or examined and released by the
quality control unit. FDA's 21 CFR 211.84 allows reliance on supplier analysis in
certain cases only when the manufacturer has established the reliability of the
supplier's results and performs the required identity or visual checks. FDA's Q&A
on component and container-closure control gives the same practical message: a
supplier certificate can support release, but only inside a validated supplier
reliability program.

That means the receiving site still needs an approved procedure for:

- supplier qualification and periodic requalification;
- defining which tests can be accepted from the supplier and which must be
  repeated in-house or by a qualified contract lab;
- identity testing or visual identification where required;
- comparing supplier CoA results against site specifications;
- periodically verifying supplier results through confirmatory testing;
- escalating discrepancies, OOS results, missing data, or unexpected trends.

This is especially important for APIs, excipients with safety history, primary
packaging, sterile components, biological materials, and any single-source or
high-risk supplier. A low-risk commodity may justify less routine retesting, but
that decision should come from documented risk assessment and supplier history,
not convenience.

## CoA vs CoC vs release certificate

A **Certificate of Analysis** reports actual analytical results for a defined
batch or lot.

A **Certificate of Conformance** or **Certificate of Compliance** usually states
that the product was made or supplied according to agreed requirements. It may not
include individual test results.

A **release certificate** or **batch release statement** is a broader quality
disposition record. Depending on the company and region, it may include or attach
the CoA, but it can also cover batch record review, deviations, changes,
environmental monitoring, reconciliation, complaints, stability commitments, or
QP/QA certification.

The distinction matters during audits. If your procedure requires CoA review, a
CoC alone may not satisfy it. If your customer quality agreement requires actual
results, a generic conformance statement may create a documentation gap.

## How QC should review a CoA

Good CoA review is more than checking that a PDF exists. A reviewer should ask:

- Does the lot number match the shipment, label, purchase order, and internal
  material code?
- Are the specifications current and aligned with the approved material master or
  product registration?
- Are all required tests present, including identity, assay, impurities,
  microbial attributes, water, residual solvents, or compendial tests where
  relevant?
- Are units, limits, decimal places, and method references clear?
- Are any results close to alert/action limits or historically unusual?
- Is the method version approved and suitable for the matrix?
- Are there unexplained "N/A," "not tested," or "conforms" entries where a number
  should be expected?
- Is the supplier approved for this exact material, grade, site, and intended
  use?
- Does the CoA show an authorized release signature and date?
- Are any deviations, retests, reworks, or special conditions disclosed?

If the answer to any of these questions is unclear, the CoA should trigger a
quality question before the material or product is released.

## Red flags auditors notice

CoA problems often reveal deeper weaknesses in laboratory control, supplier
qualification, or data integrity. Common red flags include:

- accepting supplier CoAs without periodic verification;
- using a supplier for a different grade, site, or manufacturing route than the
  approved one;
- CoA results that do not match the site's approved specifications;
- missing identity testing for incoming components where required;
- repeated "complies" entries with no actual numbers for quantitative tests;
- unexplained retest dates, expired materials, or altered PDFs;
- results copied into internal systems with transcription errors;
- release decisions made before required tests were complete;
- no link between the CoA and deviations, OOS/OOT results, or change control.

The most dangerous CoA failure is not usually a typo. It is false confidence:
believing the batch is controlled because the certificate looks polished, while
the evidence behind it was never challenged.

## Practical example

Imagine an excipient lot arrives with a supplier CoA showing assay, moisture,
identity, and microbial limits. The supplier is approved, the lot number matches,
and all results are within the agreed specification.

That still does not automatically mean the lot can be used. The receiving site
should confirm that the supplier is approved for this exact excipient grade, the
CoA specification matches the site specification, required identity testing is
performed, and the supplier's results are periodically verified. If the moisture
result is still within specification but much higher than previous lots, the site
may accept the lot but should consider trending, supplier follow-up, or additional
controls depending on product risk.

That is the real job of CoA review: not to distrust every supplier, but to keep
the evidence chain honest.

## The QC takeaway

A CoA is not the source of quality. It is a summary of evidence. The quality sits
in the approved specification, the validated method, the controlled sample, the
raw data, the analyst's execution, the supplier's reliability, and the QA/QC
decision that ties all of it together.

When you review a CoA, do not ask only, "Did it pass?" Ask, "Can we trust the
system that produced this result, and does this batch fit our approved control
strategy?"
