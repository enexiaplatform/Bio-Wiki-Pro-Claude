# OOT vs OOS: what's the difference, and why it matters

Two acronyms that get blurred in QC labs: **OOS** (out-of-specification) and **OOT**
(out-of-trend). They are not the same, and treating OOT as "not a problem because
it's still in spec" is a classic way to miss a process drifting toward failure.

## OOS — a result outside the limit

An **out-of-specification** result has crossed a defined acceptance limit. It is a
discrete event with a clear trigger, and it kicks off the phased investigation:
confirm it isn't a calculation error, assess for an assignable laboratory cause,
escalate to a full-scale investigation if none is found, and let QA decide
disposition.

## OOT — a result inside the limit but off the pattern

An **out-of-trend** result is still **within** specification, but it does not fit
the expected pattern — it is unusually high or low compared with the product's
history, or it continues a drift. Nothing has failed yet. That is exactly why OOT
matters: it is the **early-warning system** that OOS is not.

A run of results that all "pass" can still be marching toward the limit. By the
time you get an OOS, the process may have been drifting for months.

## Why investigate something that passed?

Because the trend is the signal. An OOT investigation asks: is this normal
variation, or the start of a real shift? Catching it early lets you correct a
process before it produces a failing batch — and inspectors expect a trending
program that reacts to drift, not just to limit breaches.

- **OOS** → a limit was crossed → phased investigation + disposition
- **OOT** → still in spec but off-pattern → trend investigation + impact assessment

## Where OOT lives

OOT thinking applies across QC — stability (a result drifting over time points),
environmental monitoring (counts creeping up below the action limit), and
in-process trends. The common thread: **judge the trend, not just the single
point.**
