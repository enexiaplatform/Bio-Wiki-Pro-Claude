# Visual inspection: catching the particle before the patient does

Every unit of an injectable product is inspected for **visible particles and
defects** before it reaches a patient. A glass fragment, a fibre, or a fill defect in a
vial is unacceptable, and visual inspection is the 100% check that aims to catch them.
It sounds simple. It is one of the more statistically subtle controls in sterile
manufacturing.

## 100% inspection is still probabilistic

You might assume inspecting every unit guarantees no defective unit ships. It does not.
Visual inspection — whether by a trained human or an automated machine — has a
**probability of detection** for any given defect, and that probability is never 100%.
A small, low-contrast particle in a particular orientation may be missed even under
good conditions. So "100% inspected" means every unit was *looked at*, not that every
defect was *caught*.

That is why the **acceptance criteria, lighting, presentation time, and inspector
qualification** all matter — they raise the probability of detection toward acceptable
levels.

## Qualifying the inspector (or the machine)

Because detection is probabilistic, you have to **qualify** the inspectors against
defect test sets with known defects, confirming they reliably find the defects that
matter and do not over-reject good units. Automated inspection machines are validated
the same way — proven to detect the relevant defect types at least as well as a
qualified human.

## Visible and subvisible are a continuum

Visual inspection catches **visible** particles; it does not address the **subvisible**
ones, which are controlled by particulate matter testing (USP 788) using light
obscuration or microscopy against defined limits. Together they cover the particle
size range that matters for an injectable — the eye for the larger, instruments for the
smaller.
