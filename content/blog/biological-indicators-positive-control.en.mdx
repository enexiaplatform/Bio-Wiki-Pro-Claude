# Biological indicators: why the positive control is everything

A biological indicator (BI) is a deliberately worst-case challenge: a known
population of highly resistant spores with a certified D-value — the time to
reduce the population by 90% under defined conditions. Run it through your
sterilization cycle, and if nothing grows, you have evidence the process can kill.
But that evidence is only as good as your controls.

## The positive control is non-negotiable

Here is the trap: a BI that shows no growth after exposure looks like a pass. But
"no growth" has two possible causes — the cycle killed the spores, or the spores
were never going to grow in the first place (dead on arrival, wrong recovery
media, wrong incubation).

The **positive control** — an unexposed BI from the same lot, incubated alongside
the test — rules out the second cause. If the positive control grows, you know the
spores were viable and your recovery system works, so a negative test result is
meaningful. If the positive control does **not** grow, the run is invalid and a
"no growth" test tells you nothing.

## Choose the BI for the process

- **Steam and VHP:** *Geobacillus stearothermophilus*
- **Dry heat and EO:** *Bacillus atrophaeus*

Confirm the certified population and D-value on the certificate of analysis suit
your process, and that the BI is within expiry and stored correctly. A BI whose
resistance does not match the process either over- or under-challenges the cycle.

## The false-negative traps

- **Late transfer to recovery media** — recover the BI within the labeled hold
  time; delay can let injured spores fail to grow, hiding a real survivor.
- **Easy placement** — put BIs at the validated worst-case (hardest-to-sterilize)
  locations from the load study, not where they are convenient to retrieve.
- **No positive control** — covered above; without it the test is unprovable.
- **Reading physical data alone** — the BI complements, never replaces, the cycle's
  physical lethality data (e.g. F0 for moist heat). A release rests on both.
