# SPC: telling the difference between noise and a signal

Every process varies. The hard question is not whether the number moved. It always
moves. The hard question is whether the movement is **normal process noise** or a
**real signal** that deserves action.

Statistical process control, or SPC, is the toolkit for making that decision with
data instead of instinct. It helps teams avoid two opposite mistakes:

- overreacting to common-cause variation and making the process worse;
- missing special-cause signals until the process reaches an OOS, deviation, or
  complaint.

In pharma and biotech, SPC is not only a manufacturing quality tool. It supports
continued process verification, APR/PQR review, OOT investigation, environmental
monitoring, laboratory trending, process capability, and the quality system's
ability to prove the process remains in a state of control.

## Common cause vs special cause

SPC rests on one distinction.

**Common-cause variation** is the natural variation of a stable process. It comes
from the normal system: routine material variation, equipment noise, operator
variation, environmental variation, analytical variation, and other sources that
are already built into the process.

**Special-cause variation** is a signal that something unusual has changed. It may
come from a new raw-material lot, equipment wear, a setup error, a process drift,
analyst technique, contamination, maintenance, a software change, a seasonal effect,
or a previously unknown interaction.

A process showing only common-cause variation is **in statistical control**. That
does not mean it is good. It means it is stable and predictable. A stable process
can still be incapable of meeting specification.

## Control limits are not specification limits

This is the confusion that breaks many trending programs.

- **Specification limits** come from the product requirement, filing, compendial
  requirement, method criterion, or patient/product need. They answer: is this
  result acceptable?
- **Control limits** come from process data. They answer: is the process behaving
  like it normally behaves?
- **Alert limits** are early-warning thresholds, often internal and risk-based.
- **Action limits** define a required response before or at a more serious level of
  concern.

A point can be inside specification and outside a control limit. That does not make
the batch automatically failing. It means the process may have shifted and should
be assessed. That is exactly the early-warning value of SPC.

A point can also be inside control limits and outside specification if the process
is stable but poorly centered or too variable. That is a capability problem, not a
special-cause problem.

## Why SPC matters in CPV

FDA's process validation guidance defines Stage 3 continued process verification as
ongoing assurance during routine production that the process remains in a state of
control. That requires more than annual summaries. It requires meaningful monitoring
of critical process parameters, critical quality attributes, and other signals that
show whether the process is behaving as expected.

ICH Q10 similarly puts process performance and product quality monitoring at the
center of a pharmaceutical quality system. ICH Q9(R1) explicitly discusses
manufacturing process variation and state of control as quality-risk topics.

SPC turns that principle into a practical operating system:

- define what should be trended;
- establish a justified baseline;
- set statistical rules for signals;
- investigate special causes;
- reduce common-cause variation through improvement;
- feed learning into CAPA, change control, APR/PQR, validation, and management
  review.

Without SPC discipline, CPV can become a pile of charts with no decision rules.

## Start with the right data

An SPC chart is only useful if the data are meaningful.

Before charting, confirm:

- the measurement system is suitable for trending;
- the data are collected consistently;
- the unit of analysis is clear, such as batch, run, shift, lot, sample, or unit;
- the data are time-ordered;
- the process and method were reasonably stable during baseline generation;
- outliers, known deviations, and one-time studies are handled by a documented
  rule;
- the charted value is linked to a meaningful control strategy element.

This is where measurement systems analysis matters. A noisy gauge creates noisy
charts. If measurement variation dominates process variation, the chart may signal
the measurement system rather than the process.

## Choosing the chart

Chart choice should follow the data structure.

| Data situation | Typical chart | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| One value per batch or run | I-MR chart | Batch assay result, content uniformity mean, yield |
| Rational subgroups | Xbar-R or Xbar-S chart | Multiple fill weights per time point, tablet weights by subgroup |
| Count of defects | c or u chart | Particles per unit, defects per inspected unit |
| Proportion defective | p or np chart | Percent rejected units, visual inspection fail rate |
| Rare events or time between events | g or t style charts | Days between contamination events |
| Small sustained shifts | EWMA or CUSUM | Slow assay drift, gradual impurity increase |

The most common mistake in pharma is forcing everything into an I-MR chart because
there is one value per batch in the report. That may be reasonable for many CPV
signals, but it is not universal. If you have meaningful subgroup data, use it.
If the data are attribute counts, do not pretend they are continuous measurements.

## Baseline matters

Control limits are only as good as the baseline used to calculate them.

A good baseline should be:

- representative of the current validated process;
- free from known special-cause periods unless intentionally included with
  justification;
- large enough to estimate variation reasonably;
- based on comparable product, site, equipment, method, and scale conditions;
- periodically reviewed when process understanding changes.

Do not calculate permanent limits from a handful of launch batches and forget them.
Do not recalculate limits every month just to hide a drift. Both practices weaken
the signal system.

When a process improves, limits may need revision after a justified, documented
period of stable performance. When a process changes, the old baseline may no
longer describe the new process.

## Signal rules

A single point beyond a three-sigma control limit is the classic special-cause
signal, but it is not the only one.

Common signal rules include:

- one point beyond a control limit;
- several consecutive points on one side of the centerline;
- a sustained upward or downward trend;
- two of three points beyond a two-sigma zone on the same side;
- four of five points beyond a one-sigma zone on the same side;
- unusually low variation, which may indicate rounding, data handling, or sampling
  issues;
- cycles or seasonality that match shift, supplier, maintenance, or environmental
  patterns.

Sites should predefine which rules apply to which chart and what response each
signal requires. If signal rules are invented after seeing the data, the program
becomes subjective.

## What to do with a signal

A signal does not always mean product is defective. It means the process deserves a
structured look.

A practical response is:

1. confirm the data and calculation;
2. check measurement system status, method changes, analyst/instrument history, and
   data integrity;
3. map the signal to batch chronology, material lots, equipment, environmental
   conditions, maintenance, cleaning, interventions, and deviations;
4. assess product impact separately from process-control impact;
5. define containment if the trend may affect ongoing production;
6. open deviation, CAPA, change control, or trend review according to risk and SOP;
7. document whether the process returned to control;
8. update CPV, APR/PQR, control strategy, or validation knowledge where needed.

The key is to avoid both extremes: do not treat every signal as an OOS, and do not
dismiss every in-spec signal because the batch passed.

## Tampering: the cost of overreacting

When a process is stable and only common-cause variation is present, adjusting it
after every small movement can increase variation. This is often called tampering.

Examples:

- changing compression force after every tablet-weight wiggle;
- adjusting pH control after every normal reading within the expected band;
- changing incubation conditions because one environmental result is lower than
  usual;
- retraining analysts after normal method scatter;
- changing supplier controls after one ordinary point.

SPC gives teams permission to leave stable noise alone. Improvement should focus
on the system, not individual common-cause points.

## Capability is the next question

Control and capability are different.

A process can be:

- **in control and capable** - stable and comfortably meeting specification;
- **in control but not capable** - predictable but too variable or off-center;
- **out of control but capable on average** - good potential, unstable execution;
- **out of control and not capable** - unstable and unable to reliably meet
  requirements.

Capability indices such as Cp and Cpk should be interpreted only after the process
is reasonably stable and the data distribution assumptions are understood. A high
Cpk from an unstable process is not a promise. A low Cpk from a noisy measurement
system may not reflect the process alone.

## SPC in QC and microbiology

SPC is not limited to manufacturing parameters. It can help with:

- assay and impurity trends;
- dissolution performance;
- content uniformity;
- fill volume and weight;
- bioburden and endotoxin signals;
- environmental monitoring by location or organism group;
- utility trends such as TOC, conductivity, pressure, and temperature;
- complaint rates and defect patterns;
- stability trends;
- supplier material attributes.

The chart and response rules should match the data. Microbiology counts, for
example, may be sparse, skewed, censored, or dominated by zeros. A simple normal
I-MR chart may not be the right tool for every microbiology signal.

## Common weak spots

SPC programs often fail in quiet ways:

- Control limits are confused with specification limits.
- Limits are recalculated too often, masking process drift.
- Baselines include known deviation batches without rationale.
- Charts exist but no one knows the signal rules.
- Every point outside an internal alert limit becomes a full deviation, creating
  noise and fatigue.
- In-spec special-cause signals are ignored.
- Capability is calculated before stability is assessed.
- Measurement-system suitability is assumed.
- CPV charts are reviewed annually instead of when signals occur.
- APR/PQR repeats charts without feeding learning back to control strategy.

The issue is rarely the chart itself. It is usually the missing decision system
around the chart.

## Review checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing an SPC or CPV trend:

- Is the charted parameter linked to a CQA, CPP, IPC, utility, method, or quality
  system signal?
- Is the measurement system suitable for trending?
- Is the chart type appropriate for the data structure?
- Is the baseline justified and representative?
- Are control, alert, action, and specification limits clearly distinguished?
- Are signal rules predefined?
- Are in-spec signals assessed for process-control impact?
- Are product-impact decisions separated from process-control decisions?
- Are special-cause investigations documented and trended?
- Are common-cause improvements handled through system improvement rather than
  point-by-point reactions?
- Are limits reviewed after process changes, method changes, transfers, or sustained
  improvement?
- Does CPV feed APR/PQR, CAPA, change control, validation, and management review?

## Key takeaway

SPC is not chart decoration. It is a decision discipline. It tells you when to
investigate, when to improve the system, when to leave stable noise alone, and when
an in-spec result is still warning that the process has changed.
