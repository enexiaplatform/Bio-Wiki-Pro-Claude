# SPC: telling the difference between noise and a signal

Every process varies. The hard question is never "did the number move?" — it always
moves. The question is whether a movement is **normal noise** or a **real signal**
worth acting on. Statistical process control (SPC) is the toolkit for answering that,
and getting it right keeps you from two opposite mistakes: over-reacting to noise and
missing a genuine shift.

## Common cause vs special cause

SPC rests on one idea — two kinds of variation:

- **Common-cause** variation is the normal, inherent scatter of a stable process.
  Reacting to it (tampering) usually makes things *worse*.
- **Special-cause** variation is something new — a shift, a trend, an outlier — that
  has an assignable reason. This is what you investigate.

A process showing only common-cause variation is **in statistical control**: stable
and predictable, even if not perfect.

## Control limits are not specification limits

This is the most common confusion. They answer different questions:

- **Specification limits** come from the product requirement — "is this result
  acceptable?"
- **Control limits** come from the process's own data (typically around three
  standard deviations) — "is this process behaving as it normally does?"

A result can be **within spec but out of control** — still acceptable, but signalling
the process has shifted. That is exactly the early warning an out-of-trend program
relies on. Treating control limits as spec limits (or vice versa) defeats the whole
purpose.

## What SPC gives QC

- An objective way to flag a process that is **drifting before it fails**
- A defensible basis for **trending** in continued process verification and
  environmental monitoring
- A guard against tampering — leaving a stable process alone instead of chasing every
  wobble
