# Viral safety: why no single test is enough

Biologics are made in living systems. That is why viral safety can never be
treated as a single release test at the end of manufacturing. A monoclonal
antibody, recombinant protein, or other biotechnology product may start from a
cell line of human or animal origin, use biological raw materials, and pass
through a long culture and purification process before release. Each of those
points can either reduce risk or introduce uncertainty.

The logic of **ICH Q5A(R2)** is deliberately conservative: viral safety is built
from a set of mutually reinforcing controls. You control the source, you test at
appropriate stages, and you demonstrate that the process can remove or inactivate
viruses. A negative result from one assay helps, but it cannot prove that every
possible virus is absent.

That is the mindset shift. Viral safety is not a single certificate. It is a
barrier argument.

## Why testing alone cannot carry the claim

Testing is essential, but testing has limits. Every assay has a detection limit,
a sample size, a method scope, and a set of organisms it is designed to detect.
An in vitro adventitious-virus assay, a PCR assay for a known virus, a reverse
transcriptase assay, electron microscopy, and next-generation sequencing all see
different parts of the risk picture.

None of them can answer the broad question, "Is every possible infectious virus
absent from this product?" They can only answer narrower questions:

- Was a signal detected under this method's conditions?
- Is this cell bank positive for a targeted virus?
- Is there evidence of retrovirus-like particles?
- Does this sample contain detectable viral nucleic acid above the assay limit?
- Was the sample representative of the material that matters?

That is why the absence of a test signal is not the same as proof of absence. A
viral-safety package becomes persuasive when the test results sit inside a wider
control strategy.

## Pillar 1: control and understand the source

The first barrier is the starting material. For biologics derived from cell
lines, that normally means the master cell bank (MCB), working cell bank (WCB),
and cells at or beyond the limit of in vitro cell age used for production.

The practical questions are simple, but the evidence can be deep:

- What is the origin and history of the cell line?
- Could the cell substrate contain endogenous, latent, or adventitious viruses?
- Were animal- or human-derived materials used during development or culture?
- Are raw materials controlled for viral risk?
- Are supplier changes assessed for viral-safety impact?
- Is the cell-bank testing package scientifically justified for the substrate?

Source control is not only a testing exercise. It includes genealogy,
traceability, supplier qualification, raw-material controls, change control, and
the documented rationale for why the chosen tests are appropriate.

A weak source history makes the whole package harder to defend. A well
characterized source does not remove the need for testing or clearance, but it
reduces the chance that the process starts with a hidden problem.

## Pillar 2: test at the right stages

Viral testing should be placed where it can actually inform risk. ICH Q5A(R2)
discusses testing of cell banks, cells at the limit of in vitro cell age, and
unprocessed bulk. The exact package depends on the cell substrate, product type,
process, raw materials, and prior knowledge.

Common categories include:

- in vitro assays for adventitious agents;
- in vivo assays where justified by the product and regulatory context;
- retrovirus testing such as infectivity assays, reverse transcriptase assays,
  and electron microscopy;
- specific molecular assays for known or suspected viruses;
- broader molecular methods such as next-generation sequencing when suitably
  qualified and scientifically justified.

The key is fitness for purpose. A method chosen because it is familiar is not
automatically the right method. A method chosen because it is modern is not
automatically sufficient either. The file should explain why the test panel is
appropriate for the substrate, process, material, and risk.

Testing also needs a response plan. If an adventitious-agent signal appears in a
cell bank or unprocessed bulk, the correct response is containment, assessment,
confirmation where scientifically justified, source investigation, product-impact
evaluation, and regulatory/quality decision-making. It should not become a
reflexive attempt to retest the signal away.

## Pillar 3: validate viral clearance

The third pillar is the one that turns the strategy into a quantitative safety
margin. Viral clearance studies ask whether the manufacturing process can remove
or inactivate relevant viruses.

Because it would be unsafe and impractical to contaminate the real process with
pathogenic viruses, clearance is usually studied in scaled-down models. A model
virus is spiked into process material before a step, the step is run under
representative conditions, and the amount of virus before and after the step is
measured. The difference is expressed as a **log reduction value (LRV)**.

Examples of clearance mechanisms include:

- **Inactivation** - low-pH hold, solvent/detergent treatment, heat, or other
  conditions that reduce viral infectivity.
- **Removal** - virus-retentive filtration, chromatography, precipitation, or
  partitioning steps that physically separate virus from product.

Clearance studies should not be theatrical demonstrations. They need a
scientifically defensible model:

- The scaled-down step should represent the commercial step.
- The selected model viruses should be justified.
- The study should include suitable controls.
- Interference and cytotoxicity should be understood.
- Worst-case or edge-of-range parameters should be considered where relevant.
- The result should be reproducible enough to support the claimed LRV.

The most useful clearance package does not simply report a big number. It
explains why that number can be trusted.

## Orthogonality is the hidden discipline

A viral clearance table can look reassuring when the LRVs are added together.
But the addition is only meaningful when the steps are independent enough to
provide different barriers.

That is the idea behind **orthogonality**. A low-pH hold and a virus-retentive
filter act by different mechanisms. A chromatography step and a virus filter may
also provide different kinds of separation. Two highly similar steps may not
provide the same level of independent protection, even if each has a measured
clearance value.

This matters because viral safety is a margin argument. If the same process
vulnerability affects two steps, the combined number can overstate the real
assurance. A mature viral-safety assessment asks:

- Which steps inactivate virus?
- Which steps remove virus?
- Which virus types are each step expected to clear?
- Which steps are robust to normal process variation?
- Are the claimed LRVs independent, overlapping, or conditional?
- What happens if one clearance step performs at the low end of expectation?

Orthogonality is what turns a list of clearance studies into a credible barrier
system.

## What a strong viral-safety package looks like

A strong package reads like an integrated story rather than a pile of tests. It
connects source control, testing, clearance, and lifecycle controls.

For example, the file should make it easy to see:

- cell-line origin, development history, and adventitious-agent risk;
- MCB/WCB testing strategy and acceptance status;
- raw-material viral-risk controls;
- unprocessed bulk testing and response rules;
- viral clearance study design, model-virus selection, and representative scale
  down;
- step-specific LRVs and the rationale for summing them;
- sensitivity to process parameters such as pH, hold time, temperature, filter
  load, flow rate, and chromatography conditions;
- linkage to process validation, change control, and continued verification;
- a clear plan for investigating any viral or adventitious-agent signal.

This is why viral safety belongs at the intersection of QC, process development,
manufacturing science, QA, regulatory CMC, and supplier quality. No single team
owns the whole argument alone.

## Lifecycle risk: the package is not frozen forever

Viral safety should be maintained through the product lifecycle. The risk picture
can change when the process changes, when a raw-material supplier changes, when a
new analytical method is introduced, or when a new scientific concern emerges.

Changes that deserve careful viral-safety review include:

- a new cell bank or change in cell-bank generation strategy;
- new raw materials of biological origin;
- supplier, site, or manufacturing-process changes;
- changes to low-pH hold parameters, filter type, filter load, chromatography
  resin, wash conditions, or pooling rules;
- changes to adventitious-agent, retrovirus, or molecular testing methods;
- a new product type, such as some viral-vector or vector-derived products, where
  the Q5A(R2) annexes and product-specific expectations become especially
  important.

The question is not only "did the old package pass?" The question is "does the
old package still support the current process and current product?"

## Common weak spots inspectors and reviewers notice

Viral-safety packages often weaken in predictable places. The science may be
sound, but the justification is not visible. Or the studies may be old, while the
process has drifted through small changes that were never assessed together.

Watch for these failure modes:

- treating a negative unprocessed-bulk test as the main assurance;
- listing tests without explaining why the panel fits the substrate and risk;
- relying on supplier statements without enough raw-material traceability;
- claiming additive clearance from steps that are not meaningfully orthogonal;
- using a scaled-down clearance model that no longer represents the commercial
  process;
- omitting controls for assay interference, cytotoxicity, or virus recovery;
- failing to connect viral clearance assumptions to process parameter ranges;
- having no predefined response plan for an adventitious-agent signal;
- changing a raw material, resin, filter, hold condition, or site without a clear
  viral-safety impact assessment.

The corrective action is usually not "add one more test." It is to make the
control strategy explicit and evidence-based.

## A practical review checklist

When reviewing a viral-safety package, ask these questions before trusting the
conclusion:

1. What viruses could plausibly be present from the cell substrate, raw materials,
   or process history?
2. Which tests address known and unknown adventitious-agent risks?
3. Are the tests placed at points where a result is meaningful?
4. Are the methods qualified or validated for their intended use?
5. Which process steps remove virus and which inactivate virus?
6. Are the scaled-down clearance studies representative of the real process?
7. Are model viruses justified by size, envelope status, resistance, and
   relevance?
8. Are LRVs supported by controls, recovery data, and assay suitability?
9. Are claimed LRVs mechanistically orthogonal enough to add?
10. Is there a documented response plan for a positive or suspect signal?
11. Have process and supplier changes been assessed since the package was built?
12. Does the final conclusion rely on the integrated barrier argument?

If the answer to the last question is no, the package is not yet mature.

## The barrier argument

The best way to explain viral safety is this:

Source control reduces what could enter the process. Testing looks for what may
be present at meaningful stages. Validated clearance demonstrates that the
process has a margin even when testing cannot prove universal absence. Lifecycle
controls keep the argument true as the product and process evolve.

That is why no single test is enough. Viral safety is credible only when the
whole system points in the same direction.
