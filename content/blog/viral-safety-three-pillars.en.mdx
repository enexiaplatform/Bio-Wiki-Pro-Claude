# Viral safety: why no single test is enough

Biologics are made in living cells, which carries a real risk: viral
contamination, whether from the cell line itself or from raw materials. The
central principle of viral safety, set out in **ICH Q5A**, is humbling — **no
single test can prove a virus is absent**. So assurance is built from three
complementary pillars.

## Pillar 1 — Control the source

Qualify and characterise the **cell banks** (master and working) and raw materials
for adventitious agents. If the starting materials are well-characterised and
controlled, you reduce the chance of introducing a virus in the first place.

## Pillar 2 — Test at key stages

Test the cell banks and the **unprocessed bulk** with an appropriate panel — in
vitro assays, and PCR-based methods for specific viruses. Testing detects what is
there to be found, but because absence can never be fully proven by testing, it is
necessary, not sufficient.

## Pillar 3 — Validate viral clearance

Demonstrate that the **purification process removes or inactivates virus**. This is
done with **spiking studies**: deliberately add a model virus and measure the
**log-reduction** across each step (for example a low-pH hold, nanofiltration, and
chromatography).

The crucial detail is **orthogonality**: log-reductions only add up when the steps
remove virus by **different mechanisms**. A low-pH hold and a nanofilter act
differently, so their clearances are additive. Two similar steps can overstate the
real margin.

## The barrier argument

Put together, the three pillars make a **barrier argument**: even a contamination
that slips past one layer is caught by another. That is why a positive signal in
the unprocessed bulk is **contained and investigated**, not retested away — and why
a release decision rests on the whole strategy, not one assay.

## What good looks like

- Cell banks qualified and characterised for adventitious agents
- A testing panel appropriate to the cell substrate and risk
- **Orthogonal** clearance steps with validated log-reduction
- An integrated barrier — source plus testing plus clearance — not one test
