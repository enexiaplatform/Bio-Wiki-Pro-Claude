# Microbiology investigation surge-capacity planning

Routine workload does not describe the laboratory needed during an excursion. One
unexpected result can create immediate data review, isolate work, hypothesis-driven
sampling, confirmation, product-impact assessment and management communication while
routine release testing continues.

```text
signal or unexpected result
→ immediate containment and evidence preservation
→ laboratory and process assessment
→ targeted test and sampling branches
→ identification and comparison
→ impact, disposition and CAPA decisions
→ backlog recovery and learning review
```

Atlas can model scenario demand and expose missing capability. It does not determine
root cause, authorize retesting, invalidate a result, set investigation scope or make
batch disposition decisions.

## Define investigation archetypes

Build scenarios separately for:

- invalid negative or positive control;
- microbial enumeration OOS or atypical result;
- specified-organism presumptive or confirmed detection;
- objectionable-organism assessment;
- water alert/action or adverse trend;
- environmental-monitoring excursion;
- media growth-promotion or sterility failure;
- method-suitability or recovery failure;
- incubator, storage or sample-hold excursion;
- suspected laboratory contamination or cross-contamination;
- repeated isolate or cross-area recurrence.

Each archetype has different samples, timing, containment and specialist needs. A
single generic “10% repeat allowance” cannot represent them.

## Preserve the original result and evidence

The initial response should protect:

- original plates, tubes, cultures and samples where permitted;
- raw observations, images, counts and calculations;
- audit trails and instrument/equipment records;
- media, reagent, diluent and culture lots;
- incubation and storage records;
- sample collection, transport and custody;
- analyst actions and timing;
- related work in the same area, session or equipment;
- deviations, maintenance and environmental events.

FDA's official [OOS investigation guidance](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/investigating-out-specification-oos-test-results-pharmaceutical-production-level-2-revision)
provides the broader expectation for scientifically sound evaluation of OOS results.
The site's current procedures and qualified reviewers must define how it applies to a
specific microbiology event.

## Build a scenario demand tree

For each archetype identify possible branches, without pre-authorizing them:

```text
initial assessment
├─ documentation, calculation and data review
├─ media, controls, cultures and equipment review
├─ sample and method-suitability review
├─ isolate preservation and identification
├─ related batch/location/time-window review
├─ targeted additional sampling or testing
├─ product/process impact assessment
└─ CAPA and effectiveness monitoring
```

Record the decision owner, trigger and evidence for opening each branch. Capacity
planning should allow the work; the model must not convert every branch into an
automatic test plan.

## Quantify incremental workload

Model by scenario:

- immediate analyst and supervisor hours;
- second-person, QA and specialist review;
- sample retrieval, chain-of-custody and storage;
- media, diluent, neutralizer and control demand;
- reference-culture and inoculum preparation;
- cabinet, manifold, incubator and identification occupancy;
- plates, tubes, filters and container-days;
- isolate purification, preservation and shipment;
- additional sampling routes and production coordination;
- meetings, records, reports and regulatory/customer communication;
- waste, decontamination and retained-material demand;
- routine-work displacement and backlog.

Separate touch time from elapsed time. Identification, incubation or external testing
may extend the investigation without consuming continuous analyst hours.

## Model concurrency and correlated events

Investigation demand is rarely independent. One media-lot failure can affect several
methods. One incubator excursion can expose many ongoing tests. One water-system
signal can trigger multiple locations and product-impact reviews.

Create stress scenarios such as:

- one routine invalid test;
- one high-consequence product result;
- shared-media or culture-lot impact;
- equipment excursion affecting all active loads;
- multi-location water or EM trend;
- concurrent investigation plus peak routine release;
- investigation during weekend or reduced staffing;
- external identification provider delay.

Do not add average independent percentages when the underlying failures are
correlated.

## Protect routine and time-critical work

Define during a surge:

- work that must continue;
- work that can be rescheduled;
- samples approaching hold or stability limits;
- dedicated investigation personnel or escalation pool;
- qualified cross-trained staff;
- reserved incubator, storage and identification capacity;
- pre-approved external support;
- priority and management decision rights;
- communication cadence;
- backlog-recovery target.

A laboratory may have enough total monthly hours but no qualified person available to
review an unexpected result before a release decision.

## Plan isolate and identification capacity

An investigation can multiply colonies into morphotypes, subcultures, purity checks,
identifications, confirmations and retained isolates. Model:

- triage and selection rule;
- number of distinct morphotypes/isolates;
- subculture and incubation rounds;
- platform or external-provider queue;
- database/library and confidence review;
- confirmation and discordance pathway;
- isolate storage and comparison;
- report review and investigation linkage.

Use the [Microbial identification application pack](/blog/pharmaceutical-microbial-identification-application-pack)
to keep required resolution and platform evidence explicit.

## Separate retest capacity from retest authority

Capacity scenarios may include potential retest, resample or additional testing so
the laboratory can understand resource risk. They must not imply authorization.

The controlled record should state:

- who may approve the action;
- scientific hypothesis and purpose;
- sample source and quantity;
- predefined number and method;
- handling of all original and new results;
- effect on scope and disposition;
- stopping and escalation rules.

FDA's non-binding [microbiological laboratory inspection guide](https://www.fda.gov/inspections-compliance-enforcement-and-criminal-investigations/inspection-guides/microbiological-pharmaceutical-quality-control-labs-793)
specifically calls for review of the logic and rationale for microbiological retesting.

## Create a recovery model

After urgent work, calculate:

- routine backlog by method and due date;
- samples at risk of hold-time expiry;
- incubator and storage positions still occupied;
- deferred media, maintenance or qualification work;
- analyst and reviewer fatigue/availability;
- maximum safe catch-up rate;
- external or overtime support;
- time to return to normal utilization;
- risk of creating new errors during recovery.

Backlog clearance is part of continuity. Finishing the investigation report does not
mean the laboratory has returned to a controlled operating state.

## Evidence package for surge readiness

Collect:

1. investigation archetypes and triggers;
2. controlled decision tree and authorities;
3. evidence-preservation requirements;
4. branch-specific BOM and touch-time estimates;
5. incubation, storage and identification occupancy;
6. correlated failure and peak scenarios;
7. qualified staffing and review coverage;
8. routine-work prioritization rules;
9. external laboratory and shipment readiness;
10. retest/resample governance boundaries;
11. backlog-recovery model;
12. actual event frequency, duration, resource use and variance.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas should not label surge capacity adequate when:

- one generic repeat percentage replaces scenarios;
- original data and material preservation are omitted;
- correlated media, equipment or location impacts are ignored;
- extra testing is modeled as automatically authorized;
- specialist and QA review capacity is absent;
- isolate multiplication and identification delay are hidden;
- routine-work displacement is not calculated;
- weekends, provider delays and storage limits are excluded;
- backlog recovery has no target or owner;
- real investigation effort cannot be captured for calibration.
