# Why potency is the hardest QC test for a biologic

For a small molecule, identity and assay get you a long way. If the structure is
confirmed and the amount of active ingredient is within specification, the release
decision has a strong analytical foundation.

For a biologic, that is not enough. A protein can have the right sequence, the
right mass, and an acceptable purity profile, yet still lose the biological
activity that matters clinically. It can be misfolded, modified in a critical
region, aggregated, glycosylated differently, or affected by a manufacturing
change in a way that a single physicochemical assay does not fully explain.

That is why potency is so central. Potency is the quality attribute that asks:
**does this product perform the biological function it is supposed to perform?**
It ties the release test to the product's mechanism of action. It is also why
potency is often the most difficult QC method to develop, validate, transfer, and
investigate.

The hard part is not just the laboratory work. Potency sits at the intersection
of biology, statistics, reference standards, product understanding, stability,
and regulatory expectations. A good potency assay is not simply a test. It is a
controlled biological measurement system.

## What potency means for a biologic

Potency is not the same as concentration. Concentration asks how much material is
present. Potency asks how active that material is.

For a monoclonal antibody, potency may relate to target binding, receptor
blocking, complement activation, antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity, or a
cell signaling response. For a cytokine, potency may be measured by proliferation
or reporter-gene activity. For a vaccine or advanced therapy, potency may involve
multiple biological functions and may be harder to reduce to one simple readout.

The ideal potency assay reflects the relevant mechanism of action. In practice,
the method may be a cell-based functional assay, a binding assay, an enzymatic
assay, or a justified surrogate. The closer the assay is to the intended
biological effect, the more meaningful it is. The closer it is to living biology,
the more variable it usually becomes.

That trade-off is the core tension of potency testing: the most relevant assay is
often the least tidy assay.

## Why a chemical assay is not enough

Biologics are large, heterogeneous products made by living systems. Their quality
is not captured by one number. QC builds confidence through orthogonal evidence:
identity, purity, impurity profile, charge profile, glycan profile, aggregation,
quantity, binding, and potency.

Physicochemical tests are powerful. They can detect mass shifts, fragments,
aggregates, variants, impurities, and changes in product profile. But they do not
always prove biological function.

Two lots can look similar by one analytical method and differ in activity. The
reverse can also happen: a small analytical difference may not matter clinically
if potency and other critical attributes remain controlled. Potency gives the QC
system a functional anchor. It asks whether the product still behaves like the
reference product in the biological system that matters.

This is why potency is part of the specification strategy for many
biotechnological and biological products. It is not an optional "nice to know"
test. It is one of the ways the control strategy connects the molecule on paper
to the product used by a patient.

## Cell-based assays are controlled biology

Many potency methods are cell-based bioassays. The product is applied to living
cells, and the assay measures a response: proliferation, killing, reporter
signal, receptor activation, cytokine release, or another functional output.

That means the assay is only as reliable as the cell system. The sample is not
the only thing producing the result. The cells are also producing the result.

Common sources of variability include:

- Cell passage number and passage history
- Cell viability, health, and recovery after thawing
- Seeding density and attachment behavior
- Media, serum, supplements, and reagent lots
- Incubation time, temperature, humidity, and CO2 control
- Plate position, edge effects, and evaporation
- Timing between dilution, dosing, washing, and readout
- Analyst technique and instrument settings
- Freeze-thaw history of sample and reference material
- Matrix effects from formulation, excipients, or process residues

In a chemical assay, variability often feels mechanical: weighing, pipetting,
chromatography, integration. In a cell-based potency assay, variability is often
biological. A method can be technically well executed and still fail because the
cell system is drifting.

This is why a potency method needs more than a written procedure. It needs a
living control strategy for the cell bank, working cell stock, qualified passage
range, culture conditions, thaw schedule, plate layout, controls, and system
suitability.

## Relative potency, not absolute activity

Potency for biologics is commonly reported as **relative potency**. The test
sample is compared with a qualified reference standard tested in the same assay
run, often on the same plate or within the same run structure.

The result is not "the sample contains X units of activity" in an abstract sense.
It is closer to: "the sample shows X% activity relative to the reference under
these validated assay conditions."

This is a practical response to biological variability. If the cells are slightly
more responsive today than last week, both the sample and reference should be
affected. The comparison helps normalize the result. The reference standard
anchors the measurement.

That makes the reference standard one of the most important materials in the
entire method. If the reference changes, drifts, degrades, or is bridged poorly,
the potency history of the product can shift. A new standard must be qualified
against the old standard with a scientifically justified bridging plan before it
replaces the old one.

The reference is not just a reagent. It is the memory of the assay.

## Dose-response curves and the 4PL model

Potency assays usually test multiple dilutions of both the sample and the
reference. The response is plotted against dose, producing a dose-response curve.
For many bioassays, the curve is sigmoidal and is modeled with a four-parameter
logistic fit, often called 4PL.

The 4PL model describes the lower asymptote, upper asymptote, slope, and midpoint
of the curve. Relative potency is then estimated from the horizontal shift
between the sample curve and the reference curve.

The model matters because potency is not usually calculated from one point. A
single response value can be misleading if it falls near the top or bottom of the
curve, where the response is saturated and less sensitive to dose. A well-designed
assay uses the informative part of the curve, with enough dilution points and
replicates to support the calculation.

Good QC analysts do not treat the software output as a black box. They look at
the curve shape, the control responses, the placement of sample points, the
residuals, the fit, and the suitability checks. A calculated number is only
reportable if the assay behavior supports it.

## Parallelism is the gatekeeper

Relative potency assumes that the sample and reference behave similarly in the
assay system. In practical terms, their dose-response curves must be sufficiently
parallel or similar. If the curves are not parallel, the horizontal shift between
them may not represent a true potency difference.

Non-parallelism can point to several problems:

- The sample may contain a matrix effect that changes the assay response.
- The product may have changed in a way that affects the mechanism being measured.
- The reference may be unsuitable or degraded.
- The dilution series may have been prepared incorrectly.
- The cells may be responding inconsistently.
- The selected model or reportable range may not fit the data.

This is why parallelism is a gatekeeper. If similarity is not demonstrated, the
result should not be quietly reported as if it were a normal potency value.
Non-parallelism is information. It may be assay noise, but it may also be an early
signal that the sample no longer behaves like the reference.

## System suitability protects reportability

System suitability is the set of checks that proves the run is fit to interpret
before the sample result is accepted. A potency assay without meaningful system
suitability is fragile because it can produce a number even when the biological
system was not under control.

Typical suitability checks may include:

- Reference standard curve performance
- Positive and negative control response
- Blank or background signal
- Signal-to-background ratio
- EC50 or other curve parameter range
- Slope or asymptote limits
- Replicate precision
- Plate acceptance criteria
- Cell viability or growth criteria
- Parallelism or similarity criteria
- Control sample recovery or relative potency range

The important point is sequence. Suitability comes before reportability. If the
run fails suitability, the sample result is not made valid by looking convenient.
The right question becomes: what failed, why did it fail, and what does the SOP
allow next?

## Validation is harder than checking precision

Potency validation must demonstrate that the method is suitable for its intended
use. That includes the familiar validation characteristics: specificity,
accuracy, precision, range, robustness, and other relevant performance elements.
But for a bioassay, each of those ideas has biological and statistical wrinkles.

Specificity means the assay responds to the relevant biological activity, not
just to any signal that happens to move the readout. Accuracy may be expressed
through recovery or dilutional linearity relative to a reference. Precision must
cover repeatability and intermediate precision across analysts, days, equipment,
cell passages, and reagent lots where appropriate. Range must show where the
method can measure potency reliably. Robustness must challenge small changes that
could realistically occur in routine use.

For a cell-based assay, validation also needs to define the reportable range,
curve model, replicate structure, acceptance criteria, handling of invalid runs,
and rules for repeat testing. It should prove not only that the assay can generate
a number, but that the number can support a release, stability, comparability, or
development decision.

The best validation packages are honest about variability. They do not pretend a
bioassay behaves like HPLC. They define how much variability is expected, how it
is controlled, and when it becomes a quality signal.

## Potency must be stability-indicating where it matters

Potency is often a key stability attribute. If the product degrades in a way that
reduces biological activity, the potency assay should be able to detect that
loss. This is especially important when degradation is not fully captured by one
physicochemical method.

Forced degradation and stressed samples can help show whether the assay is
stability-indicating. A stressed lot that looks different by aggregation, charge,
oxidation, or fragmentation may also show reduced potency. Those relationships
help the control strategy connect structure to function.

But the relationship is not always simple. A product may show a structural change
with little immediate potency impact, or a potency loss that is difficult to
explain from a single analytical profile. That is why biologics need a panel of
methods. Potency is powerful, but it is not the whole story.

## Reference standard lifecycle is a hidden risk

Reference standards make relative potency possible, but they also create a
lifecycle challenge.

A reference standard needs qualification, storage control, stability monitoring,
inventory control, and a replacement strategy. The replacement strategy is
especially important because a reference standard will eventually run low,
expire, or become unsuitable. When a new reference is introduced, it must be
bridged against the previous standard so that historical potency results remain
interpretable.

Poor bridging can create an artificial shift in trend. A product may appear to
gain or lose potency simply because the anchor changed. That can confuse release
decisions, stability interpretation, comparability exercises, and regulatory
commitments.

Good teams treat reference standard management as part of method control, not as
an inventory afterthought.

## Potency, binding, and surrogate assays

Not every potency method is a cell-based assay. Some products use binding assays,
enzymatic assays, or other surrogate methods. A surrogate can be appropriate when
it is scientifically justified and linked to the relevant mechanism of action or
critical quality attribute.

The risk is mistaking convenience for relevance. A binding assay may be cleaner,
faster, and more precise than a cell-based functional assay, but binding is not
always the same as function. If the clinical mechanism depends on downstream
signaling, effector function, or cellular response, a binding result may not be a
complete potency measure.

This does not mean every product must use the most complicated possible assay. It
means the method must be justified. The potency strategy should explain what
biological activity matters, how the assay measures or represents it, what the
assay does not measure, and how other tests fill the gaps.

## Potency in release, stability, and comparability

Potency results do not live in isolation.

For batch release, potency helps confirm that a manufactured lot meets functional
quality expectations. For stability, potency trends show whether biological
activity is maintained through shelf life. For process changes, potency supports
comparability by showing whether the pre-change and post-change product remain
functionally similar. For investigations, potency can help distinguish an
analytical artifact from a true product quality shift.

This is also why potency OOS and OOT results can be difficult. A low potency
result may be a real product issue, but it may also come from a weak cell day, a
bad reference preparation, a failed reagent lot, a plate effect, a dilution error,
or a model/reportability problem. The investigation has to protect both sides:
do not release a weak product, and do not reject a good product because the assay
system was uncontrolled.

## How to investigate a potency problem

A potency investigation should begin with run validity. Before asking whether the
batch is weak, ask whether the assay was reportable.

Start with:

- Were all system suitability criteria met?
- Did the reference curve behave as expected?
- Were controls within historical limits?
- Was the sample curve parallel or similar to the reference?
- Were replicate precision and plate criteria acceptable?
- Were cell passage, viability, seeding density, and culture conditions within
  the qualified state?
- Were reagents, media, and reference preparations within expiry and storage
  requirements?
- Were there dilution, timing, incubation, or instrument deviations?
- Is there a trend in control sample recovery, EC50, slope, or background signal?
- Do orthogonal methods show a product quality change?

Only after the run is understood should the team decide whether repeat testing is
scientifically justified. Repeating a noisy assay until one result passes is not
an investigation. It is a data integrity risk. Repeat testing must follow the
approved procedure, be justified by the evidence, and preserve the original result
in the quality record.

## What good looks like

A mature potency control strategy has several visible features:

- The assay is linked to the product's relevant mechanism of action.
- The reference standard is qualified, controlled, trended, and replaceable
  through a bridging plan.
- The cell system has a qualified passage range and defined culture controls.
- The dilution scheme captures the informative part of the dose-response curve.
- The curve model and reportable range are justified.
- Parallelism or similarity is checked before relative potency is reported.
- System suitability is strict enough to catch bad assay runs.
- Validation reflects routine biological variability, not idealized conditions.
- Stability studies show whether potency can detect meaningful loss of activity.
- OOS/OOT investigations separate assay failure from product failure with
  evidence.
- Analysts are trained to interpret curves, controls, and trends, not just copy a
  software result.

Potency is hard because it is supposed to be hard. It is the test that asks the
most meaningful question about a biologic: does the product still work in the way
we claim it works?

The answer is never just one number. It is the result of a controlled biological
system, a qualified reference, a suitable model, valid curves, sound statistics,
and disciplined investigation.
