# Why potency is the hardest QC test for a biologic

For a small molecule, identity and assay get you a long way. For a biologic,
regulators scrutinise **potency** most — because it is the test that ties release
to the **mechanism of action**. It is the closest measure of whether the molecule
actually does what it is meant to do. And that is exactly what makes it hard.

## Biology is noisy

Many potency methods are **cell-based bioassays**: you expose living cells to the
product and measure a biological response. That response depends on the cells as
much as the sample — their passage number, health, seeding density, and even
plate-edge effects. So most of the variability you fight in a potency assay lives
in the **cell system**, not the pipetting. Controlling the biology is the job.

## Relative potency, not absolute

You almost never report an absolute number. Instead you run the sample against a
**qualified reference standard** on the same plate and report **relative potency** —
how active the sample is compared with the reference. That is why the reference
standard is so important: it anchors every result, and when it nears exhaustion you
**bridge** a new standard against the old one before switching, so the potency
history does not take a step-change.

## Parallelism is the gatekeeper

Relative potency is only valid when the sample and reference **dose-response curves
are parallel** (similar shape). If they are not, you may be measuring something
other than the intended activity — a different mechanism, or a matrix effect.
Non-parallelism is a signal to investigate, not a number to report.

## What good looks like

- The cell line is within its **qualified passage range**
- Potency is **relative**, against a qualified reference standard
- **Parallelism / similarity** is met before any result is reported
- System suitability and controls are valid for every run
- High variability triggers an **investigation**, not a quiet re-run
