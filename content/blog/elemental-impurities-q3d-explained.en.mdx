# Elemental impurities (ICH Q3D): from heavy metals to a risk-based limit

For decades, many specifications handled metals with a broad "heavy metals" test.
It was simple, but it had a serious weakness: it did not tell you which element was
present, whether the toxicology mattered for the actual route of administration,
or whether the result reflected a real patient risk.

ICH Q3D changed the question. Instead of asking whether a product passes a generic
metal screen, Q3D asks: **which elemental impurities could reasonably be present,
how much could the patient receive each day, and is that exposure below a
toxicology-based limit?**

That shift is why elemental impurities are now controlled through a documented
risk assessment, route-specific permitted daily exposures, and a justified control
strategy. It is less mechanical than the old test, but much more defensible.

## What Q3D actually controls

Elemental impurities are elements that may be present in a drug product but do not
provide therapeutic benefit. They may come from the drug substance, excipients,
reagents, catalysts, water, equipment, container-closure systems, or processing
conditions.

ICH Q3D(R2) applies to many drug products, including products containing purified
proteins and polypeptides, but it has exclusions. It does not apply to products
such as herbal products, radiopharmaceuticals, vaccines, cell therapies, whole
blood, plasma derivatives, and elements intentionally included for therapeutic
benefit. The scope matters because an elemental-impurity assessment should be
written for the actual product type, not copied from a generic template.

The core output is a control strategy showing that elemental impurities in the drug
product do not exceed their permitted daily exposures.

## PDE is the anchor

The central number in Q3D is the **Permitted Daily Exposure (PDE)**. A PDE is the
maximum acceptable daily intake of a given element from the drug product, expressed
as micrograms per day.

The same element can have different PDEs for different routes because patient
exposure changes with the route of administration. Oral, parenteral, inhalation,
and, in Q3D(R2), cutaneous and transcutaneous routes are handled separately. This
is why an elemental-impurity assessment must know the product's maximum daily dose
and route. A limit that is acceptable for a tablet may be inappropriate for an
inhaled or injected medicine.

Q3D also allows lower limits to be used for quality reasons even when patient
safety would permit a higher exposure. For example, an element could catalyze drug
substance degradation or affect another quality attribute. The PDE is the safety
ceiling, not always the only quality consideration.

## The class system focuses the work

Q3D groups elements by toxicity and likelihood of occurrence. The classes help
decide which elements must be evaluated most carefully.

- **Class 1** elements are highly toxic and should generally be considered in every
  risk assessment: cadmium, lead, arsenic, and mercury.
- **Class 2A** elements have relatively high probability of occurrence and should
  also be considered broadly: cobalt, vanadium, and nickel.
- **Class 2B** elements are less likely to occur unless intentionally added or
  otherwise implicated by the process: thallium, gold, palladium, iridium, osmium,
  rhodium, ruthenium, selenium, silver, and platinum.
- **Class 3** elements have relatively low toxicity by the oral route, but can be
  important for parenteral and inhalation products: lithium, antimony, barium,
  molybdenum, copper, tin, and chromium.

The class system is not a substitute for thinking. It is a triage tool. If a
platinum-group metal is used as a catalyst in synthesis, it belongs in the
assessment even if it is not in the highest default class. If an excipient has
known nickel variability, nickel deserves attention even before the finished
product is tested.

## Where elemental impurities come from

A good assessment begins with sources, not with the instrument.

Common sources include:

- **Drug substance synthesis** - metal catalysts, reagents, process aids, inorganic
  salts, and carryover from upstream intermediates.
- **Excipients** - natural or mined materials, mineral-derived ingredients, colorants,
  fillers, and excipients with variable supplier histories.
- **Water and utilities** - especially where contact materials, purification systems,
  or distribution loops could contribute metals.
- **Manufacturing equipment** - stainless steel, alloys, glass-lined vessels, filters,
  sieves, mills, or process contact surfaces that may shed or leach elements.
- **Container-closure systems** - glass, elastomers, pigments, coatings, metal
  components, and packaging interactions over shelf life.
- **Cross-contamination or facility history** - shared equipment, recovered solvents,
  or campaigns using metal-containing materials.

The risk assessment should cover all plausible routes into the finished drug
product. It should also explain why a source is excluded when it is not relevant.
"Not expected" is not evidence unless it is backed by process knowledge, supplier
data, prior testing, material specifications, or scientific rationale.

## From PDE to a concentration limit

The PDE is a daily exposure limit. Specifications and test results are usually
concentrations. To connect the two, you need the product's maximum daily dose.

For a simple single-product calculation:

```text
concentration limit = PDE / maximum daily dose
```

If the PDE is in micrograms per day and the daily dose is in grams per day, the
result is micrograms per gram, which is equivalent to ppm.

For example, if an element has a PDE of 100 micrograms/day and the product maximum
daily dose is 2 grams/day:

```text
100 micrograms/day / 2 grams/day = 50 micrograms/gram = 50 ppm
```

This is why high-dose products can have tighter concentration limits than low-dose
products. The patient exposure is what matters. A 10 ppm impurity level has a very
different meaning in a product taken at 10 mg/day than in a product taken at
10 g/day.

## The control threshold

Q3D uses the idea of a **control threshold**, commonly 30% of the PDE, to help
decide whether additional controls are needed. If data show an element is
consistently below the threshold with suitable margin, routine finished-product
testing may not be necessary. If levels are near or above the threshold, the
control strategy needs more attention.

That attention might include:

- tightening raw material specifications;
- qualifying or changing a supplier;
- controlling a catalyst purge step;
- adding periodic verification testing;
- adding a finished-product specification;
- monitoring container-closure contribution over shelf life;
- improving method sensitivity or sample preparation.

The threshold is a decision aid, not a permission slip to ignore variability. The
assessment should consider data quality, number of batches, supplier variability,
process changes, and whether the sample set represents the commercial process.

## Testing is evidence, not the whole strategy

Elemental-impurity testing is usually performed with techniques such as **ICP-MS**
or **ICP-OES**. ICP-MS is often preferred when very low detection limits are needed,
while ICP-OES can be suitable for higher-level elements and simpler matrices.

Testing may be used to:

- confirm a low-risk assessment;
- quantify a known or likely impurity;
- support supplier qualification;
- investigate an unexpected source;
- set or justify a specification;
- verify that a process change did not increase exposure.

But routine testing of every element in every batch is not the purpose of Q3D. The
purpose is a risk-based control strategy. In some cases, strong process knowledge
and supplier data may justify control without routine finished-product testing. In
other cases, especially where a catalyst or high-risk excipient is involved,
testing is the only credible way to quantify the risk.

## Supplier data can help, but only if it is usable

Elemental-impurity assessments often depend on information from drug substance,
excipient, and packaging suppliers. Supplier statements are useful only when they
are specific enough to support the risk assessment.

Stronger supplier evidence includes:

- actual elemental data with methods and limits of quantitation;
- representative batch history;
- route-specific relevance to the finished product;
- specification limits or routine controls;
- change-notification commitments;
- clear indication of intentionally added catalysts or metal-containing materials.

Weak evidence includes broad letters that say "complies with Q3D" without data,
unspecified testing panels, outdated heavy-metals statements, or declarations that
do not identify which elements were assessed. A supplier letter can be an input,
but the finished-product manufacturer still owns the assessment.

## Container closure and shelf life

Elemental impurities are not only synthesis residues. Packaging can matter,
especially for liquid, parenteral, inhalation, ophthalmic, and long-shelf-life
products. Glass, elastomers, inks, pigments, and metal components may contribute
elements under certain conditions.

The assessment should consider whether container-closure contribution could change
over time. Extractables and leachables data may support the elemental-impurity
story, but they are not automatically interchangeable. The question is whether
elements that could leach into the drug product are controlled below the applicable
PDE through shelf life and under intended storage conditions.

This is where elemental impurities connect naturally with the container-closure
and extractables/leachables programs.

## Change control is where Q3D stays alive

An elemental-impurity assessment is not a one-time filing artifact. It has to
survive product lifecycle changes.

Review the assessment when there is:

- a new or changed drug substance route;
- a catalyst, reagent, solvent, or process-aid change;
- a new excipient supplier or grade;
- a packaging or container-closure change;
- a manufacturing-site or equipment-train change;
- a change in maximum daily dose or route of administration;
- a new dosage form, strength, or patient population;
- unexpected elemental data from stability, supplier testing, or investigations;
- a pharmacopoeial, ICH, or regional regulatory update.

The most common failure is treating the Q3D assessment as a static document. If the
sources change, the assessment changes. If the daily dose changes, the exposure
calculation changes. If the route changes, the PDEs may change.

## What good looks like

A defensible elemental-impurity package usually contains:

- scope and applicability for the product;
- maximum daily dose and route of administration;
- the Q3D(R2) element list and class rationale;
- mapped potential sources across drug substance, excipients, water, equipment, and
  container closure;
- supplier evidence and its limitations;
- calculations translating PDE to concentration limits where needed;
- data summary, including batch representativeness and method capability;
- clear decision on whether routine testing, periodic verification, or material
  controls are required;
- lifecycle triggers that require reassessment;
- QA approval and linkage to specifications, methods, and change control.

The package should be understandable to a reviewer who was not involved in writing
it. If the assessment requires private memory to explain why an element was
excluded, the document is not finished.

## Common audit weaknesses

Watch for these patterns:

- The assessment lists Q3D elements but does not map product-specific sources.
- The route or maximum daily dose is missing, outdated, or inconsistent with the
  approved label.
- Class 2B elements are dismissed even though metal catalysts are used upstream.
- Excipient supplier letters are accepted without data or scope.
- Finished-product testing is used as a substitute for understanding the process.
- ICP method capability is not matched to the calculated limits.
- A packaging change was made without revisiting elemental impurities.
- Change control does not identify Q3D as an impacted quality assessment.
- The assessment is copied across products with different doses or routes.

These are not just documentation issues. They can lead to limits that do not
reflect real patient exposure.

## Review checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing a Q3D assessment:

- Is Q3D applicable to this product type?
- Are the route of administration and maximum daily dose correct?
- Are PDEs taken from the current Q3D(R2) source?
- Are Class 1 and 2A elements considered appropriately?
- Are Class 2B elements assessed when intentionally added or process-relevant?
- Are Class 3 elements handled correctly for oral, parenteral, inhalation, or
  cutaneous/transcutaneous use?
- Are all plausible sources mapped: drug substance, excipients, water, equipment,
  container closure, and facility history?
- Are concentration limits calculated from PDE and daily dose where needed?
- Are test methods capable at the required levels?
- Are supplier data specific, current, and representative?
- Is the control strategy clear: no routine testing, periodic testing, raw material
  control, process control, or finished-product specification?
- Are lifecycle reassessment triggers defined?

## Key takeaway

ICH Q3D is not a metals test. It is a patient-exposure argument. The strongest
elemental-impurity packages show where elements could enter, how much the patient
could receive, why the route-specific PDE is protected, and which controls keep
that conclusion true through the product lifecycle.
