# Elemental impurities (ICH Q3D): from heavy metals to a risk-based limit

For decades, "heavy metals" in a product meant a single, non-specific colorimetric
test that could not tell you **which** metal or **how much**. ICH Q3D replaced that
with something far more useful and more demanding: a **toxicology-based, risk-assessed**
control of elemental impurities.

## PDEs, not a generic pass/fail

Q3D sets a **Permitted Daily Exposure (PDE)** for each element of concern — the
amount a patient can take in per day without harm — and it varies by **route of
administration** (oral, parenteral, inhalation), because the same element is more or
less hazardous depending on how it enters the body. Elements are grouped by toxicity
and likelihood of occurrence (the Class 1, 2, 3 system), which focuses attention on
the ones that matter most.

## Where elemental impurities come from

The control strategy starts by asking **where could these elements enter the
product?** Typically:

- The **drug substance** and synthesis (catalysts, reagents)
- **Excipients**
- **Water**
- **Manufacturing equipment** (contact surfaces)
- **Container-closure** systems

A risk assessment maps each potential source against the PDE.

## Why a risk assessment beats blanket testing

The point of Q3D is that you do not have to test every element in every batch.
Instead you build a **risk assessment**: if the data and the process show an element
cannot reach a meaningful fraction of its PDE, you can control it without routine
testing. Where the risk is real, you test or control it specifically. That is far
more scientific — and more defensible — than the old one-size-fits-all test.

## What good looks like

- A documented risk assessment covering all the potential sources
- PDEs applied for the **actual route** of administration
- Specific, validated analytical methods (e.g. ICP-MS / ICP-OES) where testing is
  warranted
- Control justified by data, not assumption
