# Part 11: what makes an electronic record trustworthy

When a GMP record lives in software instead of a paper logbook, the central
question is simple:

> Can this electronic record be trusted as much as a controlled, signed paper
> record?

21 CFR Part 11 is the US rule that answers that question for FDA-regulated
electronic records and electronic signatures. EU GMP Annex 11 covers similar
ground for computerized systems used in GMP activities. They are not just IT
requirements. They are data integrity requirements.

The purpose is not to make software look impressive. The purpose is to make sure
electronic records are accurate, attributable, complete, secure, retrievable, and
usable throughout their required retention period. In plain language: if the
system is used to make a GMP decision, the record must tell the truth and keep
telling the truth later.

## Part 11 applies because of the record, not the software label

One of the most common mistakes is asking, "Is this software Part 11 compliant?"
as if compliance is a product feature that can be bought once and filed away.

The better question is:

> Are we using this system to create, modify, maintain, archive, retrieve, or
> transmit electronic records required by FDA regulations, or to apply electronic
> signatures to those records?

Part 11 applies when electronic records are used to satisfy predicate rule
requirements. Predicate rules are the underlying GMP, GLP, GCP, device, or other
FDA regulations that require the record in the first place. Part 11 does not
create the need for a batch record, laboratory record, complaint record, or
training record. It defines conditions for relying on the electronic version of
that required record.

That means scope depends on intended use. The same platform may be low risk in
one process and high risk in another. A document management system used for lunch
menus is not the same as one used for approved SOPs, batch release records, or
quality investigations. A spreadsheet used to plan a meeting is not the same as a
spreadsheet used to calculate a release result.

## Electronic records need controls around the whole lifecycle

A trustworthy electronic record is not only the final PDF report. It includes the
data and metadata needed to reconstruct what happened.

For laboratory systems, that may include raw data, processed data, methods,
sequence files, integration parameters, calibration status, system suitability,
audit trail, result calculations, approval history, and final report. For a QMS,
it may include workflow history, attachments, reviewer comments, CAPA changes,
signature meaning, escalation history, and closure evidence.

The lifecycle question is:

- How is the record created?
- Who can create or change it?
- What metadata proves who did what and when?
- What changes are captured?
- What review is performed before approval?
- How is the record approved or signed?
- How is it protected from deletion or alteration?
- How is it retained, backed up, archived, and retrieved?
- Can the site produce a complete and readable copy during an inspection?

If the system cannot answer those questions, it may generate data, but it does
not yet generate trustworthy GMP records.

## The core controls

Part 11 and Annex 11 expectations can be grouped into several practical controls.

| Control | What it means in practice | Common weak point |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Validation | The system is proven fit for its intended GxP use | Vendor certificate accepted without testing configuration and workflow |
| Access control | Unique users, appropriate roles, no shared logins | Analysts have admin rights or shared accounts |
| Audit trail | Secure, computer-generated, time-stamped history of critical actions | Trail exists but is disabled, incomplete, or never reviewed |
| Record copies | Complete, accurate, human-readable and electronic copies can be produced | PDF omits raw data, metadata, or audit trail |
| Retention | Records remain protected and retrievable for the required period | Archive requires vendor help or old software that no longer runs |
| Electronic signature | Signature is linked to the record and shows signer, time, and meaning | Login password treated as a signature without signature meaning |
| System security | Records are protected from unauthorized alteration or deletion | Users can delete data without independent review |
| Change control | Configuration, patches, interfaces, and workflows are controlled | Updates applied without impact assessment |

These controls work together. A system can have electronic signatures but still
fail data integrity if users share accounts. It can have an audit trail but still
fail if nobody reviews critical exceptions before release. It can generate a neat
PDF but still fail if the original dynamic record cannot be reconstructed.

## Validation is not optional

Part 11 expects systems to be validated to ensure accuracy, reliability,
consistent intended performance, and the ability to detect invalid or altered
records. Annex 11 similarly expects the application to be validated and the IT
infrastructure to be qualified.

Validation does not mean testing every button with equal intensity. It means the
site understands intended use and proves the critical functions that affect
patient safety, product quality, and data integrity.

For a chromatography data system, high-risk functions include acquisition,
processing, integration, sequence control, audit trail, report generation, user
roles, backup, restore, and electronic approval. For a QMS, they may include
workflow routing, due-date control, approval, e-signature, attachments, audit
trail, and record retention. For a spreadsheet, they may include formulas, locked
cells, version control, input ranges, calculation verification, and review.

The validation package should make the story obvious:

- intended use and GxP impact;
- user requirements or critical process requirements;
- supplier assessment where relevant;
- risk assessment for patient safety, product quality, and data integrity;
- configuration baseline;
- tests or leveraged evidence proportional to risk;
- Part 11 / Annex 11 assessment;
- SOPs for use, review, backup, archive, and change control;
- approval for release to use;
- periodic review plan.

A vendor brochure saying "Part 11 ready" is not validation. The site owns the
intended use and configuration.

## Audit trails are evidence, not decoration

The audit trail is often the most visible Part 11 control. It should capture
critical actions such as creation, modification, deletion, reprocessing,
integration changes, sequence changes, method changes, privilege changes, clock
changes, record approval, and other events that affect the reliability of the
record.

But an audit trail is not useful simply because it exists. It must be:

- enabled for GMP-critical records and actions;
- secure from ordinary user alteration;
- time-stamped and attributable to unique users;
- retained with the record;
- available for review;
- included in the record review process where it affects the data;
- periodically checked for system-level risks such as role changes or disabled
  audit trail settings.

The practical question is not "does the system have an audit trail?" It is:
"which audit-trail events matter for this record, who reviews them, when are they
reviewed, what exceptions trigger escalation, and where is the review documented?"

For batch release data, audit trail review often needs to happen before final
approval of the record. For system administration events, periodic review may be
appropriate. The frequency and depth should follow risk, but the rationale must
be visible.

## Electronic signatures must mean something

An electronic signature is not just a username and password. It is a controlled
act that must be linked to a specific record and show who signed, when they
signed, and what the signature meant.

Common signature meanings include:

- performed by;
- reviewed by;
- approved by;
- verified by;
- QA approved;
- released by;
- rejected by.

The signature should not be removable, transferable, or copied to another record.
The system should require identity verification at signing according to the site's
approved procedure. Users must understand that an electronic signature has the
same seriousness as a handwritten signature.

One weak pattern is "click approve" without a clear meaning. Another is allowing a
supervisor to sign under someone else's login. Both destroy the point of the
signature: accountability.

## Static versus dynamic records

FDA data integrity guidance distinguishes static and dynamic record formats. A
static record is fixed, like a paper printout or a flat image. A dynamic record
allows interaction with the underlying data, such as changing chromatographic
scale, viewing integration, checking sequence history, or reviewing metadata.

This matters because a PDF may not be the complete GMP record. If the quality
decision depends on dynamic data, then the dynamic data and associated metadata
must remain available. A final PDF that hides manual integration changes, deleted
injections, or sequence edits is not a complete record.

Good systems make clear what the official record is. Is it the database record?
The signed PDF? The raw data plus final report? The answer must be defined in the
SOP and reflected in validation, review, backup, archive, and retrieval.

## Hybrid systems are especially risky

Many sites operate hybrid systems: part paper, part electronic. For example, an
instrument may generate electronic raw data, an analyst may print a report, and
QA may sign the paper packet.

Hybrid systems are not automatically wrong, but they create traps:

- the paper printout may omit audit trail and metadata;
- the electronic original may not be reviewed;
- raw data may be overwritten or deleted after printing;
- the paper packet may look complete while the real record lives elsewhere;
- responsibilities between QC, QA, and IT may be unclear.

The site needs to define the original record and the official review process. If
the electronic system creates the original GMP data, printing it does not make the
electronic data irrelevant. The audit trail, metadata, and raw data still need
control and review.

## Annex 11 adds lifecycle and process emphasis

EU GMP Annex 11 is not identical to Part 11, but the practical direction is
similar: computerized systems used in GMP must be validated, controlled, secure,
available, and governed through their lifecycle.

Annex 11 places strong emphasis on risk management, supplier and service provider
management, data integrity, accuracy checks, data storage, printouts, audit
trails, change control, periodic evaluation, security, incident management,
business continuity, and archiving.

The Annex 11 principle is especially useful: when a computerized system replaces a
manual operation, there should be no decrease in product quality, process control,
or quality assurance, and no increase in overall process risk.

That is a simple test for any digitalization project. If moving from paper to
software makes records easier to alter, harder to review, or less available, the
project has not improved compliance.

## "Part 11 compliant software" is not enough

Vendors can provide important capabilities: audit trails, role management,
e-signatures, backup tools, validation documentation, supplier quality evidence,
and technical security features. Those are useful inputs.

But the regulated company still has to configure, validate, use, review, and
maintain the system correctly.

Common gaps include:

- buying the compliant module but not enabling key controls;
- enabling audit trails but never reviewing them;
- granting broad admin rights to routine users;
- failing to test configured workflows;
- using shared service accounts for GMP actions;
- storing backups without restore testing;
- using exported PDFs as the official record while ignoring dynamic data;
- applying patches without change control;
- keeping retired systems without a retrievable archive strategy.

Compliance is not inside the software alone. It is in the combination of system
capability, configuration, validation, procedure, training, review, and culture.

## What inspectors often ask

During an inspection, Part 11 questions often become very concrete:

- Show me who can create, modify, approve, or delete this record.
- Show me the audit trail for this result.
- Show me where the audit trail review is documented.
- Show me how you know the audit trail cannot be disabled by the analyst.
- Show me the user access review for this system.
- Show me the validation package for this configuration.
- Show me the official record definition.
- Show me how electronic signatures are linked to records.
- Show me a complete copy of the record, including metadata where needed.
- Show me how archived records can be retrieved during the retention period.
- Show me what happens when the system is patched or upgraded.
- Show me how exceptions are escalated.

The best answer is not a long policy. It is a working system with evidence that a
reviewer can navigate calmly.

## Practical Part 11 assessment checklist

Use this as a first-pass review for any GxP system that creates or maintains
electronic records.

1. **Intended use:** What GMP process or decision does the system support?
2. **Record scope:** Which electronic records and signatures are in scope?
3. **Official record:** What is the original or official GMP record?
4. **Validation:** Has the configured system been validated for intended use?
5. **Access:** Are all users unique, role-based, and periodically reviewed?
6. **Admin rights:** Are privileged rights restricted and independently reviewed?
7. **Audit trail:** Are critical actions captured, secure, retained, and reviewable?
8. **Audit trail review:** Is review risk-based, timely, documented, and effective?
9. **E-signature:** Does the signature show signer, date/time, and meaning?
10. **Copies:** Can the site produce complete, accurate, readable copies?
11. **Retention:** Are records protected through the full retention period?
12. **Backup and restore:** Has restore been verified, not just backup creation?
13. **Interfaces:** Are transferred data complete, accurate, and reconciled?
14. **Change control:** Are configuration changes, patches, and upgrades assessed?
15. **Periodic review:** Does the system remain fit for intended use over time?

## What good looks like

A trustworthy electronic-record program has a few obvious traits:

- users have unique accounts and appropriate roles;
- analysts cannot silently delete, overwrite, or alter critical records;
- audit trails are enabled for meaningful events and reviewed by risk;
- reviewers look at exceptions before approving records;
- electronic signatures are linked, meaningful, and accountable;
- official records are defined, complete, retained, and retrievable;
- validation covers the actual configuration and intended use;
- hybrid paper/electronic workflows do not hide the electronic original;
- vendors support compliance, but the site owns use and control;
- periodic review catches drift in access, configuration, archive, and procedure.

Part 11 is not the end goal. The goal is trustworthy data. Part 11, Annex 11,
CSV/CSA, audit trail review, access control, and electronic signatures are tools
that help a site prove that trust.
