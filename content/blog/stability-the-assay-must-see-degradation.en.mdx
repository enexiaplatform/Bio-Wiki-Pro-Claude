# Stability: your assay has to be able to see degradation

A stability program decides a product's shelf life, but its whole value rests on
two unglamorous things: methods that can actually **see degradation**, and
disciplined, **on-time** sample pulls. Get either wrong and the data reassures you
while the product quietly fails.

## Stability-indicating or nothing

An assay that measures potency but cannot resolve degradation products will happily
report a **stable potency on a degrading product**. That is why stability methods
must be **stability-indicating** — validated to detect and separate the relevant
degradants, not just quantify the active. This is the single most important
attribute of a stability method.

## The conditions and the design

ICH Q1A defines the storage conditions you study in parallel:

- **Long-term** — the conditions that support the labeled shelf life
- **Intermediate** — a step up, used if accelerated shows significant change
- **Accelerated** — stress conditions that can support shelf-life decisions and
  flag degradation pathways

Bracketing and matrixing (ICH Q1D) let you reduce the number of samples tested
where justified, and Q1E governs how far you may **extrapolate** long-term data to
assign a shelf life — only as far as the data support.

## Pull on time and trend, don't just pass

- A **missed pull** is a deviation — the time point is the data
- Test against the protocol, but **trend each attribute over time** rather than
  judging pass/fail at each point in isolation
- An **out-of-trend** result triggers an investigation and a shelf-life impact
  assessment before the next pull

The signal you are hunting is the **trend**: a result drifting toward a limit over
time is more informative than any single in-spec point. That is also why ongoing
(commitment) stability continues for the life of the product.
