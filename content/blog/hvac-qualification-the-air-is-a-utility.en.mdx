# HVAC qualification: the air is a critical utility

In a cleanroom, the air is not background. It is a **critical utility and a process
input.** The HVAC system controls non-viable particles, viable contamination risk,
pressure direction, temperature, humidity, and the comfort conditions that keep
operators from fighting the room.

That is why HVAC qualification is bigger than a commissioning exercise. A fan can
turn on, dampers can move, and screens can show green values while the cleanroom
still fails its purpose. The GMP question is sharper: does the air-handling system
create and maintain the environmental conditions required for the process, the
cleanroom grade, and the contamination control strategy?

## What HVAC qualification is really proving

HVAC qualification proves that the designed air-control concept works in the
actual room with the actual filters, grilles, doors, equipment, people flow, and
operating state. It connects engineering design to GMP evidence.

At a minimum, the evidence should answer:

- Are the correct filters installed, sealed, and leak-tested?
- Is airflow delivered at the required volume, velocity, pattern, and direction?
- Are pressure differentials stable enough to protect cleaner areas from dirtier
  areas?
- Does the room recover after a disturbance within the justified time?
- Are temperature and humidity controlled within approved ranges?
- Does the qualified state support cleanroom classification and routine monitoring?

The strongest qualification packages do not treat these as isolated tests. They
explain how the tests work together. A pressure cascade with weak door discipline
does not protect much. A Grade A airflow pattern with a poorly placed obstruction
is not a Grade A control. A recovery test that passes empty may say little about
what happens after setup, cleaning, maintenance, or an intervention-heavy run.

## Start with the intended use

Before writing protocol tables, define what the room is for. A warehouse airlock,
Grade D component preparation room, Grade B background, and Grade A filling zone
do not need the same evidence depth.

Good intended-use language includes:

- product and process type;
- cleanroom grade or ISO class target;
- at-rest and in-operation states to be demonstrated;
- critical operations performed in the room;
- personnel, material, waste, and equipment flows;
- open product, exposed sterile surfaces, or high-risk aseptic connections;
- environmental control ranges for temperature and humidity;
- alarm limits and responses for differential pressure or other critical parameters.

This matters because HVAC qualification should be risk-based. The deeper the
contamination consequence, the stronger the evidence needed. EU GMP Annex 1
explicitly ties sterile manufacturing controls to quality risk management and the
contamination control strategy, so the protocol should not look like a generic
engineering checklist dropped into a GMP binder.

## The core tests

HVAC qualification normally includes IQ, OQ, and PQ logic, but the cleanroom value
comes from the technical tests underneath those labels.

### Installation checks

Installation qualification confirms that the system was built as intended:

- air handling unit identity and capacity;
- terminal HEPA or ULPA filter identity, grade, orientation, and location;
- room finishes, return-air locations, dampers, sensors, and displays;
- calibration status for instruments used in qualification;
- as-built drawings and room pressure maps;
- preventive maintenance requirements;
- alarm, interlock, and building-management-system configuration.

This stage is not paperwork decoration. If a pressure transmitter is installed in
the wrong place, or a terminal filter is not the approved type, later test results
may appear acceptable while the design intent has already been compromised.

### HEPA filter integrity

Filter integrity testing shows that the installed filter and seal do not allow a
direct particle path around or through the filter. For high-risk sterile areas,
this is one of the most important tests in the package. A clean particle count on
one day cannot compensate for an unchallenged leak path.

The report should identify the challenge method, scan method, aerosol or test
approach, acceptance criteria, repaired leaks if any, and retest evidence. It
should also state when retesting is required, such as after filter replacement,
relevant maintenance, physical damage, room modification, or defined periodic
intervals.

### Airflow volume, velocity, and pattern

Airflow testing checks whether the room receives the designed air supply. In
non-unidirectional rooms, that usually means air volume and air-change rate. In
unidirectional Grade A or critical zones, it also means velocity profile and flow
uniformity where they are part of the design basis.

However, volume alone is not enough. Air must move in a way that protects the
operation. Airflow visualization, often called smoke studies, shows whether first
air reaches the critical point, whether turbulence is created by equipment or
operators, and whether contamination can be swept toward exposed product,
components, or sterile surfaces.

FDA aseptic guidance places strong emphasis on airflow in critical areas because
aseptic processing depends on preventing contamination from reaching sterile
materials. Annex 1 similarly expects airflow patterns and cleanroom design to be
understood as part of the contamination control strategy.

### Pressure cascade

Differential pressure is the room's directional boundary. It helps ensure that air
flows from cleaner areas to less clean areas when doors open or leakage paths
exist. A pressure cascade should be mapped across the facility, not reviewed room
by room without context.

Qualification should challenge:

- steady-state pressure differentials;
- adjacent room relationships;
- door-open recovery behavior where relevant;
- alarm setpoints and response time;
- pressure effects during equipment start-up, shutdown, or mode changes;
- material and personnel transfer routes.

The danger is false confidence. A single room can meet its pressure target while
the overall cascade has an unintended reversal. The audit question is usually not
"what is the number on this gauge?" but "how do you know air moves the right way
under normal and foreseeable conditions?"

### Recovery

Recovery testing asks how quickly the room returns to its required cleanliness
state after a particle challenge or defined disturbance. It is especially useful
for understanding whether a room can recover after cleaning, setup, door opening,
maintenance, or other realistic events.

Recovery time should be justified. If the SOP says production may begin after a
fixed clean-up period, the qualification should support that waiting time. If a
room is used in campaigns, the evidence should fit the campaign rhythm, not only
the ideal empty-room condition.

### Temperature and humidity

Temperature and humidity are sometimes treated as comfort parameters, but they can
affect process control, material behavior, microbial growth risk, operator
performance, static charge, and instrument stability. Qualification should show
control across the approved ranges and identify what happens when seasonal load,
personnel count, equipment heat, or cleaning activities change the room condition.

For some products, humidity control may be directly product-critical. For others,
it may mainly protect the operation and people. Either way, the rationale should
be written.

## Classification is related, not identical

Cleanroom classification and HVAC qualification are often confused. They are
connected, but not the same thing.

HVAC qualification asks whether the air-handling system performs as designed.
Cleanroom classification asks whether the cleanroom meets the defined particle
concentration class in the required state. A room may pass a narrow classification
snapshot while still having weak HVAC lifecycle controls. The reverse can also
happen: a well-qualified HVAC system may fail classification if cleaning,
equipment layout, or operator activity undermines the room state.

For audit readiness, link the two:

- HVAC qualification establishes the technical capability of the system.
- Classification confirms the room can meet its cleanliness grade.
- Environmental monitoring shows whether the controlled state remains acceptable
  during routine use.
- The contamination control strategy explains why those controls are sufficient
  for the process risk.

That chain is more defensible than treating the classification report as a
stand-alone certificate.

## At rest versus in operation

The at-rest state shows what the room can do when equipment is installed and
operating but people are absent. The in-operation state is the harder question:
what happens when people, materials, interventions, waste movement, and equipment
heat are present?

In-operation testing is where weak assumptions surface. Smoke studies may show
that a hand movement blocks first air. Pressure may become unstable during
frequent transfers. Temperature may drift during long operations. A return grille
that looked harmless on a drawing may pull air across a critical manipulation.

For sterile areas, in-operation evidence should be connected to the actual process
and aseptic process simulation strategy. For lower-grade rooms, it should still
reflect realistic occupancy, equipment configuration, and traffic.

## Change control is where HVAC qualification survives

HVAC systems are easy to disturb. Common changes that should trigger impact
assessment include:

- filter replacement or repair;
- fan, damper, sensor, or control-loop changes;
- room layout changes;
- equipment added, moved, or removed;
- new heat or humidity load;
- changed personnel or material flow;
- altered cleaning or disinfection approach;
- new product, process, or campaign type;
- repeated pressure, particle, or EM excursions.

The question is not always "do we need full requalification?" It is "which part of
the qualified state may no longer be supported?" A small equipment move may not
change air-change rate, but it can change airflow pattern at the critical point.
A BMS software change may not touch the room, but it can alter alarms, trends, or
response timing.

## What auditors look for

HVAC findings often appear when the site has test records but cannot tell the
control story. Expect auditors to probe:

- whether room grades are justified by process risk;
- whether smoke studies reflect the current equipment layout and interventions;
- whether differential pressure alarms are investigated, trended, and linked to
  batch impact where needed;
- whether HEPA integrity failures trigger appropriate product and room impact
  assessment;
- whether recovery time supports actual SOP waiting periods;
- whether monitoring locations match risk and airflow knowledge;
- whether repeated HVAC alarms are treated as a facility trend, not nuisance
  events;
- whether changes are assessed against the contamination control strategy.

The best answer is usually a connected package: URS and design rationale, risk
assessment, qualification protocol, raw data, deviations, classification reports,
EM trends, alarm history, maintenance records, and change controls.

## Practical review checklist

Use this when reviewing an HVAC qualification package:

1. Confirm the intended use, cleanroom grade, and operating state are clearly
   defined.
2. Check that acceptance criteria are pre-approved and linked to design,
   regulation, SOPs, or risk rationale.
3. Verify HEPA integrity testing covers installed filters and leak paths.
4. Compare airflow visualization to the current equipment layout and operator
   interventions.
5. Review pressure cascade maps, not only individual room readings.
6. Confirm recovery time supports the written clean-up or room-release practice.
7. Check temperature and humidity data against actual process and occupancy loads.
8. Ensure deviations were investigated, not averaged away.
9. Verify classification and EM programs use the qualification knowledge.
10. Confirm change control defines when requalification, partial testing, or smoke
    study repetition is required.

## Common failure modes

The most common weak spots are predictable:

- generic protocols that do not reflect the room's actual risk;
- smoke studies performed only in an empty or ideal configuration;
- pressure readings recorded without explaining cascade direction;
- HEPA leaks repaired without sufficient impact assessment;
- recovery tests that do not match SOP waiting times;
- BMS alarms reviewed as engineering events instead of GMP signals;
- old qualification reports kept after room layout changes;
- classification, EM, maintenance, and change control records stored in separate
  silos with no cross-review.

None of these necessarily means the room is uncontrolled. But each one weakens
the site's ability to prove control when challenged.

## Key takeaway

HVAC qualification is not about proving that air moves. It is about proving that
air movement protects the process. In a cleanroom, air is part of the control
strategy: it separates areas, sweeps contamination away from critical points,
supports classification, and gives environmental monitoring a defensible
foundation.

If the HVAC package cannot explain that story, the room may still be running, but
the evidence is not yet doing its job.
