# HVAC qualification: the air is a critical utility

In a cleanroom, the air is not just the environment — it is a **critical utility and
a process input.** The heating, ventilation, and air-conditioning (HVAC) system
controls particle counts, microbial load, pressure relationships, temperature, and
humidity. Qualify it poorly and every downstream environmental result rests on a
shaky foundation.

## What HVAC qualification covers

Beyond the standard IQ/OQ/PQ, HVAC qualification verifies the things that actually
keep a cleanroom clean:

- **HEPA filter integrity** — filters installed correctly and leak-tested (e.g.
  DOP/PAO), because a filter leak is a direct particle and contamination path
- **Airflow** — volume and air-change rate, plus **airflow visualisation (smoke
  studies)** in critical zones to show unidirectional flow sweeps contamination away
  from the product
- **Pressure cascade** — the differential pressures that keep cleaner rooms positive
  to dirtier ones, so air flows the right way through doorways
- **Recovery** — how quickly the room returns to its cleanliness class after a
  disturbance
- **Temperature and humidity** control within the required ranges

## It underpins classification

A cleanroom's grade (A–D, or ISO class) is only meaningful if the HVAC that
maintains it is qualified and monitored. Cleanroom classification (non-viable
particle counts at rest and in operation) is, in effect, a measurement of whether
the HVAC is doing its job — so the two are inseparable.

## Maintaining the qualified state

Like any qualification, it does not end at sign-off. Filter integrity is re-tested
periodically, airflow and pressures are monitored, and changes to the system, room
use, or equipment layout are assessed through change control — because a layout
change can quietly disturb the airflow pattern a smoke study once validated.
