# What Is an Out-of-Specification (OOS) Result?

An **Out-of-Specification (OOS)** result is a test result that falls **outside**
the established acceptance criteria (specification) in a registered or compendial
method. OOS results are one of the most scrutinized areas in any FDA or EU GMP
inspection — largely because of how easy it is to handle them badly.

## OOS vs OOT vs OOE

- **OOS** — outside the *specification* limit (a pass/fail boundary).
- **OOT (Out-of-Trend)** — within spec, but inconsistent with the historical
  pattern (an early warning).
- **OOE (Out-of-Expectation)** — atypical versus what the method/analyst expected,
  even if within spec.

All three deserve attention; only OOS is an automatic spec failure.

## The two-phase investigation

The well-known framework (rooted in the *Barr* court decision and FDA guidance) is:

1. **Phase I — Laboratory investigation.** Before touching the result, check for an
   assignable lab cause: calculation errors, equipment malfunction, sample/standard
   prep mistakes. Only a *documented, assignable* lab error justifies invalidating
   the result.
2. **Phase II — Full-scale (manufacturing) investigation.** If no lab cause is
   found, the result stands and you investigate the batch: process, materials,
   other batches. This may lead to rejection.

## You can't "test into compliance"

Retesting without a justified, documented reason — hoping a passing result replaces
the failing one — is **testing into compliance**, and it's a classic data-integrity
violation. Retests must be defined *before* you run them, with scientific
justification.

## Why it matters

How a company handles a single OOS reveals the health of its whole quality
culture. Sound investigations protect patients and keep the batch decision
defensible.
