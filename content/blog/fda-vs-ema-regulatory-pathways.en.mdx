# FDA vs EMA: the two regulatory pathways

The US and EU are the two largest regulated markets, and a regulatory team usually
plans for both. They share the CTD dossier structure, but the **routes to
approval** differ — and knowing those differences shapes strategy and timelines.

## The US route (FDA)

The US Food and Drug Administration reviews and approves products through a few
main application types:

- **NDA** — New Drug Application, for new small-molecule drugs.
- **BLA** — Biologics License Application, for biological products.
- **ANDA** — Abbreviated New Drug Application, for generics that reference an
  approved product.

The FDA reviews centrally for the whole US market; a single approval gives national
access.

## The EU routes (EMA and national agencies)

The EU is several markets, so it offers more than one procedure:

- **Centralised procedure** — one application to the EMA; the CHMP issues a
  scientific opinion and the European Commission grants a single marketing
  authorisation valid across the EU. Mandatory for certain products (e.g. many
  biologics and new actives) and optional for others.
- **National procedure** — authorisation in a single member state.
- **Decentralised** and **mutual-recognition** procedures — ways to obtain or extend
  authorisation across several member states, building on a reference state's
  assessment.

So "EU approval" can mean one centralised authorisation or a coordinated set of
national ones, depending on the product and strategy.

## What differs for a regulatory team

- **Module 1** of the dossier is region-specific — the FDA and EMA expect different
  administrative content, forms, and product information.
- **Procedure choice** drives timelines and market reach: centralised gives the
  whole EU at once; national/decentralised can suit a narrower launch.
- **Lifecycle** (variations, renewals) follows different rules in each region, so
  the same change can be handled differently on each side of the Atlantic.

The dossier is largely shared; the strategy, procedure, and regional modules are
where the regulatory judgement lives.
