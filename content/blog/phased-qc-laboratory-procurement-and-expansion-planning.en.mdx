# Phased QC laboratory procurement and expansion planning

An equipment list does not tell a project what to buy first. Procurement sequence
must follow capability dependencies, long-lead risk, facility readiness,
qualification, method transfer, cash flow and the date each quality decision is
needed.

```text
capability and demand scenarios
→ minimum viable operating wave
→ asset, service and infrastructure dependencies
→ lead-time and critical-path schedule
→ procurement packages and decision gates
→ installation, qualification and ramp-up
→ measured expansion triggers
```

Atlas can structure the sequence and evidence. It does not place orders, approve
budgets, select suppliers, set construction schedules or authorize deferred quality
capabilities.

## Define procurement by capability wave

For each phase state:

- products, methods and quality decisions enabled;
- planned volume, peak and turnaround;
- in-house, outsourced or hybrid allocation;
- rooms, utilities, equipment and systems required;
- people, materials and method-readiness dependencies;
- target operational-readiness date;
- assumptions, exclusions and temporary controls;
- owner, approval gate and success measures.

A phase should deliver an operable capability, not a collection of purchased assets.

## Build the dependency network

For every procurement item connect:

- capability and method requirement;
- vendor-neutral URS and approved configuration;
- room, structural and access need;
- utility and environmental interface;
- network, cybersecurity and data-system interface;
- design review and permits;
- fabrication, factory testing and delivery;
- installation and commissioning;
- qualification, calibration and validation;
- method transfer/verification;
- training and analyst authorization;
- material and service readiness;
- operational trial and go-live gate.

The asset is available for routine work only when its downstream dependencies are
complete.

## Identify long-lead and decision-lead items

Lead time includes more than manufacture and shipping. Capture:

- requirement and scope approval;
- bidder qualification and RFQ cycle;
- technical clarification and selection;
- commercial approval and contract;
- design submittals and site interfaces;
- manufacture and factory acceptance;
- freight, customs and storage;
- access route and installation;
- utility connection and commissioning;
- qualification and method readiness;
- supplier training and service activation.

Also identify **decision lead time**: how early the project must freeze a choice so
rooms, utilities and data interfaces do not proceed on unstable assumptions.

## Create an integrated schedule

Connect all activities with logic, owners, durations and constraints. Mark:

- critical and near-critical paths;
- externally constrained milestones;
- float and schedule reserve;
- design freezes and latest decision dates;
- site shutdown or access windows;
- regulatory, validation and QA gates;
- dependencies shared by several assets;
- uncertainty and risk-response actions.

The U.S. GAO's official [Schedule Assessment Guide](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-16-89g)
describes an integrated schedule as a way to test whether milestones are realistic and
to understand the effect of change. Atlas uses this as a general planning principle,
not as a pharmaceutical or project-specific scheduling standard.

## Package procurement for comparability and interfaces

Decide whether to package by equipment class, room/system, capability wave or another
controlled strategy. Evaluate:

- interface and responsibility clarity;
- competitive comparability;
- installation and qualification coordination;
- warranty and service ownership;
- software/data integration;
- spare parts and consumables;
- schedule and commercial risk;
- local support and lifecycle continuity;
- change exposure if one package is late.

One large package can simplify interfaces but reduce competition or create supplier
concentration. Many small packages can fragment responsibility.

## Separate enabling, launch and scale capacity

Classify procurement lines:

- **enabling:** space, utilities, data, safety or access needed before assets arrive;
- **launch:** minimum qualified resources for the approved first operating scope;
- **resilience:** backup, spares or alternate capacity required at go-live;
- **scale:** additional units released when measured demand triggers are reached;
- **option:** configuration or capacity retained for a defined future scenario.

Do not call a quality- or continuity-critical item “Phase 2” only to fit a budget.
Deferred scope needs an approved operating boundary and risk treatment.

## Define measurable expansion triggers

Possible triggers include:

- approved portfolio or batch volume;
- peak utilization over consecutive periods;
- queue time approaching release tolerance;
- reviewer or analyst coverage below threshold;
- new method, product or market approval;
- outsourced cost or turnaround crossing a reviewed boundary;
- loss of qualified backup or service support;
- recurring stockout or storage-capacity pressure;
- actual workload variance exceeding the concept basis.

Each trigger needs data source, calculation, monitoring cadence, owner, threshold,
decision lead time and action. Reserve space/utilities early when purchase after the
trigger would arrive too late.

## Model temporary and hybrid capacity

During transition consider:

- contract-laboratory testing;
- shared qualified equipment;
- rented or mobile systems where appropriate;
- prepared-media or specialist external services;
- cross-site capacity;
- manual processes pending system integration;
- restricted product/method scope.

Define qualification, data, sample, quality-agreement, logistics, change and exit
requirements. Temporary arrangements often persist longer than planned.

## Tie cost and cash flow to technical progress

Phase:

- design and specialist services;
- deposits and long-lead commitments;
- facility and utility work;
- freight, duties and storage;
- installation and commissioning;
- qualification and method transfer;
- initial inventory and spares;
- training and ramp-up;
- recurring service and software;
- contingency and escalation.

Use the [controlled QC laboratory cost basis](/blog/from-concept-cost-band-to-controlled-qc-lab-cost-basis)
to keep purchase price, installed cost, implementation and OPEX separate.

## Evidence package before phase approval

Collect:

1. approved capability waves and dates;
2. demand, capacity and resilience basis;
3. item-to-capability traceability;
4. URS/configuration and interface status;
5. dependency network and integrated schedule;
6. lead-time and latest-decision evidence;
7. package and supplier strategy;
8. installation, qualification and method-readiness plan;
9. temporary/outsourced operating model;
10. cost, cash-flow and commercial assumptions;
11. expansion triggers and reserved infrastructure;
12. risk, change and governance owners.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas should not label a procurement plan executable when:

- items are not linked to capability waves;
- purchase order date replaces operational-readiness date;
- room, utility, data and qualification dependencies are absent;
- long-lead claims lack dated supplier evidence;
- critical path and latest decision dates are unknown;
- deferred critical scope has no risk treatment;
- expansion quantities lack measurable triggers;
- temporary outsourcing has no control or exit plan;
- cash flow is disconnected from technical milestones;
- change and interface ownership is unresolved.
