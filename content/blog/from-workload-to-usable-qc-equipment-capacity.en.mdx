# From workload to usable QC equipment capacity: peaks, downtime and redundancy

Equipment capacity is often calculated with a deceptively simple equation:

```text
monthly demand / nominal monthly throughput = equipment quantity
```

That equation can be useful for an early screen. It is not a defensible final
sizing method.

Nominal capacity is not usable capacity. A QC resource loses availability to
setup, cleaning, warm-up, calibration, qualification, preventive maintenance,
breakdown, changeover, review holds, environmental constraints, incompatible
loads and the arrival pattern of samples.

The planning chain should be:

```text
Physical test executions
-> resource-bearing method steps
-> demand in the resource's natural unit
-> peak and concurrency profile
-> usable qualified capacity
-> utilization and queue risk
-> redundancy and continuity decision
-> phased procurement trigger
```

This framework applies across microbiology, analytical chemistry, stability,
water/EM and specialist quality laboratories. The natural unit and scheduling
logic differ by resource.

## Start with the resource's natural demand unit

Counting "tests" hides how equipment is occupied. Use a unit that represents the
constraint.

| Resource | Better demand unit |
| --- | --- |
| Incubator | Plate-days, container-days or occupied usable positions by condition |
| HPLC/GC | Instrument-hours or sequence-hours by qualified configuration |
| Spectrometer | Run-hours plus setup/changeover time |
| Autoclave | Qualified load cycles, usable load volume and cycle-hours |
| Biological safety cabinet | Occupied hands-on hours by work type |
| Filtration manifold | Filtration positions x occupied time, including setup/cleaning |
| Stability chamber | Qualified storage positions by condition, orientation and time |
| Sterility isolator | Campaign/cycle occupancy including decontamination and transfer windows |
| Plate reader or BET platform | Plate/rack runs, sample-control layout and run time |

The same method can create demand in several units. A microbial enumeration
execution creates analyst time, cabinet time, plate-days, media/diluent demand and
waste load. Capacity planning must preserve those parallel dependencies.

## Reconcile physical executions first

Do not calculate equipment load directly from regulatory requirement rows.
Several market requirements may be satisfied by one approved physical execution,
or method differences may require separate executions.

For each product-method-market trace, decide whether the work is:

- shared across markets;
- executed separately;
- pooled or individual;
- bracketed or matrixed under an approved design;
- in-house, outsourced or hybrid; or
- unresolved.

When allocation is unresolved, retain the requirement but hold back the dependent
equipment load. Guessing can multiply or suppress demand materially.

## Build demand from method steps

For each physical execution, map the resource-bearing steps:

- setup and preparation;
- sample, standard, blank and control count;
- run, incubation or cycle duration;
- load size and usable geometry;
- required condition or configuration;
- cleaning and changeover;
- reading, unloading or post-run handling;
- repeat/invalid allowance;
- investigation-related work; and
- review or release dependency.

Then calculate demand by method and resource.

For a time-occupied resource:

```text
monthly resource-hours
= physical executions
x occupied hours per execution
x repeat/change allowance
```

For a position-and-duration resource:

```text
monthly position-days
= executions
x positions per execution
x occupied days
x repeat/change allowance
```

These are model structures, not universal coefficients. Per-execution values must
come from approved methods, representative time studies or qualified planning
assumptions.

## Incubators: use condition-specific plate-days

An incubator should not be sized from total plate count alone. A plate held for
five days consumes more capacity than one held for two days. Different temperature
conditions are not automatically interchangeable.

Calculate plate-days separately by approved incubation band or program:

```text
plate-days = plates x occupied incubation days
```

Then address:

- usable shelf positions under the qualified loading pattern;
- container height and spacing;
- segregation or dedicated-use requirements;
- door-opening and reading practices;
- staged or sequential incubation;
- weekends and scheduled readings;
- growth-promotion and suitability studies;
- environmental-monitoring peaks;
- quarantine or investigation holds; and
- mapping, maintenance and breakdown windows.

A chamber with 1,000 nominal plate positions does not provide 30,000 qualified
plate-days every month. That multiplication assumes every position is usable,
every day is available and arrivals can be packed perfectly.

## Analytical instruments: model sequences and calendars

For chromatography, the test count is rarely the instrument load.

A sequence can include:

- equilibration and stabilization;
- blanks;
- calibration standards;
- system-suitability injections;
- samples and replicates;
- bracket standards or checks;
- wash and shutdown; and
- reinjection or rerun allowance.

Calculate sequence time from the actual injection architecture and method runtime.
Then place sequences onto an operating calendar with constraints such as analyst
availability, sample/standard stability, review gates, mobile-phase life,
instrument configuration and cleaning.

Two short methods may not share an instrument efficiently if columns,
temperatures, detectors or qualification ranges differ. Treat configuration
compatibility as part of usable capacity.

## Autoclaves: count qualified cycles and load families

Sterilizer capacity should be based on qualified load patterns, not chamber volume
alone.

Separate at least:

- media or clean preparation loads;
- reusable-item loads;
- decontamination/waste loads;
- cycle type and duration;
- heating, exposure, cooling and unload time;
- clean/dirty flow and segregation;
- maximum and minimum qualified loads;
- utilities and recovery between cycles; and
- biological/chemical indicator and release practices where applicable.

The bottleneck can be the longest qualified cycle, cooling area, operator window,
utility recovery or inability to mix load families - not the vessel's nominal
litres.

## Stability chambers: positions are commitments across time

Stability demand accumulates. A batch placed today can occupy qualified positions
for years while new batches continue to enter.

Model positions by:

- storage condition;
- protocol and market commitment;
- pack size and orientation;
- number of units, reserve and contingency;
- pull schedule;
- removal/destructive-testing behavior;
- chamber mapping restrictions;
- excursion quarantine or investigation; and
- backup transfer capacity.

A monthly average cannot show a future inventory peak. Use a time-indexed position
forecast with placements and pulls.

## Convert nominal capacity into usable capacity

A simple concept allowance is:

```text
usable capacity
= nominal qualified capacity
x scheduled availability
x operating efficiency
x allowed planning utilization
```

Keep the factors separate so reviewers can challenge them.

### Scheduled availability

Accounts for planned operating hours, shutdowns, maintenance, calibration and
qualification windows.

### Operating efficiency

Accounts for setup, changeover, cleaning, imperfect packing, minor delays and
other losses not represented in the nominal rate.

### Allowed planning utilization

Creates headroom for variability, investigations, urgent samples and future
uncertainty. It is a risk decision, not a universal percentage.

Do not hide all three effects in one unexplained "70% utilization" assumption.
The same numerical factor can represent very different risks.

## Average demand does not size a release-critical resource

Samples arrive in batches, campaigns, shifts and scheduled monitoring rounds.
Stability pulls cluster by protocol dates. EM demand may peak after interventions,
shutdowns or qualification work. Launch suitability studies can cluster before
routine release.

At concept stage, apply a transparent peak factor and show it separately from
monthly demand. Replace it with an arrival profile when real data are available.

Useful peak views include:

- maximum expected day or week;
- percentile demand by week;
- campaign or launch peak;
- simultaneous stability pulls;
- scheduled EM/water rounds;
- investigation/repeat stress case; and
- downtime-overlap scenario.

A peak factor is not a queue model. It is an explicit placeholder.

## Utilization and queue risk are not the same thing

Low average utilization can still produce missed turnaround when work arrives in
bursts or cannot be pre-empted. High utilization magnifies variability and makes
recovery from downtime harder.

For each critical resource, report:

- average utilization;
- peak-period utilization;
- longest occupied step;
- arrival and completion window;
- maximum tolerable queue;
- release or stability deadline;
- recovery time after downtime; and
- workload that can move to another qualified resource or partner.

If turnaround depends on exact sequencing, use a schedule or discrete-event
simulation with actual arrival patterns, method durations and concurrency rules.

## When a queue or schedule simulation is required

Move beyond a class-level load check when one or more conditions apply:

- planned utilization is close to the approved risk threshold;
- several methods share one constrained configuration;
- arrivals are highly batched or time-critical;
- long non-pre-emptive cycles dominate;
- sample or standard stability limits the start window;
- multiple temperature/storage conditions interact;
- maintenance or qualification outages are material;
- one resource failure threatens release;
- shifts and skill availability constrain operation; or
- future expansion depends on a narrow purchase trigger.

The simulation should state its assumptions, validation approach and sensitivity.
More complex software does not compensate for weak input data.

## Redundancy is a business-continuity decision

Adding one spare unit to every equipment class can overstate CAPEX. Running every
critical method on one unit can create unacceptable release risk.

Assess redundancy using:

- criticality of the supported quality decision;
- maximum tolerable downtime;
- repair and parts lead time;
- availability of qualified alternate equipment;
- method transfer or bridging effort;
- outsource/partner lead time;
- sample stability and storage;
- backlog recovery rate;
- utility/common-cause failure; and
- future demand growth.

Possible strategies include:

- installed redundant unit;
- shared qualified backup;
- reserved space/utilities with a purchase trigger;
- qualified contract-laboratory backup;
- cross-site capacity agreement;
- critical spares and service contract; or
- risk-accepted single unit with documented recovery plan.

Two units in the same room on the same utility can still share a common-cause
failure.

## Define procurement triggers, not only future quantities

A future scenario should specify when additional capacity is released.

Example trigger types:

- approved portfolio volume exceeds a threshold;
- peak utilization exceeds a reviewed limit for consecutive periods;
- queue time approaches release/stability tolerance;
- a new method/configuration is approved;
- backup capacity is lost or lead time increases;
- a new market adds separate physical executions; or
- actual workload variance shows the concept factor is no longer representative.

Reserve compatible space, utilities, data connections and qualification windows
before the trigger if late installation would threaten the project.

## Illustrative capacity check

Suppose a condition-specific incubator has:

- 600 qualified usable positions;
- 30 calendar days in the planning month;
- 95% scheduled availability;
- 90% operating efficiency; and
- a 70% allowed planning-utilization limit.

Illustrative usable capacity is:

```text
600 x 30 x 0.95 x 0.90 x 0.70 = 10,773 plate-days/month
```

If method-derived demand is 8,000 plate-days and the peak allowance is 1.3x,
peak-adjusted load is 10,400 plate-days. The arithmetic fits narrowly, but the
decision remains sensitive to arrival timing, qualified geometry, reading
windows and downtime. This is a trigger for schedule review, not proof that one
incubator is sufficient.

These numbers are illustrative only and are not Atlas benchmarks.

## Capacity evidence package

Before freezing equipment quantity, collect:

- approved method steps and durations;
- physical execution allocation;
- actual sample-arrival history and forecast;
- qualified usable load geometry;
- cycle/sequence configuration;
- operating calendar and shifts;
- maintenance, calibration and qualification history;
- breakdown and service lead-time history;
- repeat, invalid and investigation workload;
- turnaround and maximum queue tolerance;
- alternate-resource qualification status;
- utility and room constraints;
- vendor data and budget quotation; and
- owner-approved redundancy strategy.

The evidence belongs in the project review record. A vendor brochure is not a
usable-capacity model.

## Capacity review checklist

- Is demand based on physical executions rather than regulatory rows?
- Is each resource measured in its natural occupancy unit?
- Are configurations and conditions separated?
- Are method controls, setup, cleaning and changeover included?
- Is nominal capacity converted to qualified usable capacity?
- Are availability, efficiency and utilization allowances separate?
- Are average and peak demand both visible?
- Are launch, stability, EM and investigation peaks represented?
- Is queue simulation triggered where timing matters?
- Is redundancy tied to downtime and continuity risk?
- Are common-cause failures considered?
- Are future quantities tied to measurable procurement triggers?
- Can actual utilization and downtime be captured for calibration?

If several answers are open, the equipment list is a concept scenario, not a
design-freeze or purchase basis.
