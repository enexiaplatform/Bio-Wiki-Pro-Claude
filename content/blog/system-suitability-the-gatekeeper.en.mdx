# System suitability: the gatekeeper you can't re-inject past

System suitability (SST) is the gatekeeper of chromatographic data. It confirms,
on the day and on that column, that the whole system — instrument, column, mobile
phase, and standard — can actually deliver the separation your method was
validated for. That is why it runs **before** samples, and why an SST failure
invalidates the results that depend on it.

## What SST actually checks

Run from a current reference standard, against the method's defined limits:

- **Resolution** of the critical pair — are the peaks that must be separated,
  separated?
- **%RSD** of replicate injections — is the system reproducible?
- **Tailing factor** and **theoretical plates** — is peak shape and efficiency in
  range?
- **Signal-to-noise**, where relevant for limit tests.

Pass all of them and your sample results mean something. Fail one and they do not.

## The integrity trap

The most common data-integrity finding here is simple: **re-injecting until SST
passes**. Without an assignable, documented cause, that is testing into
compliance — and the audit trail shows every injection, including the ones you
discarded.

If replicate %RSD is high, investigate the cause — injector, air bubbles, column
equilibration — and document it. Don't just keep injecting until the number looks
right.

## Why bracketing matters

Columns drift over a long run. A clean SST at injection one tells you nothing
about injection eighty. Bracketing standards (or periodic check standards) confirm
the system held suitability across the whole sequence — so if a bracket fails, you
know which results to question.

## Common mistakes

- Accepting sample results when the SST failed
- Re-injecting to pass without an assignable cause
- Using an expired reference standard or an unqualified column
- Reviewing only the result table, not the chromatograms and integration
