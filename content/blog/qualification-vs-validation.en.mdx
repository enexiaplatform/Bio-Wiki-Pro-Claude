# Qualification vs Validation: What's the Difference?

"Qualification" and "validation" get used interchangeably in everyday lab talk —
but in GMP they mean different (related) things. Getting them straight makes
protocols, audits, and CSV conversations much clearer.

## The short answer

- **Qualification** applies to **equipment, utilities, and systems** — proving a
  specific *thing* is installed correctly and operates and performs as intended.
- **Validation** applies to **processes, methods, and computerized systems** —
  proving an *activity* consistently produces a result meeting predetermined
  criteria.

A useful rule of thumb: you **qualify equipment**, you **validate processes**.
Qualification is one of the building blocks that supports process validation.

## The qualification stages (DQ/IQ/OQ/PQ)

- **DQ — Design Qualification:** the design is fit for intended use.
- **IQ — Installation Qualification:** it's installed correctly per specs.
- **OQ — Operational Qualification:** it operates across the intended ranges.
- **PQ — Performance Qualification:** it performs consistently under real
  conditions.

## Where validation sits

Process validation (now framed as a **lifecycle** — design, qualification,
continued verification) relies on qualified equipment and validated methods.
You can't validate a sterilization process on an autoclave that was never
qualified — the foundation has to be sound first.

## Why the distinction matters

Auditors expect the right word in the right place. Calling an autoclave study a
"validation" or a cleaning process a "qualification" signals a shaky grasp of the
quality system — and invites deeper questions.

> Bottom line: qualification proves the *equipment* is ready; validation proves
> the *process* using it is reliable. Qualification is part of validation, not a
> synonym for it.
