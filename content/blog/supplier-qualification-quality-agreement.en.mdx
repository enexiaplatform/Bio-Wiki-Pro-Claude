# Supplier qualification: the clause that saves you

Supplier qualification is risk management applied to your supply chain. The depth
of what you do — a questionnaire, certificates, an on-site audit, ongoing
monitoring — should scale to how much a material can hurt the product. An API or a
primary container demands far more scrutiny than a non-critical excipient.

But if you only fix one thing in your supplier program, make it the **quality
agreement**, and specifically its change-notification clause.

## Why the change-notification clause matters most

Most supplier-driven quality surprises are not bad lots — they are **unannounced
changes**: a supplier quietly switches a sub-supplier, a process step, or a site,
and the first you hear of it is a deviation. A quality agreement that obliges the
supplier to notify you *before* a change gives you the chance to assess impact and
re-qualify before the change reaches your product.

Without that clause, qualification is a snapshot that silently goes stale.

## A risk-based program in five moves

1. **Rank by criticality.** Set the qualification depth from the material's risk —
   API and primary packaging at the top, non-critical excipients lower.
2. **Evaluate the supplier.** Questionnaire, certifications, history, and an audit
   (on-site or remote) scaled to that risk.
3. **Put a quality agreement in place.** Responsibilities, specifications, audit
   rights, and the change-notification clause — signed before routine supply.
4. **Verify incoming material.** Full testing initially; reduce only with
   justification. Don't skip verification on "trusted" suppliers.
5. **Monitor and re-qualify.** Trend deviations, complaints, and OOS; re-audit on a
   risk-based interval, not "never again after approval."

## When it goes wrong

- **A supplier changed a process without telling you** — treat it as a breach of
  the agreement: assess impact, re-qualify the affected scope, and tighten the
  notification clause.
- **Recurring incoming OOS from one supplier** — escalate via their corrective
  action process, raise incoming testing, and reassess approved status.
- **A single-source critical supplier** — document the risk, strengthen monitoring,
  and progress a second source where feasible.
