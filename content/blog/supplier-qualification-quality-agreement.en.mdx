# Supplier qualification: the clause that saves you

Supplier qualification is not procurement paperwork. It is quality risk
management applied to the supply chain.

Every incoming material, component, outsourced test, sterilization service,
warehouse, distributor, contract manufacturer, or critical software provider can
affect product quality. A strong supplier program decides how much assurance is
needed before relying on that supplier, then keeps checking whether the
assurance remains true.

The depth should scale with risk. An API supplier, primary-packaging supplier,
sterilization contractor, or single-source critical material demands much more
evidence than a low-risk indirect consumable. But the principle is the same:
approve the supplier for a defined scope, verify performance, and reassess when
the risk changes.

And if you only fix one clause in the whole supplier system, fix the **change
notification clause** in the quality agreement.

## Why supplier qualification belongs in the PQS

ICH Q10 places outsourced activities and purchased materials inside the
pharmaceutical quality system. The regulated company remains responsible for
having processes that control suppliers and purchased materials. Delegating an
activity does not delegate accountability.

That means supplier qualification should connect to:

- quality risk management;
- material and service specifications;
- approved supplier lists;
- incoming material sampling and testing;
- supplier quality agreements;
- audits and questionnaires;
- supplier deviations and CAPA;
- change control;
- complaints and recalls;
- APR/PQR and management review.

When supplier qualification is isolated in purchasing, it becomes a file cabinet.
When it is part of the PQS, it becomes a living control system.

## Start with the material and service risk

Do not qualify every supplier with the same checklist. Start by asking what could
go wrong if this supplier fails.

Risk factors include:

- **Material criticality** - API, excipient, primary packaging, sterile component,
  processing aid, lab reagent, indirect material, or service.
- **Product impact** - identity, strength, purity, sterility, endotoxin,
  extractables/leachables, stability, delivery system, or patient exposure.
- **Supplier history** - new supplier, previous findings, complaint trend,
  recurring CoA issues, late notification, or strong performance history.
- **Substitutability** - many qualified sources, few sources, or sole source.
- **Regulatory status** - inspected site, registered site, GMP certification,
  unknown status, or history of enforcement action.
- **Supply-chain complexity** - brokers, distributors, repackers, relabelers,
  sub-tier suppliers, multiple manufacturing sites, or country-of-origin changes.
- **Testing strategy** - full incoming testing, reduced testing, reliance on CoA,
  skip-lot testing, or identity-only confirmation.

The output should be a risk tier that drives qualification depth. High-risk
suppliers need stronger evidence, tighter agreements, and more frequent review.
Low-risk suppliers can be managed more simply, but not invisibly.

## A lifecycle in six moves

A practical supplier program has six connected moves.

## 1. Define the qualified scope

Supplier approval should never be vague. "Supplier approved" is not enough.
Approved for what?

The file should define:

- exact material, component, service, or activity;
- grade, specification, compendial status, and intended use;
- manufacturing site, testing site, warehouse, or service location;
- distributor or broker role, if any;
- approved route of supply;
- whether sub-tier suppliers are involved;
- whether the supplier CoA can be used and for which tests;
- any restrictions, additional testing, or temporary controls.

This prevents one of the most common failures: using an approved supplier for an
unapproved grade, site, process, or material variant.

## 2. Evaluate the supplier

Supplier evaluation should be scaled to risk. Evidence may include:

- supplier questionnaire;
- GMP certificates, licenses, or registrations;
- quality-system overview;
- material specification and CoA template;
- site history and inspection status;
- samples and technical evaluation;
- stability, impurity, microbiology, or packaging compatibility information;
- quality agreement draft;
- remote audit or on-site audit;
- previous performance data.

For a low-risk indirect material, documentation review may be sufficient. For a
critical API, sterile component, or contract sterilization process, an on-site or
deep remote audit may be expected, supported by technical review and a robust
quality agreement.

## 3. Put the quality agreement in place

The quality agreement is where supplier qualification becomes operational. It
should define who does what, how information flows, and when the customer must be
notified.

Useful clauses include:

- scope of materials, services, sites, and specifications;
- batch documentation and CoA expectations;
- responsibilities for testing, release, storage, transport, and rejected
  material;
- deviation, OOS, complaint, recall, and field-action communication;
- audit rights and audit frequency;
- record retention and data access;
- sub-tier supplier control;
- change notification and approval expectations;
- regulatory inspection notification where relevant;
- business continuity and supply interruption communication;
- contact roles and escalation timelines.

The most valuable clause is change notification. A supplier who "improves" a
process, changes a site, switches a sub-supplier, changes sterilization dose,
changes resin, changes water grade, changes container closure components, or
changes test method without telling you can create a quality event before the
first shipment arrives.

The agreement should say which changes require prior notification, which require
approval before implementation, what data must be provided, and how much notice
is expected. A vague sentence like "supplier will notify customer of significant
changes" is weaker than it looks unless "significant" is defined.

## 4. Verify incoming material

Approval does not mean blind trust. Incoming material should be sampled, tested,
or examined according to the applicable regulation, risk, and approved procedure.

In the US, 21 CFR 211.84 requires lots of components, drug product containers,
and closures to be withheld from use until sampled, tested, or examined as
appropriate and released by the quality control unit. Supplier certificates can
support acceptance only within a controlled program.

For components other than certain containers and closures, identity testing is
especially important. Reduced testing or reliance on supplier results should be
based on established supplier reliability, periodic verification, and documented
risk rationale.

Practical checks include:

- supplier approved for the exact material/site/scope;
- purchase from the approved route;
- CoA matches specification and material lot;
- required identity or visual checks performed;
- supplier test results periodically verified;
- discrepancies trended and investigated;
- material held in quarantine until released by Quality.

A supplier CoA is evidence. It is not a release decision by itself.

## 5. Monitor supplier performance

Supplier qualification is a lifecycle. Once approved, the supplier should be
monitored using signals that already exist in the quality system.

Useful indicators include:

- incoming inspection defects;
- CoA errors, omissions, or late documents;
- confirmatory testing failures;
- OOS/OOT or deviation events linked to the supplier;
- complaint or recall signals linked to a component or material;
- late or incomplete change notifications;
- audit findings and CAPA effectiveness;
- delivery reliability for critical materials;
- recurring packaging, labeling, transport, or temperature-excursion problems.

Performance review should feed the approved supplier list. A supplier with
recurring failures should not remain "approved" only because the annual review is
not due yet.

## 6. Requalify when risk changes

Calendar-based requalification is useful, but risk-based triggers matter more.

Requalification or for-cause review may be needed after:

- a major supplier process, site, equipment, method, or material-source change;
- recurring incoming failures;
- a significant audit finding;
- a complaint, recall, or field issue linked to supplier material;
- regulatory inspection concerns;
- merger, acquisition, ownership change, or site closure;
- moving from full testing to reduced testing;
- adding a new grade, site, route, or intended use;
- a long supply interruption or reactivation of a dormant supplier.

The question is not "was this supplier approved once?" The question is "does the
current evidence still justify the current approved status?"

## When the change-notification clause saves you

Most supplier-driven surprises are not dramatic fraud. They are ordinary changes
that were not assessed by the customer:

- alternate raw material source;
- new sub-tier supplier;
- manufacturing site transfer;
- process parameter change;
- sterilization cycle change;
- cleaning-agent change;
- packaging component resin change;
- analytical method change;
- CoA test removed or outsourced;
- distributor or storage route changed.

Each change might be reasonable for the supplier. Each can still affect your
validated state, regulatory filing, material specification, stability, sterility,
extractables/leachables, impurity profile, bioburden, or batch-release decision.

That is why change notification is not legal decoration. It is the trigger that
lets QA, QC, regulatory, validation, and technical teams assess impact before the
change reaches product.

## What to do when suppliers fail

Supplier issues should be managed like quality events, not vendor annoyance.

If a supplier changes a process without notification:

1. Contain affected lots.
2. Determine which products and batches used the material.
3. Assess quality, validation, regulatory, and stability impact.
4. Decide whether incoming testing, quarantine, or batch hold is needed.
5. Open supplier deviation/CAPA as appropriate.
6. Reassess approved status and quality agreement terms.
7. Consider for-cause audit or requalification.

If incoming OOS or defects recur:

1. Trend by material, supplier site, lot, test, and time.
2. Check whether the issue is isolated or systemic.
3. Request supplier investigation and corrective action.
4. Increase incoming verification if justified.
5. Escalate to audit, restricted approval, probation, or disqualification.
6. Feed the signal into APR/PQR, management review, and sourcing strategy.

If a critical supplier is sole source:

1. Document the business and patient-supply risk.
2. Strengthen monitoring and quality agreement obligations.
3. Review safety stock and business continuity plans.
4. Define contingency testing or additional controls.
5. Progress second-source qualification where feasible.

## What good looks like

A mature supplier program has visible control:

- an approved supplier list with scope, site, material, and risk tier;
- documented rationale for qualification depth;
- quality agreements for critical suppliers and outsourced GMP activities;
- change-notification clauses that are specific enough to act on;
- incoming verification tied to risk and supplier reliability;
- periodic supplier performance review;
- clear triggers for requalification, for-cause audit, probation, or removal;
- supplier CAPA tracked to effectiveness;
- supplier signals included in APR/PQR and management review.

The program should make supplier status easy to defend. If an inspector asks why
you trust a supplier, the answer should be more than "we have always used them."
