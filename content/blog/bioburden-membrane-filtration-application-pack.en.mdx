# Bioburden and membrane filtration application pack

“Bioburden” can describe a finished-product microbial limit, an in-process control,
a pre-sterilization load, a cleaning or process-development signal, or another
defined enumeration decision. These applications do not automatically share a
sample preparation, recovery method, result unit, limit or consequence.

The application must connect:

```text
material, process stage and decision
→ representative sample and hold
→ preparation, neutralization and recovery
→ filtration or plating architecture
→ incubation, counting and calculation
→ specification, trend or downstream process decision
```

## Application boundary

This pack structures evidence and resource planning. It does not assign microbial
limits, claim membrane filtration is suitable for every matrix, establish a
sterilization-cycle input, determine objectionability or approve a release result.

USP's official microbiology resources distinguish microbial enumeration standards,
non-sterile bioburden control and validation of microbial recovery as connected but
separate controls. See [USP Microbiological Quality Control
Testing](https://www.usp.org/microbiology). The harmonized USP &lt;61&gt; notice also
highlights interpretation when verifying membrane-filtration suitability. See
[USP Microbial Enumeration Tests](https://www.usp.org/harmonization-standards/pdg/general-methods/microbial-enumeration).

## Define the intended decision

| Application | Primary question | Decision consequence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Non-sterile product enumeration | Does the tested article meet its approved microbial quality criteria? | Batch/material disposition and investigation |
| In-process monitoring | Is the process stage remaining within its control strategy? | Hold, adjustment, escalation or trend response |
| Pre-sterilization bioburden | Is the load entering a validated microbial-reduction process controlled? | Process acceptance, cycle governance and investigation |
| Raw material or component | Is incoming microbial load suitable for the defined use? | Supplier/material disposition and process risk |
| Cleaning/process study | What recoverable load remains under the study condition? | Development, qualification or control-strategy evidence |
| Investigation sample | Does the sample support a contamination hypothesis? | Root-cause and product/process impact assessment |

Create a distinct application record when the downstream decision, sampling basis or
recovery architecture changes.

## Build the product–preparation–recovery matrix

Capture:

- product/material identity, formulation and process stage;
- batch, sample location and sampling plan;
- expected load and relevant organism types;
- sample quantity, container, transport and hold condition;
- soluble, dispersible, oily, viscous, particulate or antimicrobial behavior;
- preparation fluid, dilution and mixing/homogenization;
- neutralizer, surfactant, enzyme or rinse where applicable;
- filtration, direct plating, pour/spread or MPN branch;
- membrane material, pore size, funnel and volume per membrane;
- medium, incubation and reading condition;
- calculation, reporting unit and decision limit;
- method-suitability status and evidence owner.

Product family grouping requires evidence that the selected representative matrices
cover the recovery risks. Similar dosage form names alone are not enough.

## Control sampling and hold time

Define the sampled population, sampling location, composite/grab logic, aseptic
handling, minimum quantity, container, transport, time zero and maximum test-start
time. Consider whether organisms may grow, die, attach, clump or become less
recoverable during the hold.

For process samples, align collection time with the actual process state. A sample
taken after an uncontrolled delay may not represent the load used by the downstream
decision.

## Design sample preparation around recovery risk

Document:

- original quantity and preparation ratio;
- diluent identity and volume;
- dissolving, shaking, stomaching, blending or sonication condition;
- temperature and maximum preparation time;
- foam, precipitation, adsorption or phase-separation risk;
- neutralizer identity, concentration and exposure;
- evidence that the neutralizer is effective and non-toxic;
- serial dilution and transfer volumes;
- recovery of low numbers through the complete preparation.

Preparation is part of the method. A clear filtrate is not proof that microorganisms
were released from the sample or remained viable.

## Decide whether membrane filtration is fit for the application

Membrane filtration may increase examined volume or separate organisms from an
inhibitory matrix, but it can fail through clogging, adsorption, residual inhibition,
excessive rinsing or physical damage.

Define:

- maximum filterable product quantity or dilution;
- membrane chemistry and compatibility;
- number of membranes and product distribution;
- filtration pressure/vacuum and time;
- rinse fluid, volume, sequence and number of rinses;
- transfer technique and membrane orientation;
- process control and negative control;
- recovery evidence after the entire filtration/rinse sequence;
- fallback method when the matrix cannot be filtered reproducibly.

Do not silently reduce the examined sample quantity to make filtration easier. Any
change must remain consistent with the decision's detection and reporting needs.

## Define alternative enumeration branches

Direct plating, pour plate, spread plate and MPN can create different detection,
precision, matrix and workload characteristics. For each approved branch record:

- sample and plated quantity;
- number of replicate plates/tubes;
- dilutions and selection rule;
- media and incubation conditions;
- countable range and reading rule;
- branch-selection criteria;
- comparability or historical continuity;
- calculation and reporting convention.

Atlas should preserve the selected physical architecture instead of converting every
result into an apparently equivalent CFU value.

## Demonstrate method suitability and recovery

The suitability package should address:

- representative products, strengths and worst-case attributes;
- relevant challenge organisms and controlled inoculum;
- recovery through preparation, neutralization, filtration and incubation;
- neutralizer effectiveness and toxicity;
- membrane and rinse effects;
- low-count variability and precision;
- interference, masking and colony morphology;
- analyst, day, media lot and equipment variability as justified;
- approved acceptance criteria and invalidation rules;
- grouping/bracketing rationale and change triggers.

Atlas records evidence status and applicability. It must not invent a recovery
criterion or extend suitability from one matrix to another without controlled basis.

## Build controls into the method and BOM

Include:

- negative/process controls;
- growth-promotion and media QC demand;
- inoculated product/recovery controls during suitability;
- diluent, rinse and neutralizer controls;
- filtration apparatus cleaning or single-use components;
- membrane integrity/handling checks where applicable;
- positive or reference recovery comparisons;
- environmental/aseptic handling controls defined by the procedure;
- repeat, investigation and training allowances.

Controls consume analyst time, filtration positions, media, incubator space and
review capacity. They are not an unexplained waste percentage.

## Preserve calculation traceability

Record:

- original sample amount;
- preparation and dilution factors;
- quantity filtered or plated per unit;
- number of membranes, plates or tubes;
- colony counts used and excluded;
- countable-range and averaging rule;
- zero, below-reportable, TNTC/confluent and atypical-colony handling;
- rounding and significant figures;
- final unit and electronic calculation configuration;
- specification interpretation and second-person review.

The reported result should allow a reviewer to reconstruct how much original sample
was actually examined.

## Connect the result to its downstream decision

Define whether the result drives specification conformance, a process alert/action,
a sterilization input, a supplier decision, a trend or an investigation hypothesis.
The application should state:

- approved limit or control rule and source;
- release/hold status while testing is in progress;
- organism identification trigger;
- related batches, stages or locations reviewed;
- retest/resample restrictions;
- escalation and product/process impact owner;
- downstream process assumptions that depend on the result;
- return-to-routine or disposition authority.

A low count does not by itself establish low risk; organism identity, product use,
process stage and exposure can change the decision.

## Convert the application into resource demand

Calculate per application and scenario:

- sampling/receipt and preparation hands-on time;
- dilution, filtration and plating time;
- manifold positions, pump and controlled-workspace occupancy;
- membranes, funnels, plates, tubes, diluent, rinse and neutralizer quantities;
- media preparation and QC demand;
- incubator positions and plate-days by condition;
- scheduled reading, calculation and review windows;
- colony isolation and identification demand;
- decontamination and waste flow;
- suitability, training, invalid test, repeat and investigation load;
- peak process-window and weekend/holiday coverage.

Model samples by matrix and branch. “One bioburden test” is not a reliable unit of
capacity when one sample filters directly and another requires multiple dilutions,
rinses, membranes and identification follow-up.

## Lifecycle and change control

Reassess the application when product composition, preservative, strength, process
stage, sample quantity, hold time, preparation, neutralizer, membrane, rinse,
medium, incubation, calculation, limit, supplier or test location changes.

Trace each change to method suitability, grouping/bracketing, trend continuity,
BOM, capacity, training, electronic configuration and downstream process decisions.

## Evidence required for an executable Atlas node

1. Intended-use and downstream-decision definition.
2. Product–preparation–recovery matrix.
3. Representative sampling and qualified hold condition.
4. Approved preparation and enumeration branch.
5. Filtration compatibility and complete-method recovery evidence.
6. Controls, calculations and result-decision configuration.
7. Grouping/bracketing and change-control rationale.
8. Observed BOM, equipment, incubation and analyst demand.
9. Invalid, failure, retest and investigation pathway.
10. Estimate-to-actual calibration across representative matrices.
