# How to plan analytical chemistry QC capability from methods, sequences and decisions

Analytical chemistry laboratories are often sized by instrument count: a number
of HPLCs, dissolution baths, spectrophotometers and balances is copied from an
existing site or assembled from vendor proposals. The method workload arrives
later.

That reverses the decision chain. Instrument demand is a consequence of product
specifications, sample preparation, sequence design, method performance,
turnaround and review—not a starting assumption.

The useful planning question is:

> Which quality decisions must be supported, which analytical procedures create
> the evidence, and what operating capability is required to execute and review
> those procedures reliably through their lifecycle?

This guide covers capability planning. It does not establish specifications,
validate procedures, approve instrument platforms or replace site-controlled
methods and qualified review.

## Map decisions before technologies

Start with the decisions supported by analytical data:

- release or rejection of materials, intermediates and finished products;
- in-process adjustment or continuation;
- confirmation of identity, strength, purity or performance;
- stability and shelf-life assessment;
- OOS/OOT and deviation impact;
- cleaning or carryover evaluation;
- method transfer and comparability;
- supplier, process, scale or site change; and
- regulatory commitment maintenance.

For each decision, define the accountable owner, target decision time, required
evidence, escalation path and consequence of delay. A release assay and a
development characterization test may use the same HPLC but require different
priority, redundancy and governance.

## Build the product-to-method inventory

One row per “HPLC method” is not enough. Preserve:

- product/material and market;
- attribute and specification role;
- procedure identifier, version and lifecycle status;
- sample type, quantity and preparation;
- test frequency and physical execution allocation;
- standards, controls and system suitability;
- instrument platform and detector;
- sequence structure and expected runtime;
- data processing and review;
- in-house/outsource decision; and
- evidence, assumptions and unresolved inputs.

ICH Q14 frames analytical procedure development around intended purpose,
measurement requirements, technology selection, risk and procedure understanding.
ICH Q2(R2) addresses demonstrating that a procedure is suitable for its intended
purpose. A capability model should preserve that intended-purpose context rather
than treating procedures as interchangeable because they use the same instrument.

## Compile the full method architecture

Each execution creates more work than the reported sample result. Model:

```text
sample receipt
→ preparation/extraction/dilution
→ standards, blanks and controls
→ instrument setup and system suitability
→ sample sequence
→ integration/calculation
→ technical review and approval
→ lifecycle trending and exception handling
```

Capture the operational drivers:

- preparations and replicates per sample;
- standards, blanks, bracketing and controls;
- injections or measurements per preparation;
- runtime, equilibration, wash and shutdown time;
- column, cell, lamp or accessory configuration;
- solvent, reagent and consumable demand;
- analyst hands-on hours;
- instrument occupancy;
- processing and review time;
- invalid, repeat and investigation assumptions; and
- sample/solution stability and hold-time constraints.

Two methods producing the same number of results can consume radically different
capacity.

## Use sequences as the capacity unit

For chromatography and other batch-oriented technologies, instrument capacity is
best modelled through sequences or runs.

```text
sequence hours
= setup + equilibration + injections × runtime + washes + shutdown
```

Then apply:

- feasible batch size and sample arrival pattern;
- instrument/method compatibility;
- column and detector availability;
- maintenance, calibration and qualification downtime;
- expected invalid/repeat load;
- data-system availability;
- campaign and peak-week demand; and
- priority rules across release, stability and investigations.

Do not assume every HPLC is interchangeable. Detector, pressure range, solvent
compatibility, column temperature, autosampler control, validated configuration,
software and method transfer status can create separate capacity pools.

## Separate analyst time, instrument time and review time

Analytical capacity usually has at least three independent constraints:

| Constraint | Typical drivers |
| --- | --- |
| Analyst | Weighing, extraction, dilution, reagent preparation, setup, troubleshooting |
| Instrument | Equilibration, sequence runtime, washes, maintenance, qualification |
| Review | Integration assessment, calculations, audit trail, exceptions, second-person approval |

A night sequence can free analyst time but still create a morning review queue.
An instrument can be available while the qualified analyst or reference standard
is not. Report the binding constraint for each scenario.

## Model non-chromatographic capability explicitly

Analytical chemistry is broader than HPLC. Depending on the portfolio, include:

- dissolution/disintegration and physical performance;
- UV/Vis, IR, Raman or other spectroscopic methods;
- water determination;
- pH, osmolality, viscosity, density and appearance;
- elemental impurities or trace analysis;
- gas chromatography and residual solvents;
- particle size and solid-state characterization;
- titration and wet chemistry;
- reference-standard characterization; and
- specialist outsourced methods.

Each has a different architecture. Dissolution capacity depends on apparatus,
media preparation, vessel/bath turnaround, sampling and downstream analysis.
Karl Fischer work depends on sample compatibility, reagent control and instrument
readiness. A generic “chemistry test” factor hides these differences.

## Treat reference standards and reagents as capacity dependencies

A qualified instrument cannot run without suitable standards, columns, solvents,
reagents and consumables. Track:

- compendial, primary, secondary and working-standard roles;
- qualification, potency correction and expiry;
- storage and dispensing conditions;
- lead time and protected inventory;
- reagent/solvent grade and preparation;
- column/accessory lifecycle;
- hazardous-material handling and waste; and
- alternate supply or business-continuity strategy.

Long-lead or single-source materials should appear in the resilience model, not
only the purchasing list.

## Include data integrity and review architecture

Analytical output is not complete when the instrument finishes. The operating
model must include:

- controlled acquisition and processing methods;
- user roles and access;
- audit-trail and metadata review;
- integration and reintegration governance;
- calculation verification;
- exception and invalid-run handling;
- result approval and transfer to downstream systems;
- backup, retention and recovery; and
- periodic review of method/instrument performance.

Review capacity should be forecast independently, especially for peak release,
stability pulls and investigations.

## Separate workload classes

| Work class | Demand behavior |
| --- | --- |
| Raw material and release | Batch/lot forecast with target turnaround |
| In-process | Production-schedule driven, often time-critical |
| Stability | Protocol calendar with clustered timepoints |
| Investigation | Event-driven and urgent |
| Method lifecycle | Transfer, validation, verification, changes and monitoring |
| Cleaning/support | Campaign, equipment or change dependent |

If these are combined into one monthly sample total, release capacity will be
overstated because project and exception work consumes the same analysts and
platforms.

## Plan method lifecycle capacity

Methods require work beyond routine execution:

- development or adaptation;
- validation/verification;
- method transfer;
- analyst qualification;
- reference-standard or reagent-lot qualification;
- changes and comparability;
- ongoing performance monitoring; and
- remediation after recurring invalid or atypical behavior.

Reserve specialist time and instrument access for this lifecycle work. Otherwise,
routine testing crowds out the very work needed to keep the methods reliable.

## Test resilience scenarios

- primary chromatography platform unavailable;
- CDS or network outage;
- critical standard, column or solvent unavailable;
- sole qualified analyst/reviewer absent;
- repeated system-suitability or invalid-sequence failure;
- stability timepoint coincides with release peak;
- external specialist laboratory misses turnaround; or
- change requires urgent transfer or verification.

For each, define time to impact, alternate qualified path, transfer constraints
and residual risk. Adding instruments alone does not resolve single-standard,
single-method-expert or single-data-system risk.

## Capability outputs

An Analytical Chemistry Domain Pack can support:

- product/attribute/method trace;
- sample preparation and method BOM;
- sequence and instrument occupancy forecast;
- analyst and reviewer capacity;
- standards, reagents, columns and consumables forecast;
- release/stability/investigation priority scenarios;
- method lifecycle plan;
- bottleneck and resilience analysis;
- in-house/outsource recommendation;
- functional space, utility and safety assumptions;
- vendor-neutral URS basis; and
- CAPEX/OPEX ranges with evidence and confidence.

## Discovery checklist

1. Product/material list, markets, batches/lots and growth forecast.
2. Specifications and procedure inventory with lifecycle status.
3. Preparations, replicates, controls, injections and runtimes.
4. Historical sample arrivals, invalid runs, repeats and OOS/OOT volume.
5. Instrument configurations, utilization, qualification and downtime.
6. Analyst/reviewer skills and shift coverage.
7. Standards, reagents, columns and critical supply risks.
8. Stability calendar and method-development/transfer pipeline.
9. Outsourced methods, lead times and quality agreements.
10. Data-system, review and retention workflow.

## Key takeaway

Analytical chemistry capacity is not “number of instruments × available hours.”
The defensible chain is:

**quality decision → attribute → procedure → preparation/sequence → analyst,
instrument and review constraints → controlled result.**

Atlas should calculate only where the method architecture is known and should
keep site approval, validation status and unresolved inputs visible.
