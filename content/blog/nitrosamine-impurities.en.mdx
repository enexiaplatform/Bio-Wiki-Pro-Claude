# Nitrosamine Impurities: what QC teams need to know

Since 2018, nitrosamine contamination has driven recalls of sartans, metformin,
ranitidine, and more. Because many nitrosamines are probable human carcinogens,
acceptable intake limits sit in the nanogram-per-day range — a serious
analytical and control challenge.

## Where nitrosamines come from

- **Secondary/tertiary amines + nitrosating agents** during synthesis.
- **Contaminated raw materials** and recovered solvents.
- **Cross-contamination** from shared equipment or supplier processes.
- **Degradation** during storage, especially with certain excipients.

## The regulatory three-step approach

1. **Risk assessment** — evaluate every product and process for nitrosamine
   potential.
2. **Confirmatory testing** — where risk is identified, test with validated,
   highly sensitive methods.
3. **Risk mitigation** — change the process, supplier, or controls; update the
   marketing authorization as needed.

## The analytical challenge

Detecting impurities at trace acceptable-intake levels typically demands
LC-MS/MS or GC-MS/MS with carefully controlled sample preparation to avoid
*in-situ* nitrosamine formation during analysis itself — a real and documented
artifact.

> For QC, the takeaway is method sensitivity and a defensible risk assessment.
> Regulators expect a documented rationale for every product, not just the ones
> that made the news.
