# Nitrosamine Impurities: What QC Teams Need to Know

Nitrosamine impurities became a global quality issue in 2018 after NDMA and
related impurities were detected in some sartan medicines. Since then, regulators
have expanded the concern beyond a few named products to a broader question:

> Could this product, process, material, packaging system, or storage condition
> generate a nitrosamine impurity at a clinically meaningful level?

For QC teams, the hard part is that many nitrosamines have acceptable intake
(AI) limits in the nanogram-per-day range. That pushes the work into trace
analysis, careful sample preparation, and a risk assessment that must be
scientifically defensible.

## What nitrosamines are

Nitrosamines are compounds containing an N-nitroso group. Many are considered
potentially carcinogenic because some members of the class have shown strong
carcinogenic activity in animal studies.

In pharmaceutical control, the important split is:

- **Small-molecule nitrosamines**, such as NDMA or NDEA, which may arise from
  common reagents, solvents, or nitrosating conditions.
- **Nitrosamine drug substance-related impurities (NDSRIs)**, which are
  structurally related to the API and can form when a vulnerable amine in or
  related to the drug substance is nitrosated.

NDSRIs are especially challenging because compound-specific toxicology data are
often missing. Regulators therefore use frameworks such as carcinogenic potency
categorization, read-across, enhanced Ames testing, interim limits, or
compound-specific data when available.

## Where nitrosamines come from

The basic chemistry is usually a vulnerable amine plus a nitrosating source, but
the real manufacturing causes can be varied:

- Secondary, tertiary, or other susceptible amines in the API, intermediates,
  reagents, catalysts, or degradation products.
- Nitrite or nitrosating agents in raw materials, excipients, water, recovered
  solvents, or process aids.
- Acidic conditions, elevated temperature, or long hold times that promote
  nitrosation.
- Recovered or recycled solvents with insufficient segregation or control.
- Cross-contamination from shared equipment, supplier operations, or contaminated
  materials.
- Packaging, storage, or formulation conditions that allow formation over shelf
  life.
- Analytical sample preparation that creates nitrosamines in situ and produces a
  false signal.

That last point matters. A QC method can accidentally create the impurity it is
trying to measure if extraction, pH, temperature, or reagents are poorly
controlled.

## The three-step regulatory pattern

Regulators generally expect a structured program:

1. **Risk assessment:** review products, APIs, excipients, processes, suppliers,
   packaging, and storage conditions for nitrosamine formation or contamination
   risk.
2. **Confirmatory testing:** where risk is identified, test with a validated,
   sensitive, and specific method.
3. **Risk mitigation:** remove or reduce the risk through process changes,
   supplier controls, formulation changes, specifications, storage controls,
   shelf-life changes, or regulatory submissions as needed.

The sequence matters. Testing everything without a good risk assessment wastes
resources. A paper assessment with no testing where real risk exists is worse.

## What a good risk assessment includes

A strong nitrosamine risk assessment usually covers:

- API route of synthesis, reagents, catalysts, quench steps, pH, temperature, and
  nitrosating conditions.
- API structure and degradation pathways, including the possibility of NDSRI
  formation.
- Excipients that may contain nitrite or promote nitrosation.
- Supplier controls for high-risk raw materials and recovered solvents.
- Cross-contamination risk in multipurpose facilities.
- Packaging and storage conditions that could influence formation over shelf
  life.
- Historical batch data, stability data, complaints, recalls, deviations, and
  change controls.
- A clear conclusion: no risk, theoretical risk, confirmed risk, or risk under
  investigation.

The output should not be a generic checklist. It should explain why the product
is or is not at risk, what evidence supports that conclusion, and what controls
keep the conclusion true over time.

## Confirmatory testing: sensitivity is not enough

Nitrosamine methods often use LC-MS/MS, GC-MS/MS, or high-resolution mass
spectrometry. Sensitivity is necessary, but it is not the whole method.

The method should also demonstrate:

- Specificity against the API, excipients, degradants, solvents, and matrix
  interferences.
- LOD and LOQ low enough for the applicable AI-based limit.
- Recovery and extraction efficiency at trace levels.
- Control of in-situ formation during sample preparation.
- Carryover control, because low-level signals are easy to misread.
- Robustness for pH, extraction time, temperature, and sample handling.
- Stability of standards and prepared samples.

For QC release or stability use, the method validation story must match the
decision being made. A screening method may support triage; a confirmatory method
used for control needs stronger validation.

## AI limits and NDSRIs

Acceptable intake limits are not all the same. Some nitrosamines have
compound-specific limits. Others may rely on read-across, interim limits,
carcinogenic potency categorization, or additional safety testing.

For NDSRIs, the key QC implication is that the target limit can change as
regulatory science evolves. A method that was adequate for one interim limit may
not be adequate after a revised AI limit. The control strategy therefore needs a
change-monitoring process, not a one-time memo.

## What mitigation can look like

Mitigation should target the source of risk. Examples include:

- Removing nitrosating agents or vulnerable amine sources where possible.
- Changing reaction conditions, order of addition, pH, temperature, or hold time.
- Tightening raw-material, excipient, or solvent specifications.
- Improving recovered-solvent segregation and supplier qualification.
- Adding purge or control steps supported by process understanding.
- Changing formulation or packaging when product-stage formation is the issue.
- Updating shelf life or storage conditions while a permanent CAPA is completed.
- Adding validated routine testing where risk remains.

The best CAPA is not "test more batches forever." Testing verifies control; it
does not replace control.

## Common QC traps

Nitrosamine programs fail in predictable ways:

- Treating only the originally publicized products as in scope.
- Ignoring excipient nitrite contribution or API-related NDSRI formation.
- Using a method with a headline LOQ but weak specificity or recovery.
- Creating nitrosamines during sample preparation and calling the result real.
- Forgetting that stability can be the formation window.
- Letting supplier changes bypass the nitrosamine risk assessment.
- Assuming a negative result on one batch proves no product risk.
- Failing to revisit AI limits, regulatory Q&A updates, or new NDSRI data.

## A practical review question

For each product, QC and QA should be able to answer:

> What nitrosamine could form, why, under what conditions, at what limit, by what
> method, and what control prevents it from increasing over shelf life?

If the team cannot answer that in plain language, the risk assessment is not yet
ready for an inspector.
