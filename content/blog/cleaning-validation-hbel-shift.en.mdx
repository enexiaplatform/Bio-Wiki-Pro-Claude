# Cleaning Validation: from 10 ppm to HBEL

For decades, cleaning-validation limits leaned on two rules of thumb: the
**10 ppm** criterion and **1/1000 of the minimum therapeutic dose**. Both were
easy to apply — and both were scientifically arbitrary. The EMA's shared-facility
guideline changed the expectation to **health-based exposure limits (HBEL)**.

## What was wrong with the old rules

Neither default looked at what a compound actually does to a patient. A potent,
sensitizing molecule and a benign one could end up with similar carryover limits
purely because of dosing math — which under- or over-controls risk depending on
the toxicology.

## How HBEL works

An HBEL is derived from real toxicology data (a NOAEL/LOAEL with adjustment
factors) and expressed as a **Permitted Daily Exposure (PDE)** — the amount a
patient can be exposed to daily with no adverse effect. That PDE then drives the
**Maximum Allowable Carryover (MACO)** and the swab/rinse residue limit your
cleaning must meet.

## What it means in practice

- **Multi-product facilities** must justify shared equipment against HBEL-derived
  limits, not just 10 ppm.
- **Highly hazardous compounds** with very low HBELs may require dedicated
  equipment or facilities.
- Your **Contamination Control Strategy** should reference the HBEL rationale.

## The takeaway

The 10 ppm rule isn't automatically "wrong" — but it's no longer a *justification*
on its own. Regulators expect a toxicology-based limit, with the old defaults used
only as an additional, more conservative check where appropriate.
