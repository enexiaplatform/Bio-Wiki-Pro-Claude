# Cleaning Validation: From 10 ppm to HBEL

Cleaning validation used to lean heavily on simple default limits: 10 ppm, a
fraction of the therapeutic dose, and visually clean. Those rules were easy to
apply, but they did not always reflect patient risk.

Modern shared-facility cleaning validation is built around health-based exposure
limits (HBELs), usually expressed as a permitted daily exposure (PDE) or
acceptable daily exposure (ADE). The shift matters because the question is no
longer "is the residue below a convenient default?" It is "is the possible
carryover toxicologically justified for the next patient?"

## Why 10 ppm was not enough

The 10 ppm rule does not know whether a compound is potent, sensitizing,
genotoxic, pharmacologically active at low dose, or relatively benign. Two
products can receive similar default cleaning limits even when their toxicology
profiles are completely different.

The old rules can be:

- **too loose** for highly potent or hazardous materials,
- **too strict** for low-risk materials, creating unnecessary failures,
- **hard to defend** when regulators ask for the patient-risk basis,
- **blind to shared-facility context**, such as batch size, maximum daily dose,
  route of administration, and next-product exposure.

Legacy defaults may still be used as additional conservative checks, but they
are weak if they are the only scientific justification.

## What HBEL changes

An HBEL is derived from pharmacological and toxicological data. The toxicologist
uses evidence such as NOAEL, LOAEL, pharmacodynamic activity, clinical exposure,
safety factors, and uncertainty modifiers to define a daily exposure level that
is expected to be without adverse effect.

In cleaning validation, that value becomes the anchor for carryover limits.

The usual logic is:

1. establish the PDE/HBEL for the previous product or residue,
2. calculate maximum allowable carryover (MACO) into the next product,
3. convert MACO into an equipment surface limit,
4. convert the surface limit into a swab or rinse acceptance criterion,
5. correct for method recovery where required,
6. verify that the cleaning process can meet the limit consistently.

That chain must be traceable. A cleaning limit without its toxicology basis and
calculation path is difficult to defend.

## PDE to MACO to swab limit

A simplified HBEL-based MACO calculation is:

```text
MACO = (PDE x minimum batch size of next product) / maximum daily dose of next product
```

Then:

```text
Surface limit = MACO / shared equipment surface area
```

For a swabbed area:

```text
Swab limit = surface limit x swabbed area x recovery correction
```

The actual protocol should define units clearly, such as mg/day, mg/batch,
micrograms per square centimeter, or micrograms per swab. Unit mistakes are a
surprisingly common way to create false confidence.

## Worst-case selection is not just solubility

Worst-case bracketing should consider both the product being cleaned and the
product that could receive carryover.

For the product being cleaned, assess:

- HBEL/PDE value,
- potency or toxicity,
- solubility and cleanability,
- batch size,
- residue detectability,
- formulation stickiness or equipment contact behavior,
- prior cleaning failures or hard-to-clean observations.

For the next product, assess:

- maximum daily dose,
- minimum batch size,
- route of administration,
- patient population,
- shared equipment path,
- whether the product is more vulnerable to carryover.

The worst case is often a combination: a hard-to-clean previous product with a
low HBEL going into a next product with a high daily dose and small batch size.

## Recovery studies matter

A clean swab result means little if the method cannot recover residue from the
surface.

Recovery studies should represent:

- actual product or marker residue,
- relevant equipment surface materials,
- realistic residue drying time,
- swab technique and solvent,
- analytical method range near the acceptance limit,
- predefined recovery acceptance criteria.

If recovery is poor, the method may under-report residue. The limit may need a
recovery correction, method improvement, or a different sampling approach.

## Visual clean is still useful, but not sufficient

Visual inspection remains an important control because it can detect gross
residue, color, film, foam, particles, or cleaning failures that a limited swab
plan may miss.

But "visually clean" does not prove HBEL compliance. A residue can be invisible
and still exceed a toxicology-based limit. Visual clean should sit alongside
analytical residue limits, not replace them.

## Do not forget non-product residues

Cleaning validation should not focus only on active ingredient carryover.

Depending on the process, the program may also need limits or controls for:

- cleaning agents and detergents,
- degradants or reaction products,
- bioburden and endotoxin,
- disinfectant residues,
- excipients that interfere with the next process or assay,
- microbial control during dirty and clean hold times.

The acceptance criteria should match the risks created by the process and the
next product, not only the active ingredient.

## What auditors usually ask for

For a shared-equipment cleaning validation program, be ready to show:

- HBEL/PDE monograph or toxicology assessment,
- MACO calculations and unit checks,
- rationale for worst-case product/equipment selection,
- cleaning validation protocol and report,
- swab/rinse method validation and recovery study,
- analytical method sensitivity against the limit,
- dirty hold time and clean hold time justification,
- visual inspection criteria,
- cleaning procedure parameters and operator controls,
- deviations, failures, re-cleaning records, and CAPA,
- periodic review showing the HBEL and cleaning matrix remain current.

This is where the old 10 ppm rule often collapses: it cannot answer enough of
those questions by itself.
