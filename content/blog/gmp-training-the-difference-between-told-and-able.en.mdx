# GMP training: the difference between 'told' and 'able'

People are one of the largest sources of variability in a GMP operation. They
weigh, sample, clean, gown, enter data, interpret results, assemble lines, review
batch records, investigate deviations, and make decisions under time pressure.
That makes training one of the most important controls in the quality system.

But GMP training is often treated as administration: assign a module, collect a
signature, close the item. That proves someone was exposed to information. It
does not prove the person can perform the task correctly.

The core distinction is simple: a person is not qualified because they were told
what to do. They are qualified when the site can show they have the education,
training, experience, and demonstrated competence needed for the assigned
function.

## What GMP actually expects

21 CFR 211.25 requires personnel involved in manufacturing, processing, packing,
or holding drug products to have the education, training, experience, or
combination needed to perform assigned functions. It also expects CGMP training
to be conducted by qualified individuals on a continuing basis and with enough
frequency to keep employees familiar with requirements that apply to them.

EU GMP Chapter 2 says the same thing in a different structure: correct
manufacture relies on people, there must be sufficient qualified personnel,
responsibilities must be understood and recorded, and personnel should receive
initial and continuing GMP training relevant to their needs.

So the audit question is not, "Did everyone click through annual GMP training?"
The audit question is, "Can this site prove each person was qualified for the
work they actually performed at the time they performed it?"

That proof usually lives across several records:

- job description or role profile;
- training matrix;
- SOP and topic assignments;
- classroom or e-learning records;
- on-the-job training records;
- observed competency checks;
- qualification sign-off;
- retraining after change, deviation, or performance gap;
- periodic requalification where skill decay is likely.

## The read-and-understood trap

"Read and understood" is useful for awareness. It is weak as the only evidence of
competence.

Reading an SOP can confirm that the person had access to instructions. It does
not prove they can:

- perform the task under real conditions;
- recognize abnormal situations;
- document contemporaneously;
- avoid common errors;
- protect data integrity;
- escalate when the procedure does not fit reality;
- explain why critical steps matter.

For low-risk awareness topics, read-and-understood may be enough. For tasks that
can affect product quality, patient safety, sterility assurance, data integrity,
or batch disposition, training should include a competency element.

Examples:

| Task | Weak evidence | Stronger evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Batch record entry | SOP read receipt | Supervised entries reviewed for completeness, corrections, and GDP behavior |
| Aseptic intervention | Annual GMP module | Observation in the qualified area, media-fill relevance, and periodic requalification |
| HPLC sample prep | Method read receipt | Observed preparation, calculation check, system suitability understanding |
| Gowning | Gowning SOP signature | Practical gowning qualification with personnel monitoring criteria |
| Deviation investigation | Investigation SOP read | Case exercise reviewed for root cause, impact assessment, and CAPA quality |

The deeper the consequence of the task, the more training must show ability, not
just exposure.

## Role-based training beats generic training

A good GMP training program starts with roles, not with a giant universal course
list. Every person should have a curriculum that reflects what they actually do.

At minimum, a role-based matrix should answer:

- What GMP topics are required for this role?
- Which SOPs apply before the person works independently?
- Which tasks require observed qualification?
- Which tasks require periodic requalification?
- Who is qualified to train and assess the person?
- What happens when the SOP changes?
- What happens when the person changes role, shift, line, product, or site?

Generic annual training has a place. It keeps broad awareness alive. But it does
not replace role-based qualification. A microbiology analyst, packaging operator,
QA batch-record reviewer, maintenance technician, and warehouse operator do not
need the same depth in the same topics.

## Continuing training is not an annual ritual

Training is a moving control. It has to respond to the life of the system.

Continuing training may be triggered by:

- onboarding into a new role;
- new or revised SOPs;
- process, equipment, facility, software, or method changes;
- deviation or CAPA findings;
- audit observations;
- recurring human-error trends;
- long absence from a task;
- periodic requalification interval;
- poor performance during observation;
- new regulatory or site expectations.

If training happens only once a year, it will miss the moments when risk actually
changes. The strongest programs connect training to document control, change
control, deviation management, CAPA, audit findings, and management review.

## What competency evidence looks like

Competency evidence should match the task.

For knowledge-heavy tasks, evidence may include:

- written assessment;
- oral questioning;
- scenario-based case exercise;
- review of completed investigation or batch-review examples.

For hands-on tasks, evidence may include:

- trainer demonstration;
- supervised practice;
- observed execution using a checklist;
- independent performance under normal conditions;
- objective acceptance criteria;
- sign-off by a qualified trainer or supervisor.

For aseptic, sterile, or microbiology tasks, evidence may also include:

- gowning qualification;
- media-fill participation relevance;
- personnel monitoring results;
- aseptic technique observation;
- contamination-control behavior in the actual area.

For data-integrity-sensitive tasks, evidence should include:

- GDP behavior;
- correct correction practice;
- unique login and electronic-signature discipline;
- audit-trail awareness;
- escalation when data or system behavior is abnormal.

The record should make the conclusion obvious: this person was qualified for this
task, on this date, by this assessor, against these criteria.

## Why "retrain the operator" is usually weak CAPA

Deviation reports often start with "operator error." Sometimes that is true at
the surface level: a person skipped a step, selected the wrong material, entered
the wrong value, or performed a technique incorrectly.

But "human error" is rarely a complete root cause by itself. A serious
investigation asks why the system allowed the error.

Possible deeper causes include:

- confusing SOP wording;
- unclear responsibility or handoff;
- poor line design or material flow;
- similar-looking labels or containers;
- unrealistic workload or staffing;
- weak supervision;
- inadequate task qualification;
- training delivered too early and never practiced;
- no periodic requalification for a decaying skill;
- culture that discourages stopping and asking.

Retraining can be appropriate when the investigation proves a training gap. It is
weak when used as a default CAPA because no one wants to redesign the process.

A stronger CAPA often combines training with system changes:

- clarify the SOP;
- redesign the form or batch-record step;
- separate similar materials;
- add a verification point;
- improve line clearance;
- change the workflow;
- update the training checklist;
- require observed requalification;
- trend recurrence after implementation.

Training should be one control in a CAPA, not the dustpan used to sweep every
human error under the rug.

[How to investigate a deviation](/blog/how-to-investigate-a-deviation).

## What inspectors look for

Training findings often come from cross-checks, not from reading the training SOP
alone.

An inspector may ask:

- Who performed this GMP task on this batch?
- Was that person qualified before the task date?
- Which SOP version was effective at the time?
- Was the training completed before the SOP effective date?
- Did the SOP change require retraining?
- Was the trainer qualified?
- Was there observed competency or only read-and-understood?
- Did the person have repeated deviations or errors?
- Did CAPA include effective training or only a signature?
- Are contractors, temporary staff, consultants, and supervisors included?
- Are overdue training items blocking GMP work or merely reported later?

The strongest answer is not a perfect-looking matrix. It is a traceable link from
task to person to current SOP to qualification evidence.

## Practical training-matrix checklist

Use this checklist to test whether a training program is robust:

- **Role mapping:** Every GMP role has a defined curriculum.
- **Task criticality:** High-risk tasks are identified and require competency
  assessment.
- **Trainer qualification:** Trainers and assessors are qualified for the task
  they teach.
- **Timing:** Training is complete before unsupervised GMP work.
- **SOP version control:** Training links to the correct SOP version and effective
  date.
- **Change trigger:** Material SOP changes trigger retraining before the revised
  task is performed.
- **Requalification:** Skills that decay have defined intervals and criteria.
- **Deviation link:** Investigations check training relevance without defaulting
  to retraining.
- **Effectiveness:** Training CAPAs have a way to verify behavior changed.
- **Temporary staff:** Contractors and temporary workers are included where they
  perform GMP tasks.
- **Supervisor competence:** Supervisors are trained and qualified for oversight,
  not only for task execution.
- **Matrix maintenance:** Role changes, transfers, and absences update the matrix
  quickly.

## Key takeaway

GMP training is not the act of telling people what the SOP says. It is the
quality-system control that makes sure people can perform assigned work correctly
and know when to stop, document, and escalate.

If the record only proves attendance, the site has evidence of exposure. If the
record proves observed, role-based competence, the site has evidence of
qualification.
