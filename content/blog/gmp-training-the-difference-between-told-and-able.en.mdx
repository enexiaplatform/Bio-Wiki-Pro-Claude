# GMP training: the difference between 'told' and 'able'

People are the largest source of variability in a GMP operation, which makes training
one of its most important controls — and one of the most under-done. The core idea is
deceptively simple: someone is only qualified to perform a GMP task once they can
**demonstrate they can do it correctly**, not merely that they were told how.

## The 'read and understood' trap

The most common weakness is treating a signature on a "read and understood" form as
training. Reading an SOP confirms exposure, not **competence**. For anything that
matters — aseptic technique, a complex test, batch record execution — qualification
needs evidence the person can actually perform: supervised practice, an assessment, a
sign-off by a qualified trainer.

The deeper a task's consequence, the more the training has to **show ability**, not
just record attendance.

## Training is a moving target

A training program is not one-and-done:

- **Onboarding** to the role and its SOPs
- **Re-training** when an SOP changes, or when performance shows a gap
- **Re-qualification** for skills that decay (gowning, aseptic technique) on a defined
  interval
- A **training matrix** that maps every person to the tasks they are qualified for —
  and keeps it current as roles change

## Why it shows up in every investigation

Look at enough deviation investigations and a pattern emerges: "operator error" is a
frequent first answer. But human error is rarely the **true** root cause — usually the
system (a confusing SOP, inadequate training, unrealistic workload) allowed it. That is
why a real CAPA digs past "retrain the operator" to ask whether the training,
procedure, or design set the person up to fail.
