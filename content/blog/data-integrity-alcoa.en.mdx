# Data Integrity and the ALCOA+ Principles

Data integrity is the reason a quality decision can be trusted. A batch release,
OOS decision, stability conclusion, media-fill result, or cleaning validation
approval is only as strong as the records behind it.

Regulators do not treat data integrity as a separate IT topic. FDA, MHRA, and WHO
guidance all frame it as a core GMP expectation: data must remain complete,
consistent, and accurate throughout its lifecycle, from creation through review,
retention, retrieval, and disposal.

ALCOA+ is the practical shorthand for that expectation.

## ALCOA+ in one table

| Principle | What it means | Weak signal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Attributable | The record shows who performed the action and when | Shared login, unsigned entry, missing analyst |
| Legible | The record can be read and understood throughout retention | Faded printout, overwritten correction, unclear scan |
| Contemporaneous | The record is made at the time the work occurs | Backfilled notebook, delayed entry, pre-signed form |
| Original | The first capture or a verified true copy is retained | Only summary printout kept, raw file deleted |
| Accurate | The record correctly reflects the activity and result | Transcription error, uncontrolled spreadsheet formula |
| Complete | All required data, metadata, repeats, and invalidated results are retained | Missing injections, excluded worksheet, no reason for repeat |
| Consistent | Records follow the expected sequence and chronology | Time stamps out of order, sample sequence altered without explanation |
| Enduring | Data remains protected and readable for the retention period | Thermal paper only, unsupported file format, weak backup |
| Available | Records can be retrieved for review, investigation, or inspection | Archived data inaccessible, vendor needed to view audit trail |

The point is not to memorize the words. The point is to test whether the data can
support the decision being made.

## What this looks like in GMP work

For a paper batch record, ALCOA+ means entries are made by the person doing the
step, at the time of the step, with corrections that preserve the original entry.
White-out, overwriting, pre-signing, or "clean copying" into a nicer form all create
integrity risk because they break the history of what happened.

For a chromatographic result, ALCOA+ means the official result includes the raw data,
sequence, method, processing method, integration changes, audit trail, failed or
aborted injections where applicable, and review evidence. A final PDF alone is not
enough if it hides how the result was generated.

For a spreadsheet, ALCOA+ means the template is controlled, formulas are protected
or verified, input cells are traceable, version history is managed, and outputs are
reviewed against source data. An uncontrolled spreadsheet can turn a correct test
result into a wrong quality decision.

## The lifecycle view

A common mistake is treating data integrity as a review activity at the end. A
stronger program designs integrity into each stage:

1. **Create:** unique users, controlled forms, validated systems, trained people.
2. **Process:** approved calculations, controlled methods, audit trails, version
   control.
3. **Review:** independent review, exception review, audit-trail review, complete
   evidence package.
4. **Retain:** secure storage, backup, migration controls, readable formats.
5. **Retrieve:** records available for release, investigation, annual review, and
   inspection.

If any stage is weak, the final record may look complete while the underlying data
is not reliable.

## Controls that make ALCOA+ real

Good data integrity is a mix of procedure, system design, and culture.

- Use unique user accounts and role-based access.
- Restrict admin rights from routine analysts where possible.
- Enable audit trails for GMP-critical systems.
- Review audit trails based on data criticality and system risk.
- Retain raw data, metadata, calculations, and invalidated or repeated work.
- Control spreadsheet templates and validate critical calculations.
- Define how records are corrected without obscuring the original.
- Train reviewers to look beyond the final result printout.
- Investigate unexplained repeats, deletions, clock changes, and missing data.
- Periodically verify backups and archive retrieval.

These controls turn ALCOA+ from a training slide into everyday evidence.

## Reviewer questions

When reviewing a GMP record, ask:

- Can I tell who did the work and when?
- Was the record created at the time of the activity?
- Is the original data or certified true copy retained?
- Are all repeats, invalidations, and exceptions explained?
- Do the timestamps and sequence of events make sense?
- Is there any sign of unofficial testing, backdating, or selective reporting?
- Can the data still be retrieved and read without special reconstruction?
- Does the audit trail support the final result?

If the answer is unclear, the issue may be a documentation gap, a system-control gap,
or a true data-integrity event. The response should be risk-based, documented, and
linked to the quality decision affected.

## Common inspection findings

Many serious findings are not exotic. They are basic ALCOA+ failures:

- analysts sharing a generic account;
- audit trails disabled or never reviewed;
- trial injections omitted from the record;
- electronic raw data overwritten after a summary report is printed;
- system clocks changed without control;
- spreadsheets with unprotected formulas;
- corrections that hide the original entry;
- archived data that cannot be retrieved during inspection.

The pattern is always the same: the site cannot prove the record is complete,
traceable, and reliable.

## Practical takeaway

ALCOA+ is not a slogan. It is a review method. For every GMP record, the reviewer
should be able to reconstruct what happened, who did it, when it happened, what data
was generated, what changed, what was excluded, why it was excluded, and whether the
final decision is still scientifically and procedurally justified.
