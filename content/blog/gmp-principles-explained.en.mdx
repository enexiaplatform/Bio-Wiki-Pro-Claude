# GMP Explained: The Core Principles

Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) is the operating system for making medicines
reliably. It is the set of controls that makes sure a drug is consistently
manufactured, tested, packaged, stored, and released to the quality required for
its intended use.

For a new QC or QA professional, GMP can feel like a pile of rules. The better
way to understand it is this: GMP exists because final testing cannot guarantee
quality by itself. Quality has to be designed into the process, controlled during
manufacture, proven with records, and improved when the system finds a problem.

## Why the "current" in cGMP matters

In the US, FDA uses the term **current good manufacturing practice (CGMP)**.
"Current" means the system should reflect modern expectations, technology, and
scientific understanding. A procedure that was acceptable years ago may be weak
today if better controls, better data protection, or better process understanding
are now expected.

FDA describes CGMP regulations as minimum requirements for the methods,
facilities, and controls used in manufacturing, processing, and packing a drug.
The goal is practical: make sure the product is safe for use and has the identity,
strength, quality, and purity it claims to have.

## Principle 1 - Quality is built in

You cannot test quality into a batch at the end. Release tests sample only a
small part of the whole process. GMP therefore puts control upstream:

- qualified facilities, utilities, and equipment;
- approved materials and suppliers;
- validated or verified processes and methods;
- in-process controls and monitoring;
- trained people following approved procedures;
- QA review before batch disposition.

Testing confirms the system worked. It does not replace the system.

## Principle 2 - Written procedures must match real work

GMP work should be defined in approved procedures and executed as written.
Procedures must be specific enough that trained people can perform the work
consistently.

This principle shows up everywhere:

- sampling and testing procedures;
- cleaning and line-clearance instructions;
- batch manufacturing and packaging records;
- laboratory methods and system suitability criteria;
- deviation, OOS, CAPA, and change-control procedures;
- data review and archive procedures.

If the SOP says one thing and the team does another, the problem is not only
documentation. It means the quality system no longer describes the real process.

## Principle 3 - Records prove what happened

In GMP, undocumented work is weak evidence. Records should be attributable,
legible, contemporaneous, original, accurate, complete, consistent, enduring, and
available.

For QC, that includes:

- raw data, worksheets, chromatograms, spectra, and calculations;
- instrument audit trails and method versions;
- sample receipt, preparation, and chain of custody;
- reference-standard and reagent records;
- media preparation, growth promotion, and incubation records;
- second-person or QA review where required.

The purpose is not bureaucracy. The record protects the patient, the batch, and
the people who made the decision.

## Principle 4 - Contamination and mix-ups must be prevented

WHO describes GMP as aimed at reducing risks inherent in production, especially
cross-contamination, mix-ups, and false labelling. Those risks are controlled by
facility design, segregation, cleaning, status labelling, line clearance, material
control, environmental monitoring, and disciplined behavior.

In practice, this means:

- materials are identified and stored correctly;
- equipment is clean, qualified, and released for use;
- labels and packaging are controlled;
- sterile and non-sterile operations have appropriate contamination controls;
- personnel gowning, hygiene, and movement are defined;
- deviations are investigated when controls fail.

GMP is often about preventing ordinary human confusion from becoming a patient
risk.

## Principle 5 - People must be trained and qualified

Training is not just reading an SOP. GMP requires people to be qualified for the
work they perform. For high-risk tasks, qualification may include observed
practice, competency checks, analyst qualification, gowning qualification, media
fill participation, or periodic requalification.

The practical question is:

> Can this person perform this task correctly, explain why it matters, and
> recognize when something is wrong?

If not, the training system is incomplete.

## Principle 6 - Deviations must teach the system

Mistakes, alarms, excursions, OOS results, and unexpected trends are not just
paperwork events. They are signals from the process.

A mature GMP system:

- records deviations promptly;
- assesses product impact;
- investigates scientifically;
- identifies root cause where possible;
- defines corrective and preventive action;
- checks whether CAPA worked;
- looks for recurrence or wider impact.

ICH Q10 frames CAPA, change management, process monitoring, and management review
as part of a pharmaceutical quality system. In plain language: the system should
learn.

## Principle 7 - Change must be controlled

Every change is a hypothesis that something might affect quality. GMP change
control makes the site ask the right questions before acting:

- Does this affect product quality, patient safety, validation, stability, data
  integrity, or regulatory commitments?
- What qualification, validation, method transfer, training, or documentation is
  required?
- Can the change be implemented under the site's quality system, or does it need
  regulatory approval first?
- How will the site confirm the change worked?

Uncontrolled change is one of the fastest ways to lose the validated state.

## What GMP looks like in QC

For a QC analyst, GMP is visible in ordinary habits:

- use the approved method and current version;
- check instrument status before use;
- prepare standards and samples as written;
- record data when the work is performed;
- review system suitability before accepting results;
- do not repeat testing without scientific justification;
- escalate abnormal results promptly;
- protect electronic data and audit trails;
- keep training current.

Small habits are the quality system in motion.
