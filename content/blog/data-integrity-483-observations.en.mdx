# 7 Data Integrity Findings That Show Up in FDA 483s

Data integrity findings keep appearing in FDA 483s and warning letters because
they strike at the foundation of GMP: can the quality decision be trusted?

FDA's data integrity guidance frames data integrity as the completeness,
consistency, and accuracy of data across the data lifecycle. In practice, that
means the record must show who did the work, when it was done, what raw data were
created, what changed, why it changed, who reviewed it, and whether the final
quality decision used all relevant evidence.

The familiar ALCOA+ principles are still the fastest way to think:

- **Attributable**
- **Legible**
- **Contemporaneous**
- **Original**
- **Accurate**
- **Complete**
- **Consistent**
- **Enduring**
- **Available**

An FDA Form 483 lists inspectional observations when investigators observe
conditions that may indicate violations. Data-integrity observations often start
small: a shared login, a missing audit-trail review, a trial injection, a
late-signed page. But the implication is large. If the record can be altered,
hidden, recreated, or selectively reported, then the batch-release decision may
not be reliable.

Below are seven recurring data-integrity findings, why they matter, what the
evidence looks like, and how to prevent each one.

## 1. Shared logins and generic accounts

Shared accounts destroy attribution. If three analysts use the same "HPLC_USER"
login, the system cannot show who created, modified, processed, deleted, or
approved the data.

That breaks the "A" in ALCOA: attributable. It also weakens investigations. If a
sequence was changed after an OOS result, the site needs to know exactly who made
the change and why. A generic account turns a technical question into a trust
problem.

Common evidence inspectors notice:

- generic analyst accounts;
- shared administrator credentials;
- passwords posted near equipment;
- users logged in under a supervisor account;
- terminated employees still active;
- no periodic access review;
- no link between system user list and job role.

Prevention:

- require unique user IDs for every GMP system;
- prohibit shared passwords;
- use role-based access;
- remove or disable dormant accounts promptly;
- review access periodically;
- separate analyst, reviewer, administrator, and QA roles;
- document emergency access and review it after use.

If a legacy system cannot support unique accounts, treat it as a risk. Add
compensating controls such as second-person contemporaneous review, restricted
physical access, logbook reconciliation, and a documented replacement or upgrade
plan.

## 2. Audit trails disabled, incomplete, or not reviewed

An audit trail is not useful simply because it exists. FDA defines an audit trail
as a secure, computer-generated, time-stamped electronic record that allows
reconstruction of events related to creation, modification, or deletion of an
electronic record.

For GMP-critical data, the audit trail should help answer:

- Who created the data?
- Was anything modified?
- Was anything deleted?
- Were methods, integrations, calculations, or timestamps changed?
- Were failed, aborted, or repeated runs investigated?
- Was the audit trail reviewed before the quality decision?

Common evidence inspectors notice:

- audit trails switched off;
- audit trail does not capture critical events;
- analysts can disable or purge audit trails;
- review procedure says "review audit trails" but no record proves review;
- audit trails reviewed only after an inspection request;
- reviewers check only the final result, not sequence changes or deleted events;
- no risk-based definition of which audit trails require routine review.

Prevention:

- enable audit trails for GMP-critical systems;
- validate that critical events are captured;
- restrict audit-trail configuration to independent administrators;
- define review frequency by data criticality;
- review audit trails before batch release when they support release data;
- document what was reviewed, who reviewed it, when, and what exceptions were
  assessed;
- trend recurring exceptions by method, system, analyst, and product.

The key control is review by meaning, not review by screenshot. A reviewer should
understand what an exception means for the record.

## 3. Testing into compliance

"Testing into compliance" means repeating analysis, reinjection, reprocessing, or
resampling until an acceptable result appears, without scientifically justified
investigation and without reporting the full data story.

This is one of the most damaging data-integrity patterns because it suggests
selective reporting. The issue is not that repeat testing is always forbidden.
Repeat testing may be appropriate under an approved OOS procedure. The issue is
using repeat testing to avoid a failing or undesirable result.

Common evidence inspectors notice:

- multiple injections before the reported result;
- "trial" runs not included in the official record;
- deleted or aborted sequences;
- integration changes that improve the result without rationale;
- OOS results invalidated without assignable cause;
- sample preparations repeated without investigation;
- analyst notes that do not match electronic raw data;
- only passing results attached to the batch record.

Prevention:

- require a pre-approved OOS/OOT procedure;
- account for every injection, preparation, sequence, and result;
- include invalidated data in the investigation package;
- justify repeat testing before execution where possible;
- prohibit unofficial trial runs for GMP samples;
- review audit trails and raw data during OOS investigation;
- require QA approval for invalidation decisions.

The quality unit should be able to reconstruct the full testing history, not only
the result that was finally reported.

## 4. Orphan data and unofficial records

Orphan data are data created during GMP work but not linked to the official
record. They may sit on an instrument hard drive, local PC, USB drive, analyst
folder, spreadsheet, notebook page, or temporary file.

The danger is that orphan data can be ignored, overwritten, or selectively used.
If the data were created as part of GMP work, they need a controlled place in the
record lifecycle.

Common evidence inspectors notice:

- raw data stored on local instrument PCs outside backup;
- chromatograms or spectra not included in the official package;
- uncontrolled spreadsheet calculations;
- USB transfer with no chain of custody;
- temporary worksheets used for final calculations;
- "system suitability trial" files near official data;
- printed records with no link to electronic originals;
- missing metadata needed to interpret the result.

Prevention:

- define the official raw data for each system and method;
- map where data are created, processed, reviewed, stored, archived, and
  retrieved;
- prohibit uncontrolled local storage for GMP data;
- control spreadsheets used for GMP decisions;
- back up raw data and metadata;
- ensure printouts are true copies where paper is used;
- reconcile electronic data with reported results.

Data integrity is a lifecycle problem. The record is not complete until the data
and metadata can be retrieved and understood later.

## 5. Backdating, late entries, and pre-signing

Contemporaneous recording means the record is made at the time the work is
performed, or as close to that time as the approved process allows. Backdating and
pre-signing make the record look cleaner than the event really was.

Common evidence inspectors notice:

- logbook pages signed before work occurred;
- entries made days after the activity with no explanation;
- identical timestamps across multiple manual entries;
- instrument printouts showing different times from handwritten records;
- training signed after the task was performed;
- batch record steps initialed before completion;
- time/date settings changed on an instrument or workstation.

Prevention:

- train staff on contemporaneous recording using real examples;
- prohibit pre-signing and backdating explicitly;
- require late-entry notation with current date, reason, and signature;
- compare manual records against electronic timestamps during review;
- lock system clocks where possible;
- review time/date changes in audit trails;
- investigate repeat late-entry patterns as a process issue.

Late entries are sometimes legitimate. Hidden late entries are the problem.

## 6. Inadequate access controls and segregation of duties

Data integrity depends on limiting what each user can do. Analysts often need to
create and process data. They should not normally be able to delete data, alter
audit-trail settings, change system time, approve their own critical work, or
modify validated calculation logic without control.

Common evidence inspectors notice:

- analysts have administrator rights;
- users can delete raw data;
- audit-trail settings editable by routine users;
- methods or processing templates changed without approval;
- no documented role matrix;
- no periodic user-access review;
- reviewer and performer are the same person for critical records;
- vendor support accounts uncontrolled.

Prevention:

- define a role matrix for each GMP system;
- separate data generation, review, and administration where practical;
- remove unnecessary admin privileges;
- control method/template changes through change control;
- review access rights periodically;
- log and review privileged actions;
- manage vendor and service accounts with time-limited, approved access.

Access control is not an IT-only control. QA owns the question of whether access
rights protect product quality and data integrity.

## 7. Paper records with uncontrolled corrections

Paper records still fail inspections when corrections obscure the original entry
or make it impossible to reconstruct what happened.

Common evidence inspectors notice:

- white-out or correction fluid;
- overwritten numbers;
- missing reason for correction;
- missing initials or date;
- blank spaces in controlled forms;
- transcribed values without verification;
- unofficial sticky notes used as source data;
- pages replaced without traceability.

Prevention:

- use single-line cross-out so the original entry remains legible;
- initial and date corrections;
- add a reason where required by SOP or where the change is not obvious;
- prohibit white-out and overwriting;
- control blank spaces;
- verify transcribed values;
- treat uncontrolled notes as records if they capture GMP data;
- audit paper records for repeat GDP behavior.

Paper is not safer than electronic data. It simply fails differently.

## What inspectors are really testing

Most data-integrity observations are not about one bad record. They test whether
the quality system can answer four questions:

1. **Can the data be attributed to the person who did the work?**
2. **Can the full data history be reconstructed?**
3. **Can the record be trusted as complete, original, and accurate?**
4. **Did QA review the data needed to support the quality decision?**

If the answer is weak, the inspection may move from a record issue to a system
issue.

## How to self-check a system in 30 minutes

Pick one GMP-critical system such as CDS, LIMS, balance software, environmental
monitoring database, autoclave software, or an electronic batch record module.

Ask:

- Who has access today?
- Are any shared or dormant accounts active?
- Can routine users delete or overwrite raw data?
- Are audit trails enabled for critical events?
- Can audit trails be disabled?
- Is audit-trail review defined by procedure?
- Can you show a recent documented review?
- Where are raw data and metadata stored?
- Are backups verified?
- Can a reviewer reconstruct an OOS, repeat run, or changed result?
- Are paper printouts true copies or just convenient summaries?
- Are incidents trended and escalated to CAPA when recurring?

If the answer depends on one person saying "we usually do it," the control is
not strong enough.

## How to respond when you find a gap

When a data-integrity gap is found internally, do not treat it as a minor
documentation cleanup until risk is understood.

Use this sequence:

1. **Contain:** protect affected records, prevent further deletion or alteration,
   and restrict risky access if needed.
2. **Scope:** identify products, batches, methods, systems, dates, users, and
   records potentially affected.
3. **Assess impact:** decide whether product quality, release decisions,
   stability, complaints, or regulatory submissions may be affected.
4. **Investigate root cause:** determine whether the issue is training, system
   design, procedure, access control, culture, or governance.
5. **Define CAPA:** fix the system weakness, not only the record.
6. **Verify effectiveness:** prove the control now works through objective
   evidence such as access review, audit-trail review samples, no recurrence, or
   successful backup/retrieval tests.

The CAPA should match the risk. A missing correction reason in one low-risk
logbook may need coaching and GDP review. A CDS where analysts can delete raw data
needs escalation, technical control, retrospective review, and management
oversight.

## The QC/QA takeaway

Data integrity is not a software feature and not a training slide. It is the
quality system's ability to trust its own evidence.

The recurring 483 themes are remarkably consistent: shared logins, unreviewed
audit trails, selective testing, orphan data, non-contemporaneous records,
excessive privileges, and weak corrections. Each one is preventable when ALCOA+
is translated into daily controls.

The practical question for every GMP record is simple:

> Could an independent reviewer reconstruct what happened and trust the quality
> decision?

If not, the record is not inspection-ready.
