# How to Investigate a Deviation (Step by Step)

A deviation is a departure from an approved procedure, specification, validated
state, batch record, method, or expected control. It may be small, but the way it
is handled shows whether the quality system is learning or merely filing
paperwork.

21 CFR 211.100 requires written production and process-control procedures, with
deviations recorded and justified. 21 CFR 211.192 requires unexplained
discrepancies and specification failures to be thoroughly investigated. ICH Q10
then connects deviation handling to CAPA, knowledge management, and continual
improvement.

The practical goal is not to write a long report. It is to make a defensible
quality decision.

## 1. Contain the situation first

Before root cause, before CAPA, before classification, control the immediate
risk.

Ask:

- Is product, material, equipment, data, or a room status affected?
- Does anything need to be quarantined, segregated, stopped, or labelled?
- Is there an immediate patient, sterility, contamination, data-integrity, or
  compliance risk?
- Who must be notified now: QA, production, QC, engineering, warehouse, QP, or
  site leadership?
- Is there a regulatory, recall, complaint, or field-impact concern?

Containment is not the same as corrective action. It prevents the problem from
spreading while the investigation is still learning what happened.

## 2. Capture the event contemporaneously

The first record should be factual and time-bound:

- what happened,
- where it happened,
- when it happened,
- who observed it,
- what product, batch, equipment, sample, method, or record was involved,
- what immediate action was taken,
- what evidence was preserved.

Avoid early conclusions such as "operator error" or "no impact" unless the
evidence already supports them. The first report should describe the event, not
solve it prematurely.

## 3. Classify by risk, not by convenience

Deviation categories vary by site, but the logic should be risk-based.

| Classification | Typical meaning | Investigation depth |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Minor | No meaningful product, patient, data-integrity, validated-state, or compliance impact; isolated and understood | Short investigation with documented rationale |
| Major | Possible impact, repeat event, procedural failure, equipment/control failure, or uncertain scope | Full investigation, QA approval, impact assessment, likely CAPA evaluation |
| Critical | Potential patient risk, sterility assurance concern, release decision impact, data-integrity breach, regulatory commitment failure, or distributed product concern | Immediate escalation, formal investigation, senior QA/site review, possible field/regulatory assessment |

Classification can change. If new evidence increases risk, upgrade the
deviation. Do not keep a deviation "minor" because the form was opened that way.

## 4. Map the impact

Impact assessment is the spine of the investigation. It determines what the
event could affect, not just what was obviously affected.

Consider:

- current batch or lot,
- previous and subsequent batches,
- other products, strengths, campaigns, or lines,
- raw materials, components, or suppliers,
- equipment, utilities, rooms, and cleaning status,
- validated state and process controls,
- QC samples, calculations, audit trails, and reportable data,
- stability, retains, complaints, trends, and APR/PQR signals,
- regulatory filings, registered process parameters, or validated acceptance
  criteria.

A strong impact assessment says why the boundary is defensible. "Only one batch"
is not enough unless the evidence explains why the issue cannot extend beyond
that batch.

## 5. Build an evidence map

Do not jump from event to root cause. Build the evidence set first.

Useful evidence may include:

- batch record pages and logbooks,
- equipment logs, alarms, calibration, and maintenance records,
- environmental monitoring or utility trends,
- audit trails and raw data,
- training and qualification records,
- procedure version history,
- material CoA and supplier records,
- photos, labels, printouts, and physical evidence,
- interviews with operators or analysts,
- previous deviations, OOS/OOT events, complaints, and CAPAs.

Interviews should be used carefully. They help explain context, but they should
not replace objective records.

## 6. Identify the root cause

Root cause analysis should explain how the system allowed the deviation to occur
and why existing controls did not prevent or detect it.

Good root-cause statements are specific:

- "The batch record did not require independent verification of sieve size before
  charging."
- "The cleaning status label was visually similar to the ready-for-use label and
  stored in the same location."
- "The CDS method allowed manual integration without second-person review for
  release assays."

Weak root-cause statements hide the system problem:

- "operator error,"
- "analyst mistake,"
- "lack of attention,"
- "procedure not followed,"
- "training issue."

Human action may be involved, but the investigation should ask what made the
wrong action possible, likely, or undetected.

## 7. Decide whether CAPA is needed

Not every deviation needs a full CAPA. But every deviation needs a justified CAPA
decision.

CAPA is usually needed when:

- the deviation is major or critical,
- the event is recurring,
- the root cause is systemic,
- the impact crosses batches, products, sites, or systems,
- the same control failed before,
- the issue affects patient risk, data integrity, sterility assurance, or
  validated state.

CAPA should map to the root cause. If the root cause is a weak control, a
training-only CAPA is usually not enough.

## 8. Write the closure package

A deviation can be closed only when the record supports the quality decision.

The closure package should include:

1. event description and timeline,
2. containment actions,
3. risk classification and any escalation,
4. impact assessment and boundary rationale,
5. evidence reviewed,
6. root cause and contributing factors,
7. correction, corrective action, and preventive action decisions,
8. product or batch disposition rationale,
9. effectiveness-check plan when CAPA is opened,
10. QA approval and any follow-up commitments.

The best closure reports are not the longest. They are the ones where an
independent reviewer can follow the evidence to the conclusion.

## Common audit traps

- Opening the deviation late with no explanation.
- Writing the root cause before the evidence is reviewed.
- Calling everything "human error."
- Releasing product before impact assessment is complete.
- Failing to assess related batches or systems.
- No documented rationale for "no CAPA required."
- CAPA actions do not match the root cause.
- Effectiveness check proves task completion, not recurrence control.
- Closure justification is a summary, not an evidence-backed decision.
