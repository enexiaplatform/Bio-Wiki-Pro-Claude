# From monthly workload to QC turnaround and queue feasibility

A laboratory can have enough monthly capacity and still miss every important
deadline.

Capacity asks:

> Can the available resources process the expected volume over a period?

Turnaround feasibility asks:

> Can each sample, event and decision move through the required sequence before its
> deadline under the actual operating calendar and competing demand?

The second question requires time, order and dependency. Dividing monthly workload
by monthly capacity cannot answer it.

## Decompose end-to-end turnaround

For each sample or event, separate:

```text
transport and receipt time
+ receipt queue
+ accessioning and status verification
+ preparation queue
+ hands-on preparation time
+ equipment or analyst queue
+ execution time
+ incubation, hold or instrument time
+ scheduled reading or collection window
+ data processing and calculation
+ technical review queue and review time
+ QA or disposition handoff
= end-to-end decision latency
```

Some components are active work. Others are elapsed time, queues or fixed biological
holds. They respond to different interventions.

Adding analysts may reduce a preparation queue. It does not shorten a compendial
incubation period. A rapid method may shorten detection time but still fail to
improve release if sample transport, batch formation or review remains the dominant
delay.

## Distinguish six kinds of time

| Time type | Meaning | Typical control |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Touch time | Active analyst or reviewer work | Standard work, staffing, automation |
| Equipment time | Resource occupied by a run or cycle | Quantity, scheduling, changeover, uptime |
| Biological/required hold | Incubation, equilibration or mandated wait | Approved method and conditions |
| Queue time | Work waits for a constrained resource | Arrival smoothing, priority, capacity, schedule |
| Handoff time | Work waits between owners or systems | Cutoffs, notifications, role coverage |
| Calendar time | Nights, weekends, holidays and unavailable shifts | Operating model, on-call and read windows |

Report these separately. A single “five-day method” label can hide hours or days of
avoidable waiting.

## Map arrival patterns, not only monthly totals

Monthly averages smooth the very peaks that create queues. Capture:

- production batch completion by day and shift;
- raw-material delivery and sampling windows;
- water and EM rounds with fixed collection times;
- stability pull dates and protocol windows;
- campaign or product-family clustering;
- weekend, holiday and shutdown effects;
- unexpected events, repeats and investigation samples;
- external-laboratory pickup and transport cutoffs.

Use timestamped history where available. If only forecasts exist, state whether
arrivals are level, scheduled batches, campaigns, fixed rounds or uncertain events.

## Define the deadline at the quality decision

“Five-day TAT” is ambiguous. Define:

- start event: collection, receipt, accession, test start or batch completion;
- end event: preliminary result, technically reviewed result, QA disposition or
  release decision;
- clock: calendar days, business days or operating hours;
- service class: routine, urgent, stability, investigation or release-critical;
- target percentile: average, 90th percentile, 95th percentile or maximum;
- pause rules: client hold, missing information or invalid sample;
- consequence and escalation when the target is threatened.

An average target can conceal a long tail. Release planning normally needs a defined
service level and exception rule.

## Build the workflow dependency graph

Turnaround is governed by the critical path. Map predecessor relationships such as:

```text
sample receipt
→ suitability-confirmed preparation
→ plating or filtration
→ incubation condition A
→ transfer or reading
→ incubation condition B where applicable
→ result calculation
→ technical review
→ disposition
```

Include shared prerequisites:

- released media and reagents;
- equipment readiness and environmental status;
- culture or standard preparation;
- analyst qualification;
- system access and data availability;
- sampling or transport completion;
- specification and method version.

The resource with the highest utilization is not always on the critical path. A
low-utilization reviewer or scheduled reading window can control the decision date.

## Put the method onto a real calendar

For every step, record:

- earliest start and latest acceptable finish;
- duration distribution or planning range;
- allowed operating days and shifts;
- required role and skill;
- equipment and room dependency;
- batch-size and batch-formation rule;
- setup, cleaning and changeover;
- fixed reading, transfer or hold window;
- predecessor and successor;
- priority and preemption rule;
- failure, repeat and escalation path.

Then test weekends and holidays explicitly. A 48-hour incubation that ends Saturday
morning may create a Monday read unless qualified coverage exists. Starting later
may change the read window and downstream release date even though total incubation
duration is unchanged.

## Batching creates both efficiency and delay

Batching samples can reduce setup, control and review effort. It also makes early
arrivals wait for a batch.

For each batched activity, define:

- minimum and maximum batch size;
- maximum wait-to-form-batch;
- scheduled start times;
- compatibility and segregation rules;
- shared controls and blank allocation;
- equipment sequence or load limit;
- urgent-work insertion rule;
- partial-batch decision authority.

Model the tradeoff between resource efficiency and decision latency. “Run once per
day” may be economical but incompatible with a same-day in-process decision.

## Model reviewer capacity independently

Review is a queue with its own arrivals, priorities and service times. Capture:

- review minutes by method/result type;
- first-pass versus exception review;
- reviewer authorization and skill coverage;
- shift, weekend and holiday availability;
- batch review or individual-result review;
- data-system and audit-trail dependencies;
- QA escalation and disposition windows;
- rework caused by incomplete records.

Adding execution capacity can worsen the review queue by releasing more results at
the same time. An FTE ratio does not prove that the right reviewer is available when
the result becomes ready.

## Include incubation reading and transfer windows

Microbiology schedules often have time-sensitive actions:

- plate reading within an approved window;
- sequential temperature incubation;
- growth-promotion observation;
- organism subculture or identification;
- result confirmation and investigation initiation;
- sample or culture disposal after approved retention.

Record the earliest, target and latest time for each action. Missing a window may
cause deviation, repeat work or invalid interpretation rather than a simple delay.

## Separate external-laboratory lead time

Outsourced testing includes:

```text
sample preparation and documentation
→ pickup cutoff
→ transport and customs where applicable
→ external receipt and accessioning
→ provider queue
→ testing and review
→ report issue
→ client technical review and disposition
```

The provider's advertised TAT may start at accession and end at report issue. The
client decision needs the complete chain. Include sample stability, rejected
shipments, investigation ownership, data access and backup-provider activation.

## Represent failures and non-routine demand

Routine schedules are fragile when they use all apparent capacity. Add scenarios
for:

- equipment breakdown or qualification hold;
- invalid run or failed controls;
- OOS/OOT or contamination investigation;
- media or reagent shortage;
- absent qualified analyst or reviewer;
- delayed sample transport;
- data-system outage;
- urgent production or regulatory request;
- method transfer or validation campaign.

Measure recovery time and backlog clearance, not only steady-state utilization.

## Use the right modeling depth

### Static duration map

Use when volumes are low, resources are dedicated and arrival timing is predictable.
It reveals the nominal critical path and calendar conflicts.

### Spreadsheet schedule

Use when activities, batches and resource calendars are finite and can be placed on
a planning horizon. It reveals overlaps, weekend reads and obvious contention.

### Queue or discrete-event simulation

Use when:

- arrivals and service times vary materially;
- several methods share analysts or equipment;
- priority classes compete;
- batching and setup affect flow;
- failures, repeats or downtime matter;
- the required service level is percentile-based;
- one resource change can move the bottleneck elsewhere.

Do not use a sophisticated simulation to hide poor input evidence. Calibrate arrival,
service, failure and queue distributions before trusting the result.

## Define operational metrics that expose the cause

Track more than total TAT:

- arrival-to-start queue time;
- touch time and elapsed processing time;
- incubation/hold time;
- equipment queue and utilization;
- reviewer queue and review time;
- percent meeting service target;
- 50th, 90th and 95th percentile TAT;
- backlog age by priority;
- missed read/transfer windows;
- first-pass completion and repeat rate;
- downtime recovery and backlog-clearance time;
- outsourced transport and provider latency.

These metrics show whether the remedy belongs in staffing, equipment, calendar,
method, batching, review or external-partner governance.

## Test scenarios before design freeze

At minimum, test:

1. routine forecast under the actual calendar;
2. peak production or sampling week;
3. weekend and holiday boundary;
4. critical resource downtime;
5. reviewer absence or review surge;
6. invalid run and repeat;
7. investigation workload;
8. future growth or new-product launch;
9. insource/outsource transition;
10. proposed rapid method or automation.

Compare not only mean TAT but service-level attainment, maximum queue, backlog and
recovery.

## Evidence required for a controlled schedule model

Collect:

- timestamped arrival and completion history;
- deadline definitions and priority classes;
- approved method steps and time windows;
- analyst, reviewer, equipment and room calendars;
- batch formation, setup and changeover rules;
- incubation, hold, reading and transfer windows;
- handoff cutoffs and escalation rules;
- downtime, invalid-run, repeat and investigation history;
- external-provider logistics and contractual TAT definitions;
- actual queue, backlog and service-level performance;
- owner, source period and data-quality assessment.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas can flag a target that is shorter than nominal workflow duration. It should
block any claim of schedule feasibility when:

- start/end events and clock basis are undefined;
- arrival patterns are replaced by monthly averages;
- batch and priority rules are missing;
- reviewer and handoff calendars are excluded;
- weekends, holidays and read windows are not modeled;
- downtime, repeats and investigations are absent;
- external-lab TAT excludes logistics or client review;
- service-level evidence is unavailable;
- simulation inputs have not been calibrated.
