# Microbial identification: why knowing your bug matters

A colony count tells you **how many**. Identification tells you **what** — and in
microbiology, the "what" is often the whole story. A recovery of a common skin
organism on a glove plate and a recovery of an objectionable Gram-negative in a
water system mean very different things, even at the same count.

## Why identification drives the investigation

Identification turns a number into intelligence:

- It tells you whether a recovery is an **objectionable organism** for your product
- It points at the **source** — a human-associated organism, a water organism, a
  facility/mould issue — which directs the investigation
- It builds your **facility flora baseline**, so a new organism stands out as a
  signal rather than blending into the noise

That is why action-limit recoveries in EM, and isolates from sterility and bioburden
investigations, are routinely identified rather than just counted.

## How far should you identify?

Not every isolate needs species-level identification. A **risk-based** approach
matches the depth of ID to the consequence:

- **Genus or species** for action-limit recoveries, sterility positives, and grade
  A/B environments
- Lighter characterisation where the count is low and the area is less critical

Over-identifying everything wastes effort; under-identifying the critical recoveries
leaves you blind in an investigation.

## The methods, briefly

- **Phenotypic** — Gram stain, morphology, and biochemical profiles (the classic
  approach)
- **Mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF)** — fast, accurate identification from a protein
  fingerprint, now widely used
- **Genotypic (gene sequencing, e.g. 16S)** — the reference approach for difficult
  or critical isolates

Whatever the method, it needs to be **suitable and verified** for the organisms you
expect to see.
