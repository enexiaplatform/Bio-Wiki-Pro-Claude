# Environmental monitoring: 5 gaps inspectors find

Environmental monitoring (EM) does not make product sterile — it provides
evidence that your contamination control strategy is working. Inspectors know
this, so they look less at single results and more at whether the program is
designed and run with intent. Here are the five gaps that draw findings most
often, and how to close them.

## 1. Sites chosen for convenience, not risk

The most common gap is a sample map built around where it is easy to reach,
rather than where contamination is most likely. Annex 1 expects site selection to
flow from a documented risk assessment — airflow, interventions, and the steps
where product is exposed.

**Close it:** justify every location against risk, and make sure your worst-case
points (not just the convenient ones) are monitored, especially during aseptic
interventions.

## 2. Trends ignored until the action limit

A run of results that all "pass" can still be drifting upward. Teams that only
react at the action limit miss the early signal — and inspectors read that as a
program that detects problems too late.

**Close it:** review per-site and aggregate trends, and open a trend
investigation when results drift, even while they are below the action limit.

## 3. The sampling intervention contaminates the sample

If taking the sample disturbs the aseptic field — or the plate is contaminated on
the way to the lab — you get a false positive that triggers a needless
investigation, or worse, masks a real signal.

**Close it:** train and qualify operators on the sampling technique itself, and
treat the intervention as part of the aseptic process, not separate from it.

## 4. Action-limit recoveries not identified

Recovering an organism at the action limit and not identifying it is a missed
opportunity — and a finding. Identification builds your facility flora baseline
and tells you whether a recovery is a human, a utility, or a facility issue.

**Close it:** identify action-limit recoveries to genus or species, and feed the
result back into your contamination control strategy.

## 5. Excursions investigated slowly, or never closed

Open excursions with no timeline, no product-impact assessment, and no effective
CAPA are a reliable way to attract attention. The investigation, not the
excursion itself, is what is judged.

**Close it:** define a timeline to open an investigation, assess product impact,
drive a CAPA, and verify it actually worked before closing.
