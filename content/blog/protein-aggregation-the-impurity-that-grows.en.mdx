# Protein aggregation: the impurity that grows on its own

Most impurities are introduced — a residual solvent, a host-cell protein, a leachable.
**Aggregates** are different: they are the product itself, sticking to copies of
itself. Two or more protein molecules associate into a larger species, and because the
process is driven by the molecule's own properties and its environment, aggregation can
**increase over time** even in a sealed vial. That makes it a product-related impurity
with a worrying habit of growing.

## Why aggregation matters so much

Aggregates are a headline concern for biologics for one reason above all:
**immunogenicity.** Aggregated protein can provoke an immune response in patients —
ranging from reduced efficacy (anti-drug antibodies neutralising the therapeutic) to,
rarely, serious safety reactions. So aggregation is not just a quality nicety; it sits
close to patient safety.

## SEC is the workhorse — and has blind spots

**Size-exclusion chromatography (SEC)** is the routine method: it separates molecules by
size, quantifying monomer, aggregates, and fragments. It is robust and quantitative,
which is why it anchors most release and stability testing.

But SEC has limits worth knowing:

- It can **under-report** very large or very insoluble aggregates that do not elute
  well
- The dilution and mobile phase can **dissociate** weak aggregates, hiding them
- It does not see **subvisible particles** in the larger size range

That is why orthogonal methods (e.g. analytical ultracentrifugation, field-flow
fractionation, light scattering, and subvisible particle counting) back up SEC for a
complete picture.

## A stability problem, not just a release one

Because aggregation can grow, it is a central **stability** attribute. A batch can
release with low aggregate and drift upward over shelf life if the formulation,
temperature, or handling are not right. Controlling it spans formulation design, the
process, and storage — not just the final test.
