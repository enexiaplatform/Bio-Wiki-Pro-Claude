# Batch release: the decision everything converges on

Every deviation, every test result, every in-process control, every CAPA — all of
it converges on a single decision: **release this batch, or not.** In the EU the
**Qualified Person (QP)** certifies it; elsewhere an authorised releaser does. Either
way, batch release is where the whole quality system has to add up.

## What gets reviewed

Before disposition, QA confirms:

- The **executed batch record** is complete, with good documentation practice and
  signatures — no blanks, no unexplained corrections
- **Critical processing steps and in-process controls** were within limits, and the
  **yield** reconciles
- All **required testing** is complete, within specification, on a released method
  and reference standard
- Any **deviations, OOS, and investigations** affecting the batch are closed with an
  impact assessment

## Why review-by-exception works

You cannot re-read every line of a large batch record on every batch. **Review-by-
exception** makes release tractable: focus on the deviations, OOS, audit-trail
exceptions, and out-of-limit results, trusting that the routine, in-control steps
are fine. But it only works if those exception systems are **trustworthy** — which is
why data integrity and a sound QMS underpin every release.

## Two non-negotiables

- **No release with an open, batch-impacting event.** A deviation or OOS that
  affects the batch must be closed first — not "released, to be closed later." If you
  cannot close it in time, the batch goes on hold.
- **The releaser is independent of production.** Disposition must be free of the
  schedule pressure that produced the batch. That independence is deliberate, and it
  is what makes the signature mean something.
