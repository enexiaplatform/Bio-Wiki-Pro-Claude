# Disinfectant qualification: proving the clean actually works

Cleaning a cleanroom feels self-evidently good — but spraying a disinfectant is not the
same as **disinfecting**. The only way to know your cleaning program actually reduces
contamination is to **qualify** the disinfectants: prove they kill the organisms you
care about, on the surfaces you have, in the time you give them. Annex 1 (2022) makes
this expectation explicit.

## Qualify against your reality

A disinfectant's marketing efficacy data is a starting point, not proof for your
facility. Qualification tests the disinfectant against:

- The **organisms you actually recover** — your environmental monitoring isolates, not
  just standard test strains
- The **surfaces in your rooms** — stainless steel, glass, vinyl, coated walls (a
  disinfectant can perform differently on different materials)
- The **contact time** your operators realistically achieve — efficacy claimed at a
  long wet-contact time is worthless if the surface dries in seconds

## Contact time is the quiet failure

The most common gap is **contact time**. Disinfectants need a defined wet-contact
period to work; if the surface dries before that, you have wetted it, not disinfected
it. Qualification establishes the required contact time, and the SOP and training have
to deliver it in practice.

## Rotation and the sporicide

Two more program elements:

- **Rotation** — alternating disinfectants (often a general disinfectant with a
  periodic stronger agent) is commonly used; the rationale should be justified, not
  ritual.
- **A sporicide** — routine disinfectants may not kill bacterial and fungal **spores**,
  so a periodic sporicidal agent is part of a complete program for higher-grade areas.

## Where it fits

Disinfectant qualification is one pillar of the contamination control strategy,
alongside gowning, air control, and environmental monitoring. EM, in turn, is how you
confirm the cleaning program is keeping the facility flora under control over time.
