# The bacterial endotoxin test: what LAL actually measures

The bacterial endotoxin test (BET) is one of the most misunderstood assays in the QC lab,
and the confusion starts with the name. It is not a test for bacteria. A sample can be
perfectly **sterile** — no living organism anywhere — and still **fail** the endotoxin
test. What the assay detects is a **molecule**: the lipopolysaccharide (LPS) that sits in
the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria and lingers long after the cell that made it
is dead.

## Why a molecule, not a microbe

Endotoxin is a pyrogen — injected into the bloodstream it can trigger fever, and at higher
doses, shock. Crucially, it is **heat-stable**: it survives the conditions that kill the
bacterium. So a parenteral product (or the water and components that go into it) has to be
controlled for endotoxin **separately** from sterility. Sterility asks "is anything alive?"
The endotoxin test asks "how much LPS is present?" — a completely different question with a
numeric limit.

## How LAL works

The reagent is **LAL** — Limulus Amebocyte Lysate, derived from the blood cells of the
horseshoe crab. (Recombinant Factor C, rFC, is the modern animal-free alternative that
targets the same trigger.) The horseshoe crab's blood clots in the presence of endotoxin
as a primitive immune defence, and the test harnesses exactly that cascade. Bring LAL
together with a sample and the amount of reaction is proportional to the amount of
endotoxin. That reaction is read in one of three formats:

- **Gel-clot** — the classic pass/fail. The mixture either forms a solid gel that holds
  when inverted, or it does not. Simple, robust, and the historical referee method.
- **Turbidimetric** — measures the cloudiness that develops as the clotting protein
  reacts; a kinetic curve gives a quantitative result.
- **Chromogenic** — uses a synthetic substrate that releases colour when the enzyme
  cascade fires; the colour intensity is read against a standard curve.

The kinetic (turbidimetric and chromogenic) methods give an actual number in endotoxin
units; gel-clot gives a result relative to a labelled sensitivity.

## The limit comes from the dose, not the product

An endotoxin limit is not an arbitrary house number. It is calculated as **K/M**, where K
is the threshold pyrogenic dose per kilogram of body weight and M is the maximum dose of
product per kilogram per hour. In other words, the more of a product a patient receives,
the **lower** the endotoxin concentration that product is allowed to contain. The limit is
driven by how the product is used — the same logic that makes objectionable-organism calls
product-specific.

## The trap: interference

Here is where labs get caught. The sample itself can **enhance** or, more often,
**inhibit** the LAL reaction — a chelating buffer, an extreme pH, or a protein can mask
endotoxin so the test under-reports. This is why the method is not valid until you have run
**inhibition/enhancement** testing: spike a known amount of endotoxin into the product and
prove the assay recovers it (typically within 50 to 200 percent). Until you have
established a non-interfering dilution (at or below the Maximum Valid Dilution), a "pass"
means nothing — the product may simply be hiding the endotoxin from the reagent.

## Where it fits

Endotoxin testing sits on every water-for-injection sample, on incoming components, and on
the final parenteral. It is the back-end check that depyrogenation actually worked, and one
of the last numbers that converges on a sterile batch-release decision.
