# Equipment qualification: what IQ, OQ, and PQ prove

Equipment qualification is often taught as four boxes: DQ, IQ, OQ, PQ. That is a
useful starting map, but it misses the real point.

Qualification is the documented evidence that equipment is suitable for its
intended GMP use, performs as required, and remains in a controlled state over its
lifecycle. The value is not the binder. The value is being able to say, with
evidence: **this equipment can do the job, in this process, within these limits,
and we know when that claim needs to be reassessed.**

## Start with intended use

Before any protocol is written, define what the equipment is for. A balance used
for non-GMP weighing, a calibrated production scale, an autoclave, an HPLC, a
granulator, a stopper washer, and a filling line do not need the same
qualification depth.

Good intended-use language covers:

- what the equipment does;
- where it is installed;
- which products, materials, or samples it touches;
- which process parameters it controls or measures;
- which GMP records it creates;
- which quality attributes it can affect;
- whether cleaning, sterilization, data integrity, or software control is in scope;
- which users and operating modes are expected.

If intended use is vague, every later qualification decision becomes vague too.

## URS: the anchor document

The user requirements specification, or URS, is where the user defines what the
equipment must do. It should not be a copy of the vendor brochure. It should state
the site's GMP, process, safety, cleaning, maintenance, calibration, data, and
documentation needs.

A strong URS includes:

- process capacity and operating ranges;
- critical process parameters and control needs;
- product-contact materials;
- cleaning and sanitation expectations;
- utility requirements;
- alarms, interlocks, and safety functions;
- data and audit trail requirements where computerized controls exist;
- calibration and maintenance access;
- documentation and spare parts expectations;
- acceptance criteria or traceable requirements.

The URS gives DQ, IQ, OQ, and PQ something objective to test. A weak URS forces
the qualification team to invent acceptance criteria late, usually when the
equipment is already installed and expensive to change.

## Risk decides depth

Not every function deserves equal testing. Qualification should be risk-based.

Ask:

- Can this function affect product quality, patient safety, sterility, strength,
  purity, identity, or data integrity?
- Could failure be detected before batch release?
- Is the function controlled elsewhere?
- Is the vendor evidence reliable and relevant?
- Is the system new, modified, or well understood?

This is where quality risk management becomes practical. A non-critical display
setting may need only verification. A temperature control loop that determines
sterilization exposure needs deep challenge testing, calibrated instruments, and
clear deviation rules.

## DQ: does the design meet the need?

Design qualification asks whether the proposed design can meet the URS and GMP
needs before the site commits too far.

DQ should verify:

- user requirements are complete enough to buy against;
- vendor proposal or design matches the URS;
- critical design features are identified;
- product-contact materials are suitable;
- cleaning, maintenance, calibration, and access are feasible;
- software and data requirements are addressed;
- risk assessment drives qualification scope;
- gaps are resolved before procurement or installation.

DQ is where many future problems are cheapest to fix. If the drain location,
surface finish, software audit trail, range, access panel, or utility demand is
wrong, finding out during PQ is painful.

## IQ: is it installed correctly?

Installation qualification verifies that the equipment, utilities, documentation,
and supporting systems are installed as specified.

IQ commonly checks:

- equipment identity, model, serial number, and configuration;
- installation location and layout;
- utilities and connection points;
- product-contact materials;
- manuals, drawings, certificates, and spare parts;
- critical instruments and calibration status;
- software or firmware versions;
- safety features and guards;
- cleaning access;
- preventive maintenance requirements;
- as-built differences from design.

IQ is often described as static, but it matters deeply. If the wrong probe is
installed, a utility is undersized, or a critical instrument is not calibrated, OQ
may produce attractive data on an equipment state that is not actually approved.

## OQ: does it operate across its range?

Operational qualification challenges whether the equipment works across its
intended operating ranges and functions.

OQ should include:

- normal operating ranges;
- upper and lower setpoint challenges;
- alarms and interlocks;
- control-loop behavior;
- start-up and shutdown behavior;
- emergency stop and recovery where relevant;
- recipe or parameter security;
- data capture and printouts;
- fault conditions;
- cleaning or sterilization functions where applicable;
- repeatability of critical functions.

OQ is where edge behavior matters. A mixer that works at the nominal speed but
fails at the low-speed hold point has not been qualified for the process range. A
freezer that controls well empty but alarms poorly during recovery may not protect
materials. A filling machine that rejects correctly in normal operation but fails
reject-bin reconciliation under fault conditions has a release risk.

## PQ: does it perform in the real process?

Performance qualification demonstrates that the equipment performs consistently
under actual or simulated routine conditions.

PQ should reflect:

- trained operators;
- approved SOPs;
- representative product, placebo, or load where justified;
- routine setpoints and ranges;
- expected batch size or load pattern;
- worst-case or bracketing rationale;
- in-process controls;
- cleaning or changeover conditions where relevant;
- repeated runs or cycles according to risk and protocol.

OQ proves the equipment can operate. PQ proves it can support the intended process
in a repeatable way.

PQ should not be used to discover basic installation or operating problems. If PQ
becomes troubleshooting, the qualification package probably moved forward too
fast.

## Traceability makes the package defensible

Auditors like to pick one requirement and follow it. For example:

1. URS says the oven must maintain 250 degrees C +/- defined tolerance.
2. DQ shows the selected oven design is capable.
3. IQ verifies the correct oven, probes, utilities, and calibration.
4. OQ challenges the range, alarms, and temperature distribution.
5. PQ shows the routine load achieves the required performance.
6. Calibration and maintenance keep that claim current.

That thread is the qualification story. Without traceability, the package becomes
a pile of documents rather than evidence.

## Vendor documentation can help, but it does not replace ownership

Vendor FAT, SAT, certificates, and commissioning data can reduce duplication. They
do not transfer GMP responsibility away from the site.

Before using vendor evidence, assess:

- whether the test matches the site's URS;
- whether instruments were calibrated;
- whether raw data and deviations are available;
- whether the configuration tested matches the delivered equipment;
- whether the vendor test environment is relevant;
- whether QA approved use of the evidence.

Vendor documents are strongest when they are mapped into the site's qualification
matrix and reviewed for gaps.

## Calibration, maintenance, and cleaning keep the state alive

A qualification report is a point-in-time decision. The qualified state survives
only if the equipment is maintained.

The ongoing controls include:

- calibration schedule for critical instruments;
- preventive maintenance;
- cleaning and sanitation procedures;
- spare-part control;
- logbooks or electronic equipment records;
- alarm and breakdown review;
- deviation and repair impact assessment;
- periodic review;
- change control;
- requalification triggers.

This is where 21 CFR Part 211 equipment expectations become practical: equipment
must be suitable for its intended use, maintained and cleaned appropriately, and
automatic or electronic equipment must be calibrated, inspected, or checked under
a written program where used.

## Requalification is not only a calendar event

Periodic review is useful, but requalification should also be event-driven.

Assess requalification after:

- equipment relocation;
- major repair or replacement;
- software or firmware change;
- control-loop change;
- utility change;
- new product or process range;
- cleaning method change;
- recurring deviations or drift;
- out-of-tolerance calibration result;
- change in load pattern, batch size, or operating mode;
- long shutdown or storage period.

The right question is not "do we repeat everything?" It is "which qualification
claims may no longer be supported?" A moved balance may need IQ and calibration. A
new autoclave load pattern may need OQ/PQ elements. A software recipe change may
need data integrity and functional challenge testing.

## Qualification and validation connect

Equipment qualification is not isolated from process validation. Process
validation assumes equipment, utilities, methods, and computerized systems are
fit for use.

For example:

- PPQ cannot support a process if critical equipment is not qualified.
- Cleaning validation depends on equipment design, cleanability, and product
  contact surfaces.
- Sterilization validation depends on qualified chambers, probes, utilities, and
  load patterns.
- Analytical method results depend on qualified instruments and calibrated
  measuring devices.
- Batch release depends on equipment status at the time the batch was made.

When qualification is weak, every downstream validation and release decision gets
weaker.

## Common audit weaknesses

Equipment qualification findings are often boring in the most dangerous way:

- URS written after the equipment was bought;
- DQ skipped or reduced to vendor brochure approval;
- risk assessment not linked to protocol scope;
- IQ missing calibration status for critical instruments;
- OQ tests only nominal settings, not ranges or alarms;
- PQ uses ideal conditions that do not match routine production;
- deviations closed without impact on qualification conclusions;
- vendor data accepted without gap assessment;
- maintenance or calibration lapsed after release;
- changes made without requalification assessment;
- no periodic review of equipment performance;
- equipment logbooks and batch records do not reconcile.

The finding rarely says "no IQ/OQ/PQ binder exists." It says the binder does not
prove the current equipment state.

## Practical review checklist

Use this checklist before QA releases equipment for GMP use:

1. Intended use is clear and approved.
2. URS requirements are testable.
3. Risk assessment identifies quality-critical functions.
4. DQ shows design suitability and closes gaps.
5. IQ verifies installation, utilities, documentation, and calibration.
6. OQ challenges ranges, alarms, interlocks, and critical functions.
7. PQ reflects routine or justified worst-case operating conditions.
8. Deviations are investigated and linked to qualification conclusions.
9. Traceability matrix maps URS to evidence.
10. Calibration, maintenance, cleaning, and requalification controls are active.

## Key takeaway

DQ, IQ, OQ, and PQ are not ritual labels. They are a chain of evidence.

DQ asks whether the design can meet the need. IQ asks whether the equipment was
installed correctly. OQ asks whether it operates across the required range. PQ asks
whether it performs in the intended process. The qualified state then depends on
calibration, maintenance, change control, periodic review, and requalification
when risk changes.

The best qualification package is not the thickest. It is the one where every
critical requirement can be traced to a meaningful test and kept true after the
equipment enters routine use.
