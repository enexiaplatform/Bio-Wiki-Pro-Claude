# Equipment qualification: what IQ, OQ, and PQ prove

Qualification is the equipment-level subset of validation, and the V-model keeps
every check tied back to a requirement. The four stages are not bureaucracy for
its own sake — each one answers a specific question.

## DQ — does the design meet the requirement?

Design qualification starts from a user requirements specification (URS). Before
you buy or install, you confirm the proposed design can actually do what you need,
and you risk-assess the aspects that affect product quality. Skip this and the
later stages have nothing to verify against.

## IQ — is it installed correctly?

Installation qualification verifies the equipment arrived and was installed per
specification: the right components, utilities connected correctly, documentation
present, and critical instruments calibrated. It is a static check — nothing is
running yet.

## OQ — does it operate across its range?

Operational qualification challenges the operating ranges and functions against
acceptance criteria — including alarms, interlocks, and worst-case settings. This
is where you prove the equipment behaves correctly at the edges, not just at the
comfortable nominal setpoint.

## PQ — does it perform under real conditions?

Performance qualification demonstrates consistent performance under actual or
simulated production conditions, over a defined number of runs. OQ proves it
*can* work; PQ proves it *does* work, repeatedly, in your process.

## The often-missed half: staying qualified

A qualified label is only true on the day it was signed unless you maintain it.
Calibration, preventive maintenance, periodic review, and change control are what
keep the equipment in a qualified state. The most common finding is not a botched
OQ — it is a requalification or calibration that quietly lapsed.
