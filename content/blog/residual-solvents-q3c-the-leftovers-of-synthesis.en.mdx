# Residual solvents (ICH Q3C): the leftovers of synthesis

Solvents make pharmaceutical chemistry possible. They dissolve, extract,
crystallize, wash, purify, and sometimes participate in the process itself. But
practical manufacturing does not remove every molecule. The volatile organic
chemicals left behind are **residual solvents**.

Residual solvents are not controlled because they are visible, active, or usually
the largest impurity in a batch. They are controlled because patient exposure can
matter. Some solvents are carcinogenic or environmentally hazardous. Some have
specific organ toxicity. Some are low enough in toxicity that broad limits are
reasonable. ICH Q3C(R9) gives the framework for deciding which is which.

The useful way to remember Q3C is this: residual solvent control is not a generic
"pass GC" exercise. It is a toxicology-based exposure control system.

## What is in scope

Q3C covers residual organic volatile chemicals used or produced in the manufacture
of:

- drug substances;
- excipients;
- drug products.

It is not limited to the final synthesis step. Solvents can enter through starting
materials, reagents, crystallization, extraction, purification, granulation,
coating, cleaning carryover, excipients, or packaging-related processes. If a
solvent can reasonably remain in the material given the process, it belongs in the
risk assessment.

The guideline also makes a development point: choose less toxic solvents whenever
practical. The best residual solvent control is often solvent selection, not a
more heroic drying step at the end.

## The three solvent classes

ICH Q3C groups solvents by toxicological risk.

**Class 1 solvents should be avoided.** These include known human carcinogens,
strongly suspected human carcinogens, and environmental hazards. Benzene is the
example everyone remembers. If a Class 1 solvent is unavoidable, its presence must
be justified and tightly controlled.

**Class 2 solvents should be limited.** These solvents have inherent toxicity but
may be acceptable below a permitted daily exposure. Methanol, acetonitrile,
dichloromethane, toluene, NMP, and many other commonly discussed solvents sit in
this risk space.

**Class 3 solvents have low toxic potential.** They are generally controlled with
a less restrictive default approach when used within reasonable levels. Ethanol,
acetone, ethyl acetate, and isopropyl alcohol are familiar examples, though the
actual solvent list should always be checked against the current guideline.

This class system is not a lab convenience. It is a prioritization system. Class 1
demands avoidance and justification. Class 2 demands exposure calculation and
control. Class 3 usually demands confirmation that use and removal are reasonable.

## PDE is the anchor

For Class 2 solvents, the key number is the **permitted daily exposure**, or PDE.
The PDE is the amount of a solvent considered acceptable for a patient to receive
per day, based on toxicological evaluation and uncertainty factors.

That means the concentration limit in a product depends on dose. A solvent level
that is acceptable in a 10 mg daily-dose product may be unacceptable in a 10 g
daily-dose product because the patient exposure is very different.

This is the calculation logic:

```text
concentration limit = PDE / maximum daily dose
```

The units must be handled carefully. In practice, Q3C provides concentration
limits and options to simplify control, but the exposure principle is the reason
those limits exist.

## Option 1 versus Option 2

Q3C provides two main ways to apply limits.

**Option 1** uses fixed concentration limits for Class 2 solvents based on an
assumed daily intake of 10 g of product. It is simple and conservative for many
products. If every component and the final product meet Option 1 limits, the
residual solvent control story is usually straightforward.

**Option 2** uses the actual maximum daily dose to calculate a product-specific
concentration limit. This can be useful when Option 1 is unnecessarily strict or
when the dosage is low enough that a higher concentration still stays below the
PDE.

Option 2 is not a loophole. It needs the maximum daily dose, the solvent PDE, the
calculation, and a clear specification rationale. If multiple components
contribute the same solvent, the total daily exposure still matters.

## Component contributions matter

Residual solvents may come from more than the drug substance. Excipients,
coatings, capsules, inks, processing aids, and intermediates may contribute.

For a finished drug product, the risk assessment should ask:

- Which solvents are used or produced in the drug substance process?
- Which solvents may be present in excipients?
- Which solvents are introduced in drug product manufacturing?
- Are any Class 1 solvents used upstream or listed in supplier statements?
- Do multiple components contribute the same Class 2 solvent?
- Does the maximum daily dose make the Option 1 limit conservative enough?
- Are supplier specifications and certificates scientifically meaningful?

The finished-product control strategy may rely on supplier data, process
knowledge, skip testing, or final product testing, but the rationale should be
visible.

## GC headspace is the workhorse

Residual solvents are volatile, so the most common analytical approach is gas
chromatography, often with headspace sampling. The exact detector, column,
headspace conditions, diluent, temperature program, and sample preparation depend
on the solvent set and matrix.

A good method is not just "a GC run." It should be able to separate and quantify
the relevant solvents at or below the required limits in the actual sample matrix.

For method validation or verification, pay attention to:

- specificity and peak separation;
- limit of detection and limit of quantitation;
- accuracy and recovery in the matrix;
- precision at relevant levels;
- linearity across the intended range;
- robustness of headspace conditions;
- carryover between injections;
- standard preparation and volatility losses;
- sample equilibration;
- system suitability;
- data integrity for integration and manual changes.

Class 1 solvents deserve special care because the limits are tight and the
consequence of a false negative is serious.

## Testing is not the whole control strategy

A residual solvent control strategy usually has four layers.

First, **solvent selection**. Avoid Class 1 solvents and prefer lower-toxicity
alternatives where feasible.

Second, **process design**. Choose reaction workups, crystallization, washing,
drying, and hold conditions that reliably remove or reduce solvents.

Third, **process understanding**. Know which solvents are likely to remain, which
steps remove them, and which parameters matter: temperature, vacuum, time, batch
size, wet cake thickness, agitation, airflow, purge gas, and endpoint criteria.

Fourth, **analytical confirmation**. Use testing, supplier data, or justified
periodic verification to show the control strategy remains true.

If a solvent is routinely close to its limit, the solution is rarely "test harder."
It is usually process development, drying optimization, supplier control, or a
specification/risk reassessment.

## When reduced testing can make sense

Routine testing may be reduced when process knowledge and data show a solvent is
consistently absent or well below the limit. But reduced testing should be earned.

A defensible reduced-testing rationale includes:

- solvent use map across the process;
- historical batch data;
- method capability;
- validated or verified drying process;
- supplier qualification for excipients or intermediates;
- change-control triggers;
- periodic verification plan;
- clear rule for reinstating routine testing after deviations or changes.

Skipping a solvent because "it has always passed" is weak. Skipping it because the
process cannot introduce it, validated purge data support clearance, and supplier
controls are monitored is much stronger.

## Specifications and CoA wording

Residual solvent specifications should match the control strategy. For a drug
substance, specs may list each relevant Class 1 and Class 2 solvent and any
process-specific solvents. For a drug product, specs may be risk-based and may rely
partly on component controls.

Common CoA wording issues include:

- "complies with ICH Q3C" with no solvent list;
- Class 1 solvents not stated;
- Class 2 solvent limits not tied to Option 1 or Option 2;
- no daily-dose rationale for product-specific limits;
- no clarity whether testing was performed on the batch or statement is based on
  process knowledge;
- supplier reports Class 3 only by loss on drying when a specific solvent risk
  exists.

For audit readiness, the site should be able to explain exactly which solvents are
controlled, why those solvents are relevant, and how each limit was set.

## Changes that should trigger reassessment

Residual solvent risk can change quietly. Reassess after:

- solvent, reagent, or supplier changes;
- route-of-synthesis changes;
- crystallization or purification changes;
- drying equipment or cycle changes;
- batch size scale-up;
- new excipient source;
- change in maximum daily dose;
- formulation or coating change;
- site transfer;
- repeated OOS/OOT solvent trends;
- new toxicology or Q3C maintenance update.

Q3C is maintained over time because solvent toxicology data can change. A
compliant strategy should therefore be able to absorb guideline updates without
panic: identify impacted solvents, compare old and new PDEs, assess batches and
specifications, and update methods or controls where needed.

## Common audit weaknesses

Residual solvent findings often come from missing rationale, not missing GC data.
Watch for:

- solvent list copied from a generic method rather than the actual process;
- Class 1 solvent risk not assessed for starting materials or supplier processes;
- Option 2 limits calculated without maximum daily dose evidence;
- multiple component contributions ignored;
- Class 3 solvents dismissed even when high levels could affect quality;
- validated method does not cover the solvents in the process;
- peak integration changes not reviewed;
- reduced testing with no periodic verification;
- process change not linked to residual solvent reassessment;
- CoA says "not detected" without an LOQ or method reference.

The audit question is simple: can the site trace each solvent from process
knowledge to limit, method, specification, and batch decision?

## Practical review checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing a residual solvent strategy:

1. List every solvent used or potentially produced in the drug substance,
   excipient, and drug product process.
2. Classify each solvent using the current ICH Q3C revision.
3. Identify Class 1 solvents and document avoidance or justification.
4. For Class 2 solvents, document PDE and whether Option 1 or Option 2 is used.
5. If Option 2 is used, verify maximum daily dose and calculation.
6. Consider total contribution from multiple components.
7. Confirm the analytical method covers relevant solvents at suitable sensitivity.
8. Review process capability and drying controls.
9. Define reduced-testing and periodic-verification logic, if used.
10. Set change-control triggers for process, supplier, dose, and guideline updates.

## Key takeaway

Residual solvents are not just leftovers. They are patient exposure risks created
by manufacturing choices. ICH Q3C(R9) gives a practical framework: avoid the worst
solvents, limit the toxic ones by PDE, justify the low-risk ones, and make the
analytical method match the real process.

The strongest control strategy starts before the GC vial: solvent selection,
process design, supplier control, and dose-based specifications do most of the
quality work. Testing confirms that the story is still true.
