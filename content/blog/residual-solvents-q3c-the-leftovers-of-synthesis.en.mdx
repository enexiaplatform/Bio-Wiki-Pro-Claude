# Residual solvents (ICH Q3C): the leftovers of synthesis

Making a drug substance takes solvents — to dissolve, react, crystallise, and purify.
Not all of that solvent leaves the product. The traces left behind are **residual
solvents**, and because some solvents are genuinely toxic, ICH Q3C sets out how to
classify, limit, and control them.

## Classified by hazard, not by habit

Q3C groups solvents by toxicological risk, which tells you how hard to work to remove
them:

- **Class 1** — solvents to **avoid** (known or strongly suspected human toxicity /
  carcinogens, e.g. benzene). Used only when unavoidable, with very tight limits.
- **Class 2** — solvents to **limit**, each with a defined Permitted Daily Exposure
  (PDE) because of less severe but real toxicity.
- **Class 3** — solvents of **low toxicity**, with a generous default limit; usually
  controlled by loss-on-drying rather than specific testing.

The framework nudges development toward **less hazardous** solvent choices, which is the
point — the cheapest way to control a Class 1 solvent is to not use it.

## Limits come from exposure, not tradition

Like elemental impurities, Q3C limits are **PDE-based** — derived from how much a
patient can safely take in per day — and can be expressed as a concentration once you
account for the maximum daily dose. So the same solvent can have a different acceptable
concentration in a high-dose versus low-dose product.

## How they're measured and controlled

Residual solvents are volatile, so the workhorse method is **gas chromatography (GC)**,
often with headspace sampling. But the real control is the **process**: drying,
purification, and solvent selection do most of the work, and testing confirms it. A
process that consistently clears a solvent well below its limit may justify reduced
routine testing.
