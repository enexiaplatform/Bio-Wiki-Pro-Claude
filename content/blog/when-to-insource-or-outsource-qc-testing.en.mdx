# When to insource or outsource QC testing

The cheapest quoted test is not always the lowest-cost operating model. The most
frequent test does not automatically belong in-house. A defensible make-or-buy
decision connects the quality decision, demand pattern, method-control needs,
turnaround, continuity and full lifecycle responsibility.

```text
test application and decision consequence
→ demand, timing and evidence needs
→ internal and external capability options
→ risk, control and lifecycle-cost comparison
→ approved operating model and transition plan
→ performance review and re-evaluation triggers
```

Atlas can structure the comparison. It does not qualify a contract laboratory,
approve a method transfer, negotiate a quality agreement or delegate the product
owner's regulatory responsibilities.

## Decide at the application level

Do not classify an entire technology as in-house or outsourced. One laboratory may
retain routine product testing internally, outsource specialist confirmation and use
a second provider only for continuity.

Create a record for each product/matrix × method × decision combination:

- release, in-process, stability, monitoring, characterization or investigation use;
- applicable markets, registration and specification basis;
- sample type, hazard, stability and transport constraints;
- routine volume, peak arrivals and forecast confidence;
- required result and review time;
- method ownership, transfer and lifecycle status;
- raw data, records, samples and retained-material needs;
- consequence of invalid, delayed or unavailable testing;
- internal and provider capability status;
- final disposition and approval owner.

The same assay can require different sourcing decisions for release and development
because the timing, evidence and governance differ.

## Screen non-negotiable constraints first

Before cost comparison, identify constraints that can eliminate an option:

- registration or approved-method location requirements;
- sample stability or maximum transport time;
- biosafety, dangerous goods or import/export restrictions;
- data-residency, confidentiality or intellectual-property limits;
- unavailable qualified specialist, platform or reference material;
- required independent or orthogonal testing;
- local release-time commitment;
- inability to audit, transfer or supervise the work;
- provider subcontracting that cannot be controlled;
- facility or utility conditions that cannot be delivered internally.

Record the source and reviewer. An assumed constraint should be resolved before it
drives a major capital decision.

## Compare six decision dimensions

### 1. Technical and quality capability

Assess method experience, matrix suitability, validation/verification status,
equipment configuration, standards and reagents, analyst qualification, data
systems, investigations, change control and inspection history relevant to the
actual application.

### 2. Demand and capacity

Model routine, peak, launch, stability, repeat and investigation demand. For an
internal option include analyst, instrument, review, incubation/storage and
maintenance capacity. For an external option include provider slots, batching,
queue, shipping calendars and surge commitments.

### 3. Turnaround and decision timing

Separate collection, packaging, shipment, customs, accession, queue, execution,
review, query resolution, report transfer and internal disposition. A quoted
laboratory turnaround may start after accession and exclude shipping or sponsor
review.

### 4. Control, knowledge and data

Compare access to raw data, metadata, audit trails, samples, observations,
investigators and method knowledge. Consider how quickly a product, process or
contamination signal can be connected across other site evidence.

### 5. Continuity and concentration risk

Assess single analysts, instruments, utilities, sites, transport routes, customs,
reagents, proprietary software and subcontractors. An outsourced option is not
automatically resilient; several providers may depend on the same platform or
supply chain.

### 6. Lifecycle economics

Compare costs over the approved horizon and demand scenarios, not only price per
reportable result.

## Build a comparable cost model

### Internal option

Include:

- equipment, accessories, facility and utility implementation;
- qualification, computer-system validation and method transfer;
- standards, reagents, media, cultures and consumables;
- staffing, training, supervision and technical review;
- calibration, service, software and data retention;
- invalid, repeat, investigation and downtime demand;
- waste, safety and controlled storage;
- backup capacity and obsolescence;
- residual value and decommissioning.

### External option

Include:

- method transfer, verification/validation and onboarding;
- audit, qualification and quality-agreement work;
- per-test, minimum-volume, rush and cancellation charges;
- sample kits, packaging, courier, customs and temperature control;
- standards, method-specific materials and retained samples;
- data transfer, portal, archive and report review;
- deviations, invalid tests, repeats and investigation support;
- change requests, annual review and requalification;
- internal contract management and QA oversight;
- alternate-provider or emergency-transfer readiness.

Keep fixed, variable, step-change and one-time costs separate. Test low, expected,
peak and growth demand. Internal cost per test can rise sharply at low utilization;
external cost and turnaround can deteriorate at high or volatile demand.

## Evaluate a hybrid operating model

Common patterns include:

- routine internal testing with specialist confirmation outsourced;
- external testing during startup, followed by controlled internal transfer;
- internal release testing with an external continuity provider;
- internal sample preparation with specialized instrumental analysis external;
- central network laboratory with site-local time-critical tests;
- dual-source external providers for high-consequence methods.

Every split creates interfaces. Define sample custody, method version, standards,
data review, deviations, changes, final approval and investigation leadership.

## Govern outsourced work as part of the quality system

ICH Q10 states that the pharmaceutical quality system extends to control and review
of outsourced activities, including prior assessment of suitability and competence,
defined responsibilities and communication, and ongoing performance monitoring. See
the official [ICH Q10 Pharmaceutical Quality System](https://database.ich.org/sites/default/files/Q10_Guideline.pdf).

FDA's official [Quality Agreements guidance](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/contract-manufacturing-arrangements-drugs-quality-agreements-guidance-industry)
describes written agreements that delineate manufacturing activities and CGMP roles;
it also makes clear that such agreements do not remove statutory or regulatory
responsibilities.

For a testing relationship, define at least:

- method, specification and version ownership;
- sample receipt, acceptance, custody and retention;
- standards, reagents and critical material responsibilities;
- execution, calculations, second-person review and report approval;
- raw data, metadata, audit-trail and record access;
- deviations, OOS/OOT, invalid tests and investigation leadership;
- retest/resample authorization;
- change notification and approval;
- subcontracting restrictions;
- inspection, audit and regulatory communication;
- data retention, return and exit/transfer assistance;
- performance review, escalation and termination.

## Use performance measures that expose decision risk

Monitor:

- samples and reports delivered on time;
- end-to-end and laboratory turnaround distributions;
- accession rejection and transport excursions;
- invalid, repeat and amended-report rates;
- investigation response and closure time;
- right-first-time data package completeness;
- deviations and changes notified within agreement;
- audit and CAPA commitments overdue;
- capacity reservation and surge performance;
- method/version discrepancies;
- continuity events and recovery time;
- cost per accepted decision package, not only per test.

Trend by method and application. A portfolio average can hide a failing release test.

## Define re-evaluation and transition triggers

Revisit the model when:

- demand crosses a controlled volume or utilization threshold;
- turnaround threatens release or stability decisions;
- a new product, market, matrix or method is introduced;
- transfer, deviation or repeat burden exceeds the planned basis;
- provider capacity, ownership, site, subcontractor or quality status changes;
- logistics or geopolitical risk changes;
- critical data or method knowledge must be internalized;
- an internal asset becomes obsolete or underused;
- actual lifecycle cost differs materially from the approved case;
- continuity evidence no longer supports the selected option.

An insourcing transition also requires equipment, facility, people, method transfer,
comparability, records, inventory and regulatory-impact planning. It is not complete
when the instrument arrives.

## Evidence package for approval

Before approving the operating model, collect:

1. application and decision scope;
2. non-negotiable constraints and sources;
3. demand, peak and turnaround scenarios;
4. internal capability and capacity evidence;
5. provider capability, audit and method evidence;
6. end-to-end workflow and responsibility map;
7. fixed, variable, transition and lifecycle cost comparison;
8. continuity and concentration-risk assessment;
9. data, records, sample and investigation controls;
10. implementation or transfer plan;
11. performance measures and governance cadence;
12. re-evaluation triggers, residual risks and approvals.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas should not recommend an option when:

- the method application and decision consequence are unclear;
- sample transport or stability feasibility is assumed;
- capacity is compared using average demand only;
- quoted turnaround excludes material handoffs;
- internal cost omits qualification, people, downtime or lifecycle support;
- external cost omits oversight, logistics, repeats or transfer;
- raw data, investigation and method-change responsibilities are unresolved;
- one provider or internal resource creates unreviewed concentration risk;
- the recommendation depends only on a weighted score with critical gaps;
- no transition, performance review or exit trigger exists.
