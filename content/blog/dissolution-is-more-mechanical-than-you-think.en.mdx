# Dissolution: it's more mechanical than you think

Dissolution testing looks like chemistry. A tablet or capsule goes into a vessel,
the medium is held at temperature, the apparatus rotates, samples are taken, and
an assay gives percent dissolved. But in practice, a large part of dissolution
quality is mechanical.

Hydrodynamics, medium preparation, vessel geometry, shaft alignment, wobble,
temperature control, sampling timing, filtration, and analyst technique can all
move the result. That is why a dissolution failure should not immediately be
treated as "the product failed." It may be a true product problem, but the system
has to be checked first.

Dissolution is a performance test. For many oral solid dosage forms, it supports
release, stability, formulation development, post-approval change assessment, and
sometimes bioequivalence reasoning. A weak setup can create false OOS results,
hide true product drift, or make a method look less discriminatory than it really
is.

## What dissolution is trying to prove

At its simplest, dissolution measures how much drug substance is released from a
dosage form into solution over time under defined conditions. For immediate-
release oral solids, FDA describes dissolution testing as a tool for assessing
lot-to-lot quality, guiding formulation development, supporting continuous
product quality after certain manufacturing changes, and, in some cases,
supporting biowaiver decisions.

That is a lot of responsibility for a test that is highly sensitive to physical
setup. The result is only meaningful when the method is performed exactly as
validated or as described in the applicable monograph.

A dissolution result depends on:

- the dosage form and formulation;
- apparatus type and geometry;
- medium composition, volume, pH, and deaeration;
- temperature;
- agitation speed;
- sampling position and timing;
- filtration and sample handling;
- assay method;
- acceptance criteria and the product's Q value.

If any of these drift, the dissolution profile may drift too.

## The mechanical variables that bite

Before blaming the tablet, check the quiet mechanical variables.

**Medium preparation:** Wrong pH, wrong buffer concentration, wrong surfactant
level, incorrect volume, poor deaeration, or inadequate equilibration can shift
release. Dissolved gas can form bubbles on the dosage form, basket, paddle,
vessel, or sampling cannula. Those bubbles change wetting and flow.

**Temperature:** The usual target for many oral dosage forms is 37 degrees C, and
USP &lt;711&gt; specifies tight temperature control during the test. A small
temperature error can change solubility, viscosity, and release rate.

**Vessel and shaft geometry:** Vessel centering, shaft verticality, paddle or
basket height, wobble, and vessel shape affect hydrodynamics. Two vessels can
look similar but behave differently if they are out of mechanical qualification.

**Basket or paddle condition:** Bent shafts, damaged baskets, residue, corrosion,
loose clips, or worn components can change the flow pattern or trap material.

**Coning:** Under paddles, undissolved material can mound at the vessel bottom.
That cone can reduce exposure to fresh medium and slow dissolution. Coning is a
method-development and investigation signal, not just an observation to ignore.

**Sampling:** Wrong sampling height, late sampling, early sampling, slow sampling,
disturbing the vessel, or inconsistent replacement medium can change the reported
profile. At short time points, seconds matter.

**Filtration:** An unsuitable filter can adsorb drug, allow undissolved particles
through, or keep releasing retained material. Filter suitability is part of the
method, not an accessory choice.

These variables are why apparatus qualification and analyst discipline matter.
Dissolution is not just "drop and read."

## USP staged acceptance is not retesting until it passes

For immediate-release conventional dosage forms, USP &lt;711&gt; uses staged
acceptance: S1, then S2, then S3 where required. The exact criteria depend on the
product's Q value and the applicable table.

In the common immediate-release staged logic:

| Stage | Units | Practical meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| S1 | 6 units | A tight first-stage screen. Each unit must be comfortably above Q. |
| S2 | 12 units total | Adds 6 more units and evaluates the 12-unit average plus individual-unit floor. |
| S3 | 24 units total | Adds 12 more units and applies the final average and low-unit controls. |

The important point is mindset. Staged acceptance is a predefined compendial
decision scheme. It is not a loophole to keep testing until the batch looks good.

Good documentation shows:

- the Q value used and its source;
- the stage entered;
- the exact unit results included at each stage;
- the reason for moving from S1 to S2 or from S2 to S3;
- whether the final result meets the applicable criteria;
- whether any investigation was triggered by unusual variability or a failure.

to sanity-check staged calculations, then document the result under your approved
SOP and monograph.

## A failing unit is a signal, not a conclusion

When a single unit is low at S1, the next step may be staged testing. But the low
unit is still a signal. The lab should notice the pattern.

Ask:

- Was it one isolated unit or several low units?
- Did the low unit show coning, sticking, floating, capping, or incomplete
  disintegration?
- Was the vessel position associated with previous variability?
- Was there a timing or sampling note?
- Was the medium clear, correctly prepared, and degassed?
- Was the apparatus qualification current?
- Did the analyst observe anything abnormal?

A result may technically proceed through S2 or S3, but a recurring pattern can
still require investigation or method improvement. Passing a staged table does
not erase a signal of method weakness.

## Method setup checklist before testing

Before starting the run, confirm the basic controls:

- Correct product, strength, lot, method, and specification.
- Correct apparatus: basket, paddle, or other specified apparatus.
- Correct vessel volume and medium composition.
- Medium pH and temperature verified before use.
- Medium deaeration performed if required or justified by the method.
- Paddle/basket height, shaft centering, and mechanical qualification current.
- Rotation speed set and independently verified where required.
- Dosage units equilibrated and handled per method.
- Sinkers used only where specified and justified.
- Sampling times, positions, filter type, and replacement volume defined.
- Assay method, standards, and system suitability ready.
- Analyst understands the staging rules before results appear.

This checklist feels basic. That is the point. Dissolution problems often come
from basic physical controls being treated as routine background.

## Investigation checklist for a dissolution failure

If a dissolution result is low, erratic, or fails after the applicable stage,
investigate in a disciplined order.

**1. Confirm the data**

- Were calculations, dilution factors, units, and transcription correct?
- Was the correct Q value and acceptance table applied?
- Were the correct units included in S1, S2, or S3?
- Did chromatographic or UV assay system suitability pass?

**2. Review the apparatus**

- Was qualification or mechanical calibration current?
- Were vessel, paddle, basket, shaft, height, wobble, and speed within limits?
- Was the correct apparatus used?
- Were any components damaged, dirty, mismatched, or recently changed?

**3. Review the medium**

- Was the medium made with the correct composition, pH, volume, and surfactant?
- Was deaeration performed correctly where required?
- Was temperature maintained throughout the test?
- Were replacement volumes handled correctly after sampling?

**4. Review execution**

- Were dosage units introduced consistently?
- Were sampling times exact?
- Was the sampling probe at the correct location?
- Was the vessel disturbed?
- Were filters suitable and preconditioned where required?
- Was there coning, sticking, floating, capping, or basket clogging?

**5. Review the product**

- Is there a manufacturing signal: compression force, hardness, coating weight,
  granulation, particle size, blend uniformity, moisture, or aging?
- Are related lots or stability lots showing the same trend?
- Did a raw-material, process, site, scale, or packaging change occur?
- Is this a true formulation or process issue rather than a laboratory artifact?

Only after the system is understood should the team conclude whether the result
is lab error, method limitation, mechanical setup, or true product failure.

## Dissolution and post-approval change

Dissolution is not only a release test. It is also one of the most practical
signals for whether a manufacturing change altered product performance.

Changes that can affect dissolution include:

- API particle-size distribution;
- polymorph or salt form;
- excipient grade, source, or level;
- granulation endpoint or drying profile;
- compression force and tablet hardness;
- coating formulation, weight gain, and curing;
- capsule shell or gelatin crosslinking risk;
- scale, mixer, blender, granulator, dryer, or tablet press changes;
- manufacturing site or equipment train;
- packaging that changes moisture exposure.

That is why FDA dissolution guidance links dissolution to formulation
development, specification setting, bioequivalence considerations, and
post-approval manufacturing changes. A dissolution profile is often the first
place where a "small" process change becomes visible.

For broader change logic, see [Post-approval variations: changing an approved
product](/blog/post-approval-variations-explained).

## Common mistakes

Dissolution errors tend to repeat across labs:

- Treating USP staged acceptance as optional retesting logic.
- Entering the wrong Q value or using the wrong acceptance table.
- Ignoring degassing because the method "usually works."
- Sampling late at early time points.
- Letting filters adsorb analyte without filter suitability evidence.
- Missing coning or floating observations.
- Using apparatus with expired qualification or questionable mechanical state.
- Investigating only the product and ignoring the vessel, shaft, paddle, basket,
  medium, and analyst execution.
- Closing a failure because S2 or S3 passed, without assessing a recurring
  pattern.

The common theme is overconfidence. Dissolution is familiar, so teams can forget
how sensitive it is.

## Key takeaway

Dissolution is a chemistry result produced by a mechanical system. The number on
the report depends on the product, but also on medium, temperature, hydrodynamics,
apparatus condition, sampling, filtration, and staged acceptance logic.

When dissolution is controlled well, it becomes a powerful indicator of product
performance. When the setup is loose, it becomes a machine for creating confusing
data. The best QC teams respect the mechanics before they interpret the chemistry.
