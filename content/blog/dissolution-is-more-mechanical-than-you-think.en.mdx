# Dissolution: it's more mechanical than you think

Dissolution testing looks like chemistry, but a surprising amount of it is
mechanics. Most of the variability — and most false out-of-specification results —
come from the **apparatus and the medium**, not the product. Knowing that changes
how you set up the test and how you investigate a failure.

## The mechanical variables that bite

Before you blame the tablet, check the things that quietly skew results:

- **Undegassed medium** — dissolved gas forms bubbles on the dosage form and
  vessel, changing dissolution; degas the medium and equilibrate it to 37 degrees C
- **Vessel alignment and wobble** — a shaft that wobbles or a misaligned vessel
  changes the hydrodynamics
- **Coning** — undissolved material mounding under the paddle, slowing release
- **Sampling** — wrong depth, wrong time, or disturbing the vessel

Apparatus calibration or mechanical qualification exists precisely because these
factors dominate.

## Staged acceptance is a safeguard, not a loophole

USP 711 uses a staged acceptance scheme — S1, then S2, then S3 — each with defined
criteria tied to the product's **Q value** (the amount dissolved at a given time).
It is a statistical safeguard against an unlucky sample, **not** a way to keep
testing until the batch passes. You move to the next stage per the method, with the
result documented, not because the first stage disappointed you.

## Investigate the system first

When a profile looks erratic or a unit fails, the first question is **mechanical or
method**, not **product**:

- Check degassing, alignment, wobble, and coning
- Confirm the apparatus calibration / MQC is current
- Then, if those are sound, investigate the product
