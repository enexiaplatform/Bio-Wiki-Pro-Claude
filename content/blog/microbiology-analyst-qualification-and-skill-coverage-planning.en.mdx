# Microbiology analyst qualification and skill-coverage planning

Headcount does not prove capability. A laboratory needs enough currently qualified
people to execute, observe, review and investigate each required activity across the
operating calendar.

```text
method and decision portfolio
→ task and role decomposition
→ training and observed competency evidence
→ controlled authorization by scope
→ shift, absence and surge coverage
→ requalification and performance monitoring
```

Atlas can model qualification coverage and workload. It does not assess an
individual's competence, authorize GMP work, set universal qualification criteria or
replace the site's training and personnel system.

## Build a task-level qualification matrix

Map people to controlled tasks, not broad labels such as “microbiology trained.”
Separate as applicable:

- sample receipt, accession and protected subsampling;
- media and diluent preparation;
- reference-culture revival, stock and inoculum preparation;
- enumeration, filtration and specified-organism branches;
- method suitability and recovery studies;
- growth-promotion and media QC;
- water and environmental sampling;
- colony counting and isolate selection;
- identification platform operation and interpretation;
- incubator, autoclave and storage operation;
- data entry, calculation and technical review;
- deviation, invalid-test and investigation roles;
- waste, decontamination and emergency response.

Each authorization should state method/version, product or matrix scope, equipment or
configuration, permitted role, effective date, expiry/review date and approver.

## Separate training from demonstrated competency

Evidence can include:

1. prerequisite education or experience;
2. procedure and method understanding;
3. observed demonstration under representative conditions;
4. successful controls, calculations and records;
5. handling of atypical or simulated exceptions;
6. independent execution where required;
7. reviewer assessment and authorization;
8. periodic monitoring or requalification.

Reading an SOP may establish awareness. It does not by itself show that a person can
perform aseptic transfers, recognize atypical growth, preserve traceability or respond
correctly to a failed control.

The European Commission's official [EudraLex Volume 4](https://health.ec.europa.eu/medicinal-products/eudralex/eudralex-volume-4_en)
provides current access to Chapter 2 on personnel and Chapter 6 on Quality Control.
The project must apply current regional and site requirements through qualified
personnel governance.

## Define role and independence boundaries

For each activity identify who may:

- prepare and execute;
- witness or independently verify;
- enter or calculate;
- perform second-person technical review;
- interpret atypical observations;
- approve invalidation, repeat or additional work;
- lead an investigation;
- approve a report or release-supporting result;
- train, assess and authorize another person.

One individual may hold several roles, but the required independence and conflict
controls must remain visible.

## Calculate coverage by method and calendar

For every critical task calculate:

- qualified executors per shift;
- qualified reviewers per result window;
- trainer/assessor availability;
- routine demand and peak concurrency;
- weekend, holiday and on-call coverage;
- planned leave, training and administrative time;
- expected absence and turnover;
- investigation and urgent-work coverage;
- supervision required for trainees;
- backup across teams, sites or approved partners.

A team of six can still have a single point of failure if only one person can review a
specified-organism result or prepare a controlled culture stock.

## Model the qualification pipeline

New or cross-training demand should include:

- prerequisite gaps;
- trainer and trainee hours;
- method materials, samples and controls;
- equipment and workspace occupancy;
- incubation and elapsed calendar time;
- number and type of demonstrations;
- assessment and documentation time;
- failed or repeated qualification work;
- authorization lead time;
- restricted production during supervised practice.

Adding headcount does not immediately add productive capacity. The ramp profile must
reflect how long qualification actually takes.

## Control expiry, inactivity and change impact

Define triggers for review or requalification, such as:

- elapsed qualification period;
- extended inactivity;
- method, product, matrix or equipment change;
- significant procedure revision;
- recurring documentation or control errors;
- failed proficiency or observed assessment;
- investigation finding;
- transfer to a different environment or shift;
- new interpretation or reviewer responsibility.

The system should prevent expired, suspended or scope-limited authorization from
appearing available in the capacity model.

## Use performance signals carefully

Monitor task-level signals such as:

- invalid or repeated tests;
- control failures;
- documentation corrections;
- delayed starts or readings;
- atypical colony selection or interpretation discordance;
- investigation contributions;
- proficiency and observation outcomes;
- overdue requalification;
- reviewer corrections and recurring error modes.

These signals support system and competency review. They should not become simplistic
individual rankings without context, investigation and fair personnel governance.

## Plan cross-training by risk and value

Prioritize cross-training where:

- one person is the only qualified executor or reviewer;
- a task is release- or stability-critical;
- timing cannot wait for the next shift;
- investigation demand is material;
- external support has long lead time;
- a new product or method is approaching;
- turnover or retirement risk is high;
- qualification itself consumes long elapsed time.

Cross-training every person on every method can create excessive maintenance burden.
Use criticality, demand and continuity evidence.

## Evidence package before staffing approval

Collect:

1. method-to-task decomposition;
2. role and independence requirements;
3. qualification and authorization criteria;
4. current person × task × version matrix;
5. shift and review-calendar coverage;
6. leave, absence and turnover scenarios;
7. training pipeline and ramp time;
8. expiry, inactivity and change triggers;
9. proficiency and performance-monitoring process;
10. trainer and assessor capacity;
11. cross-training and external-support plan;
12. actual qualification effort and coverage-loss history.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas should not label staffing adequate when:

- headcount replaces task-level authorization;
- SOP completion is treated as observed competence;
- method/version or product scope is missing;
- executor and reviewer coverage are merged;
- shifts, leave and weekends are ignored;
- expired or inactive qualifications appear available;
- training time is counted as immediate productive capacity;
- single-person dependencies are hidden;
- investigation and surge roles are absent;
- actual ramp and requalification effort cannot be calibrated.
