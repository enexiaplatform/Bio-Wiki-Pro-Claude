# Internal audit: finding it before the inspector does

The internal audit — or self-inspection — is the quality system looking in the mirror.
It is your scheduled chance to find your own problems, on your own timeline, before an
external inspector finds them for you. Used honestly, it is one of the most valuable
things a quality system does. Used as a formality, it is a missed opportunity wearing a
checklist.

## Independence and honesty are the whole point

A self-inspection only works if the auditor can be **objective**. That usually means
auditing an area you do not own, so you have no stake in a clean result. The paradox of
a good internal audit is that **finding problems is success** — a self-inspection that
never finds anything is far more worrying than one that surfaces real issues, because
the issues are almost certainly there.

The cultural test is what happens to findings: are they welcomed and fixed, or
quietly minimised? An organisation that punishes internal findings teaches its people
to stop looking.

## Plan it by risk

A mature program is **risk-based**, not just an annual march through every SOP:

- Audit higher-risk areas (sterile operations, data systems, recent changes) more often
- Cover the whole quality system over a defined cycle
- Trigger **for-cause** audits after a serious deviation, a trend, or a supplier issue

## Findings, CAPA, and follow-up

The value is not in the audit report — it is in what happens after. Findings are
classified by significance, fed into **CAPA**, and **followed up** to closure and
effectiveness. An audit finding that is logged and never fixed is worse than no audit,
because now you have documented that you knew.

## Why it pays off at inspection time

When a regulator arrives, the organisations that do best are usually the ones whose
internal audits already found and fixed the issues. The inspector sees a system that
catches its own problems — which is exactly the maturity they are looking for.
