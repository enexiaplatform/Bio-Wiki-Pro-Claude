# Internal audit: finding it before the inspector does

The internal audit, or self-inspection, is the quality system looking in the
mirror before a regulator, customer, partner, or corporate auditor does it for
you.

Done well, it is one of the most valuable routines in GMP. It finds weak signals
early, turns hidden drift into visible action, and gives management an honest
view of the site before small issues become observations, commitments, warning
letters, rejected batches, or patient risk.

Done badly, it becomes theater: a friendly checklist, a tidy report, a few minor
comments, and no real change.

The difference is not the audit template. The difference is whether the program
is independent, risk-based, evidence-driven, and connected to CAPA and management
review.

## What self-inspection is meant to do

EU GMP Chapter 9 describes self-inspection as a way to monitor implementation and
compliance with GMP principles and to propose necessary corrective measures.
PIC/S GMP uses the same practical idea: the manufacturer should regularly and
systematically examine its own quality system, facilities, processes, and
records.

That purpose is important. A self-inspection is not only a compliance ritual. It
is a control mechanism. It asks:

- Are procedures actually implemented?
- Are records complete, accurate, and retrievable?
- Are deviations and CAPA reducing recurrence?
- Are changes protecting the validated state?
- Are suppliers, materials, equipment, utilities, and computerized systems under
  control?
- Are people trained for the work they perform?
- Are prior audit commitments closed and effective?
- Are there weak signals management needs to see?

The audit should produce a truthful picture of the system, not a comforting one.

## The uncomfortable truth: findings are success

Many teams quietly treat audit findings as failure. That is backwards.

An internal audit that finds a real problem gives the site control over timing,
scope, communication, and remediation. The organization can contain risk,
investigate root cause, assign CAPA, verify effectiveness, and show the next
inspector that the quality system can detect and correct itself.

A clean report is only valuable if the audit was strong enough to deserve it.

The worrying pattern is a program that audits every year and finds almost
nothing, while deviations repeat, CAPA is overdue, complaints trend upward,
audit trails are weak, or inspection observations keep recurring. That usually
means the audit program is looking where the risk is not, asking shallow
questions, or softening findings to avoid conflict.

Internal audit should make the organization slightly uncomfortable. That is part
of its job.

## Independence and objectivity

An auditor should not audit their own work. Independence does not require
hostility, but it does require enough distance to see the evidence clearly.

In a small site, perfect independence can be difficult. The practical goal is to
avoid obvious conflicts: production should not audit its own batch execution,
QC should not audit its own laboratory data review, validation should not audit
only its own protocols, and the document owner should not be the only person
judging whether the procedure is effective.

Good auditor independence includes:

- defined auditor qualification criteria;
- assignment rules that avoid auditing your own area;
- access to records and personnel without unnecessary filtering;
- authority to write findings based on evidence;
- QA oversight of classification and follow-up;
- escalation if an area disputes a finding without evidence.

Objectivity also means writing what the evidence shows. "The team seems
overloaded" is an opinion. "Seven of twelve CAPA effectiveness checks were
overdue, including two major investigations linked to repeat deviations" is
audit evidence.

## Plan the program by risk

A weak internal audit program is calendar-based only: every department once per
year, same checklist, same depth, same report format.

A mature program is risk-based. It still covers the full quality system over a
defined cycle, but it adjusts frequency, depth, and audit style based on risk and
history.

High-priority audit areas often include:

- sterile operations and contamination control;
- data integrity and computerized systems;
- OOS/OOT, deviations, CAPA, and batch release;
- supplier qualification and quality agreements;
- change control and validated-state protection;
- cleaning, sterilization, and process validation;
- complaints, recalls, and pharmacovigilance interfaces;
- high-volume or high-risk QC methods;
- areas with repeat deviations, overdue actions, or recent changes;
- new facilities, new product transfers, new equipment, or new outsourcing.

Lower-risk areas may still be audited, but less frequently or with lighter depth
if history supports it.

Risk-based scheduling should be documented. If sterile manufacturing is audited
twice a year and office training records are audited once every two years, the
program should explain why. The rationale should use product risk, process
complexity, compliance history, change volume, supplier performance, deviation
trends, and prior findings.

## For-cause audits

The annual schedule is not enough. A site also needs for-cause audits when the
quality system receives a signal that routine monitoring may not explain.

For-cause triggers can include:

- a serious or repeated deviation;
- a significant OOS or OOT pattern;
- a data integrity event;
- a complaint trend or recall signal;
- supplier quality deterioration;
- overdue or ineffective CAPA;
- repeated late batch record review;
- recurring environmental monitoring excursions;
- a major change, tech transfer, or new product launch;
- a failed inspection, customer audit, or regulatory observation;
- a management review concern.

A for-cause audit is not a punishment. It is a focused way to understand whether
the problem is isolated or systemic.

## Audit the evidence chain, not only the SOP

Many weak audits stop at procedure review. They ask whether an SOP exists, whether
it is approved, and whether people are trained. Those checks matter, but they do
not prove the system works.

A stronger audit follows evidence chains.

For a deviation/CAPA audit, trace a real event from detection to containment,
product impact, root cause, CAPA, effectiveness check, and recurrence trend.

For batch release, trace a released batch through production record, QC results,
deviations, OOS/OOT history, audit trails, equipment status, material status, and
QA disposition.

For data integrity, trace a reported result back through raw data, audit trail,
user access, processing, review, backup, and archival retrieval.

For supplier quality, trace an incoming material lot through supplier approval,
quality agreement, purchase specification, CoA review, incoming testing, deviations,
and periodic review.

This evidence-chain approach shows whether the system works across handoffs. It
also finds the gaps that checklists miss.

## How to write a good finding

An audit finding should be specific enough that a competent owner can understand
the issue, assess risk, and begin investigation without guessing what the auditor
meant.

A strong finding usually includes:

- the requirement or expected control;
- the objective evidence reviewed;
- the observed gap;
- the scope or sample size;
- the quality or compliance risk;
- any repeat or related history;
- a clear classification or severity.

Weak finding:

> Training records need improvement.

Stronger finding:

> Three of ten analysts sampled had not completed the current version of SOP
> QC-014 before performing HPLC system suitability review. Two reviewed release
> data for Product A batches within the affected period. The gap indicates the
> training matrix is not reliably preventing unqualified GMP data review.

The stronger version gives the process owner something to investigate. It also
helps QA assess product impact, data integrity risk, and CAPA scope.

## Classify findings by risk

Finding classification should be risk-based, not political. Labels vary by site,
but many programs use critical, major, and minor.

**Critical** findings involve a direct or significant risk to patient safety,
product quality, data integrity, sterility assurance, batch disposition, or legal
compliance. They usually require immediate containment and senior escalation.

**Major** findings indicate a significant system weakness, repeat failure,
inadequate control, or potential product or compliance impact. They normally
require formal investigation and CAPA.

**Minor** findings are lower-risk gaps that still require correction. A minor
finding can become major if it repeats or indicates the system is not learning.

Classification should consider:

- patient and product risk;
- data integrity impact;
- batch release or validated-state impact;
- repeat history;
- extent of affected records, batches, products, or systems;
- whether the issue was known but not acted on;
- whether existing controls detected the issue before the audit.

Avoid classification negotiation that strips meaning from the report. If every
finding becomes minor after a tense meeting, the program is protecting comfort,
not quality.

## CAPA and follow-up are where the value appears

The audit report is not the outcome. The outcome is risk reduction.

Every significant finding should move into the quality system with ownership,
due dates, risk assessment, root-cause evaluation, correction, CAPA where needed,
and effectiveness verification.

The common failure is to close audit findings on activity:

- procedure updated;
- personnel retrained;
- checklist revised;
- meeting held;
- memo filed.

Those may be useful actions, but they do not prove the issue was fixed. A CAPA
effectiveness check should ask whether the failure mode stopped recurring.

Examples of stronger effectiveness evidence:

- three months of on-time audit trail review with no overdue records;
- a post-CAPA sample of batch records shows no repeat unexplained blanks;
- supplier defect rate returns below the predefined alert level;
- training qualification is verified by observed performance, not only attendance;
- change controls after the CAPA consistently include validation impact review;
- repeat deviation rate drops against a defined baseline.

If the effectiveness check fails, the CAPA should not be quietly closed. The site
learned that the action was not enough.

## Trend audit findings

One audit finding can be local. Repeated minor findings can be a system problem.

Internal audit data should be trended by area, process, severity, root cause,
recurrence, overdue status, and CAPA effectiveness. The goal is not to create a
beautiful dashboard. The goal is to see what the site keeps failing to fix.

Useful trend questions include:

- Which areas have repeat findings across audit cycles?
- Which findings recur after CAPA closure?
- Which departments have the most overdue actions?
- Which procedure gaps appear in multiple systems?
- Which findings are linked to deviations, complaints, or inspection observations?
- Are finding classifications stable, or being downgraded over time?
- Are high-risk areas audited deeply enough to find meaningful issues?

This is where internal audit connects to ICH Q10. The pharmaceutical quality
system should use monitoring, CAPA, management review, knowledge management, and
quality risk management to improve. Audit findings are part of that knowledge
stream.

## Management review needs the truth

Senior management cannot resource quality risks it never sees. Internal audit
should feed management review with more than a count of open and closed actions.

A useful management review package includes:

- audit schedule adherence;
- significant findings and risk themes;
- repeat findings and failed CAPA effectiveness checks;
- overdue high-risk actions;
- areas not audited as planned and why;
- resource constraints affecting closure;
- links to deviations, complaints, supplier issues, inspection observations, and
  APR/PQR trends;
- decisions needed from leadership.

The message should be clear enough for leaders to act. "Audit program on track"
is not useful if the same data integrity finding has appeared in three audits.

## Common traps

**Using the same checklist forever.** Static checklists become predictable. Keep
core questions, but refresh sampling, evidence chains, and risk focus.

**Auditing documents instead of behavior.** An SOP can be perfect while practice
is weak. Observe the process and review executed records.

**Downgrading uncomfortable findings.** If classification becomes a negotiation,
the report loses credibility.

**Closing findings without effectiveness evidence.** Closure on paperwork is not
the same as closure on risk.

**Ignoring repeat minors.** Repetition is evidence. A recurring minor gap may be a
major system weakness.

**Letting overdue actions drift.** An overdue finding says the site knows the
problem and has not controlled it.

**Treating customer audits as self-inspection.** External audits can provide
useful input, but the site still needs its own planned self-inspection program.

**Punishing transparency.** If people are punished for surfacing findings, they
will stop surfacing them.

## Practical internal-audit checklist

Use these questions when reviewing or redesigning the program:

1. Does the schedule cover the full quality system over a defined cycle?
2. Is frequency adjusted by product, process, compliance, and history risk?
3. Are for-cause audit triggers defined?
4. Are auditors trained and independent of the audited area?
5. Does the audit sample real records and evidence chains?
6. Are findings written with requirement, evidence, gap, risk, and scope?
7. Is severity classification risk-based and consistent?
8. Are significant findings linked to investigation and CAPA?
9. Are corrective actions checked for effectiveness with objective evidence?
10. Are repeat findings trended and escalated?
11. Are overdue actions visible to management?
12. Does management review lead to resource decisions and risk reduction?

If the answer to several questions is no, the audit program may be producing
reports without producing control.

## The practical takeaway

The best internal audit program is not the one with the fewest findings. It is
the one that finds the right problems early, explains them with evidence, drives
proportionate CAPA, verifies that the system improved, and gives leadership the
truth while there is still time to act.

An inspector finding a gap is not the first moment the gap exists. It is only the
first moment the site can no longer control the timing.

Find it first.
