# Quality laboratory startup, commissioning and operational readiness

Installed equipment is not an operating laboratory. A team can occupy the rooms only
after facility, utility, equipment, method, people, materials, data and quality-system
dependencies converge for the intended work.

```text
approved Blueprint and design basis
→ installation and commissioning evidence
→ qualification and method readiness
→ people, materials, systems and procedures
→ integrated operating trials
→ residual-risk and go-live decision
→ controlled ramp-up and performance review
```

Atlas can structure readiness and expose open dependencies. It does not commission
or qualify systems, validate methods, authorize GMP operation or replace engineering,
EHS, validation and quality-unit decisions.

## Define readiness by operating scope

Create a separate readiness record for each release wave, such as:

- sample receipt and storage;
- media and reagent preparation;
- non-sterile enumeration and specified-organism testing;
- water or environmental monitoring;
- microbial identification;
- specialist or outsourced interfaces;
- investigation and continuity operation.

Each wave should state products, methods, markets, rooms, shifts, equipment,
dependencies, permitted use and exclusions. A laboratory may be ready for one scope
while another remains under qualification or method transfer.

## Build the integrated readiness matrix

For every capability confirm the status and evidence for:

| Readiness domain | Decision evidence |
| --- | --- |
| Facility and flow | Approved room use, personnel/material/sample/waste routes, cleaning and environmental controls |
| Utilities | Point-of-use availability, monitoring, alarms, capacity and recovery |
| Equipment | Installed configuration, commissioning, qualification, calibration, maintenance and data setup |
| Methods | Approved procedure, applicability, transfer/verification/validation, controls and calculations |
| People | Current task-level authorization, reviewer coverage, shifts and emergency roles |
| Materials | Approved suppliers/lots, media QC, standards/cultures, storage, expiry and reserve |
| Data and records | Validated or approved systems, master data, access, review, retention and fallback |
| Quality system | SOPs, deviations, change, OOS/OOT, CAPA, training and document control |
| Continuity | Backup resources, outages, external support, spare parts and recovery tests |

A green equipment line cannot override a red method, reviewer, material or data
dependency.

## Separate commissioning, qualification and operational readiness

Commissioning demonstrates that installed systems function against the engineering
and design basis. Qualification provides controlled evidence for intended regulated
use as defined by the project's validation strategy. Operational readiness confirms
that the complete organization can execute and govern the work.

The European Commission's official [EudraLex Volume 4](https://health.ec.europa.eu/medicinal-products/eudralex/eudralex-volume-4_en)
provides access to Annex 15 on qualification and validation. The project must define
its exact commissioning/qualification lifecycle, documents and approval roles.

Avoid using one label—“validated”—for rooms, equipment, methods, software and people.
Their evidence and acceptance decisions differ.

## Control turnover and evidence completeness

For each asset or system reconcile:

- approved requirement and design reference;
- as-built configuration and asset identifier;
- vendor documents and certificates;
- test, inspection and commissioning results;
- deviations, punch items and concessions;
- qualification and calibration status;
- software/firmware and configuration;
- spare parts, manuals and service contacts;
- preventive maintenance and periodic review;
- owner, location and record repository.

Do not accept a document count as turnover completeness. The evidence must match the
installed configuration and intended-use scope.

## Prove method and application readiness

For every method/application verify:

- current controlled procedure and source;
- product/matrix and market applicability;
- sample quantity, container and hold condition;
- preparation, controls, calculations and acceptance basis;
- suitability, verification, transfer or validation status;
- equipment and material configuration;
- analyst and reviewer authorization;
- data template/master-data configuration;
- investigation and repeat boundaries;
- report and downstream decision interface.

“Compendial method” is not enough when product-specific suitability, local procedure,
equipment configuration and analyst qualification remain open.

## Run integrated operating trials

Before routine go-live, use approved simulations, engineering runs, method work or
other controlled trials to challenge handoffs such as:

- sample arrival through accession and test start;
- media preparation, release and allocation;
- culture and inoculum readiness;
- test execution through incubation and reading;
- raw data through review and result approval;
- waste segregation and decontamination;
- alarm, deviation and escalation;
- equipment or utility outage;
- after-hours and weekend coverage;
- external laboratory shipment and result receipt.

The test design and permitted materials must follow site governance. Atlas records
coverage and findings; it does not authorize GMP simulation or use of product.

## Build a go-live decision package

The package should contain:

1. approved scope and release wave;
2. readiness matrix and evidence references;
3. integrated-trial results;
4. open punch items and restrictions;
5. deviations and change controls;
6. unresolved assumptions and residual risks;
7. temporary controls and expiry dates;
8. staffing, materials and continuity confirmation;
9. first-work schedule and monitoring plan;
10. owners, approvers and decision date.

“No critical open items” needs an agreed criticality method and evidence. Low-severity
items can combine into an operational risk when they affect one workflow.

## Plan a controlled ramp-up

Define:

- first methods, products and sample volumes;
- supervision and enhanced review;
- reduced concurrency or reserved capacity;
- daily/weekly readiness review;
- rapid escalation contacts;
- deviations and query thresholds;
- supplier and service support;
- criteria for increasing load;
- stop/hold and rollback conditions;
- transition to routine governance.

Do not assume design capacity on day one. Qualification, training and stable handoffs
may require a staged productivity curve.

## Evidence package before go-live

Collect:

- scope and operating-wave definition;
- as-built and turnover reconciliation;
- commissioning, qualification and calibration status;
- method/application readiness;
- task-level qualification and shift coverage;
- material, culture and inventory readiness;
- data, records and review readiness;
- procedures and quality-system operation;
- integrated-trial and failure-response evidence;
- continuity and external-provider readiness;
- punch items, restrictions and residual risks;
- ramp-up metrics and decision authorities.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas should not label a scope operationally ready when:

- readiness is inferred from equipment installation;
- intended method/product scope is undefined;
- turnover evidence does not match the installed configuration;
- commissioning, qualification and method readiness are conflated;
- people, materials, data or reviewers are absent;
- integrated handoffs and failure responses are untested;
- temporary controls have no owner or expiry;
- residual risk is hidden in a punch list;
- ramp-up assumes immediate design capacity;
- no authorized go-live or stop-work decision exists.
