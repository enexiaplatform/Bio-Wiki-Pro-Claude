# Calibration: the traceability hiding under every number

Every weight, temperature, pH, and pressure reading in your lab carries a silent
assumption: that the instrument is telling the truth. Calibration is what makes that
assumption defensible. It connects your instrument's reading to a **traceable
reference standard** — a chain that runs back to a national or international standard —
so a kilogram here means the same as a kilogram everywhere.

## Calibration vs verification vs qualification

These get blurred, but they are distinct:

- **Calibration** compares the instrument to a reference standard and, where needed,
  adjusts it.
- **Verification / calibration check** confirms it is still within tolerance between
  full calibrations.
- **Qualification (IQ/OQ/PQ)** is the broader process of proving the equipment is fit
  for use; calibration of its critical measurements is part of keeping that
  qualification valid.

## Tolerance is the number that matters

A calibration is only meaningful against a defined **tolerance** — how far the reading
may deviate and still be acceptable for its use. A balance used for trace impurity
weighing needs a tighter tolerance than one weighing bulk media. Setting tolerances
from the **use**, not the instrument's spec sheet, is the craft.

## As-found and as-left — and the out-of-tolerance trap

A calibration record captures **as-found** (the state before any adjustment) and
**as-left** (after). The as-found is the one that bites: if an instrument is found
**out of tolerance**, every result it produced since its last good calibration is
suspect. That triggers an impact assessment — which batches, which results, what
disposition. It is the single biggest reason calibration intervals and prompt
out-of-tolerance handling matter.

## Why it underpins everything

Method validation, system suitability, process control — all of it assumes the
instruments are calibrated and traceable. Calibration is not glamorous, but it is the
foundation that every quantitative claim quietly stands on.
