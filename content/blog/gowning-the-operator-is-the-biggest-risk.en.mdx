# Gowning: the operator is the biggest contamination risk

People shed microorganisms constantly. In an aseptic processing area, the gowned
operator is the **single largest source of contamination** — which makes gowning
the primary barrier between that operator and the sterile field. Gowning
qualification is really a test of that barrier.

## Why both halves are weighted equally

A gowning qualification has two parts, and a pass needs **both**:

- **Technique** — a qualified assessor watches the full aseptic gowning sequence
  against the SOP and flags any breach.
- **Microbial counts** — contact plates taken at defined locations (gloves,
  forearms, chest, hood) immediately after gowning, incubated and compared with the
  area-grade limits.

Why both? A within-limit plate count after a sloppy technique tells you the plate
location got lucky — not that the operator is qualified. So a technique breach is a
fail on its own, even if the counts pass.

## It's a program, not a certificate

Annex 1 (2022) expects gowning to be a **program**, not a one-off:

- **Initial qualification** over several consecutive successful sessions
- **Continuous monitoring** during routine production, not just at qualification
- **Periodic re-qualification** on a defined interval
- **Retraining and investigation** after any gowning excursion

A re-qualification that quietly lapses is a common finding.

## When it goes wrong

- **Repeated glove failures** — review glove-change technique and sanitisation;
  retrain and re-qualify before granting grade A/B access.
- **A facility-wide rise in excursions** — look at changing-room airflow, gown
  sterilisation and supply, and disinfectant rotation, not just individuals.
