# You can't test quality into a batch: AQL and acceptance sampling

"You cannot test quality into a product" is one of those GMP lines that sounds
obvious until a batch is late, demand is high, and the only remaining question in
the room is whether enough extra testing can make everyone comfortable.

Testing matters. Visual inspection matters. Sampling plans matter. Release tests
matter. But they are evidence about a batch, not a manufacturing process for
quality. They can detect some failures after the fact. They cannot reverse poor
mixing, uncontrolled bioburden, a weak cleaning process, a mislabeled component,
an unqualified sterilization cycle, or a trend that the site ignored for months.

That is the point of the maxim: quality is built through product design, process
understanding, validated control, trained execution, data integrity, monitoring,
investigation, and lifecycle learning. Final testing is one signal in that system.
It is not the system.

Acceptance sampling is a useful place to see this clearly. It is powerful when it
is used to make a defined statistical decision about a controlled lot. It becomes
dangerous when people treat it as proof that the uninspected units are defect-free.

## What final testing can and cannot do

Final testing can confirm that tested samples met approved specifications by
approved methods. It can detect some defects. It can support batch release. It can
trigger investigations, holds, rejections, recalls, CAPA, or process improvement.

But final testing has hard limits:

- it looks at samples, not every unit, unless 100% inspection is performed;
- it usually happens late, after value and risk have already accumulated;
- it may miss rare, localized, intermittent, or clustered defects;
- it cannot show what was not sampled, not recorded, or not trended;
- it cannot compensate for a process that is not in a state of control.

For many quality attributes, the most important controls are upstream. Sterility
assurance is built through contamination control, sterilization validation,
aseptic process simulation, environmental monitoring, personnel qualification,
bioburden control, filtration integrity, and process discipline. Blend uniformity
is built through formulation, mixing design, raw material control, equipment
scale, sampling strategy, and process monitoring. Label accuracy is built through
line clearance, reconciliation, vision systems, controlled artwork, and human
verification.

The final test or inspection is a checkpoint. It is not a time machine.

## AQL in plain language

AQL means Acceptance Quality Limit. In attribute sampling standards such as
ANSI/ASQ Z1.4 or ISO 2859-1, it is a quality level used with lot size, inspection
level, sample size, and accept/reject numbers to decide whether a lot is accepted
or rejected based on a sample.

The important wording is often misunderstood. AQL is not a target defect rate. It
is not a promise that the lot contains no defects. It is not the maximum number of
defects that may exist in the full lot. It is a parameter in a sampling plan that
helps define the probability of accepting lots at different underlying quality
levels.

In practical terms:

- smaller AQL numbers are stricter;
- larger samples make the decision sharper but do not remove risk;
- the accept number and reject number define the decision rule;
- different defect classes often need different AQLs;
- critical defects may require zero acceptance or a different control strategy.

For example, a site may inspect a sample of packaging units for major and minor
visual defects. If the sample contains defects at or below the accept number, the
lot is accepted under that plan. That does not mean the remaining units are
perfect. It means the sampling evidence met the pre-approved statistical rule.

That distinction is the whole story.

## Producer risk and consumer risk

Every sampling plan balances two risks.

**Producer risk** is the chance of rejecting a lot that is actually acceptable.
This is the false alarm side. It creates cost, delay, rework, investigation, and
potential supply impact.

**Consumer risk** is the chance of accepting a lot that is actually poor quality.
This is the patient, customer, and compliance side. It is the more serious risk
when defects could affect safety, identity, strength, quality, purity, usability,
or regulatory commitments.

You cannot drive both risks to zero with a finite sample. If you sample more, the
plan generally detects poor quality better, but inspection becomes slower and more
expensive. If you sample less, the plan becomes easier to execute but less able to
separate good lots from bad lots.

Sampling standards encode this trade-off. The operating characteristic curve is
the statistical picture behind it: for each possible true defect level, the curve
shows the probability that the plan will accept the lot. Teams do not need to
memorize the math to use the concept well. They do need to understand that a
sampling plan always leaves a non-zero chance of the wrong decision.

## Accepted does not mean defect-free

The most harmful misunderstanding is simple:

**"The lot passed AQL, so there are no defects."**

No. The lot passed the sampling plan. Defects may still exist in uninspected units,
and even inspected samples may have detection limitations. The plan controls
decision risk; it does not make a physical guarantee about every unit.

This matters in batch release language. "Accepted under the approved sampling
plan" is a defensible statement. "Defect-free" is usually not.

It also matters in complaint investigations. If a customer finds a defect in a lot
that passed AQL, the first response should not be "impossible." The better
question is whether the defect was consistent with the known residual risk of the
plan, or whether it suggests a process shift, detection failure, inspector
fatigue, clustering, supplier issue, equipment problem, or flawed defect
classification.

Sampling is evidence. Complaints, deviations, trends, and field signals are also
evidence.

## Why sampling cannot rescue an uncontrolled process

Acceptance sampling assumes the lot is meaningful enough to sample. If the process
is unstable, the lot may contain pockets of different quality. A small sample can
miss a localized failure, especially when defects cluster by time, equipment head,
operator, material container, pallet, lane, cavity, filling needle, or packaging
line section.

That is why sampling is weakest when the process most needs rescue.

Examples:

- a packaging line had intermittent label skew for twenty minutes;
- one filling needle produced low fill volumes during part of the run;
- a lyophilizer shelf showed a localized thermal problem;
- an upstream bioburden excursion affected only a subset of hold vessels;
- one vision-system camera was misaligned for a short interval;
- a supplier component lot was mixed with another lot during staging.

Random sampling may or may not hit the affected units. Extra testing after the
fact may improve detection, but it does not replace understanding what happened,
which units were at risk, whether the issue was bounded, and what control failed.

When the process is not in control, the release question is not "Can we sample
enough to feel better?" It is "Can we scientifically define the affected
population and support a disposition decision?"

## Where quality is actually built

Quality is built through a connected control strategy. In a mature GMP system,
that usually includes:

- product and process development that understands critical quality attributes;
- raw material, component, and supplier controls;
- qualified facilities, utilities, equipment, instruments, and computerized
  systems;
- validated manufacturing, cleaning, sterilization, and analytical processes;
- trained personnel and controlled procedures;
- in-process controls, alarms, line checks, and independent verifications;
- process parameter limits and material attribute controls;
- environmental, utility, and contamination-control monitoring where relevant;
- deviation, OOS, OOT, CAPA, and change-control systems;
- batch record review, audit trail review, and data integrity controls;
- continued process verification and APR/PQR trending;
- management review and quality-risk decisions.

Testing sits inside that network. It should confirm that the network is working,
detect signals when it is not, and feed learning back into the system.

If the network is weak, testing becomes overloaded. That is when sites start to
ask release tests to answer questions that should have been answered by design,
validation, and process control.

## How this connects to process validation

FDA's process validation lifecycle makes the same point in operational language.
Stage 1 designs and understands the commercial process. Stage 2 qualifies the
process at commercial scale. Stage 3 uses routine production data to keep showing
that the process remains in a state of control.

Final testing cannot replace any of those stages.

During **Stage 1**, the team should know which attributes matter, which variables
drive them, and what controls prevent failure. If the only control for a critical
attribute is a final test, the design package may be thin.

During **Stage 2**, PPQ should challenge the commercial process under a defined
control strategy. Passing release specifications alone is not enough if deviations,
interventions, variability, or weak sampling strategy show the process is not
reproducible.

During **Stage 3**, continued process verification should detect drift before the
site sees repeated OOS results, recurring deviations, complaints, or rejected
batches. SPC and trend review are how the site sees the process changing while
results may still be inside specification.

The release test answers, "Did these tested samples meet criteria?" Process
validation answers, "Can this process consistently deliver quality when operated
as designed?"

Those are related questions, but they are not the same question.

## QbD and ICH Q10: testing as evidence, not the control strategy

Quality by Design pushes assurance upstream. ICH Q8(R2) frames pharmaceutical
development as designing a quality product and manufacturing process to
consistently deliver intended performance. That means the control strategy should
come from product and process understanding, not from hope that end testing will
catch everything.

ICH Q10 then gives the quality-system frame: process performance and product
quality monitoring, CAPA, change management, management review, knowledge
management, and quality risk management across the lifecycle.

In plain terms, a pharmaceutical quality system should be able to explain:

- what the product needs to do;
- which quality attributes are critical;
- which process and material variables affect those attributes;
- which controls prevent or detect unacceptable variation;
- which routine data prove the process remains controlled;
- how deviations, complaints, CAPA, and changes update the knowledge base.

If the answer is mainly "we test at the end," the control strategy is probably
too late.

## Where AQL is still useful

None of this means acceptance sampling is bad. It is useful when the defect type,
lot definition, detection method, and risk are appropriate.

Common uses include:

- incoming component or packaging inspection;
- visual defect inspection for classified major and minor defects;
- labeling and print-quality checks when combined with line controls;
- non-critical attribute checks on stable, well-understood processes;
- periodic verification of supplier or internal process performance;
- reduced or tightened inspection under defined switching rules;
- independent audit sampling where the process already has primary controls.

AQL works best when it is a decision layer on top of a controlled process. It
works poorly when it is the only thing standing between a weak process and release.

Good sampling plans should define the lot, sampling method, defect classes,
inspection level, AQLs or acceptance criteria, accept/reject numbers, escalation
rules, switching rules, retest or resampling restrictions, and links to deviation
and supplier quality procedures.

They should also explain why the plan is appropriate for the risk.

## When AQL thinking becomes dangerous

AQL is not appropriate for every quality question. Some risks need prevention,
100% verification, zero-acceptance logic, validated detection systems, or a
different quality strategy.

Be especially careful with:

- defects that could directly affect patient safety;
- identity, mix-up, or labeling failures;
- sterility assurance, endotoxin control, and contamination risks;
- data integrity failures;
- critical container closure or delivery defects;
- small lots where sample sizes give weak assurance;
- unstable processes with clustered or time-bound defects;
- lots made during unresolved deviations or equipment problems;
- inspection methods with poor detection capability;
- attempts to "test into compliance" after a known failure.

For a critical defect, saying "the AQL allowed it" is not a strong defense. The
right question is whether the quality system should tolerate any occurrence, and
what upstream control prevents recurrence.

## Batch release uses sampling as one signal

Batch release should consider the totality of evidence. Sampling and final QC
results are important, but they sit beside batch record execution, deviations,
OOS/OOT investigations, environmental and utility data, equipment status, material
genealogy, audit trails, validation state, complaint history, stability signals,
CPV trends, and regulatory commitments.

A batch that passes release testing can still be unreleaseable if a critical
deviation is unresolved. A batch with an atypical trend can require investigation
even if every result remains within specification. A lot that passes AQL can still
trigger supplier CAPA if defect trends worsen over time.

Release is not a game of finding one passing document. It is a documented quality
decision built from the full evidence package.

## Common misreadings

**"Accepted means zero defects."** No. It means the sample met the plan's decision
rule.

**"A bigger AQL number is stricter."** No. A smaller AQL is stricter.

**"If we increase the sample size enough, we can fix the process."** No. Larger
samples can improve detection, but they do not create process control.

**"AQL is the defect rate we are allowed to ship."** No. It is not a target and
should not be treated as permission to run at that quality level.

**"Passing final testing proves validation."** No. Validation is lifecycle evidence
that the process can consistently deliver quality.

**"In-spec means no investigation is needed."** Not always. In-spec data can still
show special-cause signals, OOT behavior, process drift, or release-relevant
exceptions.

**"More testing is always more assurance."** Not if the sampling is poorly
designed, the defect is clustered, the method lacks sensitivity, or the real issue
is process understanding.

## Practical checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing a sampling or release strategy:

1. What quality risk is the sampling plan meant to control or detect?
2. Is the lot definition scientifically meaningful?
3. Are defects classified by severity with justified criteria?
4. Are AQLs or acceptance criteria risk-based, approved, and current?
5. Does the inspection method have adequate detection capability?
6. Are inspectors trained and qualified for the defect types?
7. Are switching, escalation, resampling, and rejection rules defined?
8. Does the plan handle clustered, time-bound, or equipment-specific defects?
9. What upstream controls prevent the defect before sampling?
10. How do results trend over time by supplier, line, product, shift, or defect
    class?
11. When does a trend trigger deviation, CAPA, supplier action, or change control?
12. What evidence beyond sampling is needed before batch release?

If the checklist reveals that sampling is carrying the whole control strategy,
the system is asking the wrong tool to do too much.

## The practical takeaway

Acceptance sampling is a disciplined way to make a lot decision under uncertainty.
It is not a declaration of perfection.

Final QC testing is essential evidence. It is not a substitute for product and
process understanding.

The mature quality question is not, "How much can we test at the end?" It is,
"What controls make quality likely before the final test, and what data prove
those controls are still working?"

That is what "you can't test quality into a batch" really means.
