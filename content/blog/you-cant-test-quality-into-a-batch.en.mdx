# You can't test quality into a batch: AQL and acceptance sampling

A famous quality maxim: **you cannot test quality into a product** — you build it
in. Acceptance sampling is the honest version of that idea. When you inspect a
sample of a batch (say, visual inspection of vials, or attributes of a finished
device) you are making a **statistical decision about the whole batch from a part of
it.** It is powerful, and it is routinely misunderstood.

## What AQL actually means

**AQL** — the Acceptance Quality Limit — is the worst quality level that is still
considered **acceptable as a process average.** It is not a target, and it is not a
promise that an accepted batch has zero defects. A plan with an AQL of, say, a small
percent will **accept** batches running at around that defect level most of the time.

So "we sample to an AQL" does not mean "defect-free." It means "we accept batches
whose underlying quality is at or better than this limit, with a defined
probability."

## Two risks, always in tension

Every sampling plan balances two risks:

- **Producer's risk** — rejecting a genuinely good batch (a false alarm)
- **Consumer's risk** — accepting a genuinely bad batch (the dangerous one)

You cannot drive both to zero with a finite sample. A bigger sample sharpens the
decision; a smaller sample is cheaper but blunter. The standard plans (e.g.
ANSI/ASQ Z1.4, ISO 2859) encode this trade-off into defined sample sizes and
accept/reject numbers by lot size and inspection level.

## The common misreadings

- **"Accepted means zero defects."** No — it means the sample met the
  accept number; defects can still exist in the un-inspected units.
- **"A bigger AQL number is stricter."** It is the opposite — a smaller AQL is
  tighter.
- **"Sampling replaces process control."** It does not. Sampling is a final check on
  a process that should already be in control — which is the whole point of "you
  cannot test quality in."
