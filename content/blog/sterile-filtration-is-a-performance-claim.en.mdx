# Sterilizing filtration is a performance claim, not a pore size

It is tempting to think a sterilizing-grade filter is defined by its pore size. It
is not. The "0.22 micron" rating is a **label**; the real claim is that the filter
achieves **full bacterial retention under your worst-case process conditions**.
That distinction drives the whole control strategy.

## Retention is validated, not assumed

You cannot assume retention from the rating. You **demonstrate** it with a
product-specific **bacterial retention validation** — challenging the filter with
*Brevundimonas diminuta* under worst-case conditions for that filter, product, and
process. Different products behave differently; a filter that retains in one matrix
may not in another.

## The integrity test is a surrogate

You cannot retention-test every production filter, so you run an **integrity test**
(bubble point or diffusion) instead. The integrity test is only meaningful because
its limits were **correlated to bacterial retention during validation**. It is a
routine surrogate for the validated performance, not an independent pass/fail.

- **Pre-use** (post-sterilization) integrity test — and PUPSIT where required
- Filter within **validated parameters** (pressure, time, volume)
- **Post-use** integrity test — the one that matters most

## Why the post-use test is decisive

The post-use integrity test confirms the filter that **actually processed the
batch** was still integral. If it fails, the sterility of the entire batch is in
question — so a failure triggers an investigation and a batch-impact assessment,
not a release.

## Common mistakes

- Relying on the 0.22 micron rating without product-specific retention data
- Mis-correlating or skipping the integrity-test limits
- Exceeding validated pressure or batch volume
- Treating a marginal integrity result as a pass
