# Sterilizing filtration is a performance claim, not a pore size

It is tempting to think a sterilizing-grade filter is defined by its pore size.
It is not. The "0.22 micron" rating is a useful label, but the real claim is
that the filter, in the actual product and process, can deliver a sterile
effluent by retaining microorganisms under defined worst-case conditions.

That distinction changes the way you review the process. A filter catalog page
does not release a batch. A pore-size label does not prove bacterial retention.
And an integrity test is not magic by itself. Sterilizing filtration is a chain
of evidence: filter selection, product compatibility, bacterial-retention
validation, validated operating limits, pre-use and post-use integrity testing,
bioburden control, aseptic connections, and QA disposition.

The practical question is not "Did we use a 0.22 micron filter?" The practical
question is: "Can we prove that this filter assembly, used this way, for this
product, within these limits, remained capable of its validated function?"

## The filter rating is only the starting point

Filter ratings are not interchangeable promises. Two filters with similar
nominal ratings can behave differently because membrane material, pore
structure, wetting fluid, product chemistry, pressure, temperature, flow rate,
protein load, viscosity, surfactants, preservatives, and fouling profile all
matter.

That is why sterile filtration should be treated as a process step, not a
component purchase. The filter must be selected for the product and then
validated for the intended use. A rating can help choose candidates, but it does
not replace the validation package.

Good filter selection asks:

- Is the product compatible with the membrane and housing materials?
- Does the product wet the membrane correctly for the chosen integrity test?
- Is there unacceptable adsorption of API, preservative, protein, or excipient?
- Can the filter handle the intended volume, time, pressure, temperature, and
  bioburden load?
- Is prefiltration needed to protect the sterilizing filter from fouling?
- Is the filter assembly sterilized, installed, tested, and used in a way that
  preserves the aseptic boundary?

If these questions are not answered, the 0.22 micron label is doing more work
than it should.

## Retention is validated, not assumed

The core evidence is product-specific bacterial retention validation. In the
classic model, the filter is challenged with *Brevundimonas diminuta* because it
is small enough to challenge a sterilizing-grade membrane. But the important
point is not just the organism name. The study must represent the product and
process conditions that make retention hardest.

Worst-case conditions may include:

- the maximum validated filtration pressure;
- the longest processing time or hold exposure;
- the highest intended product volume or filter loading;
- the most challenging temperature range;
- minimum or maximum product concentration where that affects viscosity,
  surface tension, wetting, or microbial survival;
- the most difficult formulation, if a platform filter is used across related
  products;
- the sterilization method and number of sterilization cycles applied to the
  filter;
- the expected process bioburden before filtration.

A strong validation package explains why the study conditions are worst case.
A weak package simply says "tested with B. diminuta" and leaves the reviewer to
guess whether the real process could be more stressful than the validation run.

## Integrity testing is a surrogate for retention

You cannot perform a destructive bacterial-retention challenge on every
production filter. Routine manufacturing therefore uses physical integrity tests
such as bubble point, forward flow, diffusive flow, or pressure hold. These
tests are valuable because they are correlated to retention data during filter
validation.

That correlation is the key. An integrity-test limit is meaningful only when it
is connected to the validated bacterial-retention claim. If the limit is copied
from a generic certificate without being reconciled to the filter, product,
wetting fluid, test gas, temperature, and validated method, the routine test can
become a ritual instead of evidence.

When reviewing an integrity-test record, look for:

- the correct filter part number, membrane, lot, and area;
- the correct test method and instrument;
- verified wetting fluid and wetting procedure;
- temperature compensation where required;
- the validated acceptance limit;
- clear pass/fail result with no manual reinterpretation;
- traceability between pre-use test, production filter, batch record, and
  post-use test.

The best integrity record is boring. It matches the approved method, stays
within validated limits, and leaves no ambiguity about which filter was tested.

## Pre-use, PUPSIT, and post-use are different controls

Pre-use, post-sterilization integrity testing (PUPSIT) is intended to verify
the sterilized filter assembly before product filtration begins. EU GMP Annex 1
expects sterilizing filters to be integrity tested before use after
sterilization where applicable, or for the contamination control strategy and
quality risk management rationale to justify the chosen approach.

PUPSIT is not the same control as post-use integrity testing:

| Control | What it answers | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pre-use before sterilization, where used | Was the filter intact before sterilization? | Useful setup check, but does not prove the sterilized assembly is intact. |
| PUPSIT | Is the sterilized filter assembly intact before product exposure? | Detects damage or setup problems before the batch passes through the filter. |
| Post-use integrity test | Was the filter that processed the batch still integral after filtration? | The decisive batch-impact control. If it fails, sterility assurance is in question. |

The post-use test is especially important because it confirms the filter that
actually processed the batch remained integral. FDA's aseptic processing
guidance emphasizes post-filtration integrity testing to detect leaks or
perforations that may have occurred during use, and expects the production
filter integrity specification to be consistent with bacterial retention
validation data.

In plain language: PUPSIT helps prevent a flawed filter from being used; the
post-use test helps decide whether the batch can still be trusted.

## The process limits are part of the claim

Sterilizing filtration is valid only inside the qualified operating window. If
the process exceeds validated limits, the filter may still be physically intact
but the validated claim is no longer clean.

Common parameters that need control include:

- maximum differential pressure;
- maximum flow rate, if defined;
- maximum process time;
- maximum volume per filter area;
- product temperature;
- product concentration or formulation variant;
- prefilter configuration;
- hold time before and after filtration;
- bioburden before the sterilizing filter;
- number of sterilization cycles for the filter assembly;
- assembly orientation, venting, flushing, and wetting steps.

A deviation in any of these areas should be assessed against the validation
package, not explained away as "the filter passed integrity." Integrity is only
one part of the evidence. The process also has to remain inside the conditions
under which retention was demonstrated.

## Bioburden before filtration still matters

Sterilizing filtration should not be used as a way to rescue an uncontrolled
upstream process. High or unusual bioburden increases risk in several ways:

- It can challenge the filter beyond the validated load.
- It can increase fouling and pressure.
- It can reveal a loss of control in solution preparation, hold time, water,
  equipment cleaning, or transfer.
- It can create endotoxin or other product-quality concerns even if cells are
  retained.

The prefiltration bioburden result is therefore not decorative data. It is part
of the filtration control strategy. If the result exceeds alert/action limits,
or if the organism identity is concerning, QA should connect the event to the
validated filtration conditions and the broader contamination control strategy.

For the upstream microbiology context, see [Bioburden testing: what it is and
why it matters](/blog/what-is-bioburden-testing) and
[Microbial identification: knowing your bug](/blog/microbial-identification-knowing-your-bug).

## When a post-use integrity test fails

A failed post-use integrity test is not a paperwork inconvenience. It challenges
the sterility assurance of the batch that passed through the filter.

A disciplined investigation should establish:

1. Was the correct filter assembly tested?
2. Was the integrity-test method performed correctly?
3. Was the filter wetted correctly and fully?
4. Were instrument setup, gas, temperature, tubing, and connections correct?
5. Did the failure repeat after an approved retest procedure, if retesting is
   allowed by SOP?
6. Did filtration exceed validated pressure, volume, time, or temperature?
7. Was there unusual fouling, slow flow, high bioburden, or product
   incompatibility?
8. Was the filter damaged during installation, sterilization, use, or removal?
9. Was redundant filtration used, and what is the integrity status of each
   filter?
10. What is the batch-impact conclusion and QA disposition?

The investigation should separate test execution error from true filter failure,
but it should not use retesting to shop for a passing result. If the evidence
cannot support the validated retention claim, the batch impact remains serious.

## Redundant filtration does not remove the need for evidence

Some processes use two sterilizing-grade filters in series. Redundant
filtration can reduce risk, especially where there is concern about a single
filter failure. But it does not make the process self-justifying.

The validation package still needs to define which filter is the primary
sterilizing filter, how each filter is sterilized and tested, what the allowed
pressure and loading are, and how batch impact is assessed if one filter fails
post-use integrity. The presence of a second filter should be part of a
documented strategy, not a vague reassurance.

In a strong record, the two-filter setup is reflected in:

- approved process flow diagrams;
- batch record steps;
- filter lot and assembly traceability;
- pre-use/PUPSIT and post-use integrity records;
- validation rationale for the filtration train;
- deviation logic for each possible failure pattern.

## Practical review checklist

Before release or QA approval of a sterile filtration step, check whether the
package can answer these questions:

- **Filter selection:** Is the selected filter compatible with the product,
  process, sterilization method, and container/line setup?
- **Retention validation:** Does product-specific bacterial-retention evidence
  cover worst-case pressure, time, volume, temperature, and formulation?
- **Integrity correlation:** Are routine integrity-test limits linked to the
  retention validation and approved method?
- **PUPSIT rationale:** Is PUPSIT performed where required, or is the CCS/QRM
  rationale for not performing it documented and approved?
- **Process control:** Did the actual batch stay inside validated pressure,
  volume, time, temperature, and hold limits?
- **Bioburden:** Was prefiltration bioburden controlled and assessed?
- **Aseptic setup:** Were connections, sterilization, transfer, and hold steps
  consistent with the approved sterile process?
- **Post-use integrity:** Did the production filter pass post-use integrity
  testing without ambiguity?
- **Deviations:** Were any excursions investigated against the validation
  package, not only against the batch record?
- **Disposition:** Does QA have enough evidence to support the sterility
  assurance claim?

## Key takeaway

Sterilizing filtration is not a pore-size shortcut. It is a validated performance
claim that must survive real manufacturing conditions. The filter rating starts
the conversation; bacterial retention validation, integrity-test correlation,
PUPSIT or documented risk rationale, controlled process parameters, bioburden
control, and post-use integrity testing finish it.

When the evidence is complete, the batch record tells a simple story: the right
filter was selected, validated, installed, tested, used within its proven limits,
and confirmed integral after use. When that story has gaps, the 0.22 micron
label cannot fill them.
