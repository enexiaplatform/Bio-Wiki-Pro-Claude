# Microbiology result review, data flow and release-capacity planning

Testing is not complete when incubation ends or a colony count is entered. The
decision package must preserve the original observations, method context, controls,
calculations, exceptions, review and authorized approval.

```text
sample and method execution
→ contemporaneous raw observations and metadata
→ calculation and control assessment
→ exception and investigation linkage
→ independent technical review
→ authorized result/report approval
→ downstream release, trend or escalation decision
```

Atlas can map this flow and its capacity. It does not approve results, define the
site's electronic-record controls, invalidate data or make batch disposition.

## Define the decision package

For each method/application identify the records needed to reconstruct:

- sample identity, source, custody and preparation;
- method, specification and version;
- analyst, date, time and location;
- media, reagents, cultures and consumable lots;
- equipment, incubation and storage records;
- controls and acceptance evidence;
- original counts, observations, images or instrument files;
- calculations, dilution and reporting unit;
- atypical colonies, invalidity or deviations;
- repeats, resamples or additional testing and authority;
- result, interpretation and applicable limit;
- executor, reviewer and approver actions;
- links to trend, investigation and downstream decision.

A summarized result without its context cannot support an effective technical review.

## Map the data lifecycle

Record where data are:

- created;
- temporarily held;
- transcribed or transferred;
- calculated or transformed;
- corrected or annotated;
- reviewed and approved;
- reported or interfaced;
- retained, retrieved and archived;
- linked to investigations or changes.

Include paper, spreadsheets, LIMS, instruments, images, counters, incubator records,
identification systems and email/manual handoffs. The system boundary must not omit
“temporary” or supporting data used to reach the conclusion.

FDA's official [Data Integrity and Compliance With Drug CGMP Q&A](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/data-integrity-and-compliance-drug-cgmp-questions-and-answers)
describes data integrity in terms of complete, consistent and accurate data across the
data lifecycle. The site's current requirements determine the exact control set.

## Separate review layers

Possible layers include:

### Execution self-check

Confirm completion, legibility, traceability, calculations, controls and immediate
exceptions before the record leaves the analyst.

### Independent technical review

Assess method adherence, sample preparation, material/equipment status, controls,
raw observations, calculations, atypical findings, audit trails or relevant metadata,
and consistency with the reported result.

### Authorized approval

Confirm the package is complete for its intended report or downstream decision and
that open deviations/investigations are appropriately handled.

### Downstream decision

Product release, water-system action, EM escalation, media release or another quality
decision may require evidence beyond the laboratory result. Do not merge laboratory
approval with final disposition unless governance explicitly assigns both.

## Build a review requirements matrix

For each data source define:

- original record and system;
- metadata or audit trail required;
- calculation or transcription check;
- control and acceptance review;
- reviewer role and qualification;
- independence requirement;
- review timing and due event;
- exception escalation;
- approval and signature meaning;
- retention and retrieval requirement.

FDA's [Laboratory Controls Q&A](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/guidances-drugs/questions-and-answers-current-good-manufacturing-practice-requirements-laboratory-controls)
states that laboratory records should be complete and subject to adequate review,
including failing, passing, suspect and obvious-error data in the context discussed.

## Convert review into workload

For each result package estimate:

- analyst completion and self-check time;
- data transcription or interface verification;
- calculation review;
- plate/image/observation review;
- audit-trail or metadata review where applicable;
- deviation and investigation cross-reference;
- technical-review time;
- query, correction and resubmission loops;
- approval and report issuance;
- trend entry or follow-up;
- archive and retrieval checks.

Use different times for routine negative results, countable enumeration, presumptive
detections, identification packages, suitability studies and investigations.

## Model reviewer queues and cutoffs

Place packages on a calendar using:

- incubation/read completion time;
- analyst record-completion time;
- reviewer shift and qualification;
- batch-release or reporting deadline;
- weekend and holiday coverage;
- priority and escalation rules;
- query turnaround;
- dependency on identification or investigation;
- external laboratory report receipt;
- QA or client decision windows.

A result may finish on time and still miss release because the only qualified reviewer
is unavailable or the record reaches review after a daily cutoff.

## Control corrections and queries

Define:

- how the original entry remains visible;
- who may correct, annotate or reopen;
- reason and timestamp;
- supporting evidence;
- whether calculation/result changes;
- need for second review;
- effect on issued reports or downstream decisions;
- recurring-error trend and CAPA trigger.

Do not optimize throughput by encouraging reviewers to correct analysts' records
without traceable return, explanation and ownership.

## Link exceptions without losing routine evidence

The package should show:

- failed or atypical control;
- deviation, OOS/OOT or investigation identifier;
- immediate action and material/sample status;
- authorized additional testing;
- all original and subsequent results;
- scientific conclusion and scope;
- approval and product/process impact;
- report wording and unresolved limitation.

An investigation system link should not remove the original observation from the
laboratory record.

## Design resilience and continuity

Assess:

- electronic-system or network downtime;
- manual fallback and later reconciliation;
- reviewer absence or workload spike;
- external report or interface failure;
- image/file storage loss;
- audit-trail access delay;
- urgent result and after-hours approval;
- backlog recovery after outage;
- common-cause failure across data and backup systems.

Fallback records need controlled identifiers, completeness, review and reconciliation.
Printing a blank form after the system fails is not a complete continuity process.

## Evidence package before workflow approval

Collect:

1. method/application decision-package definition;
2. data-source and lifecycle map;
3. review requirements and roles matrix;
4. calculation, metadata and audit-trail scope;
5. executor/reviewer qualification coverage;
6. result-completion and review calendar;
7. exception, query and correction workflow;
8. approval/signature meaning;
9. downstream decision interfaces;
10. retention, retrieval and archive evidence;
11. downtime and fallback process;
12. actual review time, query, correction and backlog history.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas should not label result flow complete when:

- only the final reported value is defined;
- raw observations and contextual metadata are omitted;
- paper, spreadsheet and temporary handoffs are outside the map;
- technical review and downstream disposition are conflated;
- reviewer qualification and independence are unknown;
- review workload and calendar are absent;
- corrections can overwrite original data;
- failing, suspect or invalid data disappear from the package;
- downtime fallback and reconciliation are untested;
- actual queue and correction performance cannot be calibrated.
