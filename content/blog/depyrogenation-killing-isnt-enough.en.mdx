# Depyrogenation: why killing the bug isn't enough

Here is a counterintuitive truth of sterile manufacturing: you can sterilise a glass
vial perfectly and still have a problem. Sterilization kills microorganisms — but the
**endotoxin** (lipopolysaccharide) from dead Gram-negative bacteria is a heat-stable
molecule that survives. For a parenteral product, that endotoxin is a pyrogen — it can
cause fever and worse. Removing or destroying it is **depyrogenation**, and it is a
separate, harder job than sterilization.

## Much harsher than sterilization

Endotoxin is far more robust than the bacteria that made it. Where steam sterilization
works at around 121 degrees C, **dry-heat depyrogenation** of glassware typically runs
at much higher temperatures (often around 250 degrees C or more) for a defined time. The
conditions that destroy endotoxin comfortably also sterilise — so a validated
depyrogenation cycle delivers sterility as a bonus, but not the other way around.

## The standard: a 3-log reduction

Depyrogenation is validated to a defined performance: typically a **3-log (1000-fold)
reduction** in endotoxin. You do not assume the heat works — you prove it.

## The challenge uses endotoxin, not spores

This is the key difference from sterilization validation. A sterilization cycle is
challenged with **biological indicators** (spores). A depyrogenation cycle is challenged
with **endotoxin indicators** — items deliberately spiked with a known amount of
endotoxin, placed at worst-case locations. After the cycle, you measure the remaining
endotoxin (by LAL) and confirm the required log reduction was achieved. Using a spore BI
would tell you nothing about endotoxin destruction.

## Where it fits

Depyrogenation of glass containers (and sometimes equipment) sits right before aseptic
filling in the contamination control story — the container has to arrive at the filling
line both sterile and pyrogen-free.
