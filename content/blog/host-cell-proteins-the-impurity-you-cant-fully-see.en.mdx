# Host-cell proteins: the impurity you can't fully see

Host-cell proteins (HCPs) are the headline **process-related impurity** for a
biologic — the residual proteins from the cell line used to make it. The whole
control strategy hinges on one uncomfortable fact: the usual test, an **ELISA**,
can only report what its antibody reagent recognises.

## Coverage is the whole game

An HCP ELISA uses an antibody raised against a mixture of host-cell proteins. If
that antibody only recognises a fraction of the HCPs your process can leave behind,
the assay can read reassuringly "clean" while real impurities go undetected. That
is why an **antigen-coverage assessment** is central — not a footnote. A
low-coverage assay is a blind spot, not a green light.

## Where the real assurance comes from

The ELISA confirms what purification achieves; it does not, by itself, prove the
product is pure. The assurance comes from:

- **Demonstrated clearance** across orthogonal purification steps — showing HCP
  drops step by step
- An ELISA whose **coverage is understood**
- **Orthogonal methods** (mass spectrometry, 2D gels) to back up critical claims and
  check what the ELISA might miss

## The traps to avoid

- **Trusting an ELISA without a coverage assessment** — the most important one
- **The hook effect / matrix interference** — at too low a dilution, the signal can
  read falsely low; confirm **dilutional linearity** and report within the validated
  range
- **Reporting outside the validated range** — common when a result is unexpectedly
  high
- **No orthogonal confirmation** for a critical or trending result

## What good looks like

- Reagent/antibody coverage assessed and adequate
- Dilutional linearity confirmed (no matrix interference)
- Result within the validated range, against a qualified standard
- Limits justified by clearance and safety data
