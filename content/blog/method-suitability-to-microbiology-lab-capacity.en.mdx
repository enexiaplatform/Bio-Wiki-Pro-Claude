# Microbial method suitability and recovery application pack

A non-sterile microbiology laboratory cannot be sized reliably from product count
and batch volume alone.

The product matrix changes the method. A preserved liquid, oily preparation,
poorly soluble powder or strongly acidic product may inhibit microbial recovery,
interfere with sample preparation or require a different dilution,
neutralization, filtration or washing strategy.

That means method suitability is not only a validation document. It is an input
to the operating model:

```text
Product formulation and sample matrix
-> suitability challenge
-> recovery strategy
-> approved method architecture
-> media, diluent, neutralizer and control demand
-> hands-on time and incubation load
-> equipment, staffing and turnaround capacity
```

If suitability is unknown, Atlas can preserve the requirement and propose a
concept method architecture. It should not freeze the final consumable bill,
capacity load or procurement specification.

## Application boundary

This pack structures the evidence needed to connect a product or justified matrix
family to a suitable enumeration or specified-organism method. It does not define
challenge organisms, inoculum, recovery acceptance, neutralizer effectiveness,
bracketing, routine dilution or an approved method.

Create one application record per product/matrix × method × decision combination.
Enumeration suitability and specified-organism suitability may share preparation
evidence, but they do not automatically share recovery claims or acceptance logic.

## What method suitability is trying to prove

For microbial enumeration and specified-microorganism testing, a negative or low
result is meaningful only when the method can recover microorganisms in the
presence of the product preparation.

Method suitability asks whether the selected preparation and test procedure can
overcome relevant product effects while preserving recoverability. Depending on
the method and product, this can involve:

- dilution of antimicrobial components;
- a chemically appropriate neutralizer;
- membrane filtration followed by washing;
- dispersal or solubilization steps;
- alternate recovery media;
- a combination of approaches; or
- a justified alternative procedure.

The approved study defines the exact challenge organisms, inoculum, controls,
sample preparation, recovery comparison and acceptance criteria. A planning tool
must not invent those details.

USP's public information for chapters &lt;61&gt; and &lt;62&gt; distinguishes
method-suitability work from routine negative controls and discusses the use of
chapter strains or equivalent culture-collection strains. The current applicable
pharmacopoeial text and the approved site protocol remain authoritative.

## GPT and method suitability answer different questions

Growth-promotion testing asks whether a medium can support expected growth under
the defined conditions.

Method suitability asks whether the **complete product test procedure** can
recover organisms when the product matrix is present.

A media lot can pass GPT while an antimicrobial product still suppresses
recovery. Conversely, a suitability problem should not automatically be blamed
on the media lot.

For capability planning, the distinction creates different workload nodes:

| Activity | Typical planning demand |
| --- | --- |
| Media GPT | Media lots, organism panel, positive/negative controls, incubation and reading |
| Product method suitability | Product/matrix families, preparations, dilutions, neutralizers, challenge work, recovery calculations and review |
| Routine product testing | Approved preparation, routine controls, physical executions, incubation and reporting |

Combining all three into one generic "microbiology test" count hides resource
demand and review risk.

## Build a product-family suitability matrix

The planning record should be product-specific but does not need to expose a
confidential formulation in an uncontrolled system. Capture the minimum facts
needed to distinguish method behavior:

- product or justified matrix family identifier;
- dosage form and physical characteristics;
- preservative or antimicrobial-risk note;
- solubility, dispersibility and filtration considerations;
- microbial enumeration requirement;
- specified-organism requirement and applicable panel source;
- sample amount and initial preparation concept;
- proposed or approved dilution scheme;
- neutralizer or rinse strategy;
- suitability status;
- controlled protocol/report reference;
- approved method identifier and revision;
- products covered by bracketing or matrixing, if justified; and
- change triggers that require reassessment.

Use explicit status values such as:

| Status | Planning meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Approved | Controlled evidence is available for the represented product/matrix and method |
| Pending | Work is planned or under review; dependent outputs remain provisional |
| Unknown | No defensible status was supplied; treat as a blocker |
| Not applicable | A qualified rationale establishes why suitability is not required for the represented scope |

"We have always used this diluent" is not a suitability status.

## Translate the approved recovery strategy into method architecture

Once suitability evidence is available, update the operational method model.
Record each step that materially affects workload or resources:

1. Sample receipt and controlled storage.
2. Sample quantity, subdivision and preparation.
3. Dilution, dispersal, neutralization or membrane-filtration setup.
4. Rinsing, enrichment or transfer steps.
5. Enumeration plating or specified-organism media sequence.
6. Routine negative, process and positive controls where applicable.
7. Incubation conditions and occupied capacity.
8. Reading, colony interpretation and confirmatory work.
9. Calculation, second-person review and reporting.
10. Waste decontamination and cleaning.

Do not copy the controlled SOP into a public planning record. The objective is to
represent resource-bearing steps and maintain a trace to the approved method.

## How suitability changes the method bill of materials

A concept BOM may assume standard media, diluent, plates and controls. Suitability
can materially change that list.

### Diluent volume

Additional dilution may increase prepared diluent, vessels, pipettes, tips and
handling steps. It can also increase the number of plates or filtration units.

### Neutralizer

A product-specific neutralizer creates procurement, preparation, expiry,
compatibility, storage and QC dependencies. The neutralizer name in a planning
record is not proof of effectiveness or lack of toxicity.

### Membranes and rinse fluid

Membrane filtration can shift demand toward filtration assemblies, membranes,
rinse fluid, pumps/manifolds and aseptic manipulation time. Product filterability
and organism recovery remain method-specific.

### Selective and enrichment media

Specified-organism procedures may require multiple enrichment and selective
stages. The actual panel and sequence determine media types, containers,
incubation and transfer workload.

### Reference organisms and controls

Suitability work creates challenge-organism preparation, inoculum verification,
control and documentation demand beyond routine batch testing.

Atlas should label quantities as site-confirmation-required until the controlled
method and execution allocation are reconciled.

## Convert method steps into capacity demand

Suitability affects at least five capacity dimensions.

### Analyst hands-on time

More dilution, neutralization, filtration, washing, transfers and controls increase
preparation and execution time. A standard time must include setup, labeling,
handling, reading and documentation, not only inoculation.

### Biological safety cabinet or controlled work area

Added manipulations can increase occupied cabinet time and create concurrency
limits. Aggregate monthly hours may look acceptable while peak preparation windows
conflict.

### Incubator plate-days

Additional plates, media stages and conditions increase occupied capacity. Count
usable positions under the approved loading pattern, not nominal empty-chamber
volume.

### Autoclave and waste load

Prepared media, diluent, reusable items and contaminated waste can compete for
sterilizer cycles. Prepared-media purchasing may remove some preparation load but
does not remove receiving, storage, GPT or waste dependencies.

### Review and investigation capacity

Recovery calculations, atypical controls, failed suitability work and method
changes require technical review. A staffing model based only on routine execution
hours will understate specialist demand.

## Separate one-time qualification demand from routine demand

Method-suitability studies are not necessarily executed with every routine batch,
but they create lifecycle workload.

Model at least three demand classes:

| Demand class | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Initial/transfer | New product, site transfer, new method or new matrix family |
| Change-triggered | Formula, preservative, strength, supplier, sample preparation, neutralizer, equipment or method change |
| Routine | Approved batch testing and its required controls |

For a launch portfolio, many suitability studies can cluster before commercial
testing begins. That peak technical workload may drive temporary staffing,
contract-laboratory support, incubator demand and reference-strain planning even
when steady-state batch volume is modest.

## Bracketing and family coverage require evidence

Grouping products can reduce redundant studies only when the scientific rationale
shows that the selected worst case represents the covered matrices and method
challenges.

A useful coverage record states:

- the products and strengths covered;
- critical matrix attributes compared;
- the proposed worst-case rationale;
- preservative or antimicrobial differences;
- preparation and dilution equivalence;
- method and organism-panel equivalence;
- exclusions; and
- change triggers that break the bracket.

Atlas should not infer family coverage from dosage-form name alone. Two creams or
two oral liquids can behave very differently.

## Change control closes the lifecycle

Suitability evidence must remain connected to product and method change.
Reassessment may be needed when a change affects:

- formulation, preservative or active concentration;
- raw-material grade or supplier;
- manufacturing process or hold time;
- container or sample presentation;
- sample amount or preparation;
- neutralizer, diluent, rinse or membrane;
- media or organism panel;
- test method or equipment platform;
- testing site or contract laboratory; or
- applicable specification or market requirement.

The change assessment should identify affected product-method nodes, BOM items,
capacity loads, SOPs, training and validation commitments. Updating the method
without updating the Blueprint leaves procurement and capacity assumptions stale.

## Outsourcing does not remove the suitability dependency

When testing is outsourced, confirm:

- which site or laboratory owns the approved method;
- whether suitability covers the exact product/matrix and receiving laboratory;
- method transfer or verification status;
- sample quantity, shipping and hold-time constraints;
- raw-data and report review responsibilities;
- change-notification requirements;
- investigation interfaces; and
- backup-laboratory strategy.

The internal workload changes from execution to sample logistics, technical
oversight, review and exception management. The requirement remains in the
capability map.

## What Atlas should block when suitability is unresolved

When a selected product has pending or unknown suitability, the Blueprint should:

- retain the product and regulatory requirement trace;
- show the concept method node and evidence gap;
- mark the approved method detail as unresolved;
- prevent a proposed neutralizer from becoming an approved BOM item;
- qualify media, diluent, membrane and control quantities;
- qualify hands-on time and equipment loads;
- expose the impact on procurement and design freeze; and
- route the record to qualified microbiology review.

It should not delete the demand, assume a generic method passes, or produce a
false-precision equipment list.

## Suitability-to-capacity review checklist

- Is every product linked to an approved, pending, unknown or justified
  not-applicable suitability status?
- Does the controlled evidence cover the exact product/matrix, site and method?
- Are enumeration and specified-organism requirements treated separately?
- Are dilution, neutralization, filtration and rinse steps represented?
- Are routine controls separated from qualification challenge work?
- Does the BOM trace to approved preparation and recovery strategy?
- Are analyst hours based on observed or reviewed method steps?
- Are incubation conditions converted into usable plate-day demand?
- Are launch/change studies separated from steady-state routine demand?
- Is family bracketing supported by a documented worst-case rationale?
- Do outsource interfaces retain suitability and transfer dependencies?
- Will product or method changes update the capability model?

If several answers are open, the method architecture is suitable for discovery,
not for final capacity or procurement approval.

## Evidence required for a controlled Atlas node

1. Product/matrix × method × decision application record.
2. Controlled sample preparation and recovery architecture.
3. Challenge-organism, inoculum and control genealogy.
4. Neutralizer effectiveness and toxicity evidence where applicable.
5. Complete-method recovery results and approved acceptance criteria.
6. Product-family grouping, worst-case and exclusion rationale.
7. Routine method version and suitability coverage trace.
8. Observed BOM, touch time, equipment and incubation demand.
9. Invalid/failure, investigation and change-control pathway.
10. Estimate-to-actual cases across representative matrices and changes.

The current Atlas node is an executable concept: it can block false precision and
carry planning dependencies, but it is not a site-approved recovery strategy.
