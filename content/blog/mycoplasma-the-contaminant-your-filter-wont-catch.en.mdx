# Mycoplasma: the contaminant your filter won't catch

Most of the contamination-control story in sterile manufacturing rests on a single
assumption: a **0.2-micron filter** holds back the bugs. For mycoplasma, that assumption
breaks. Mycoplasmas are the smallest free-living organisms, they have **no cell wall**, and
they are deformable enough to **pass through a sterilizing-grade filter**. In a cell-culture
process — where you cannot terminally sterilise the living cells you are trying to grow —
that makes mycoplasma the signature contaminant, and one of the reasons biologics carry a
testing burden that small molecules do not.

## Why it hides

Mycoplasma is insidious precisely because it is undramatic. A heavy bacterial contamination
turns your culture cloudy and crashes the pH overnight — you know immediately. Mycoplasma
can reach enormous densities while leaving the medium **visually clear**. It does not kill
the host cells outright; it quietly competes for nutrients, alters their metabolism, and
skews every result you generate downstream. A contaminated bank or bioreactor can run for
weeks looking healthy while silently invalidating the data.

## No cell wall changes everything

The missing cell wall is the thread running through all of mycoplasma's awkward properties:

- **Filtration doesn't stop it** — without a rigid wall it squeezes through 0.2 micron.
- **Cell-wall antibiotics don't touch it** — penicillins and cephalosporins target wall
  synthesis, so the antibiotics routinely added to culture are useless against it.
- **It's hard to grow** — many strains are fastidious and slow, which is exactly why the
  reference detection method takes so long.

## How it's detected

The compendial expectation has two complementary arms, because no single method sees every
strain:

- **Culture method** — inoculate into broth and onto agar and watch for growth. Thorough,
  but the incubation runs up to **28 days**. That is a long time to wait when it sits on a
  release path.
- **Indicator cell culture (DNA stain)** — grow the sample on a Vero (or similar) indicator
  cell line and stain the DNA with a fluorochrome such as Hoechst; mycoplasma shows up as
  pinpoint fluorescence studding the cytoplasm around the host nuclei. This catches the
  non-cultivable strains the broth misses.

A **nucleic-acid amplification (NAT/qPCR)** method is the modern rapid alternative — a
result in hours or days rather than weeks — and is increasingly accepted once validated for
specificity and the limit of detection against the compendial methods. For a process where
the cells are perishable and the schedule is unforgiving, that speed is the whole point.

## Where it fits

Mycoplasma testing lands at the points where a living system enters or leaves a controlled
state: cell-bank qualification, the unprocessed bulk before purification, and at stages of
the production train. It sits alongside sterility and viral safety as one of the three
microbial-safety questions a biologic has to answer that a chemical drug never faces.
