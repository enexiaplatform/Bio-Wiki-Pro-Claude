# Objectionable organisms: why it's not just the count

Non-sterile products are not expected to be sterile. They may have microbial
count limits, specified-organism requirements, preservative controls, water
system controls, raw-material controls, and product-specific acceptance
criteria.

But "within the count limit" is not the same as "microbiologically acceptable."

A batch can meet its total aerobic microbial count (TAMC) and total yeast and
mould count (TYMC) limits and still be unacceptable because the organism
recovered is **objectionable** for that product, route, patient population, or
formulation.

That is the decision many weak microbiology programs miss. They review the
number, tick the limit, and forget to ask what the organism is capable of doing.

## Count answers one question

Microbial count tells you how much recoverable growth was found under the test
conditions.

That matters. A high count can signal poor raw-material quality, contaminated
water, weak cleaning, inadequate preservation, packaging failure, or process
drift. Counts support product specifications, stability review, trend review,
and investigation triggers.

But the count is only one layer of control. It does not tell you whether the
organism is pathogenic, water-associated, preservative-resistant, biofilm-forming,
spore-forming, mould-related, source-relevant, or capable of proliferating in
the product.

The number tells you **how many**.

Identification asks **what**.

Objectionability asks **so what for this product and this patient?**

## What "objectionable" means

FDA's non-sterile microbiological quality guidance explains objectionable
microorganisms as organisms that may be objectionable because of their harmful
effect on the product, potential harm to patients, or the total number of
organisms. The older CGMP preamble language is also useful: microorganisms may
be objectionable because of their number, their detrimental effect on the
product, or their potential to cause illness in the people using the product.

So objectionability is not only a species name. It is a risk conclusion.

An organism may be objectionable if it could:

- cause illness for the intended patient population;
- survive or grow in the formulation;
- degrade the product or preservative system;
- produce metabolites, odour, visible growth, gas, turbidity, or pH shift;
- indicate a water-system, biofilm, raw-material, or hygiene failure;
- be inappropriate for the route of administration;
- recur across lots, locations, or ingredients;
- appear in a product type associated with recalls or adverse events.

This is why a single low-count recovery can matter. A count of 1 CFU is not
automatically acceptable if the identity and route make it a credible patient or
product risk.

## Specified organisms are not the whole list

USP microbial limits testing separates enumeration from specified-organism
testing. USP &lt;61&gt; is the enumeration chapter. USP &lt;62&gt; covers tests for
specified microorganisms. USP &lt;1111&gt; provides acceptance criteria by product
type and route.

Specified organisms are named organisms or groups that must be absent under the
applicable criteria. Examples often include organisms such as *Escherichia coli*,
*Salmonella*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Staphylococcus aureus*, bile-tolerant
Gram-negative bacteria, *Candida albicans*, or *Clostridia*, depending on the
material, dosage form, and route.

But passing the specified-organism panel does not prove that every possible
objectionable organism is absent. It proves that the test did not detect the
specified organisms under the validated method and acceptance criteria.

"Specified" is a compendial or product-specification requirement.

"Objectionable" is a product-risk judgement.

The two overlap, but they are not identical.

## Why there is no universal list

There is no single universal list of objectionable organisms because the risk is
context-dependent.

The same organism can have different meaning in different products:

- A low-level environmental organism in a dry oral tablet may be a trend item.
- A waterborne Gram-negative in an aqueous oral solution may be a product-impact
  concern.
- *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* in a topical product used on damaged skin may be
  serious.
- A mould in a product used by immunocompromised patients may be more concerning
  than the same recovery in a lower-risk context.
- *Burkholderia cepacia* complex in an aqueous non-sterile product can be a
  major red flag because of survival in water systems, association with recalls,
  and risk to vulnerable patients.

This is not microbiology trivia. It is batch disposition.

## The route changes the risk

Route of administration is one of the biggest drivers.

**Oral solid products** are often lower risk than aqueous products because low
water activity may prevent growth. That does not remove the need for microbial
control, but it changes the risk argument.

**Oral liquids and suspensions** carry more concern when they are aqueous,
preserved, multidose, pediatric, geriatric, or used by patients with compromised
health. Waterborne Gram-negative organisms deserve special attention.

**Topical products** depend on intended use. Intact-skin use is not the same as
use on broken skin, wounds, mucosa, burns, or compromised barriers.

**Nasal, inhalation-adjacent, otic, and ophthalmic-adjacent products** raise the
bar because the route may bring organisms close to sensitive tissues or
vulnerable anatomy.

**Products for immunocompromised populations** require a more conservative
decision. The organism does not need to be a classic pathogen in healthy adults
to become meaningful in a vulnerable population.

That is why a sound assessment starts with product use, not the colony count.

## Product formulation changes the risk

Formulation determines whether organisms can survive, die off, or grow.

Key questions include:

- Is the product aqueous?
- What is the water activity?
- Is it preserved, and has preservative effectiveness been demonstrated?
- Could the recovered organism tolerate the preservative, pH, osmolarity, or
  excipients?
- Is the container multidose?
- Can the package introduce contamination during use?
- Is the product stored at room temperature, refrigerated, or used over a long
  in-use period?
- Could the organism degrade active ingredient, excipients, preservative, or
  product appearance?

An organism that cannot grow in one product may proliferate in another. A
microbial limit is useful only when it sits inside a broader understanding of
the formulation.

## Burkholderia cepacia complex is the classic warning sign

*Burkholderia cepacia* complex (BCC) is one of the organisms that taught the
industry not to rely on count alone.

FDA has repeatedly highlighted BCC contamination risk in non-sterile,
water-based drug products. BCC organisms can survive in aqueous environments,
persist in manufacturing systems, and cause serious infections, especially in
vulnerable patients. They have been associated with recalls and contamination
events involving non-sterile products.

This does not mean BCC is the only organism that matters. It means BCC is a
useful case study: an organism's ecology, patient risk, and product route can
make it objectionable even when the numerical count looks small.

For an aqueous non-sterile product, an unidentified Gram-negative rod should not
be waved through simply because TAMC passes. The site should identify it,
evaluate product risk, investigate likely sources, and document the disposition
rationale.

## Where objectionable organisms come from

The organism identity often points toward the source.

Common source categories include:

- purified water systems and points of use;
- wet equipment, hoses, tanks, gaskets, drains, and dead legs;
- raw materials with natural bioburden;
- preserved ingredients or excipients with poor incoming control;
- inadequate cleaning or hold-time control;
- poor environmental controls in non-sterile manufacturing;
- operator handling, open processing, or transfer steps;
- contaminated packaging components or closures;
- laboratory contamination during sampling or testing.

The investigation should not stop at "organism found." It should ask how that
organism could enter, survive, recur, and reach the batch.

## What a good SOP should define

Because objectionability requires judgement, the site needs a written decision
process. Without it, batch decisions become inconsistent and vulnerable to bias.

A strong SOP or microbial risk assessment defines:

- when isolates must be identified;
- how deep the ID must go: group, genus, species, or sequencing;
- which product routes require conservative escalation;
- which organism groups are pre-defined as high concern for each product family;
- how to evaluate organisms not on the specified-organism list;
- when QA, microbiology, medical safety, regulatory affairs, or product
  development must be consulted;
- how recurring organisms are trended;
- how batch impact is assessed;
- what evidence is needed to release, reject, reprocess, or continue
  investigation.

The procedure should be written before the event. A batch under pressure is a
bad moment to invent microbiological logic.

## A practical assessment framework

When an isolate appears in a non-sterile product or relevant material, work
through the decision in a fixed order.

1. Confirm the test validity. Check controls, growth promotion, incubation,
   calculation, sample handling, and method suitability.
2. Confirm the result. If the organism matters to disposition, make sure the
   culture is pure enough and the ID method is suitable.
3. Identify the organism to the level needed for the decision.
4. Check whether it is a specified organism for the product, route, or
   specification.
5. Check route risk and patient population.
6. Check formulation risk: aqueous nature, preservative, pH, water activity,
   in-use period, and package.
7. Assess organism ecology: water, human, soil, raw material, mould, spore,
   biofilm, opportunistic pathogen.
8. Review history: previous lots, raw materials, water system, EM, equipment,
   complaints, and recalls.
9. Decide batch impact: acceptable with rationale, hold pending more evidence,
   reject, recall assessment, or broader investigation.
10. Feed the conclusion back into trends, specifications, supplier controls,
    water controls, cleaning, and CAPA.

That sequence prevents the most common mistake: jumping straight from "count
passed" to "batch passes."

## Documentation that survives audit

An objectionable-organism decision should be easy for QA and an inspector to
follow.

Good documentation includes:

- product, lot, route, patient population, and intended use;
- sample source and test method;
- TAMC/TYMC result and specified-organism result;
- isolate identity and method confidence;
- relevant product specification and compendial expectations;
- formulation factors such as water activity, preservative, pH, and in-use
  risk;
- prior history for the organism, product, water system, raw material, or site;
- patient/product risk rationale;
- batch disposition decision and approvers;
- investigation and CAPA links where needed.

The goal is not to write a long essay for every colony. The goal is to show that
the decision was scientific, consistent, and made by the right functions.

## Common weak decisions

These patterns create regulatory risk:

- releasing because TAMC/TYMC passed without identifying a concerning isolate;
- treating USP &lt;62&gt; as the complete objectionable-organism list;
- using "not pathogenic" without considering route or vulnerable users;
- accepting an unidentified Gram-negative in an aqueous product;
- ignoring repeat low-level recoveries because each individual lot passed;
- failing to connect product isolates with water-system or raw-material trends;
- relying on supplier CoA results without incoming-risk logic;
- changing preservative, supplier, or formula without updating microbial risk;
- having no pre-defined escalation path for BCC or other high-concern organisms;
- documenting "no impact" without explaining why the organism is not
  objectionable.

Most of these are not testing failures. They are reasoning failures.

## Key takeaway

For non-sterile products, microbiological quality is not only a count limit. It
is a decision about organism identity, product formulation, route of
administration, patient vulnerability, survival/growth potential, and historical
signals.

A product can pass the count and still fail the risk question.

The best microbiology programs count accurately, identify deeply where the
decision needs it, define objectionability before a crisis, and document the
batch decision in a way QA can defend.
