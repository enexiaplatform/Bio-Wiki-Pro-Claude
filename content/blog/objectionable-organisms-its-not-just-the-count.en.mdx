# Objectionable organisms: why it's not just the count

Non-sterile products are allowed to contain some microorganisms — there are microbial
**count** limits, not a requirement for sterility. But here is the catch that trips
people up: a product can be **within its count limit and still fail**, because it
contains an **objectionable organism.** For non-sterile products, the identity of what
you recover can matter more than the number.

## "Objectionable" is not a fixed list

There is no universal list of objectionable organisms, and that is deliberate. Whether
an organism is objectionable depends on the **product, its route of administration, the
patient population, and the dose.** The pharmacopeias name some **specified** organisms
to test for and exclude (the ones that must be absent in a given quantity), but
"objectionable" is broader and **risk-based** — it is a judgement, not a checkbox.

An organism is objectionable if it could:

- **Harm the patient** given the route and population (an oral product for the immune-
  compromised, a topical for broken skin, a nasal spray)
- **Spoil or degrade the product**, compromising its quality or efficacy
- **Proliferate** in the formulation rather than die off

## Why identity drives the decision

This is exactly why microbial identification matters in non-sterile QC. A high count of a
harmless environmental organism may be acceptable; a **single** recovery of a relevant
Gram-negative (think *Burkholderia cepacia* complex in an aqueous product, or *P.
aeruginosa* in a topical) can be a reason to reject — and historically a cause of
recalls. The count tells you how many; the ID tells you whether it is the kind that
matters.

## The decision is documented and justified

Because it is a judgement, a robust program defines **how** the objectionable-organism
assessment is made — considering the factors above — so the call is consistent and
defensible, not ad hoc. It is one of the places where micro QC is genuinely about
reasoning, not just reading a plate.
