# Characterizing a protein: why it's never one test

A small-molecule drug is a single, well-defined structure you can pin down with a
handful of tests. A therapeutic protein is the opposite: large, complex, and
**heterogeneous** by nature — a population of closely related molecules rather than one
exact entity. That is why characterizing a biologic is never one test; it is a panel of
**orthogonal** methods, each looking at a different facet.

## The facets you have to cover

Different attributes need different tools, and no single method sees them all:

- **Primary structure** — the amino acid sequence and modifications, often confirmed by
  peptide mapping with mass spectrometry
- **Higher-order structure** — the folding (secondary, tertiary structure) that
  determines whether the protein actually works; probed by spectroscopic methods
- **Charge variants** — deamidation, C-terminal lysines, and other modifications that
  shift charge, resolved by techniques like ion-exchange or capillary isoelectric
  focusing
- **Size variants and aggregates** — fragments and aggregates, separated by size
  (e.g. SEC)
- **Glycosylation** — the sugar structures that can affect activity, half-life, and
  immunogenicity

## Why higher-order structure is the subtle one

You can have the right sequence and still have a non-functional molecule if it is
**misfolded**. Higher-order structure is harder to measure than sequence but often more
directly tied to function — which is why characterization pairs structural methods with
the potency assay, the most direct functional readout.

## Characterization vs routine release

Most of this deep characterization is done extensively during **development** and to
support **comparability** (e.g. after a process change), establishing what the molecule
is. Routine **release** then monitors a focused set of the critical quality attributes
that the characterization identified as important. You do not run the entire panel on
every batch — you run the ones that matter, justified by what characterization taught
you.
