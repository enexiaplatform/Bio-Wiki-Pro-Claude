# Characterizing a protein: why it's never one test

A small-molecule drug can often be pinned down with a defined structure and a
focused set of identity and purity tests. A therapeutic protein is different. It is
large, folded, post-translationally modified, and made by a living system. Even a
well-controlled monoclonal antibody is not one perfectly identical molecule. It is
a population of closely related forms: charge variants, glycoforms, clipped forms,
oxidized forms, deamidated forms, fragments, aggregates, and other product-related
species.

That is why protein characterization is never one test. It is a case built from
orthogonal evidence. Each method asks a different question, and the answer becomes
meaningful only when the questions are connected.

ICH Q6B frames characterization broadly: physicochemical properties, biological
activity, immunochemical properties, purity, impurities, and quantity all matter.
The purpose is not academic completeness. Characterization is how you understand
what the product is, which attributes matter, which methods belong in routine
control, and which changes might alter safety or efficacy.

## The molecule is a pattern, not a single point

The hardest mental shift in biologics QC is accepting that heterogeneity is not
automatically a defect. Some variants are expected. Glycoforms, C-terminal lysine
variants, or other product-related substances may be part of the normal product
profile if they are consistent and have comparable safety and activity.

The problem starts when the pattern changes:

- a new acidic peak appears after a process change;
- glycan distribution shifts after a media or feed change;
- fragments increase during stability;
- aggregates rise after shipping simulation;
- potency drifts while mass and purity still look acceptable;
- an impurity becomes enriched in one purification step.

Characterization gives the team a language for those differences. Without it, a
change is just a chromatographic surprise. With it, the team can ask whether the
new pattern changes identity, purity, potency, stability, immunogenicity risk, or
process consistency.

## The core characterization questions

A useful protein characterization package answers at least nine questions.

1. **Is the primary sequence correct?** Peptide mapping and mass spectrometry help
   confirm that the amino acid sequence matches the intended molecule.
2. **Are terminal variants understood?** N-terminal and C-terminal heterogeneity can
   affect identity, charge, or comparability.
3. **Are disulfide bonds and free thiols controlled?** Incorrect disulfide pairing
   can alter folding, function, and aggregation tendency.
4. **What is the molecular mass profile?** Intact mass and reduced mass confirm
   chain composition, glycoform distribution, clipping, or unexpected shifts.
5. **What charge variants exist?** icIEF, CEX, or related methods reveal acidic,
   main, and basic species.
6. **What size variants exist?** SEC, CE-SDS, AUC, or related methods show
   fragments, high-molecular-weight species, and aggregates.
7. **What glycosylation pattern is present?** Released-glycan and glycopeptide
   methods connect structure to function, clearance, and immunogenicity risk.
8. **Is higher-order structure preserved?** Spectroscopy, thermal analysis,
   binding, and potency evidence help show whether the folded structure is intact.
9. **Does structure connect to biological activity?** Potency, binding, or
   functional assays show whether the molecule behaves as intended.

No one method owns the answer. The answer is the pattern across methods.

## Primary structure: peptide mapping is the anchor

Peptide mapping is one of the central identity tools for protein products. The
protein is enzymatically digested into peptides, separated, and compared to the
expected map. With mass spectrometry, the method can confirm sequence coverage,
identify post-translational modifications, locate oxidation or deamidation, and
detect unexpected variants.

In routine QC, the map may be simplified into a validated identity or purity
procedure. In development and comparability, it becomes more powerful: you can ask
whether a process change creates a new modification or changes the relative
abundance of known modifications.

The trap is treating a matching peptide map as proof that everything is fine. A
protein with the right primary sequence may still be misfolded, over-aggregated,
glycosylated differently, or functionally weaker. Sequence is necessary. It is not
sufficient.

## Mass: intact and reduced views tell different stories

Intact mass looks at the whole molecule. Reduced mass breaks a multi-chain protein,
such as an antibody, into chains so each can be assessed separately. Together they
help identify:

- expected chain masses;
- glycoform envelopes;
- missing or extra chains;
- clips and truncations;
- unconjugated or over-conjugated forms in conjugated products;
- unexpected mass shifts after process or storage stress.

Mass methods are especially useful in investigations because they can turn a vague
"new peak" into a chemical hypothesis. Is it oxidation? Deamidation? Glycation?
Clipping? A glycan shift? That hypothesis then guides the next method.

## Charge variants: not every peak is equal

Charge methods such as icIEF and cation-exchange chromatography separate acidic,
main, and basic variants. These profiles are often sensitive to deamidation,
isomerization, sialylation, C-terminal lysine, glycation, clipping, and other
modifications.

The QC mistake is to assume all charge variants have the same risk. Some are mostly
benign product-related substances. Others can affect binding, potency, clearance,
or stability. A charge profile only becomes useful when peaks are characterized and
linked to functional or clinical relevance where possible.

This is why comparability exercises care about charge. A post-change product can
meet total purity and still show a shifted charge pattern that needs explanation.

## Size and aggregation: purity is method-dependent

Size-related methods answer different questions:

- **SEC** measures soluble high-molecular-weight and low-molecular-weight species
  under chromatographic conditions.
- **CE-SDS** measures size purity under denaturing conditions, often reduced and
  non-reduced.
- **AUC or FFF** can support deeper characterization of soluble aggregates and
  larger species.
- **Particle methods** capture visible and subvisible particle risks that SEC may
  not see.

ICH Q6B is blunt about purity for biologics: it is difficult and method-dependent,
so it is assessed using a combination of analytical procedures. That principle is
especially important for aggregates and fragments. A "purity" number from one
method is not the whole purity story.

## Glycosylation: small sugar changes can have large effects

For glycoproteins, glycans can affect effector function, receptor interaction,
half-life, clearance, solubility, stability, and immunogenicity. In antibodies,
fucosylation can influence ADCC, high mannose can influence clearance, and non-human
glycan structures can raise immunogenicity concerns.

Glycan analysis can be done as released-glycan profiling, glycopeptide mapping,
intact mass support, or site-specific characterization. The right level depends on
the molecule and the risk. A simple relative glycan profile may be enough for
routine trend control, while a comparability exercise may need site-specific
evidence.

The practical QC question is not "did we measure glycans?" It is "do we know which
glycans matter for this product, and can we detect a meaningful shift?"

## Higher-order structure: the subtle part

Higher-order structure means the folded shape of the protein: secondary, tertiary,
and for multimeric proteins, quaternary structure. It is subtle because you can
have the correct sequence and mass but still lose the native conformation that
makes the molecule function.

Physicochemical tools can probe folding and thermal stability: circular dichroism,
FTIR, fluorescence, DSC, DSF, HDX-MS, NMR for smaller cases, and other appropriate
methods. But for complex biologics, Q6B notes an important reality: biological
activity can often be the strongest practical inference that higher-order structure
is intact.

That is why potency is not an afterthought. It is the functional cross-check on the
structural story. If the structural data look acceptable but potency changes, the
product is telling you that your structural panel may be missing something.

## Immunochemical properties and binding

For antibodies and receptor-binding proteins, characterization may include binding
assays, affinity, avidity, epitope specificity, cross-reactivity, or interaction
with target antigens. These methods sit between structure and function: they do not
always prove biological activity, but they can show whether the molecule still
recognizes the intended target.

Binding is also useful in comparability. A small structural difference may be less
concerning if binding and potency remain aligned. A small structural difference may
be more concerning if binding shifts in a way that could affect mechanism of
action.

## Characterization versus routine release

Deep characterization is most intensive during development, process validation,
comparability, reference standard qualification, and significant investigations.
Routine release is more focused. It uses a justified subset of methods that monitor
the critical quality attributes known to matter.

That distinction prevents two opposite mistakes:

- running an oversized development panel on every batch without a clear control
  purpose;
- using a narrow release panel as if it fully characterizes the product.

The release panel is the dashboard. Characterization is how you designed the
dashboard and learned what the warning lights mean.

## Comparability is characterization under pressure

Process changes are where characterization earns its keep. A new cell culture
condition, purification resin, site, scale, formulation, container, or analytical
method can change the product profile. The comparability question is not "are the
chromatograms identical?" It is "are any observed differences meaningful for
quality, safety, efficacy, or stability?"

Modern biosimilar and comparability guidance emphasizes a comprehensive analytical
understanding of physicochemical and biological characteristics. That logic applies
beyond biosimilars. Any biologics change control should decide which attributes are
most sensitive to the change and which methods can detect relevant differences.

A good comparability package usually includes:

- pre-defined quality attributes and risk ranking;
- side-by-side testing where appropriate;
- method qualification or validation status;
- lot selection that represents process variability;
- stability or forced-degradation support where needed;
- functional assays tied to mechanism of action;
- a clear rationale for why differences are acceptable or require action.

## What a reviewer should look for

When reviewing a protein characterization package, ask:

1. Does the method panel cover sequence, mass, charge, size, glycans, potency,
   purity, impurities, and higher-order structure where relevant?
2. Are methods orthogonal, or are several methods measuring the same narrow thing?
3. Are expected variants distinguished from impurities and degradation products?
4. Are acceptance criteria justified by clinical lots, development lots,
   manufacturing consistency, stability, and reference material data?
5. Is there a link between structural attributes and biological activity?
6. Are method limitations stated plainly?
7. Are reference standards and in-house reference materials adequately
   characterized?
8. Are comparability conclusions based on the full pattern, not one favorable test?
9. Are stability-indicating attributes included for degradation-prone changes?
10. Are investigations designed to identify what changed, not only to retest until
    the result looks familiar?

## Common weak spots

The first weak spot is method stacking without a story. A dossier can contain many
methods but still fail to explain what the product is. Orthogonality is not a word
count exercise. Each method should earn its place by answering a distinct risk
question.

The second weak spot is treating heterogeneity as either all acceptable or all bad.
Biologics need nuance. Some variants are expected product-related substances; others
are degradation products or impurities. The difference must be justified.

The third weak spot is separating potency from structure. A beautiful structural
profile does not rescue a weak functional assay. A noisy potency assay does not
excuse the absence of structural understanding. The two are meant to challenge each
other.

The fourth weak spot is losing the reference standard story. If the reference
material is poorly characterized, every comparison becomes softer. A strong
reference standard gives continuity to release, stability, comparability, and
method performance.

## Key takeaway

Protein characterization is not a hunt for one perfect identity test. It is a
coherent argument built from orthogonal methods. Sequence, mass, charge, size,
glycans, higher-order structure, purity, impurities, potency, and reference
standards all contribute a different piece. The value is not the size of the panel.
The value is whether the panel can explain the product, detect meaningful change,
and support confident control decisions.
