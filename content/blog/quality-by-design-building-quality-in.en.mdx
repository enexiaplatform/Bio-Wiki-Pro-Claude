# Quality by Design: building quality in, not testing it in

Quality by Design (QbD) is easy to reduce to a buzzword. In a real pharmaceutical
development program, it is more practical than that: it is the discipline of
showing how product quality is designed, understood, controlled, and maintained
through the lifecycle.

The older habit was often empirical: make a batch, test it, adjust the process,
and repeat until the results looked acceptable. QbD does not remove testing. It
changes what testing means. Testing becomes evidence that a designed control
strategy is working, not the main mechanism by which quality is created.

The useful question is:

**What do we understand about this product and process that allows us to control
quality before the final test result arrives?**

ICH Q8(R2) frames pharmaceutical development as the work of designing a quality
product and manufacturing process that consistently deliver intended performance.
That development knowledge supports specifications, design space, and
manufacturing controls. In other words, QbD is the bridge between development
science and routine GMP control.

## The QbD chain

QbD terms only make sense as a chain. If they are disconnected, they become
decorative language in a report.

| QbD element | Plain meaning | Evidence question |
| --- | --- | --- |
| QTPP | What the product must do for the patient and user | What clinical, route, dosage, strength, stability, and performance needs define success? |
| CQA | A product attribute that must be controlled to meet the QTPP | Which quality attributes could affect safety, efficacy, performance, or acceptability? |
| CMA | A material attribute that can influence a CQA | Which input attributes matter, and how variable are they? |
| CPP | A process parameter that can influence a CQA | Which process settings must be controlled because they affect quality? |
| Design space | A proven multidimensional operating region | What combinations of variables have evidence of acceptable quality? |
| Control strategy | The planned controls that keep quality consistent | What prevents, detects, and responds to variation? |
| Lifecycle management | Ongoing learning after approval or validation | How do routine data, changes, deviations, and reviews update the knowledge base? |

Read top to bottom, the logic is:

patient need -> product attributes -> material and process drivers -> proven
operating range -> controls -> lifecycle learning.

When a QbD package is weak, the chain breaks. A team may list CQAs without linking
them to patient or product performance. It may call a parameter "critical" without
showing the data that makes it critical. It may claim a design space that is really
just a wide operating range. Or it may write a control strategy that is only a list
of release tests.

## Start with the QTPP, not the equipment

The Quality Target Product Profile (QTPP) is the product-facing starting point.
It describes what the product is intended to be: route of administration, dosage
form, strength, delivery, release profile, sterility or microbial expectations,
stability, container closure, and other performance needs.

For example, a sterile injectable, an immediate-release tablet, a modified-release
tablet, a biologic, and an ophthalmic solution do not have the same quality risks.
Their QTPPs pull different attributes into focus.

The QTPP matters because it prevents development from starting with the wrong
question. The question is not "what process do we already have?" It is "what
product performance must the process reliably deliver?"

## CQAs are not just specification tests

Critical Quality Attributes (CQAs) are the attributes that can affect product
safety, efficacy, or quality performance. They may appear in release
specifications, stability specifications, in-process controls, characterization
studies, or process monitoring.

Examples can include:

- assay or potency;
- purity and impurities;
- degradation products;
- dissolution or release profile;
- content uniformity;
- particle size;
- polymorphic form;
- water content;
- pH, osmolality, or viscosity;
- bioburden, endotoxin, or sterility assurance;
- protein aggregation, charge variants, glycans, or host-cell protein for
  biologics;
- container closure or delivery performance.

A CQA is not "critical" because the acronym looks serious. It is critical because
a credible link exists between the attribute and product performance or patient
risk.

That link should be explicit. If dissolution is a CQA, why does it matter for
performance? If aggregation is a CQA, what is the patient or immunogenicity
concern? If endotoxin is a CQA-related risk for an injectable, where is it
controlled: water, process, components, final product, or all of them?

## Criticality changes with knowledge

Early in development, teams often cast a wide net. Many attributes and parameters
may be considered potentially critical because uncertainty is high.

As knowledge increases, criticality should become sharper:

- prior knowledge suggests likely drivers;
- risk assessment ranks what might affect CQAs;
- screening studies identify important variables;
- design of experiments tests interactions and ranges;
- scale-up reveals equipment and mixing effects;
- PPQ confirms the commercial control strategy;
- CPV and APR/PQR data show whether assumptions remain true.

QbD therefore does not mean freezing the first risk assessment forever. It means
using risk assessment as a living knowledge tool. ICH Q9(R1) is especially useful
here because it stresses that quality risk management should be commensurate with
risk, based on evidence and science, and reviewed as new knowledge appears.

## From variables to design space

The design space is one of the most misunderstood QbD ideas.

A design space is not a generous specification. It is not "the widest range we
think we can defend." It is the multidimensional combination of input variables
and process parameters demonstrated to provide acceptable quality.

The word multidimensional matters. A process can behave acceptably when one
parameter changes alone but fail when two parameters move together. For example,
granulation endpoint, water addition rate, impeller speed, drying temperature, and
raw material particle size may interact. A meaningful design space understands the
combination, not only each one-factor range.

A real design-space claim usually needs:

- a clear CQA link;
- material attributes and process parameters selected from risk assessment;
- development studies or prior knowledge;
- statistically designed experiments where appropriate;
- interaction understanding;
- scale relevance;
- model assumptions and limitations;
- verification that the proposed range supports the control strategy;
- regulatory clarity on what is filed and what can move without prior approval.

ICH Q8(R2) notes that working within the approved design space is not considered a
change from a regulatory filing perspective. That flexibility is valuable, but it
is earned by data. Without the evidence, the "design space" is only a wish.

## Control strategy is more than release testing

QbD changes the role of the control strategy. Instead of relying mainly on final
product testing, the control strategy moves assurance upstream.

A mature control strategy may include:

- raw material and supplier controls;
- incoming tests or supplier qualification;
- equipment qualification and calibration;
- process parameter ranges;
- in-process controls;
- PAT or online monitoring where justified;
- hold-time controls;
- sampling plans;
- validated analytical procedures;
- microbial or contamination controls;
- environmental and utility controls;
- statistical process control;
- release and stability specifications;
- deviation, CAPA, and change-control triggers;
- continued process verification and APR/PQR review.

Final testing still matters. But final testing is a late signal. It cannot inspect
quality into a batch that was not controlled during manufacture.

and [Process validation: the three stages](/blog/process-validation-three-stages).

## How QbD changes process validation

FDA's process validation guidance aligns validation with the product lifecycle and
with ICH Q8, Q9, and Q10. That alignment is where QbD becomes operational.

The validation stages can be read as a QbD evidence path:

| Validation stage | QbD question |
| --- | --- |
| Stage 1: Process design | What do development and scale-up data show about CQAs, CPPs, material attributes, and control strategy? |
| Stage 2: Process qualification | Does the commercial process, equipment, and PPQ design reproduce the expected state of control? |
| Stage 3: Continued process verification | Do routine batches remain controlled, capable, and consistent over time? |

QbD is strongest when PPQ is not treated as a ceremony at the end. PPQ should be
the confirmation of knowledge built earlier. If the team cannot explain why the
sample size, acceptance criteria, process parameters, and monitoring plan are
linked to CQAs, the validation package is thin even if the batches pass.

Use the [Process Validation workflow](/workflows/process-validation) to connect
development knowledge to PPQ and CPV.

## How QbD changes specifications

Specifications should be justified by product performance, process understanding,
stability, method capability, and risk. They should not be arbitrary historical
numbers.

A QbD-minded specification asks:

- Which CQAs must be controlled by specification?
- Which controls are better placed in-process rather than at release?
- Which attributes need stability monitoring?
- Which analytical method performance characteristics matter?
- What variability is normal for a capable process?
- What trend would indicate loss of control before failure?
- What prior knowledge or platform knowledge supports the limit?

For analytical procedures, the same logic now appears through ICH Q14 and the
analytical procedure lifecycle: define the analytical need, understand method
parameters, validate fitness for purpose, and monitor performance. For more, see
[Analytical method validation: what ICH Q2 actually asks](/blog/analytical-method-validation-what-q2-asks).

## QbD and change control

QbD does not remove change control. It makes change control more intelligent.

When the product and process are well understood, a team can assess changes
against the real drivers of quality:

- Does the change affect a CQA?
- Does it move a CPP outside the validated range or approved design space?
- Does it alter a critical material attribute?
- Does it affect a model, PAT method, in-process control, or sampling plan?
- Does it require regulatory reporting or a post-approval variation?
- Does it change the CPV plan or APR/PQR review focus?
- Does it invalidate prior PPQ or method validation evidence?

ICH Q10 places change management inside the pharmaceutical quality system and
expects changes to be evaluated with appropriate expertise and risk management,
then checked after implementation for unintended impact. That is QbD in the
commercial lifecycle: do not just approve the change; verify the knowledge remains
true.

## What good QbD evidence looks like

Good QbD evidence lets a reviewer follow the story without guessing.

It usually includes:

- QTPP linked to intended use and patient or user needs;
- CQA rationale linked to safety, efficacy, performance, or quality;
- risk assessments with assumptions and uncertainty stated;
- development studies that test likely variables;
- material attribute understanding;
- process parameter classification with data support;
- interaction and scale-up knowledge;
- design-space or operating-range rationale;
- control strategy mapped to CQAs and CPPs;
- validated methods suitable for the control strategy;
- PPQ protocol rationale based on process knowledge;
- CPV charts and alert/action logic;
- change-control triggers;
- APR/PQR feedback into continual improvement.

The strongest packages are traceable. You can start at a CQA and trace backward to
the QTPP, sideways to the process and material drivers, forward to PPQ, and onward
to CPV and change control.

## What weak QbD looks like

Weak QbD is often visible quickly:

- QTPP copied from a template with no product-specific thinking;
- every attribute listed as critical, which means none are meaningfully ranked;
- risk scores with no scientific rationale;
- FMEA tables full of numbers but no learning;
- CPPs declared without data linking them to CQAs;
- one-factor-at-a-time studies used to claim complex design space;
- design space wider than the evidence supports;
- control strategy that relies mostly on final release testing;
- PPQ acceptance criteria copied from another product;
- CPV plan that tracks only pass/fail specification results;
- change control that does not revisit the development assumptions.

The common pattern is performative documentation: many tables, little knowledge.

## A practical QbD review checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing a development report, validation package, tech
transfer package, or major change.

- Can the team explain the QTPP in plain language?
- Are CQAs justified by product performance or patient risk?
- Are material attributes and process parameters linked to CQAs?
- Is criticality based on evidence, uncertainty, and risk rather than habit?
- Were interactions studied where they could matter?
- Is the design space clearly distinguished from normal operating ranges?
- Does the control strategy prevent and detect variation before release testing?
- Are analytical methods suitable for the decisions they support?
- Does PPQ confirm the control strategy rather than merely produce passing
  batches?
- Does CPV monitor meaningful CQAs, CPPs, in-process controls, and trends?
- Does APR/PQR feed learning back into the product knowledge base?
- Are changes assessed against design space, validated ranges, CQAs, and
  regulatory commitments?
- Are assumptions and uncertainty documented so future teams know what is known
  and what is only believed?

If the answer is unclear, the QbD story is not yet audit-ready.

## Key takeaway

Quality by Design is not a slogan about being modern. It is a traceable chain of
knowledge:

**QTPP -> CQAs -> material and process drivers -> design space or justified
operating ranges -> control strategy -> validation -> monitoring -> change and
continual improvement.**

The final test result still matters, but it should not be the first time you learn
whether the process made quality. QbD earns confidence earlier: during design,
through development knowledge, in PPQ, and through routine lifecycle evidence.
