# From hands-on hours to resilient QC staffing: skills, review capacity and shifts

A staffing model can be mathematically correct and operationally impossible.

The common calculation is:

```text
analyst FTE = monthly hands-on hours / productive hours per analyst-month
```

That is a useful starting point. It does not show whether the right people are
available at the right time, whether reviewers can release the work, whether
critical methods have backup coverage, or whether training and investigations
consume the apparent reserve.

A more complete planning chain is:

```text
Physical test executions
-> method-step workload
-> role and skill demand
-> time-window and shift demand
-> productive capacity
-> review and exception capacity
-> minimum coverage and resilience
-> hiring, training and qualification plan
```

Atlas currently reports aggregate analyst and review FTE as a concept estimate.
Before controlled use, that estimate must be reconciled with site methods,
observed time studies, skill matrices, operating calendars and quality-system
responsibilities.

## Separate elapsed time from hands-on time

Microbiology methods can take several days while requiring only a fraction of
that duration as analyst work. Analytical sequences can run unattended for hours
but still require preparation, setup, monitoring, review and exception handling.

For every method architecture, separate:

- sample receipt and accessioning;
- preparation and setup;
- active execution/manipulation;
- instrument or incubation elapsed time;
- scheduled handling or reading;
- calculation and data processing;
- technical review;
- QA or release review where applicable;
- cleaning and waste handling; and
- investigation/repeat work.

Do not count all elapsed time as labor. Do not exclude short but mandatory touches
that occur outside the main working window.

## Build workload by role, not one generic analyst

Different activities may require different qualifications and independence.

| Work category | Possible capacity owner |
| --- | --- |
| Sample receipt and chain of custody | Sample-management or QC support role |
| Media/reagent preparation | Qualified preparation analyst or centralized support |
| Routine method execution | Method-qualified QC analyst |
| Organism/strain handling | Qualified microbiology analyst with appropriate controls |
| Instrument setup and sequence execution | Platform- and method-qualified analyst |
| Data processing and calculations | Trained analyst under approved procedure |
| Second-person technical review | Independent qualified reviewer |
| OOS/OOT/deviation investigation | Senior analyst, supervisor, SME and QA |
| Method transfer/suitability/validation | Technical specialist or method owner |
| Equipment lifecycle activities | System owner, metrology/validation and vendor support |

One person may perform several roles. The model should still preserve the role
demand so separation, authorization and bottlenecks remain visible.

## Define productive hours explicitly

Paid hours are not equal to test-execution hours.

Productive test capacity can be reduced by:

- breaks and shift handover;
- team meetings and planning;
- GMP and safety training;
- initial and periodic qualification;
- SOP reading and change implementation;
- leave, sickness and holidays;
- equipment checks and housekeeping;
- material receipt and inventory work;
- deviations, CAPA and change control;
- audits and inspection support;
- method lifecycle projects; and
- supervision, coaching and performance management.

Use a transparent capacity ledger:

```text
paid hours
- planned non-testing time
- expected absence
- quality-system/lifecycle work
= productive hours available to modeled methods
```

Avoid applying both a low productive-hours input and a large generic reserve for
the same losses. State what each factor covers to prevent double counting.

## Calculate base execution FTE

For each role or skill pool:

```text
base FTE
= monthly role-specific workload hours
/ productive hours per qualified FTE-month
```

Then apply only the additional allowances that are not already included:

- demand variability;
- investigation/repeat load;
- minimum shift coverage;
- critical-skill backup;
- launch/transfer workload;
- growth headroom; and
- continuity requirement.

Rounding is a staffing decision. A calculated 0.35 FTE does not automatically
mean hiring 0.35 of a person. The work may be combined with another role, covered
by a shared service, outsourced or require one full qualified presence during a
critical window.

## Shifts change coverage, not only available hours

Multiplying a one-shift capacity by two does not prove a two-shift operating model.

For each shift, check:

- minimum number of qualified executors;
- required reviewer/supervisor availability;
- sample receipt and handover;
- method steps that cross shifts;
- incubation readings or instrument interventions;
- organism, reference-standard and controlled-material access;
- waste and cleaning support;
- maintenance/metrology response;
- deviation escalation; and
- emergency and lone-working restrictions.

A second shift can increase equipment utilization but also adds handover,
supervision, training, security and support demand. Some technical-review work may
remain on day shift, creating a release bottleneck the execution model does not
show.

## Reviewer capacity is an independent constraint

Review work is not a fixed percentage of analyst FTE.

Reviewer demand depends on:

- number and complexity of data packages;
- manual calculations and transcription;
- chromatographic or image review depth;
- audit-trail review requirements;
- atypical results and invalid tests;
- method changes and deviations;
- release/stability deadlines;
- report and certificate structure;
- raw-data access from external laboratories; and
- reviewer qualification by method/platform.

Model review hours per execution or package, plus exception load. Then check the
daily/weekly release calendar. A single reviewer can have acceptable monthly
hours but still block all Friday batch releases.

Reviewer independence and authorization are governance requirements; they cannot
be solved by dividing total analyst FTE by an arbitrary ratio.

## Skill matrices reveal hidden bottlenecks

Create a matrix with people on one axis and critical capabilities on the other.
Track status such as:

- not trained;
- training in progress;
- trained but not independently qualified;
- qualified executor;
- qualified reviewer;
- trainer/SME; and
- qualification expired or suspended.

Then test coverage under:

- normal roster;
- one person absent;
- peak demand;
- night/weekend shift;
- investigation requiring an independent reviewer; and
- concurrent method transfer or validation work.

Three analysts do not provide redundancy if only one is qualified for the
release-critical method.

## Model investigations and repeat work separately

Embedding all exception work in a generic reserve hides why staffing changes.

Use site history where available:

- invalid-test rate;
- OOS/OOT and microbial-excursion frequency;
- average investigation hours by role;
- repeat or resample demand permitted by procedure;
- deviation/CAPA workload;
- audit and inspection peaks; and
- recurring equipment/method issues.

Separate expected recurring load from stress scenarios. A severe investigation
can consume SME and reviewer capacity disproportionally even if routine analyst
hours remain stable.

## Launch and lifecycle work need their own plan

A new laboratory or portfolio has temporary demand that steady-state batch models
miss:

- recruitment and onboarding;
- initial GMP and safety training;
- method transfer, verification and suitability;
- equipment qualification support;
- SOP authoring and approval;
- reference-standard/strain setup;
- media and reagent qualification;
- data-system configuration and validation;
- mock runs and readiness exercises; and
- inspection or partner-audit preparation.

If the same future routine team must deliver all launch work, show the overlap.
Temporary specialists, vendor support, contract testing or phased method transfer
may be needed.

## Outsourcing shifts work rather than deleting it

An outsourced method still creates internal labor for:

- sample planning, preparation and shipment;
- chain of custody and stability control;
- purchase/order administration;
- method transfer and suitability oversight;
- contract-laboratory communication;
- data/report/raw-data review;
- OOS/deviation/change interfaces;
- vendor performance review; and
- continuity and backup management.

Model internal oversight hours separately from external execution. A global
outsource percentage applied to hands-on testing can understate governance work.

## Peak demand and time windows matter

Monthly average workload can hide:

- production campaign peaks;
- end-of-week release clustering;
- stability pull dates;
- scheduled water/EM rounds;
- media preparation days;
- product-launch suitability studies;
- annual shutdown/requalification activity; and
- simultaneous absences or training.

At concept stage, use an explicit peak factor. For higher-risk decisions, create
a weekly or shift-level roster model with arrival times, method-step windows,
skill requirements and reviewer deadlines.

## Resilience is more than a percentage reserve

A people-capacity reserve should answer named risks:

- planned leave;
- sickness;
- training and qualification;
- turnover and hiring lead time;
- peak workload;
- investigations;
- critical-skill coverage;
- reviewer independence; and
- growth uncertainty.

Possible resilience controls include:

- cross-training and dual qualification;
- designated backup reviewers;
- staggered shifts;
- shared specialist pool;
- qualified external laboratory;
- temporary project staffing;
- succession and trainer development; and
- controlled overtime within safety and quality limits.

The reserve should not be used to conceal chronic overtime or an unqualified
roster.

## Staffing triggers should be measurable

Define when hiring, cross-training or shift expansion begins.

Example triggers:

- forecast workload exceeds productive capacity for consecutive periods;
- peak shift coverage falls below the minimum qualified roster;
- reviewer queue approaches release tolerance;
- only one qualified person remains for a critical method;
- overtime or backlog exceeds an approved limit;
- launch/transfer workload enters the active window;
- outsourced lead time threatens turnaround; or
- estimate-to-actual time-study variance exceeds the reviewed range.

Account for recruitment, notice, onboarding and qualification lead time. Hiring at
the moment demand arrives is too late.

## Illustrative staffing calculation

Suppose method-derived workload is 720 analyst hands-on hours per month.

Assume, for illustration:

- 20 working days;
- 6 productive method hours per analyst-day; and
- a 20% reserve covering explicitly defined variability not already deducted.

Then:

```text
productive hours/FTE-month = 20 x 6 = 120
reserve-adjusted workload = 720 x 1.20 = 864 hours
base execution demand = 864 / 120 = 7.2 FTE
```

This result does not yet prove that eight analysts are sufficient. Review capacity,
skill coverage, shifts, launch work, absence correlation and hiring/qualification
lead time remain separate decisions.

The numbers are illustrative and are not Atlas staffing benchmarks.

## Evidence needed before staffing approval

- approved product-method portfolio;
- physical execution forecast;
- observed step-level time studies;
- productive-hours ledger;
- operating calendar and shift design;
- skill/qualification matrix;
- review and release workflow;
- investigation/repeat history;
- leave, training and turnover history;
- launch and lifecycle project plan;
- outsource oversight workload;
- role descriptions and decision rights;
- local labor-cost basis; and
- hiring and qualification lead times.

Use ranges and scenarios when evidence is incomplete. A precise FTE output from
unverified standard times is still an assumption.

## Staffing review checklist

- Are hands-on and elapsed time separated?
- Is workload allocated by role and skill?
- Are productive hours defined without double counting losses?
- Are reviewer hours modeled independently?
- Are shift-level minimums and handovers visible?
- Does every critical method have executor and reviewer coverage?
- Are investigation and repeat loads explicit?
- Is launch/lifecycle work separated from routine demand?
- Is outsourced oversight retained?
- Are peak periods and absence scenarios tested?
- Does the reserve answer named risks?
- Are hiring and qualification triggers early enough?
- Can actual time and staffing data feed calibration?

If several answers are open, the FTE output is a concept labor allowance, not an
approved organization design.
