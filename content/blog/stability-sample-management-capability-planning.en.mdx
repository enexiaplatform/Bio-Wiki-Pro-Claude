# How to plan stability and sample-management capability as a time-based operating system

Stability work is easy to underestimate because future timepoints are invisible
when a project begins. A batch enters the program today, but its samples and tests
can continue for months or years. Every new product, strength, pack, market,
condition and commitment adds future inventory and scheduled work.

The result is a time-based operating system, not a collection of chambers.

The planning question is:

> Which protocols and lifecycle commitments create future sample inventory,
> pulls, tests and decisions—and what capacity and governance are required to
> execute them without missing timepoints or compromising evidence?

This guide supports capability planning. It does not design a stability protocol,
assign shelf life, justify bracketing/matrixing or replace regulatory and QA
approval.

## Map the stability decision chain

```text
product/batch/package/market
→ protocol and condition
→ quantity and timepoints
→ chamber/storage inventory
→ pull and sample allocation
→ analytical methods
→ result review and trending
→ shelf-life/change/investigation decision
```

Each link creates a different capacity constraint. Chamber space may be adequate
while the laboratory cannot process a clustered pull. Analytical capacity may be
adequate while sample reconciliation and review are not.

ICH Q1A(R2) describes stability testing as evidence for how quality varies with
time under environmental factors and supports establishing a retest period or
shelf life and recommended storage conditions. A capability model should preserve
that protocol and decision context.

## Build the protocol registry

For every active or planned protocol, record:

- product, strength, formulation and batch;
- container-closure/pack configuration;
- market or regulatory commitment;
- study type and lifecycle purpose;
- storage condition;
- timepoints and pull windows;
- tests at each timepoint;
- sample quantity, reserve and contingency units;
- bracketing/matrixing status where approved;
- start date, expected completion and owner;
- chamber/location allocation; and
- evidence, assumptions and approval state.

Do not multiply every market trace into physical inventory automatically. Where
one approved study supports several markets, preserve the regulatory links but
allocate one physical study. Where conditions, packs, tests or commitments differ,
retain separate demand.

## Forecast inventory over time

Stability capacity is a stock-and-flow problem.

```text
closing inventory
= opening inventory + new study placements − scheduled pulls − approved disposals
```

Forecast by month and storage condition. Include:

- current active inventory;
- planned launches, validation and registration batches;
- ongoing/commitment studies;
- excursions, investigations or change-related studies;
- reserve/contingency samples;
- pack dimensions and usable shelf geometry;
- segregation and access constraints;
- completed-study retention/disposal rules; and
- growth and portfolio rationalization scenarios.

Nominal chamber volume is not usable capacity. Shelves, airflow clearance,
mapping boundaries, packaging geometry and segregation reduce available space.

## Convert timepoints into pull workload

Each timepoint creates more than analytical tests:

- pull authorization and location lookup;
- controlled chamber access;
- sample count and reconciliation;
- conditioning or equilibration where applicable;
- allocation across methods;
- chain of custody and laboratory receipt;
- remaining-sample return or controlled disposition;
- protocol/timepoint completeness check; and
- documentation and approval.

Model pulls separately from analytical executions. A program with many small
protocols may have greater administrative and handling work than one large study
with the same number of samples.

## Compile the timepoint-to-method matrix

Not every test occurs at every timepoint. Build a matrix linking each protocol
timepoint to its required methods, preparations and sample quantities.

This produces:

- samples and executions by month;
- analyst/instrument/reviewer demand;
- destructive sample requirements;
- microbiology and incubation demand;
- outsourced specialist testing;
- reference-standard/reagent demand; and
- the release/stability workload collision.

Feed the method architecture into the Analytical Chemistry, Microbiology or
Biologics Domain Pack rather than applying a generic “tests per timepoint” factor.

## Model clustering and pull windows

Stability demand is lumpy. Many studies begin after validation or launch
campaigns, causing recurring clusters at common timepoints. Forecast weekly or
daily where pull windows and method turnaround are tight.

Consider:

- weekend and holiday pulls;
- allowable pull/test windows under controlled procedures;
- chamber access and sampler coverage;
- concurrent release workload;
- multi-day or long-incubation methods;
- external laboratory shipment schedules;
- result review and approval deadlines; and
- late, missed or compromised pull escalation.

Monthly averages can hide a week in which the stability program overwhelms the
same instruments used for batch release.

## Separate sample populations

Sample management may include:

- stability samples;
- reserve/retention samples;
- reference samples;
- investigation samples;
- development or comparability samples;
- raw-material retains; and
- controlled standards or critical reagents.

These populations can have different ownership, storage, access, reconciliation,
retention and disposal requirements. A capability map should not treat all stored
items as one inventory pool merely because they share a cold room.

## Plan chamber and storage lifecycle

For each storage condition or controlled area, model:

- mapped usable capacity;
- current and projected inventory;
- alarms and response time;
- monitoring and data review;
- calibration, maintenance and qualification;
- door-open/access patterns;
- backup power and emergency response;
- alternate qualified capacity;
- sample transfer procedure and time; and
- excursion assessment workload.

The resilience question is not only “is there a spare chamber?” It is whether
samples can be transferred within the approved response window to a qualified
condition with sufficient mapped space and traceability.

## Include data and reconciliation controls

The operating model needs reliable links between protocol, batch, sample,
container, location, timepoint, condition, test and result. Plan for:

- unique sample identity and label control;
- protocol and amendment versioning;
- placement and pull records;
- inventory/location reconciliation;
- missed/late pull alerts;
- test assignment and result linkage;
- chamber data and excursion linkage;
- review/approval status;
- retention/disposal authorization; and
- audit trail, backup and recovery.

A spreadsheet can support early discovery, but high-volume or high-risk programs
may require controlled sample-management functionality. Atlas should define the
information and workflow requirement, not attempt to replace a LIMS or stability
system.

## Model review, trending and investigation capacity

Stability results require more than specification comparison. Include capacity
for:

- timepoint completeness review;
- OOS/OOT and atypical trend assessment;
- comparison across batches, strengths, packs and conditions;
- degradation and shelf-life trend review;
- chamber excursion impact assessment;
- protocol deviations and missed pulls;
- annual/periodic program review; and
- regulatory/change-control decisions.

Review demand often grows faster than test count because more products and longer
histories increase the comparisons required.

## Test resilience scenarios

- chamber or controlled-storage failure;
- alarm/monitoring outage;
- insufficient mapped backup capacity;
- sample identity or reconciliation discrepancy;
- missed or late pull;
- stability peak collides with release demand;
- stability-indicating method unavailable;
- external laboratory delay;
- protocol amendment changes future inventory; or
- acquisition or transfer adds legacy studies with incomplete data.

For each, document time to impact, decision owner, sample-transfer path, data
needed for assessment and residual risk.

## Capability outputs

A Stability & Sample Management Domain Pack can produce:

- protocol and commitment registry;
- condition/timepoint inventory forecast;
- mapped chamber/storage capacity and headroom;
- monthly/weekly pull calendar;
- timepoint-to-method workload;
- sample quantity and contingency forecast;
- analyst, instrument and review demand;
- release-versus-stability collision scenarios;
- chamber excursion and continuity plan;
- sample/data workflow requirements;
- CAPEX/OPEX and phased expansion; and
- unresolved-input and expert-review register.

## Discovery checklist

1. Active protocols, conditions, timepoints and approval status.
2. Current inventory by chamber, condition and sample population.
3. Product launch, validation and commitment-study forecast.
4. Pack dimensions, quantities and contingency rules.
5. Mapped usable chamber/storage capacity.
6. Pull windows, handling and reconciliation procedures.
7. Timepoint test matrix and method lifecycle status.
8. Historical late pulls, excursions, OOS/OOT and investigation volume.
9. Backup capacity, transfer procedure and response times.
10. Data systems, ownership, retention and disposal workflow.

## Common planning errors

- Sizing only for current inventory, not future placements and long study tails.
- Using nominal chamber volume instead of mapped usable geometry.
- Treating all markets or strengths as separate physical studies without checking
  approved bracketing, matrixing or shared commitments.
- Counting tests but omitting pull, reconciliation and review work.
- Using monthly averages that hide clustered timepoints.
- Ignoring release/stability competition for the same platforms.
- Treating reserve and stability samples as one governed population.
- Assuming a spare chamber is qualified and large enough for emergency transfer.
- Omitting protocol amendments, deviations and disposal workload.
- Treating a sample-management spreadsheet as inherently controlled.

## Key takeaway

Stability is accumulated future work. The defensible chain is:

**protocol commitment → condition/timepoint → inventory → pull → method workload
→ trend/review → shelf-life or lifecycle decision.**

Capacity must be forecast across time, storage geometry, laboratory workload and
governance—not only the number of chambers owned today.
