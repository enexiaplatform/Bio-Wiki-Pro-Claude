# From product portfolio to QC capability map: a cross-domain planning framework

The most expensive laboratory-planning mistake is to confuse a list of equipment
with a definition of capability.

A laboratory does not create value because it owns an HPLC, incubator, particle
counter or stability chamber. It creates value when it can make the required
quality decisions for the products and markets it supports — with appropriate
methods, competent people, controlled data, sufficient capacity and resilient
operating processes.

That is why laboratory planning should begin with a **QC capability map**:

> A traceable model connecting products and quality requirements to the tests,
> methods, resources, evidence and governance needed to support real release,
> stability, investigation and lifecycle decisions.

This framework applies across regulated manufacturing. The detailed rules differ
between pharmaceuticals, biologics, food and beverage, cosmetics and medical
devices. A shared planning core is still possible, provided those domain-specific
rules are never treated as interchangeable.

## What a capability map is — and is not

A capability map answers five linked questions:

1. What decisions must the quality organization make?
2. Which product, process, utility or environmental requirements support those
   decisions?
3. Which tests and method architectures produce the necessary evidence?
4. Which people, equipment, materials, space, systems and partners execute them?
5. What capacity, resilience and governance are required for dependable delivery?

It is not:

- a generic equipment catalogue;
- a copy of another site’s laboratory;
- a final room layout or HVAC design;
- an automatically generated regulatory interpretation;
- a claim that a method is approved or validated;
- a LIMS, sample schedule or batch-release system; or
- a substitute for qualified QC, QA, regulatory and engineering review.

The capability map sits upstream of those activities. It gives each specialist a
clearer and more defensible basis for their work.

## The shared Compiler Core

Across domains, the planning sequence remains stable:

```text
Product and market profile
→ quality decision and requirement
→ test and method architecture
→ sample and workload demand
→ resource and operating model
→ capacity, turnaround and resilience
→ cost, phasing and controlled outputs
```

The shared core should contain facts and calculations that are genuinely common:

- products, dosage or presentation, markets and lifecycle stage;
- batches, lots, sample points, test frequencies and growth scenarios;
- target turnaround and release dependencies;
- in-house, outsourced and hybrid execution choices;
- analyst time, equipment utilization and consumable demand;
- space and utility assumptions;
- CAPEX, OPEX, maintenance and service assumptions;
- downtime, redundancy and business-continuity scenarios;
- evidence, confidence, unresolved inputs and review status; and
- estimate-to-actual variance after implementation.

The shared core must not contain hidden domain assumptions. For example, “one
sample equals two analyst hours” is not a universal rule. Analyst effort depends
on the method, preparation, instrument sequence, controls, review burden and site
procedure.

## Domain Packs: where specialized reasoning belongs

A Domain Pack supplies the evidence and rules unique to a testing family or
regulated context. It should declare its scope, sources, applicability,
limitations, version and expert owner.

| Domain Pack | Examples of specialized reasoning |
| --- | --- |
| Non-sterile microbiology | Product matrix, enumeration, specified microorganisms, method suitability, media, incubation and recovery controls |
| Water and environmental microbiology | Sampling points, frequencies, alert/action frameworks, recovery conditions, trend review and response dependencies |
| Sterile and biologics quality | Sterility assurance context, bioburden, endotoxin, environmental controls, rapid methods, cell-based or molecular capabilities |
| Analytical chemistry | Product specifications, sample preparation, chromatographic/spectroscopic methods, reference standards, solvents, sequence design and instrument qualification |
| Stability and sample management | Protocol pulls, chamber conditions, storage capacity, timepoint clustering, reserve samples and lifecycle commitments |
| Food and beverage quality | Product- and hazard-specific microbiological, chemical, sensory and process-control requirements |

A new pack should not be created simply because a market appears attractive. It
needs a credible source corpus, qualified subject-matter ownership, representative
cases and demand strong enough to justify maintenance.

## Step 1: map the decisions, not only the tests

Tests exist to support decisions. Start by naming those decisions:

- Can this incoming material be used?
- Can the batch proceed to the next manufacturing stage?
- Can the finished batch be released to a specific market?
- Is a water system, environment or process in a state of control?
- Does a stability timepoint support the approved shelf life?
- Is an unexpected result valid, assignable or product-impacting?
- Can a method, supplier, site or process change be accepted?
- Should a capability remain outsourced, move in-house or be duplicated?

This prevents two problems. First, it exposes tests that have no clear decision
purpose. Second, it reveals decisions that depend on evidence the planned
laboratory cannot produce.

For each decision, record:

- accountable owner;
- required evidence;
- specification, procedure or regulatory basis;
- target decision time;
- escalation and exception path; and
- consequence of delay or incorrect output.

A release-critical method and a characterization method may use similar equipment
but require different capacity, redundancy and governance.

## Step 2: build the requirements inventory

The requirements inventory should be granular enough to preserve applicability.
A single row labelled “HPLC testing” is not useful. A better row identifies:

- product or material family;
- market and approved specification;
- attribute or decision supported;
- method identifier and lifecycle status;
- sample type, quantity and preparation;
- frequency or triggering event;
- physical execution allocation;
- in-house or outsourced owner;
- turnaround target; and
- evidence and open questions.

Requirements from different markets should remain traceable even when one
physical execution can satisfy them. Conversely, two requirements should not be
merged merely because they use the same instrument platform.

The map must distinguish at least four evidence states:

| State | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Verified site fact | Supported by an approved, current site-controlled source |
| External requirement | Supported by an applicable external source that still needs site interpretation |
| Planning assumption | A transparent placeholder used for scenario modelling |
| Unresolved | Insufficient information; dependent outputs must remain open or qualified |

That distinction is more important than false numerical precision.

## Step 3: describe method architecture

A method architecture converts a requirement into operational demand without
pretending to reproduce a controlled method. Depending on the domain, it can
capture:

- preparation and extraction steps;
- instrument or incubation stages;
- media, solvents, reagents, standards and controls;
- sample, blank, standard and system-suitability injections;
- hold times and stability constraints;
- analyst hands-on time and elapsed cycle time;
- equipment and utility dependencies;
- data processing and review effort;
- waste, cleaning and segregation needs; and
- validation, verification or transfer boundaries.

This is where a generic “tests per month” model becomes a defensible workload
model. Two methods with the same monthly test count can create radically
different instrument occupancy, analyst demand, consumable spend and turnaround.

## Step 4: reconcile physical execution

Regulatory traceability and physical workload are related but not identical.
Before calculating capacity, decide what creates a distinct execution:

- one execution shared across multiple market specifications;
- separate executions due to method or reporting differences;
- repeat or confirmatory work required by procedure;
- pooled versus individual samples;
- bracketed or matrixed stability testing where approved;
- campaign-specific environmental or utility sampling; or
- outsourced testing with an internal receipt/review workload.

If the allocation is unknown, preserve the requirement but hold back dependent
resource calculations. This avoids two common errors: multiplying demand by every
regulatory trace, or collapsing genuinely separate work into one convenient test.

## Step 5: compile the operating capability

For every method architecture, map the required capability across seven resource
classes:

| Resource class | Planning output |
| --- | --- |
| People | Roles, qualifications, hands-on time, review time and shift coverage |
| Equipment | Functional requirement, occupancy, usable capacity, qualification and backup strategy |
| Materials | Reagents, media, standards, columns, cultures, consumables and expiry/lead-time risk |
| Space and utilities | Functional zones, storage, flows, environmental needs and utility assumptions |
| Data systems | Acquisition, calculations, audit trail, review, retention and integration needs |
| External partners | Scope, quality agreement, lead time, capacity, method status and continuity |
| Governance | SOPs, change control, deviations, OOS/OOT, trending, lifecycle review and approval |

WHO’s 2024 good-practice guideline for pharmaceutical QC laboratories emphasizes
the laboratory as a managed quality system, not merely a technical room. That is
consistent with capability planning: instruments without competent personnel,
controlled methods, traceable materials, reliable records and quality oversight
do not constitute an operating laboratory.

## Step 6: model capacity as several constraints

Capacity is not one number. A method can be constrained by:

- analyst hands-on time;
- instrument or chamber occupancy;
- incubation or hold duration;
- preparation-room throughput;
- media, reagent or standard availability;
- sample receipt and storage;
- data review and approval;
- maintenance and qualification windows;
- contract laboratory lead time; or
- investigation and repeat-testing demand.

The model should show which resource is binding in each scenario. It should also
separate average demand from peak demand and state the uptime/utilization
allowances applied.

At concept stage, a load check can identify likely bottlenecks. A decision close
to the utilization limit may require a queue or schedule simulation using actual
arrival patterns, cycle times and concurrency rules. More decimal places do not
replace a better model.

## Step 7: treat outsourcing as part of the capability map

An outsourced test remains a site capability dependency. The owner still needs a
defined method and specification interface, sample logistics, data review,
deviation communication, change notification, quality oversight and continuity
plan.

FDA guidance on quality agreements explains that written agreements can define
which party performs specific CGMP activities, but they do not transfer away the
parties’ regulatory responsibilities. In a capability map, therefore, “outsource”
does not mean “remove the row.” It changes the resource and risk model.

Model at least:

- contract laboratory lead time and capacity;
- sample shipping and stability;
- method transfer/suitability status;
- result and raw-data review responsibilities;
- OOS, deviation and change-control interfaces;
- business continuity and alternate-provider strategy; and
- demand/cost threshold for future insourcing.

## Step 8: create decision-ready scenarios

A useful capability map supports choices, not only documentation. Typical
scenarios include:

- current demand versus launch/growth demand;
- minimum viable capability versus resilient capability;
- in-house versus outsource versus hybrid;
- one shift versus multiple shifts;
- prepared materials versus internal preparation;
- single resource versus redundant critical capacity;
- centralized testing versus site-local capability; and
- phase 1 launch scope versus later domain expansion.

For each scenario, report the same outputs:

- capabilities included and excluded;
- demand and capacity basis;
- critical assumptions;
- unresolved inputs;
- turnaround and continuity risks;
- indicative CAPEX/OPEX; and
- decision triggers for moving to the next phase.

Consistency makes trade-offs visible.

## Step 9: produce controlled commercial outputs

Once qualified reviewers have challenged the model, the capability map can become
the basis for project outputs such as:

- test portfolio and capability architecture;
- method bill of materials;
- equipment sizing and utilization;
- staffing and shift scenarios;
- consumables forecast;
- space and utility assumptions;
- CAPEX/OPEX ranges;
- bottleneck and redundancy analysis;
- outsource-versus-insource recommendation;
- vendor-neutral URS basis;
- RFQ technical compliance matrix; and
- phased procurement or expansion plan.

Each output should carry its source version, assumptions, exclusions, confidence,
open questions and review state. A polished export does not transform a planning
assumption into an approved requirement.

## How Atlas should expand

Atlas can serve a broad regulated-manufacturing boundary without building every
domain at once. The coherent expansion path is:

1. Prove the shared Compiler Core on a narrow, high-knowledge wedge.
2. Capture corrections and estimate-to-actual variance from real projects.
3. Convert repeated reasoning into versioned rules and evidence records.
4. Add the next Domain Pack only with qualified ownership, source coverage and
   validation cases.
5. Keep cross-domain outputs consistent while allowing domain logic to differ.

This is how breadth becomes trustworthy: not through a long menu, but through a
common decision architecture backed by progressively verified specialist packs.

## A capability-map review checklist

- Are the quality decisions and accountable owners explicit?
- Is every requirement linked to a product, market, process, utility or lifecycle
  commitment?
- Are approved site facts separated from external requirements and assumptions?
- Does every material workload number trace to a method architecture?
- Has physical execution been reconciled across markets and specifications?
- Are analyst time, equipment occupancy and review effort modelled separately?
- Are peak demand, downtime and critical-resource redundancy visible?
- Are outsourced tests retained as governed dependencies?
- Are unresolved inputs blocking the outputs they materially affect?
- Can reviewers see what changes if a key assumption changes?
- Are domain-specific rules versioned and owned by qualified reviewers?
- Will actual project outcomes be captured to calibrate the next estimate?

If several answers are “no,” the project is not yet ready for design freeze or a
vendor-led equipment list.
