# Cell banks: the foundation everything in a biologic rests on

Every batch of a biologic traces back to a vial of cells. Long before the bioreactor,
the purification train, the release assays, or the stability program, there is a
more basic question: **what cells are you using, and how do you know they are still
the same controlled source?**

That is the role of the cell bank. It is not just frozen inventory. It is the
controlled biological starting point for the product. A weak cell-bank strategy can
turn into viral-safety uncertainty, drifting product quality, ambiguous impurity
profiles, and painful comparability questions years later. A strong one gives the
process a traceable, characterized, and renewable foundation.

ICH Q5D treats the characterization and testing of banked cell substrates as a
critical part of controlling biotechnology and biological products. WHO makes the
same point from the cell-culture side: the cell substrate and the events linked to
cell growth can affect the quality and safety of the resulting biological product.
In plain language, the cells are not background scenery. They are part of the
control strategy.

## Why biologics need a banking system

Small-molecule manufacturing usually begins with chemicals that can be specified,
tested, and re-purchased. Biologics begin with living systems. Those systems divide,
adapt, respond to culture conditions, and can carry biological risks from their
origin or from materials used during development.

A cell bank system solves three practical problems:

- **Consistency** - production starts from a controlled source rather than from a
  newly derived or repeatedly re-handled cell population.
- **Traceability** - each production run can be traced back through a working bank
  to a characterized master source.
- **Conservation** - the most important bank is protected from unnecessary handling
  and depletion.

Without banking, the product history becomes fuzzy. With banking, the manufacturer
can say which source cell line was used, how it was derived, how it was tested, how
far it was expanded, where it was stored, which working bank supplied production,
and whether the production process stayed within the demonstrated cell-age limit.

## The two-tier system: MCB and WCB

Most biologics use a two-tiered system:

- The **Master Cell Bank (MCB)** is the definitive bank. It is made once from the
  selected production cell line, filled into many containers, stored under
  controlled conditions, and characterized deeply.
- The **Working Cell Bank (WCB)** is made from one or more MCB containers and used
  for routine production. It is normally characterized with a more focused panel,
  because it derives from the already characterized master source.

The logic is simple but powerful. You do not want every manufacturing campaign to
touch the MCB. Each access adds handling risk and consumes a finite source. The WCB
acts as the operational bridge: it is close enough to production to be practical,
but still traceable to the protected MCB.

When a WCB runs low, the usual expectation is not to invent a new lineage. You make
a new WCB from the MCB under controlled conditions, with documented comparability
to the approved banking strategy. If you need to replace or regenerate the MCB,
that is a much bigger lifecycle event and should be treated accordingly.

## What the MCB has to prove

The MCB is where the strongest evidence belongs. It should support confidence in
the identity, purity, suitability, and stability of the cell substrate.

Important MCB evidence usually includes:

- **History and derivation** - source, lineage, cloning history where relevant,
  selection pressure, transfection or expression construct history, and materials
  of animal or human origin used during development.
- **Identity** - evidence that the cells are the intended cell line or clone.
- **Purity and freedom from contaminating cells** - evidence that the bank is not
  mixed with another cell line or microbial contaminant.
- **Adventitious agent testing** - bacteria, fungi, mycoplasma, relevant viruses,
  endogenous agents, and other tests justified by cell origin and process risk.
- **Expression construct and product-related evidence** - for recombinant products,
  confirmation that the genetic construct and product sequence are as expected.
- **Suitability for manufacture** - evidence that the cells grow and produce the
  intended product under the proposed manufacturing strategy.

The exact panel is not one-size-fits-all. A CHO cell line for a monoclonal antibody,
an insect cell substrate for a recombinant protein, a microbial cell substrate, and
a vaccine cell substrate do not carry identical risks. ICH Q5D expects the testing
program to reflect the biological properties, cultivation history, raw materials,
and available methods for the specific substrate.

## What the WCB has to prove

The WCB usually does not repeat every deep MCB characterization test. Its role is to
confirm that the operational bank is still connected to the controlled source and
is suitable for use.

Typical WCB evidence includes:

- identity or limited identity confirmation;
- sterility or bioburden controls appropriate to the bank and process;
- mycoplasma testing;
- selected adventitious-agent testing where justified;
- viability, recovery, and growth-performance checks;
- documentation that the WCB was derived from the approved MCB under controlled
  conditions.

The WCB question is not "can we discover the cell line from scratch again?" It is
"does this working supply remain traceable to, and consistent with, the qualified
master source?"

## In-vitro cell age is a hard boundary

One of the quietest but most important controls is the **limit of in-vitro cell
age**. Cells change as they divide. The risk may show up as altered productivity,
sequence or construct instability, glycosylation shifts, impurity differences, or
changes in growth behavior.

The cell-age limit defines how far the cells may be expanded from bank thaw through
the end of production. For some cell types this is tracked as population doublings;
for others it may be passages or another justified measure. The important point is
that the manufacturing process must stay within the age range for which stability
has been demonstrated.

This is why end-of-production or limit-of-cell-age studies matter. They are not
academic exercises. They answer the practical question: if production runs at the
maximum intended expansion, do the cells still support the intended product and
quality profile?

If a commercial process later increases scale, extends culture duration, changes
seed-train design, or adds a new production site, the cell-age calculation should
be revisited. A process that looks unchanged on a batch record can quietly exceed
the characterized cell-age envelope.

## Cell banks and viral safety

Cell banks sit inside the viral-safety strategy. ICH Q5A(R2) frames viral safety as
a combination of source control, testing at appropriate stages, and validated viral
clearance. Cell banks are central to the first two.

The source-control question is: what could have entered the system through the
cell line, raw materials, development history, or banking operation? The testing
question is: what evidence shows that the MCB, WCB, unprocessed bulk, and other
relevant materials do not contain undesirable infectious viruses?

Negative viral tests are important, but they do not carry the whole burden. Testing
has detection limits, sample-size limits, and method-specific blind spots. That is
why viral clearance through purification remains essential for products where it is
applicable. The cell bank does not replace clearance validation; it gives clearance
validation a cleaner and better-understood starting risk.

## Storage is part of the control strategy

A cell bank can be scientifically excellent and operationally fragile. Storage and
inventory control are where many avoidable failures begin.

Good storage control should answer:

- where each vial is located;
- which bank, lot, passage, and container each vial belongs to;
- how temperature is monitored and alarmed;
- how excursions are assessed;
- how many vials remain and which are reserved;
- how access is controlled and documented;
- how shipping, transfer, and site-to-site movement are qualified;
- how backup storage protects against a single freezer or site failure.

The practical risk is not only loss of viability. It is loss of confidence. If the
record cannot prove where a vial came from, how it was stored, or whether it was
exposed to an uncontrolled condition, the bank becomes harder to defend in an
inspection or comparability package.

## Traceability should be boring

Cell-bank traceability should be so clear that it feels boring. A reviewer should
be able to move from a commercial batch to the WCB vial, from the WCB to the MCB,
from the MCB to the clone history, and from the clone history to the characterization
package without needing heroic spreadsheet archaeology.

The trace should include:

- batch or lot identifiers for MCB and WCB;
- cell-line or clone identifier;
- passage or population-doubling history;
- date and location of bank manufacture;
- raw materials and critical reagents used in banking;
- test methods, certificates, and deviations;
- storage location and inventory status;
- chain of custody for transfers or shipments;
- link to approved specifications and acceptance criteria.

If that trace is scattered across notebooks, old folders, uncontrolled spreadsheets,
and vendor emails, the scientific story may still be true, but the quality system
will struggle to prove it.

## Changes that should trigger a banking review

Cell banks are lifecycle assets. They should not be treated as "approved once,
ignore forever." Some changes may not affect the bank directly, but they can affect
how the bank is used or how the evidence should be interpreted.

Common triggers for formal review include:

- creating a new WCB;
- replacing, regenerating, relocating, or transferring an MCB;
- changing seed-train scale, culture duration, or maximum in-vitro cell age;
- changing media, supplements, serum status, or raw materials with biological risk;
- changing storage site, freezer type, shipping configuration, or monitoring system;
- changing adventitious-agent, mycoplasma, identity, or genetic-stability methods;
- detecting unexpected viral, microbial, identity, or productivity signals;
- introducing a new manufacturing site or contract manufacturer;
- preparing a major regulatory filing, tech transfer, or comparability package.

The review question is not simply whether the change touches a freezer. It is
whether the change alters source control, traceability, stability, viral-safety
assurance, or the relationship between banked cells and the approved manufacturing
process.

## Common audit weaknesses

Cell-bank problems often look administrative until they become scientific. Watch
for these weak spots:

- The MCB and WCB relationship is described, but the traceability evidence is thin.
- Cell-age limits exist in a report but are not embedded in production controls.
- Genetic-stability evidence does not cover the actual maximum manufacturing age.
- A new WCB was made as routine inventory work, with weak change-control review.
- Viral testing is treated as a checklist instead of a risk-based program tied to
  cell origin and raw-material history.
- Storage excursions are documented but not scientifically assessed for bank
  suitability.
- Inventory is reconciled locally but not governed as a critical GMP asset.
- Vendor or external testing reports are filed without method suitability,
  acceptance criteria, or QA review.

These are fixable, but they require the cell bank to be seen as an active part of
the product lifecycle rather than a freezer record.

## Review checklist

Use this quick checklist when reviewing a cell-bank package:

- Can every production batch trace to a specific WCB and approved MCB?
- Is the MCB derivation history clear enough to understand biological and raw
  material risks?
- Are identity, purity, mycoplasma, adventitious-agent, and relevant viral tests
  justified for the cell substrate?
- Is the WCB testing panel appropriate and linked to the MCB evidence?
- Is the maximum in-vitro cell age defined, justified, and respected by the
  manufacturing process?
- Do end-of-production or limit-of-cell-age data support the intended process?
- Are storage, alarm, excursion, transfer, and inventory controls documented?
- Are new WCBs, site transfers, method changes, and process extensions governed by
  change control?
- Does the viral-safety assessment connect source control, testing, and clearance
  rather than relying on a single negative result?

## Key takeaway

A cell bank is not just the first step in a biologics process. It is the controlled
source that makes the rest of the process interpretable. If the MCB/WCB system is
well characterized, traceable, stable, and governed, downstream data have a clear
origin. If it is weak, every later quality question becomes harder to answer.
