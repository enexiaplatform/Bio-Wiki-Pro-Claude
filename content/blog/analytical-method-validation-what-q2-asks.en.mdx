# Analytical method validation: what ICH Q2 actually asks

Analytical method validation answers one practical question:

**Can this procedure reliably support the decision it is being used for?**

That sounds simple, but many validation packages drift away from it. They become
collections of tables: linearity, accuracy, precision, range, LOD, LOQ,
robustness. The tables may look impressive while the central argument is weak.
The real argument is not "we ran every study." It is:

> For this product, matrix, analyte, level, method type, and quality decision, the
> procedure is fit for its intended purpose.

ICH Q2(R2) is built around that idea. It gives the validation characteristics and
principles for evaluating analytical procedures. ICH Q14 then places those
procedures inside a lifecycle: define the need, develop the procedure, validate
it, transfer it, monitor it, and manage changes with scientific justification.

Validation is therefore not a ceremony at the end of method development. It is the
documented evidence that the method can do the job it was designed to do.

## Start with intended use

Before writing a protocol, define the method's intended use in plain terms:

- What analyte or attribute is being measured?
- What product, matrix, sample type, and concentration range are in scope?
- Is the method for release, stability, cleaning verification, process monitoring,
  comparability, investigation support, or development only?
- Is it an identification test, assay, impurity test, limit test, quantitative
  method, semi-quantitative method, or qualitative method?
- What decision will the result support?
- What level of uncertainty can the decision tolerate?

This is where weak validations often begin to fail. A method may be "validated"
for a neat standard solution but then used for a complex finished product matrix.
A method may show beautiful linearity across a wide range but not prove accuracy
near the specification limit. A microbial or biological method may use language
borrowed from chromatography even though the real validation question is agreement,
detection capability, precision, or matrix interference.

If the intended use is fuzzy, acceptance criteria become arbitrary. If the
intended use is clear, the validation design almost writes itself.

## The Q2 characteristics are not a checklist

ICH Q2(R2) discusses validation characteristics such as specificity/selectivity,
working range, accuracy, precision, robustness, detection limit, and quantitation
limit. These are not equally important for every method.

The test type decides what evidence matters.

| Method use | The validation question | Typical emphasis |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Identification | Can the method confirm identity without confusing related materials? | Specificity/selectivity |
| Assay or content | Can the method quantify the analyte accurately across the needed range? | Specificity, accuracy, precision, range |
| Quantitative impurity test | Can the method quantify impurities at relevant levels in the matrix? | Specificity, accuracy, precision, range, LOQ |
| Limit test | Can the method detect whether an impurity is above a defined limit? | Specificity, detection capability |
| Stability-indicating method | Can the method measure the analyte and relevant degradants over time? | Specificity, forced degradation, range, precision |

This is why "we validated everything" is not automatically a strength. A good
protocol explains why each characteristic is included, why any characteristic is
not applicable, and how each acceptance criterion connects to the method's purpose.

## Specificity protects meaning

Specificity, often discussed alongside selectivity, asks whether the method
measures the intended analyte or attribute in the presence of other components.
Those other components may include excipients, impurities, degradants, solvents,
process-related impurities, matrix components, or closely related biological
species.

For a stability-indicating assay, specificity is the heart of the method. If the
active ingredient co-elutes with a degradant, the assay can report a comforting
number while hiding degradation. If an impurity method cannot separate a critical
impurity from a related peak, the result is not decision-grade. If an ELISA is
affected by matrix interference, the number may reflect binding behavior rather
than true analyte amount.

Practical specificity evidence may include:

- blank, placebo, matrix, and diluent interference checks;
- spiked samples at relevant levels;
- known impurities and degradants;
- peak purity or orthogonal confirmation where appropriate;
- forced degradation for stability-indicating methods;
- negative and positive controls for qualitative methods;
- interference assessment for biological or immunochemical methods.

Specificity is also where the validation package should explain what the method
does not see. No method is omniscient. A good validation report is honest about
known limitations and the control strategy around them.

## Accuracy and precision must match the decision

Accuracy asks how close the measured value is to the true or accepted reference
value. Precision asks how close repeated measurements are to each other. They are
different failures.

A method can be precise but wrong: every result clusters tightly around a biased
value. A method can be accurate on average but imprecise: the mean looks fine, but
individual results scatter too widely to support release or trend decisions.

ICH Q2(R2) frames precision at levels such as repeatability, intermediate
precision, and reproducibility:

- **Repeatability** covers short-term variation under the same operating
  conditions.
- **Intermediate precision** challenges normal within-laboratory variation such as
  different days, analysts, instruments, columns, or reagent lots.
- **Reproducibility** becomes important when results must agree across
  laboratories or sites.

Acceptance criteria should be justified against the method's intended use. A
release assay close to a tight specification needs stronger precision than a
screening method. A trace impurity method needs accuracy and precision at relevant
low levels, not only at comfortable mid-range concentrations.

For many QC methods, the most important question is not "does the method pass
precision?" It is "is the method precise enough that a result near the limit still
supports the right decision?"

## Range is where the method is allowed to speak

Range is the interval over which the method has been shown to perform acceptably.
It is not merely the lowest and highest standard in a calibration curve.

For quantitative methods, range should be supported by the combined evidence:
linearity or response relationship, accuracy, precision, specificity, and sometimes
LOQ. For an assay, the range should cover expected sample results and likely
specification or stability limits. For an impurity method, the range should cover
the reporting threshold, specification limit, and relevant higher levels where
needed.

Reportable range matters because it protects data use. If a result is outside the
validated range, the lab cannot simply report it as though the method has proven
performance there. The result may require dilution, re-preparation, alternative
methodology, or a justified investigation path.

This is especially important in stability studies, where degradation can move
results away from the nominal release condition. A stability method should remain
meaningful when the product is stressed or aged, not only when the sample looks
perfect.

## LOD and LOQ are not decorative numbers

Detection limit and quantitation limit matter most for impurity, contaminant, and
trace-level methods.

- **LOD** asks whether the method can detect that something is present.
- **LOQ** asks whether the method can quantify it with suitable accuracy and
  precision.

The practical distinction matters. A method may detect a small peak but not
quantify it reliably. For a limit test, detection capability around the limit may
be enough. For a quantitative impurity method, the LOQ must be scientifically
usable at or below the level where decisions are made.

Weak validation packages often state LOD or LOQ from a formula without showing
that real samples near the level behave acceptably. A stronger package connects
LOD/LOQ to the sample matrix, noise, recovery, precision, response behavior, and
the reporting rules used in routine QC.

## Robustness prevents routine surprises

Robustness asks whether small, deliberate changes in method conditions affect
performance. It is where method development knowledge meets routine reality.

For chromatography, robustness may challenge pH, mobile-phase composition, flow
rate, column temperature, gradient timing, extraction time, wavelength, injection
volume, column lot, or equilibration. For biological assays, robustness may involve
incubation time, cell passage, reagent lot, plate position, analyst technique, or
sample handling. For microbiological methods, it may include media lot, incubation
conditions, recovery conditions, and organism challenge design.

Robustness is not busywork. It prevents common failure patterns:

- method transfer fails because another site uses a slightly different column lot;
- system suitability becomes fragile during normal QC operation;
- a small pH drift changes impurity resolution;
- sample preparation timing changes recovery;
- analysts pass training but still produce different results because the method
  instructions are ambiguous.

ICH Q14 expands this thinking by encouraging a science- and risk-based development
approach. If the method's important variables are understood, routine changes can
be managed more intelligently. In an enhanced approach, that knowledge may support
a defined operating region and more efficient post-approval change management.

## System suitability is not validation

System suitability is a routine check that the system is working for a specific
run. It is not proof that the method is valid.

Validation says the method can perform for its intended purpose when correctly
executed. System suitability says today's system, standards, reagents, column,
instrument, and run conditions are suitable before or during sample analysis.

Confusing the two creates audit risk. You cannot rescue a poorly validated method
by adding a long list of system suitability checks. You also cannot ignore system
suitability because a validation report exists. They answer different questions
and both are needed.

Strong validation packages define system suitability criteria using validation and
development knowledge. For example, if resolution between two peaks is critical to
specificity, that resolution should become a routine gate. If injection precision
is essential to quantitative performance, replicate standard %RSD may be a useful
run check.

## Validation, verification, transfer, and lifecycle

Validation is often mixed up with related activities:

- **Validation** proves a non-compendial or modified procedure is fit for its
  intended purpose.
- **Verification** confirms that an established or compendial procedure works as
  used for a specific product, matrix, lab, and conditions.
- **Transfer** demonstrates that another lab or site can perform the method
  comparably.
- **Ongoing monitoring** checks whether routine performance remains in control.
- **Change control** evaluates whether method changes affect validated performance.

These activities should connect. A transfer failure may expose a robustness gap
from validation. Repeated system suitability failures may show that the method is
too fragile for routine use. An OOS trend may indicate sample preparation
variability rather than product drift. A post-approval change may require
comparability data, partial revalidation, or a justified lifecycle update.

In the Q14 view, the analytical procedure is living knowledge, not a frozen PDF.

## What a good protocol contains

A good validation protocol is clear before any data are generated. It usually
defines:

- purpose and scope;
- product, matrix, analyte, sample type, and method version;
- method category and intended decisions;
- validation characteristics to be evaluated;
- study design, concentration levels, replicates, analysts, instruments, days, and
  lots;
- sample preparation and bracketing strategy;
- predefined acceptance criteria;
- statistical methods and rounding rules;
- deviation handling;
- data integrity expectations;
- reportable range and limitations;
- system suitability linkage;
- revalidation or lifecycle triggers.

The most important phrase is "predefined acceptance criteria." Criteria invented
after seeing the data are not acceptance criteria. They are storytelling.

## Review checklist

When reviewing a method validation package, ask:

- Is the intended use explicit?
- Does the validation design match the method type?
- Are all relevant matrices, strengths, sample types, and concentration ranges in
  scope?
- Are specificity studies strong enough for the method's risk?
- Does forced degradation support stability-indicating claims where relevant?
- Are accuracy and precision evaluated at decision-relevant levels?
- Is the reportable range justified by combined validation evidence?
- Are LOD and LOQ connected to real sample behavior, not only formulas?
- Was robustness studied before routine QC use?
- Do system suitability criteria reflect method-critical performance?
- Are acceptance criteria predefined and scientifically justified?
- Are deviations assessed for impact on the validation conclusion?
- Are method limitations stated honestly?
- Are transfer, monitoring, and change-control expectations defined?

If the answer to these questions is clear, the validation package is probably
usable. If the package only says "passes ICH Q2" without showing why, it is thin.

## Key takeaway

ICH Q2(R2) does not ask for validation theater. It asks for evidence that an
analytical procedure is suitable for its intended purpose. ICH Q14 adds the
lifecycle logic around that evidence.

The best method validation packages are not the longest. They are the ones where a
reviewer can follow the scientific argument from intended use to method design,
validation studies, acceptance criteria, routine controls, and lifecycle
maintenance.
