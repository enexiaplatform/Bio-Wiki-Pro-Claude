# What sterility testing can — and can't — prove

The compendial sterility test (USP 71 / EP 2.6.1) is one of the most
misunderstood tests in QC. It is a release test, but it is **not** what makes a
product sterile — and it cannot, on its own, prove that every unit in a batch is
sterile.

## The statistics problem

Sterility is the absence of viable microorganisms. To prove a whole batch sterile
by testing, you would have to test — and destroy — every unit. Instead you test a
small sample. A passing result means **the units tested showed no growth under the
test conditions**; it says little about the rest of the batch, especially at low
contamination rates that a small sample is unlikely to catch.

That is why **sterility assurance is built in, not tested in**: it comes from a
validated sterilisation or aseptic process, environmental control, and process
design. The sterility test is a final confirmation, not the source of assurance.

## What the test actually does

- Exposes a sample to growth media that support a broad range of organisms
- Incubates for a defined period and reads for growth (turbidity)
- Relies on **method suitability** (bacteriostasis/fungistasis) to show the product
  does not inhibit the recovery of any contaminant
- Depends on rigorous aseptic technique and **negative controls** — because the
  biggest risk is contaminating the test itself

## The false-positive trap

Because the test is done aseptically by hand, a result can be positive because the
**test** was contaminated, not the product. That is why an apparent failure
triggers an investigation: was it a true contamination, or a test artifact? The
rules for invalidating a sterility test result are deliberately strict — you need
clear evidence of a lab cause.

## What good looks like

- Method suitability established for the product
- Aseptic technique controlled; negative controls run every time
- A failure is **investigated**, not assumed to be a lab error
- Release rests on the whole sterility assurance system, not the test alone
