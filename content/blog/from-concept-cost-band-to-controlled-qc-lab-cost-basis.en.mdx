# From concept cost band to a controlled QC laboratory cost basis

An equipment price is not a project cost. A first-year budget is not a lifecycle
cost. A low–high range is not credible unless the scope, date, currency, exclusions
and uncertainty behind it are visible.

The useful chain is:

```text
approved capability scope
→ cost breakdown structure
→ quantity and technical basis
→ unit-rate evidence
→ installation and implementation cost
→ recurring operating cost
→ schedule, escalation and uncertainty
→ reviewed funding decision
```

Atlas starts with vendor-neutral concept allowances because detailed project
evidence is not yet available. The output should help a team discover what it must
price and verify. It must never present a concept band as a quotation, approved
budget or investment-grade estimate.

## Label estimate maturity by scope definition

Estimate maturity should follow the maturity of the technical definition, not the
number of spreadsheet rows. AACE describes estimate classification as a way to
communicate the degree of project definition and expected use; its process-industry
guidance may inform portions of pharmaceutical projects but is a guideline rather
than a universal standard. See the official [AACE 18R-97 table of contents and
sample](https://web.aacei.org/docs/default-source/toc/toc_18r-97.pdf?sfvrsn=4).

For Atlas, use transparent internal maturity labels:

| Atlas maturity | Available basis | Permitted use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Concept allowance | Capability classes, broad quantities and generic ranges | Compare scenarios, identify large drivers, plan evidence collection |
| Budgetary basis | Reviewed scope, preliminary URS, local quotes and installation assumptions | Funding discussion with explicit exclusions and contingency basis |
| Procurement basis | Approved URS/RFQ scope, bidder clarifications, commercial terms and implementation plan | Bid evaluation and authorization under client governance |
| Controlled baseline | Approved contracts, schedule, change controls and cost ownership | Project control and estimate-to-actual learning |

Do not map these labels automatically to an external classification. The project
owner must define its own stage-gate and approval terminology.

## Build a cost breakdown structure

A defensible estimate is organized so omissions and double counting can be found.
The GAO Cost Estimating and Assessment Guide emphasizes a standardized work
breakdown structure, explicit assumptions, lifecycle coverage, sensitivity and
risk/uncertainty analysis. See the official [GAO Cost Estimating and Assessment
Guide](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-20-195g).

For a QC laboratory, separate at least:

### Direct equipment and systems

- analytical and microbiology equipment;
- incubators, chambers, refrigerators and freezers;
- biosafety cabinets, isolators and containment devices;
- washers, autoclaves and media systems;
- benches, casework and specialist furniture;
- monitoring, alarms, data acquisition and software;
- initial accessories, racks, carts, fixtures and standards;
- recommended commissioning and operating spares.

### Facility and utility implementation

- architectural modifications and finishes;
- HVAC, exhaust and environmental controls;
- electrical distribution, UPS and emergency power;
- water, drainage, steam, gases, vacuum and compressed air;
- network, cybersecurity and system integration;
- structural support, equipment pads and penetrations;
- fire, accessibility, biosafety and EHS controls;
- temporary works and operational disruption.

### Delivery, installation and qualification

- freight, insurance, customs and taxes;
- rigging, unloading, storage and installation route;
- vendor installation and field service;
- commissioning and acceptance testing;
- calibration, mapping and metrology support;
- DQ/IQ/OQ/PQ or site-equivalent qualification;
- computer-system validation and data-integrity assessment;
- method transfer, verification or validation;
- SOP development, training and analyst qualification;
- project management and specialist engineering.

### Recurring operating cost

- loaded staffing by role and shift;
- media, reagents, standards, cultures and consumables;
- supplier and contract-laboratory spend;
- service contracts, calibration and preventive maintenance;
- utilities and conditioned storage;
- software licenses, hosting and support;
- proficiency testing and external quality programs;
- waste treatment and disposal;
- training, requalification and method lifecycle;
- insurance, permits or local overhead where applicable.

### End-of-life and change cost

- decommissioning and decontamination;
- data migration and record retention;
- disposal of hazardous or controlled assets;
- facility reinstatement;
- replacement, obsolescence and major upgrade cycles.

## Keep price, installed cost and total project cost separate

For each line, state which boundary is being reported.

```text
equipment purchase price
+ accessories and initial spares
+ freight, duties and taxes
+ installation and utility connection
+ commissioning and qualification
= installed and ready-for-intended-use cost
```

Total project cost can also include facility work, design, project management,
validation, owner costs, escalation and contingency. The boundary varies by
organization, so every subtotal needs an explicit definition.

## Use dated, comparable unit-rate evidence

Every rate should carry:

- description and technical configuration;
- quantity and unit of measure;
- supplier or historical source;
- quote/reference identifier;
- quote and validity dates;
- currency and exchange-rate date;
- location and delivery terms;
- inclusions and exclusions;
- tax, freight and duty treatment;
- installation and qualification boundary;
- confidence and reviewer;
- normalization or escalation method.

Do not average incomparable quotes. A low price that excludes software, service,
qualification and critical accessories may be more expensive at ready-for-use.

## Separate escalation from contingency

Escalation addresses price movement over time. Contingency addresses uncertainty and
risk within the defined project scope. They should not be hidden inside one markup.
The US Department of Energy's official [Cost Estimating
Guide](https://www.energy.gov/documents/cost-estimating-guide) calls for documented
bases for indirect rates, escalation and contingency and connects contingency to
project risk or risk analysis.

For every allowance, record:

- base date and planned expenditure date;
- index or market evidence;
- currency exposure;
- calculation method;
- risks covered and risks excluded;
- owner and approval status.

Contingency is not scope growth funding and should not conceal known missing items.
Known scope belongs in the base estimate.

## Time-phase CAPEX and OPEX

A total cost without timing cannot support cash-flow or procurement decisions.
Phase costs across:

1. definition and design;
2. long-lead procurement deposits;
3. facility and utility construction;
4. delivery and installation;
5. commissioning and qualification;
6. method transfer and operational readiness;
7. ramp-up and initial inventory;
8. routine operation and future expansion.

Link payment milestones to schedule evidence and commercial terms. Model the effect
of schedule delay on escalation, temporary testing, outsourcing and duplicated
operating cost.

## Build lifecycle cost by scenario

Purchase-price comparison can favor an option with high recurring cost or weak
service support. Compare alternatives over an agreed horizon using consistent:

- demand and utilization;
- staffing and productivity;
- consumable and proprietary-kit use;
- maintenance and service coverage;
- energy and utility consumption;
- software and data costs;
- downtime and backup testing;
- replacement and residual-value assumptions;
- discounting and escalation policy approved by finance.

Atlas may organize these inputs. Finance must approve the economic method, discount
rate, tax treatment and funding assumptions.

## Quantify uncertainty without false precision

A concept estimate should expose ranges and drivers. Use sensitivity scenarios for:

- equipment quantity and configuration;
- construction and utility scope;
- local labor rate and productivity;
- freight, duty, tax and exchange rate;
- validation and method-transfer effort;
- schedule delay;
- demand growth and insource/outsource choice;
- consumable waste, lead time and supplier pricing;
- maintenance model and downtime;
- contingency and escalation basis.

Point estimates alone can obscure uncertainty. The GAO guide describes risk and
uncertainty analysis, sensitivity testing and continual comparison with actual cost
as characteristics of credible estimating. Atlas calibration should therefore retain
the frozen estimate, actual cost, aligned scope, variance driver and reviewer
interpretation rather than learning from a naked variance percentage.

## Treat exclusions as decision data

Common exclusions include:

- land, shell building or major site infrastructure;
- production shutdown and temporary operations;
- LIMS/ELN replacement or enterprise integrations;
- detailed architectural and HVAC design;
- taxes, duties, financing or corporate overhead;
- client staffing during the project;
- regulatory submissions;
- remediation of existing utilities or structural conditions;
- hazardous-material surveys and disposal;
- commercial escalation after the estimate date.

Every exclusion needs an owner and disposition: outside project, covered elsewhere,
pending evidence or intentionally not funded.

## Reconcile cost to technical scope

Every material cost line should trace to one of:

- capability or quality decision;
- method or workload;
- equipment requirement;
- space or utility need;
- staffing/shift model;
- consumable supply item;
- validation or governance deliverable;
- risk treatment or resilience decision.

Unlinked costs may be unnecessary. Uncosted technical requirements are omissions.
This reconciliation is the bridge between the Blueprint and finance.

## Evidence required before budget approval

Collect:

1. approved scope and cost boundary;
2. cost breakdown structure and coding;
3. quantity basis linked to the Blueprint;
4. dated local budgetary quotations;
5. installation, construction and utility assumptions;
6. qualification, CSV and method-transfer basis;
7. staffing and recurring supply basis;
8. schedule and cash-flow profile;
9. currency, tax, freight and commercial assumptions;
10. escalation and contingency methodology;
11. exclusions, interfaces and owner costs;
12. sensitivity/risk review and approval roles.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas should block budget or investment use when:

- the cost boundary is undefined;
- quantities cannot be reconciled to technical scope;
- generic global rates are presented as local quotations;
- quote date, currency or commercial terms are missing;
- installed cost is confused with equipment price;
- validation, software, utilities or owner costs are silently excluded;
- contingency and escalation have no documented basis;
- recurring OPEX and lifecycle costs are absent;
- estimate maturity and permitted use are not stated;
- finance, procurement, engineering and QA reviewers are unnamed.
