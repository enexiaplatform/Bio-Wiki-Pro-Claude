# Growth promotion testing: what good looks like

Media is the silent variable behind almost every microbiology result. Growth
promotion testing (GPT) is what turns a bag of agar into a qualified, defensible
test system — it proves the lot will recover low numbers of the organisms you
care about before you trust it with a release decision.

## Why GPT matters

A medium that looks fine can still fail to recover stressed or low-level
organisms, or — for selective media — fail to suppress the organisms it is meant
to exclude. GPT is the check that catches both. Run it on each lot before
release-testing use, and quarantine the lot until it passes.

## The compendial strains

Non-selective recovery is challenged with a panel that spans the major groups —
typically a Gram-positive, a Gram-negative, a spore-former, a yeast, and a mould
(for example S. aureus, P. aeruginosa, B. subtilis, C. albicans, and
A. brasiliensis). The point is breadth: if the lot recovers all of them from a
low inoculum, it will recover what your samples contain.

Use a low challenge inoculum (not more than 100 CFU) so the test actually probes
recovery rather than swamping the plate.

## Selective media: recovery AND inhibition

This is where teams most often cut the test short. A selective or differential
medium has two jobs, and GPT must demonstrate both:

- **Recovery** of the target organism (for example E. coli on MacConkey).
- **Inhibition** of the organisms the medium is meant to exclude (for example
  suppression of Gram-positives).

A medium that grows the target but also grows everything else is not selective —
and reporting from it is a finding waiting to happen.

## Common GPT mistakes

- Using a media lot with no GPT, or one that is past expiry
- Over-autoclaving — it degrades selective agents and harms recovery of
  fastidious organisms
- Challenging with too high an inoculum, so weak recovery is hidden
- Testing recovery on a selective medium but never demonstrating inhibition

## Make it routine

GPT is only burdensome when it is improvised. With a defined strain panel, a
prep record, and a place to log results, it becomes a quick, repeatable gate.
