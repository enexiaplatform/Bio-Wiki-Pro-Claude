# From QC capability map to space, zoning and flow basis

A square-metre allowance is not a laboratory design.

The useful planning chain is:

```text
quality decision
→ method and workflow
→ activity, material and equipment state
→ segregation and adjacency need
→ room function
→ usable area and utility load
→ qualified engineering design
```

If this chain is skipped, a project can have enough total floor area and still be
operationally impossible. Equipment may not fit with service clearance, dirty and
clean flows may cross, samples may queue in uncontrolled staging, or utilities may
be unavailable where the method needs them.

Atlas should produce an **engineering-ready space and flow basis**. It should not
claim to produce CAD layouts, classified-area design, HVAC calculations, fire-code
approval or construction documents.

## Start with activities, not room names

Room lists inherited from another site are weak inputs. Begin with the activities
required by the capability model:

- sample receipt, verification and accessioning;
- controlled sample staging and storage;
- weighing, dilution and sample preparation;
- media receipt, storage, preparation, sterilization and release;
- membrane filtration, plating and aseptic manipulations;
- incubation under defined conditions;
- colony reading, identification and technical review;
- environmental-monitoring kit preparation and return;
- washing, decontamination and waste removal;
- reagent, culture and consumable storage;
- equipment cleaning, maintenance and calibration access;
- records, data review and investigation work;
- gowning, hand hygiene and personnel support.

For every activity, record inputs, outputs, duration, frequency, people, equipment,
materials, waste, environmental sensitivity and required utilities.

## Convert activities into functional zones

Activities may share a room only when their control needs are compatible. A useful
early zoning model distinguishes:

| Functional zone | Primary purpose | Key questions |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Receipt and staging | Control incoming samples and materials | Status segregation, temperature, queue time, chain of custody |
| General preparation | Weighing, dilution and non-critical preparation | Dust, spills, cleaning, shared equipment, ergonomic capacity |
| Protected microbiology work | Controlled manipulations and filtration | Biosafety, contamination protection, BSC access, cleaning |
| Media operations | Prepare, sterilize, cool, hold and release media | Clean/dirty direction, heat, steam, water, load handling |
| Incubation and controlled storage | Hold plates, cultures, reagents and samples | Temperature segregation, alarms, rack geometry, backup |
| Reading and review | Observe, count, identify, interpret and review | Data capture, lighting, queue, reviewer access |
| Wash and decontamination | Clean reusable items and manage biological waste | Dirty flow, drains, steam, heat rejection, waste exit |
| Support and governance | Gowning, documentation, investigation and supervision | Access, confidentiality, handover and proximity |

Zone names are planning abstractions. The engineering team determines final room
boundaries, finishes, pressure relationships and code compliance.

## Define segregation by hazard and control objective

“Separate rooms” should never be a reflex. State the reason for separation:

- protect the sample or method from contamination;
- protect personnel from biological or chemical exposure;
- prevent cross-contamination between organisms or products;
- separate clean and dirty equipment states;
- preserve quarantine, released and rejected material status;
- control temperature, humidity, light or vibration;
- protect data, records or controlled access;
- avoid incompatible simultaneous activities;
- meet approved biosafety, GMP, EHS or building-code requirements.

Then test whether separation requires a dedicated room, time segregation, a
qualified enclosure, controlled storage, procedural controls or a combination.
Atlas can expose the decision. Qualified site functions must approve the control.

## Model personnel, sample, material and waste flows separately

A single arrow called “flow” hides conflicts. Create at least four paths.

### Personnel flow

Record entry, gowning, hand hygiene, work transitions, supervisory access, breaks,
emergency egress and exit. Ask whether staff cross from dirty or organism-handling
activities into protected work without an adequate transition.

### Sample flow

Trace receipt, status verification, staging, preparation, testing, incubation,
reading, retain/disposition and investigation hold. Include time and storage
conditions at every queue.

### Material and equipment flow

Trace media, reagents, cultures, consumables, clean glassware, instruments,
maintenance parts and returned monitoring kits. Distinguish clean, used, quarantined
and released states.

### Waste flow

Trace biological, sharps, chemical, general and packaging waste from generation to
decontamination, temporary hold and facility exit. Define ownership and maximum hold
conditions.

Overlaying these paths reveals crossings and backtracking that a room-area table
cannot show.

## Build an adjacency matrix before drawing a layout

Classify every functional pair:

- **direct adjacency required** — frequent or time-critical transfer;
- **nearby preferred** — useful operational relationship;
- **neutral** — no meaningful relationship;
- **separation preferred** — contamination, hazard or disturbance concern;
- **separation required** — approved control or code basis.

Attach the reason and source. For example, media preparation may need direct or
near adjacency to sterilization and controlled cooling, while dirty waste staging
may require a protected route away from sample receipt.

The matrix gives engineering a testable basis. It also allows later layout options
to be scored consistently.

## Size usable activity envelopes

Equipment footprint alone understates space. For every major item, include:

```text
equipment footprint
+ operator working envelope
+ door, drawer and load movement
+ maintenance and calibration clearance
+ utility connection and isolation access
+ material staging and mobile cart space
+ safe lifting or handling path
```

For benches and rooms, add capacity for peak concurrent work rather than average
monthly demand. Incubators, refrigerators and stores need qualified usable rack or
shelf geometry, not only external cabinet volume.

## Translate workflow concurrency into area demand

Monthly hours do not reveal how many activities occur simultaneously. Determine:

- arrival pattern by day and shift;
- sampling rounds and fixed-time events;
- batch grouping and campaign peaks;
- preparation, execution and reading windows;
- number of analysts working concurrently;
- equipment sharing and queue constraints;
- investigation or repeat work during routine demand;
- future equipment and expansion triggers.

A small total workload can still require multiple protected work positions if tasks
have narrow simultaneous windows. A high monthly workload may fit a smaller area if
work is deliberately level-loaded and equipment cycles support it.

## Build the utility load register

For each room function and equipment class, capture:

- electrical load, voltage, phase, UPS and emergency-power need;
- purified water, potable water and drainage;
- clean steam or plant steam;
- compressed air, vacuum and gases;
- exhaust, heat rejection and local ventilation;
- network, data ports, monitoring and alarm integration;
- temperature and humidity operating range;
- acoustic, vibration or electromagnetic sensitivity;
- floor loading and delivery/installation route;
- maintenance isolation and safe shutdown requirement.

Concept values may identify utility classes. Final loads must come from selected
equipment, vendor data and qualified engineering calculations.

## Account for storage by state and condition

Storage demand comes from more than samples. Include:

- incoming, quarantine, released, rejected and returned materials;
- ambient, refrigerated, frozen, flammable and light-protected conditions;
- media before and after preparation;
- reference cultures and critical reagents;
- clean and used equipment states;
- monitoring kits before and after use;
- target consumable inventory and safety stock;
- investigation holds and retained samples;
- records and controlled forms where physical.

Connect the inventory model to physical pack dimensions and shelf occupancy. A
supply target that does not fit qualified storage is not a viable resilience plan.

## Treat support space as an operational requirement

Circulation and support percentages are acceptable only at concept stage. Replace
them progressively with explicit needs:

- gowning and lockers;
- handwash and hygiene transitions;
- mobile-equipment parking;
- cleaning-material storage;
- staging and pass-throughs;
- maintenance access;
- waste hold;
- documentation and review stations;
- supervisor and investigation collaboration;
- accessibility and emergency egress.

Support space often determines whether the laboratory remains orderly during peak
work, maintenance and deviations.

## Use scenarios and design triggers

Test at least:

1. current routine demand;
2. future approved demand;
3. a peak campaign or sampling event;
4. critical equipment downtime;
5. investigation or contamination response;
6. insourcing or new-method introduction;
7. protected expansion without disrupting routine work.

Create measurable triggers such as concurrent analysts, samples per peak day,
incubator rack utilization, target inventory volume, additional equipment count or
new segregation requirement.

## Evidence package for qualified engineering

Before design freeze, provide:

- approved capability and product/test matrix;
- workflow and method-step maps;
- equipment list with usable envelopes and service access;
- people, shift and concurrency assumptions;
- sample, material, personnel and waste flow maps;
- adjacency and segregation matrix with rationale;
- storage inventory by state and condition;
- utility load register and vendor data status;
- cleaning, decontamination and waste strategy;
- biosafety, EHS, fire, building and accessibility constraints;
- future scenarios and expansion triggers;
- open assumptions, owners and approval records.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas may report a concept allowance while clearly exposing limitations. It should
block controlled design use when:

- approved workflows and equipment are incomplete;
- concurrency and peak events are unknown;
- segregation decisions have no stated control objective;
- personnel, sample, material or waste flows are missing;
- utility classes or selected-equipment loads are unavailable;
- storage conditions and physical inventory are not reconciled;
- maintenance, calibration and installation access is excluded;
- qualified engineering and EHS owners are unnamed.

## Practical review checklist

- Can every room function be traced to a required capability?
- Are incompatible activities separated for an explicit reason?
- Are all four flow types mapped?
- Does the adjacency matrix state rationale and ownership?
- Do equipment envelopes include operation and service access?
- Is area based on concurrency and peaks, not only monthly averages?
- Does target inventory fit condition-qualified storage?
- Are utilities tied to equipment and operating states?
- Can maintenance and downtime occur without unsafe improvisation?
- Are future expansion triggers measurable?
- Are engineering, EHS and QA approvals outside Atlas explicit?
