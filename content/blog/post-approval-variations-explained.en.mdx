# Post-approval variations: changing an approved product

Approval is not the finish line. Over a product's life you will change a supplier,
a manufacturing site, a process parameter, a specification, or the shelf life. Each
of those changes to the marketing authorisation is a **variation**, and how you
file it depends on how much it could affect quality, safety, or efficacy.

## Classification by risk

Both major regions tier variations by risk, though the names differ:

- **EU** — **Type IA** (minor, often "do and tell" notifications), **Type IB**
  (minor, notify before implementing), and **Type II** (major, require assessment
  and approval before implementation). Some changes need a new application or an
  extension.
- **US (FDA)** — changes are filed as a **Prior Approval Supplement (PAS)** for the
  highest-risk changes, **Changes Being Effected (CBE-30 or CBE-0)** for moderate
  changes, or reported in the **Annual Report** for the lowest-risk changes.

The principle is shared: the bigger the potential impact on the product, the more
oversight the change needs **before** it reaches patients.

## The lifecycle link

Variations are where regulatory affairs and the quality system meet. A change is
usually driven through **change control** internally, then translated into the
right regulatory filing externally. Get the classification wrong — implement a
major change as a minor notification — and you have a compliance gap.

## ICH Q12 and established conditions

**ICH Q12** gives a framework for managing post-approval changes more predictably.
Its core idea is **established conditions**: the elements of the dossier that are
legally binding (and so require a variation to change) versus the supporting
information that can be managed under the company's own quality system. Defining
these up front reduces unnecessary filings for low-risk changes.
