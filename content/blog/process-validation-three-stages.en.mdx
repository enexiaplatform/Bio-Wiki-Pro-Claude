# Process Validation: The Three-Stage Lifecycle

Process validation moved long ago from "make three batches and file the binder"
to a **lifecycle**. FDA defines process validation as collecting and evaluating
data from process design through commercial production to establish scientific
evidence that the process can consistently deliver quality product.

That wording matters. Validation is not a single campaign at the end of tech
transfer. It starts when the process is designed, is challenged during process
performance qualification (PPQ), and continues while commercial batches are made.

## The lifecycle in one view

The FDA model has three connected stages:

1. **Stage 1 - Process Design:** define the commercial process from development,
   scale-up, prior knowledge, and risk assessment.
2. **Stage 2 - Process Qualification:** confirm that the designed process works
   at commercial scale under the proposed control strategy.
3. **Stage 3 - Continued Process Verification:** monitor routine production data
   to confirm the process remains in a state of control.

The stages are sequential, but the learning loop is continuous. CPV can reveal
drift that triggers investigation, CAPA, change control, or even a return to
process characterization work.

## Stage 1 - Process Design

Stage 1 is where the commercial manufacturing process is defined. A weak Stage 1
often shows up later as vague PPQ acceptance criteria, unexplained parameter
ranges, or a CPV program that trends convenient data rather than meaningful
signals.

A useful Stage 1 package should connect:

- The quality target product profile and critical quality attributes (CQAs).
- Critical process parameters (CPPs), critical material attributes, and known
  sources of variability.
- Development and scale-up studies that explain why the proposed ranges are
  appropriate.
- Risk assessments that show which variables need tighter control, enhanced
  monitoring, or additional characterization.
- The control strategy, including in-process controls, sampling points,
  acceptance criteria, hold times, cleaning constraints, and analytical methods.

ICH Q8(R2) is useful here because it frames pharmaceutical development as a way
to present product and process knowledge gained through scientific approaches and
quality risk management. In practical terms, Stage 1 should explain why the
process is expected to work before PPQ tries to prove that it does.

## Stage 2 - Process Qualification

Stage 2 asks whether the designed process can perform reproducibly at commercial
scale. It includes qualification of the facility, utilities, and equipment, then
the PPQ study itself.

A strong PPQ protocol normally defines:

- The commercial process version, batch size, equipment train, and qualified
  supporting systems.
- The number of PPQ batches and the rationale for that number.
- Enhanced sampling, test methods, statistical approach, and acceptance criteria.
- Expected operating ranges and the evidence behind them.
- How deviations, atypical trends, failed criteria, and unplanned interventions
  will be evaluated.
- The release decision path and what must be complete before moving to routine
  commercial control.

The number of PPQ batches should not be treated as a magic universal constant.
FDA and EMA expectations both point toward a justified approach based on product
and process understanding, process complexity, variability, site experience, and
risk. Three successful batches can be part of the evidence; they are not the
whole validation strategy.

## Stage 3 - Continued Process Verification

Stage 3 is the part many programs underbuild. After PPQ, routine commercial data
should be reviewed to show that the process remains in control over time.

Continued process verification usually includes:

- A CPV plan that identifies which CQAs, CPPs, in-process controls, and yield or
  defect signals will be trended.
- Alert and action limits that are justified, periodically reviewed, and tied to
  investigation expectations.
- Statistical process control where it is useful, including control charts,
  capability metrics, and trend rules.
- Periodic review through annual product review or product quality review.
- Triggers for deviation investigation, CAPA, change control, requalification, or
  process improvement.

CPV should not wait for annual review if the data shows a meaningful shift. The
point is to catch drift before it becomes repeated OOS results, recurring
deviations, complaints, or batch rejection.

## What the validation package should prove

A complete lifecycle package should make a simple argument with evidence:

- We understand the product and process well enough to define the commercial
  control strategy.
- The equipment, utilities, facilities, methods, and operators are ready to run
  that process.
- PPQ demonstrated reproducible performance under defined conditions.
- Routine monitoring continues to verify the process and detects drift early.
- Knowledge from deviations, complaints, CAPA, changes, and CPV is fed back into
  the control strategy.

This is where ICH Q10 becomes important. A pharmaceutical quality system should
support lifecycle management, knowledge management, change management, and
continual improvement. Process validation is one of the places where those
systems become visible.

## Common traps

The most common failure mode is treating PPQ as the whole story. Other traps are
more subtle:

- Writing Stage 1 as a retrospective justification instead of a real development
  knowledge package.
- Choosing PPQ acceptance criteria after seeing the data.
- Treating deviations during PPQ as paperwork problems rather than evidence
  about process control.
- Trending too many low-value metrics and missing the few signals that actually
  predict drift.
- Letting CPV reports become annual summaries with no clear alert/action logic.
- Running change control without asking whether the validated state has changed.
- Ignoring method variability, raw material variability, or operator-dependent
  steps when explaining process performance.

## A practical mental model

Use this question at each stage:

- **Stage 1:** Do we understand why this process should work?
- **Stage 2:** Did the commercial process actually work under defined, qualified
  conditions?
- **Stage 3:** Is it still working, and would we know early if it started to
  drift?

If the answer to any question is weak, the validation package is not just
missing documentation. It is missing confidence.
