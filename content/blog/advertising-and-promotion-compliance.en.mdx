# Advertising and promotion: staying on-label

Promoting a medicine is not like marketing a consumer product. A clever slogan
can become a regulatory problem if it overstates benefit, hides risk, implies an
unapproved use, or creates a misleading impression.

The basic rule is simple: promotional communication must be truthful,
non-misleading, fairly balanced, and consistent with the approved product
information. The practical work is harder. Teams have to apply that rule across
sales aids, websites, emails, congress booths, slide decks, videos, social posts,
search ads, patient materials, speaker programs, and field conversations.

Advertising and promotion compliance is where regulatory affairs, medical
affairs, legal, pharmacovigilance, and commercial teams meet the same question:

**Can we say this, to this audience, in this way, with this evidence and risk
context?**

## The on-label principle

The approved labeling or product information is the anchor. In the US, that
means the FDA-approved Prescribing Information and associated approved patient
labeling where applicable. In the EU, it means the approved Summary of Product
Characteristics (SmPC), package leaflet, and national or centralized product
information.

Promotional claims should stay within that authorized use. They should not
promote an unapproved indication, unapproved patient population, unapproved
dosing regimen, unapproved combination, unapproved route, or unsupported
comparative position.

The issue is not only whether each sentence is technically true. A piece can be
misleading because of what it emphasizes, omits, visually highlights, compares,
or implies. A chart, headline, image, footnote, testimonial, or sequencing of
claims can create a broader message than the literal words.

This is why labeling control and promotion control are inseparable. Read
[Labeling and product information](/blog/labeling-and-product-information) for
the source document side of the system.

## Claim types and evidence

Different claims need different levels of support.

**Indication claims** say what the medicine is approved to treat, prevent, or
diagnose. They should match the approved indication and relevant limitations.

**Efficacy claims** describe benefit. They need a clear basis in the approved
data, with endpoints, population, comparator, timeframe, and magnitude presented
accurately.

**Safety claims** describe risk, tolerability, contraindications, adverse
reactions, warnings, interactions, or monitoring. They should not minimize or
selectively frame risk.

**Comparative claims** say or imply that one product is better, safer, faster,
more convenient, cheaper, or otherwise preferable to another. These need
particularly careful support because implied superiority can appear even when
the word "superior" is not used.

**Convenience or adherence claims** may seem less risky, but they still need
evidence and should not imply clinical superiority without support.

**Patient-experience claims** can be powerful and risky. Testimonials,
case stories, and images must not imply typical outcomes that are not supported
or understate the seriousness of treatment risks.

A good review file maps every claim to its source: approved labeling, pivotal
study, published data, approved claims library, health-economic analysis,
real-world evidence package, or other substantiation accepted by the company and
region.

## Fair balance and risk information

Fair balance means benefit and risk are presented together in a way that allows
the audience to understand both. FDA evaluates prescription drug promotion for
whether risk information is presented with appropriate prominence, readability,
and context. OPDP's mission includes helping ensure prescription drug promotion
is truthful, balanced, and accurately communicated.

Fair balance is not achieved by hiding safety information in tiny text, placing
it far away from benefit claims, rushing through it in audio, burying it behind
a click, or using visuals that distract from it.

A practical review asks:

- Are the most important risks presented near the main benefit claims?
- Is the risk text readable on the actual device or format?
- Are contraindications, warnings, and common serious risks clear?
- Does the visual design give benefits much more weight than risks?
- Are quantitative benefits and risks shown with compatible denominators,
  timeframes, and populations?
- Does the piece leave a balanced overall impression?

The last question matters most. Promotional review is about the net impression,
not only sentence-by-sentence correctness.

## What MLR review is checking

Most companies use a medical, legal, and regulatory review process, often called
MLR review or promotional review. The names vary, but the core purpose is the
same: stop non-compliant material before it reaches the field, healthcare
professionals, patients, payers, or the public.

Regulatory review usually checks approved labeling consistency, required
disclosures, regional rules, submission requirements, and whether the material
stays within the authorization.

Medical review checks scientific accuracy, clinical context, substantiation,
data interpretation, citations, and whether the communication could mislead a
healthcare professional or patient.

Legal review checks broader legal risk: false or misleading communication,
comparative claims, fair-market-value and inducement concerns, privacy,
copyright, competition issues, and local code requirements.

The best MLR process is not a late-stage proofreading queue. It starts with
claim strategy, approved references, audience definition, and channel
requirements before the creative work becomes expensive to unwind.

## Promotional material lifecycle

A promotional piece has a lifecycle, and compliance needs controls at each step.

**Concept:** define audience, intended use, channel, claim territory, and whether
the piece is promotional, medical, educational, training, investor, payer, or
disease awareness. Classification matters because rules change by category.

**Drafting:** build from approved claims, current labeling, and approved
references. Avoid creating a piece first and looking for support later.

**Review:** route through the right reviewers, resolve comments, document claim
support, and preserve version history.

**Approval and release:** assign approval date, expiry date, market, audience,
use conditions, and owner. Field teams should know which version is current.

**Use:** control distribution, speaker decks, local adaptation, translations,
digital posting, and field customization.

**Withdrawal:** retire material when labeling changes, claims expire, references
are superseded, safety information changes, or the campaign ends.

Many findings happen after approval: old slides remain in use, local teams edit
approved claims, risk information is removed for space, or a website is not
updated after a labeling change.

## Digital and social promotion

Digital channels make the old rules more difficult, not less relevant.

A social post, banner ad, search result, short video, podcast sponsorship, or
interactive webpage can still be promotional labeling or advertising. Space
limits do not remove the need for truthful, non-misleading communication and
appropriate risk context.

Digital review should consider:

- what the audience sees before clicking;
- whether benefit and risk are separated across screens;
- whether links are obvious and functional;
- whether character limits force over-simplified claims;
- whether comments, sharing, or influencer content create new messages;
- whether paid search terms imply unapproved use;
- whether metadata, thumbnails, captions, and alt text create claims;
- whether adverse event reporting responsibilities are defined.

The review file should capture the final live format, not only a Word document
or static screenshot. A compliant draft can become non-compliant when rendered
on a mobile device.

## Disease awareness vs product promotion

Disease-awareness education can be valuable, but it becomes promotional when it
directly or indirectly drives use of a specific product.

Risk factors include brand cues, product colors, unique mechanism language,
links to product pages, sales-force distribution, timing around launch,
selective symptom framing, or disease descriptions that point strongly to one
medicine.

The test is the overall context. A piece does not need to name the product to
create a product message. Reviewers should ask whether a reasonable audience
would understand the communication as encouraging use of a particular medicine.

## Off-label and scientific exchange

Companies need a way to respond to unsolicited medical questions and share
scientific information appropriately. That is different from promotion.

Promotional teams should not use scientific exchange as a workaround for
off-label marketing. Medical information responses should be reactive,
balanced, evidence-based, non-promotional, and handled through the approved
medical process. Sales teams should know when to stop and refer a question to
medical affairs.

A common risk pattern is a field team using medical slides, congress abstracts,
or investigator data as promotional proof before the labeling supports the
claim. The fact that data exist does not automatically make the claim
promotional-ready.

## Regional oversight

In the US, FDA's Office of Prescription Drug Promotion (OPDP) oversees
prescription drug promotion. OPDP conducts surveillance, issues enforcement
letters, educates stakeholders, and reviews certain submissions.

In the EU, Directive 2001/83/EC provides the core legal framework for
advertising medicinal products, including restrictions on advertising products
without marketing authorization and requirements that advertising conform to the
approved particulars. Member-state law and industry self-regulatory codes add
important local requirements.

Global teams should not assume one approved asset can be used everywhere. The
claim, format, audience, disclosure, pre-clearance, submission, and retention
requirements can change by market.

## Common audit and enforcement risks

Promotional compliance problems often follow familiar patterns:

- implied use outside the approved indication;
- benefit claims without matching risk information;
- selective presentation of favorable endpoints;
- comparative claims without adequate support;
- subgroup claims that overstate exploratory data;
- patient stories that imply typical outcomes;
- old materials used after labeling changes;
- sales representatives modifying approved slides;
- disease-awareness materials functioning as disguised promotion;
- digital pieces where risk information is hard to find;
- claims libraries not synchronized with current labeling;
- missing approval, expiry, or withdrawal records.

The strongest control is not a longer checklist. It is a claims system that ties
approved messages, references, labeling, material versions, field use, and
withdrawal together.

## Practical MLR checklist

Use this checklist before approving a promotional material:

1. Define audience, market, channel, and intended use.
2. Confirm whether the piece is promotional, medical, disease awareness,
   training, payer, investor, or another category.
3. Map every claim to approved labeling or accepted substantiating evidence.
4. Check indication, population, dosing, comparator, endpoint, and timeframe.
5. Assess the overall net impression, not only individual sentences.
6. Present risk information with appropriate prominence and readability.
7. Verify comparative and superiority claims have adequate support.
8. Check visuals, graphs, testimonials, captions, metadata, and hyperlinks.
9. Confirm local submission, pre-clearance, code, and disclosure requirements.
10. Assign approval, expiry, owner, version, market, and withdrawal rules.
11. Confirm field teams cannot edit the piece outside approved controls.
12. Reassess the material after labeling or safety changes.

## Key takeaway

Promotional compliance is not about making marketing timid. It is about making
communication trustworthy. A strong promotional piece says what the product is
approved to say, supports it with appropriate evidence, gives risks their proper
weight, and leaves the audience with an accurate impression.
