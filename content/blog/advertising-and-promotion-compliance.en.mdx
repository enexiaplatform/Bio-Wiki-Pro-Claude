# Advertising and promotion: staying on-label

Promoting a medicine is not like marketing a consumer product. What you can say is
bounded by what the medicine is **approved** to do — and saying more is a
regulatory and legal risk, not just a marketing choice.

## The on-label principle

The central rule across regions: promotional claims must be **consistent with the
approved product information**. That means:

- No promotion for **unapproved (off-label) uses**.
- Claims must be supported by the data behind the authorisation.
- **Fair balance** — benefit claims are accompanied by the relevant risk
  information, not buried or omitted.

A claim that the labeling does not support is, in regulatory terms, misbranding —
regardless of how the data might look in isolation.

## How material gets cleared

Most companies run promotional content through a defined **review process** before
use — typically medical, legal, and regulatory sign-off (often called MLR review).
The reviewers check each claim against the approved labeling and the referenced
evidence, and confirm fair balance and required disclosures.

## Regional oversight

- In the **US**, the FDA's **Office of Prescription Drug Promotion (OPDP)**
  oversees prescription-drug advertising and can act on misleading promotion.
- In the **EU**, promotion is governed by EU law and member-state rules, often with
  self-regulatory industry codes layered on top.

The details differ, but the spine is the same: truthful, non-misleading, on-label,
and fairly balanced.

## The regulatory craft

Promotional review is where regulatory, medical, and legal judgement meet a
deadline. The skill is being able to map every claim back to the labeling and the
evidence — and to say no to the claim that cannot be supported, before it ships.
