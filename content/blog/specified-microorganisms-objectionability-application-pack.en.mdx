# Specified microorganisms and objectionability application pack

An enumeration result answers “how much recoverable growth was detected under the
counting method.” A specified-microorganism test answers a different question:

> Was a defined organism or organism group detected under an approved sequence of
> resuscitation, enrichment, selection and confirmation conditions?

Neither question alone establishes whether every other recovered organism is
acceptable for the product.

```text
product, route and market requirement
→ organism/group and absence decision
→ representative sample and preparation
→ resuscitation and enrichment
→ selective/differential isolation
→ confirmation and valid controls
→ specification plus broader objectionability assessment
```

## Application boundary

This pack structures evidence for non-sterile pharmaceutical laboratory planning. It
does not assign organisms to a product, reproduce compendial procedures, establish a
sample quantity, validate neutralization, identify an isolate, determine
objectionability or make a batch-disposition decision.

USP's official [&lt;62&gt; FAQ](https://www.usp.org/frequently-asked-questions/microbial-enumeration-nonsterile-products-tests-specified-microorganisms)
explains controls, suitability and selected procedural questions but states that USP
FAQs are informational rather than official interpretations. Current chapters,
monographs, registrations and approved procedures remain authoritative.

## Separate three related decisions

### Compendial or registered specified-organism decision

Determine presence, absence or limited occurrence of the organism/group required for
the defined article, sample amount and market.

### Product-specific objectionability decision

Assess whether any organism—not only a listed specified organism—could adversely
affect safety, quality, stability, use or the manufacturing control strategy.

### Contamination-control decision

Use organism and process evidence to determine whether materials, water, equipment,
holds, personnel or the environment remain appropriately controlled.

A compliant specified-organism test does not prove absence of all objectionable
microorganisms.

## Build the product–organism–decision matrix

For each application, capture:

- product/material family, strength and dosage form;
- route of administration, use and patient population;
- water activity, pH, preservative and antimicrobial attributes;
- manufacturing process, hold and exposure risks;
- market, monograph, registration and internal specification;
- organism, complex, group or quantitative category required;
- sample quantity and pooling/composite rule;
- preparation, dilution, neutralization and dispersal;
- pre-incubation/resuscitation and enrichment architecture;
- selective/differential media and conditions;
- presumptive reaction and confirmation method;
- controls, method-suitability and growth-promotion evidence;
- reporting convention and acceptance decision;
- additional objectionability review and escalation;
- current method/version, owner and lifecycle status.

Create separate records when organisms share a product but require different
enrichment, media, incubation, confirmation or decision logic.

## Establish applicability from controlled sources

Trace every required organism or group to:

- current product monograph or general acceptance criteria;
- approved registration or market commitment;
- product specification and risk assessment;
- route, formulation and patient risk;
- raw-material and manufacturing history;
- water and environmental organism profile;
- complaint, recall or adverse-event knowledge;
- applicable site procedures and quality agreements.

FDA states that “objectionable” must be assessed case by case using factors such as
species, count, dosage form, intended use, patient population and route. See the
official [FDA CGMP Questions and Answers](https://www.fda.gov/drugs/guidances-drugs/questions-and-answers-current-good-manufacturing-practice-regulations-production-and-process).

Do not turn a generic organism table into an unreviewed product specification.

## Control sampling and sample quantity

Define:

- batch/material population and sampling plan;
- sample quantity required for each decision;
- number of containers and composite/pool construction;
- aseptic collection and container;
- transport, hold time and temperature;
- relationship to enumeration samples;
- reserve sample and repeat restrictions;
- heterogeneous or difficult-to-sample product risks;
- reconciliation between tested quantity and reported absence claim.

If several tests share one preparation, verify that splitting, pooling and timing do
not reduce the required examined quantity or compromise recovery.

## Design preparation around recovery

The preparation must release and preserve a low number of target organisms while
controlling product antimicrobial activity. Document:

- product-to-diluent ratio;
- diluent, buffer, surfactant and neutralizer;
- dissolution, dispersion, homogenization or warming;
- pH and osmotic effects;
- neutralizer effectiveness and toxicity;
- maximum preparation and hold time;
- insoluble, oily, viscous or particulate behavior;
- filtration or washing where approved;
- organism adsorption, clumping or injury risk;
- branching into organism-specific test sequences.

Clear appearance or absence of growth in an uninoculated product does not demonstrate
recovery suitability.

## Preserve the detection architecture

```text
defined product quantity
→ sample preparation
→ pre-incubation or resuscitation
→ selective enrichment
→ selective/differential agar or other isolation
→ presumptive colony/reaction
→ confirmation
→ detected / not detected / invalid decision
```

Record actual volumes, transfers, timings and incubation windows at every step. A
workflow label such as “test for E. coli” is not enough to calculate media, vessels,
incubator occupancy or turnaround.

## Treat resuscitation and enrichment as distinct controls

Resuscitation may allow stressed organisms to recover before stronger selective
conditions. Enrichment increases the opportunity to detect low numbers but can also
create competition or selectivity effects.

Define:

- inoculation event and product quantity represented;
- broth identity, fill volume and headspace;
- target transfer volume and timing;
- temperature and incubation window;
- mixing and aerobic/anaerobic condition where relevant;
- selective pressure and target/non-target behavior;
- maximum delay before subculture;
- handling of turbidity, no visible growth or atypical reactions;
- contamination and carryover controls.

The USP FAQ clarifies that, for certain suitability applications, the challenge
organism is added at the mixing/resuscitation stage so it experiences the whole
recovery sequence.

## Control media properties by method step

Each medium may require one or more demonstrated properties:

- growth promotion for the target organism;
- inhibition of a defined non-target organism;
- indicative or differential reaction;
- sterility/negative control;
- neutralizer performance where relevant;
- performance through the shortest or longest applicable incubation boundary.

Link all media demand to the [Growth Promotion and Media QC Application
Pack](/blog/growth-promotion-media-qc-application-pack). A medium released for one
application is not automatically suitable for every organism pathway.

## Demonstrate method suitability for every claimed application

Suitability should address:

- representative products, strengths and worst-case matrices;
- target organism and controlled low inoculum;
- complete preparation, resuscitation, enrichment and isolation sequence;
- neutralizer effectiveness and toxicity;
- expected target recovery and presumptive reaction;
- product or background-flora interference;
- confirmation from the recovered target;
- negative controls and invalidity rules;
- grouping/bracketing rationale;
- change triggers requiring reassessment.

If the organism cannot be recovered, investigate preparation, neutralization and
selectivity before concluding that the product's antimicrobial activity makes the
test unnecessary.

## Separate presumptive and confirmed results

Define:

- colony morphology, color or biochemical reaction considered presumptive;
- number and selection of colonies for confirmation;
- purity/subculture requirements;
- confirmation tests or identification platform;
- acceptable and discordant profiles;
- handling when typical colonies are absent but atypical growth is present;
- external laboratory escalation;
- reporting while confirmation is pending;
- retention of relevant isolates.

A selective plate is evidence in a decision chain, not definitive organism identity
by itself.

## Build controls and validity into every routine run

Include:

- negative control performed with product testing;
- released media and required property evidence;
- incubation equipment and condition checks;
- process/handling contamination controls;
- method-defined positive controls where applicable;
- sample and transfer traceability;
- analyst qualification;
- invalidity, repeat and resample rules;
- second-person review of the complete sequence.

The USP FAQ states that a positive negative control indicates contamination during
testing and the test may be regarded as invalid. An invalid control cannot support a
product absence decision.

## Add the objectionability assessment

For any relevant recovery, assess:

- organism identity and confidence;
- amount and frequency of recovery;
- product route, formulation and patient population;
- potential for growth during shelf life or use;
- preservative resistance and biofilm potential;
- toxin, pathogen or opportunistic-pathogen concerns;
- impact on product stability, efficacy or container closure;
- raw-material, water, environment and process source plausibility;
- recurrence across batches or sites;
- complaint and adverse-event context;
- adequacy of the routine detection method.

USP &lt;60&gt; provides a defined absence test for Burkholderia cepacia complex under
the conditions described in that chapter. See [USP &lt;60&gt;](https://doi.usp.org/USPNF/USPNF_M12455_02_01.html).
Applicability still requires product- and market-specific assessment.

## Investigate detected, atypical and invalid outcomes

1. Preserve all plates, broths, isolates and raw observations as allowed.
2. Verify sample, media, controls, incubation and transfers.
3. Confirm presumptive organisms with the approved method.
4. Review laboratory contamination and cross-contamination hypotheses.
5. Assess related enumeration, water, environment and material data.
6. Review manufacturing holds, cleaning, equipment and process events.
7. Determine affected batches/materials and product impact.
8. Define retest/resample only through an approved scientific rationale.
9. Implement containment, CAPA and effectiveness review.
10. Reassess method scope if an unlisted objectionable organism is implicated.

A passing retest does not erase the original detection or invalid control.

## Convert the application into resource demand

Calculate by product–organism pathway:

- sample receipt, preparation and branching time;
- diluent, neutralizer, pre-enrichment and enrichment volumes;
- tubes, bottles, plates, filters and transfer consumables;
- media preparation and lot-release demand;
- incubator positions and vessel-days by condition;
- scheduled transfer and reading windows;
- weekend/holiday and off-shift interventions;
- presumptive-colony isolation and confirmation;
- microbial identification demand;
- technical review and batch-decision turnaround;
- suitability, training and growth-promotion load;
- positive, invalid, repeat and investigation scenarios;
- decontamination, waste and segregation flow.

One product sample can create several organism-specific enrichment trees. Use the
physical tree, not a single “USP &lt;62&gt; test” unit, for capacity planning.

## Lifecycle and change control

Reassess the application when product, strength, preservative, process, route,
market, specification, sample quantity, preparation, neutralizer, media, organism
requirement, incubation, confirmation, identification provider or test site changes.

Trace changes to method suitability, grouping/bracketing, trend continuity, training,
BOM, capacity, electronic records and objectionability risk assessment.

## Evidence required for a controlled Atlas node

1. Product–organism–decision matrix with controlled applicability.
2. Sampling and examined-quantity basis.
3. Complete preparation, resuscitation, enrichment and isolation architecture.
4. Media property and negative-control framework.
5. Product-specific method-suitability evidence.
6. Presumptive, confirmation and reporting rules.
7. Objectionability and detected-result investigation pathway.
8. Observed BOM, vessel-day, touch-time and review demand.
9. Grouping, lifecycle and change-control evidence.
10. Estimate-to-actual cases including positive/invalid scenarios.
