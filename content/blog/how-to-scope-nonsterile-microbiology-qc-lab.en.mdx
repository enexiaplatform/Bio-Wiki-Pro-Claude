# How to scope a non-sterile pharmaceutical microbiology QC lab before design freeze

A microbiology laboratory is often scoped backwards. Someone starts with an
available room, a vendor quotation, or a familiar equipment list. Only afterwards
does the project ask which products, markets, specifications, sample volumes,
methods and release targets the laboratory must actually support.

That sequence is expensive because a laboratory is not a collection of
instruments. It is an operating capability. The correct starting question is:

> Given this product portfolio, these markets, this testing demand and these
> release expectations, what microbiology capability must exist — and what must
> still be verified before a design or procurement decision is made?

This guide provides a discovery framework for **non-sterile pharmaceutical
microbiology**. It is deliberately a planning framework, not a registered
specification, approved method, GMP design qualification, or engineering design.
The exact obligations remain determined by the approved product dossiers,
applicable pharmacopoeias, local requirements and the site quality system.

## The planning chain: product to operating model

The useful unit of planning is not an incubator or an analyst. It is a traceable
chain:

```text
Product and market
→ applicable quality requirement
→ test and method architecture
→ sample, frequency and physical execution model
→ analyst, equipment, media, consumables, space and utilities
→ capacity, turnaround, redundancy and cost assumptions
→ qualified review and controlled project outputs
```

If any link is missing, a number at the end of the chain is only a placeholder.
For example, a count of “batches per month” does not reveal whether products have
different dosage forms, whether a test is performed in-house, which markets share
one physical test execution, whether a product is inhibitory, or whether media is
prepared internally. Those facts change the required operating capability.

## 1. Define the decision boundary first

Before collecting data, write down the decision that this work is supposed to
support. Common boundaries include:

- a new laboratory for a product launch or site expansion;
- a capacity and bottleneck review of an existing laboratory;
- an in-house versus contract-testing decision;
- a vendor-neutral equipment and URS basis;
- a phased transition from outsourced to in-house testing;
- a budget or investment-committee estimate; or
- a technical input to a broader engineering project.

This distinction matters. A discovery model can identify what should be checked
before design freeze. It cannot certify a cleanroom classification, approve a
method, replace a site risk assessment or sign off a facility.

Set the boundary explicitly:

| Include | Exclude unless separately commissioned |
| --- | --- |
| Product portfolio, markets and testing demand | Detailed architectural, HVAC or construction design |
| Method and sample-preparation assumptions | Method validation or regulatory approval |
| Equipment, analyst, media and consumables logic | Vendor selection or product equivalence claims |
| Capacity, redundancy and turnaround scenarios | A LIMS replacement or production schedule |
| Risks, evidence gaps and expert-review questions | A final GMP compliance conclusion |

This protects the project from a familiar failure mode: a preliminary sizing
worksheet quietly becoming a “validated laboratory design.”

## 2. Build a product-to-requirement inventory

Start with individual product families, not a total SKU count. One line per
product family is a practical minimum. Record:

- dosage form and route-relevant context;
- markets supplied and the status of the applicable dossier/specification;
- monthly batches, samples per batch and expected growth;
- microbial examination and specified-organism requirements from the approved
  specification;
- product matrix features that may affect recovery, such as preservatives, low
  pH, surfactants, solvents or high osmolarity;
- method-suitability status and available evidence;
- whether execution is in-house, outsourced or changing by phase; and
- whether multiple markets can use a shared physical test execution.

USP &lt;61&gt; describes microbial enumeration tests for non-sterile products, while
USP &lt;62&gt; addresses tests for specified microorganisms. Neither title alone tells
you the product-specific acceptance criteria, organism panel, sample preparation
or number of executions needed for a real product. Those must be traced to the
approved specification and method.

### Do not multiply physical tests by market automatically

When a product is supplied to more than one market, its requirements may need to
be traced separately. That does **not** automatically mean the laboratory must
perform a separate physical test for every market. The correct allocation depends
on the approved method, specification, product registration strategy, sample and
reporting arrangements.

Use one of three explicit states:

| Allocation state | What it means for a planning model |
| --- | --- |
| Shared physical execution | One justified test execution supports the selected markets. |
| Separate execution by market | Each market requirement creates its own physical workload. |
| Unresolved | Preserve the regulatory trace, but do not calculate method BOM or site capacity from it. |

The last option is valuable. “Unresolved” is more honest than silently choosing
the cheapest or most conservative interpretation without evidence.

## 3. Turn requirements into a method architecture

For each product/requirement pair, describe a **method architecture**, not an
imagined approved procedure. An architecture may include:

- microbial enumeration or specified-microorganism examination;
- representative sample quantity and dilution scheme;
- recovery and neutralization considerations;
- media, diluent, plates, controls and reference strains to be confirmed;
- incubation and read-out dependencies;
- analyst hands-on steps, including preparation, dilution, plating and transfer;
- equipment resources used; and
- the verification boundary.

The verification boundary should be prominent. An illustrative media requirement
is not a purchasing specification. A proposed neutralizer is not evidence that a
product’s antimicrobial effect has been neutralized. A method is not ready for
controlled use simply because a benchmark workflow is familiar.

Method suitability is especially important for non-sterile products. USP’s
official material for &lt;61&gt; states that the method must be able to detect
microorganisms in the presence of the product. In planning terms, any unknown or
pending suitability status is a blocker to freezing the final method BOM,
recovery assumptions and associated capacity.

## 4. Separate workload from capacity

Workload describes what must be done. Capacity describes whether the proposed
operating model can absorb that work with the required reliability.

For every in-house method, calculate transparent demand drivers such as:

- executions per month = batches × samples per batch × allocated physical tests;
- plates, media, diluent and controls per execution;
- incubator plate-days by incubation profile;
- analyst hands-on hours;
- autoclave or prepared-media equivalent; and
- quality-review and documentation effort where it has a material impact.

Then assess the resource plan against a stated basis. A useful concept-level
capacity check declares its own assumptions, for example:

```text
Available monthly resource capacity
= planned units × operating days × usable capacity per day
  × utilization allowance × uptime allowance
```

This is not a queue simulation. It does not capture every rack geometry,
campaign pattern, media batch, changeover, investigation, maintenance event or
concurrent activity. It is a deliberately limited decision check that can reveal
where more operational modelling is necessary.

### Include peak-week pressure and downtime

Average monthly demand is rarely the true design condition. A laboratory can look
comfortable on a monthly average and still fail when release samples arrive in a
campaign, an incubator is unavailable, or media preparation collides with routine
testing.

At a minimum, model:

- a declared peak-week allowance;
- planned equipment downtime;
- a redundancy policy for critical resources;
- the effect of outsourced versus in-house execution; and
- a growth or expansion scenario.

Do not present a single utilization percentage as a promise of turnaround time.
It is an input to a risk conversation. If it is near the project’s planning
threshold, the next step is a schedule-level review, not a more precise-looking
spreadsheet number.

## 5. Size the operating system, not only equipment

Equipment is a consequence of the method and workload model. A vendor-neutral
capability plan should articulate the function needed, its demand basis and its
qualification impact.

| Capability | Planning questions |
| --- | --- |
| Controlled incubation | Which method loads, temperatures, hold times, rack geometry and redundancy requirements apply? |
| Aseptic manipulation | Which sample-preparation and plating tasks need controlled protection, and what workflow segregation is required? |
| Media strategy | Will media be prepared on site, purchased prepared, or use a hybrid model? What are the quality, storage and lead-time controls? |
| Sterilization | Which loads are truly required on site, and what validated alternatives exist for prepared media or waste handling? |
| Support equipment | Which refrigerators, balances, pH meters, water sources, colony counters or identification capabilities are needed by the actual method architecture? |
| Data and review | How will raw data, calculations, deviations, trend review, method status and approval records be controlled? |

For each capability, write a functional basis before discussing brands:

> “Support the product-method load shown in the planning model, maintain the
> approved conditions, allow defined downtime and redundancy, and provide the
> records required for qualified site operation.”

That statement can later become an input to a controlled URS. It is not yet an
issued URS or an RFQ.

## 6. Treat in-house versus outsource as an operating-model decision

Outsourcing is not simply “zero equipment.” It changes risk, cost, lead time and
evidence responsibilities.

When evaluating an outsourced method, consider:

- release and transport timelines, including sample stability;
- method transfer, method suitability and specification alignment;
- quality agreement responsibilities;
- the contract laboratory’s actual method and capacity, not only its brochure;
- investigation, OOS/OOT and deviation communication paths;
- continuity, second-source and business-continuity requirements; and
- the threshold at which in-house capability becomes economically or
  operationally justified.

Conversely, bringing work in-house creates requirements beyond the first
instrument purchase: analyst qualification, media and culture controls, method
verification, data governance, equipment lifecycle, backup capacity and ongoing
quality oversight.

Use scenarios rather than a binary decision. A credible first Blueprint might
show a low-volume outsourced phase, an intermediate hybrid phase, and a future
in-house case. Each should disclose what would trigger the transition.

## 7. Make assumptions, evidence and open questions visible

Good planning does not hide uncertainty in formulas. Every material output should
state:

- its source or evidence basis and version where known;
- applicability conditions;
- user-provided facts;
- planning assumptions and confidence;
- unresolved inputs;
- the direction of impact if an assumption changes; and
- whether qualified expert review is required.

Examples of questions that should remain open until evidence resolves them:

- Which approved specifications apply to each marketed product?
- Does method suitability establish acceptable recovery and neutralization for
  each product matrix?
- Which market requirements share a physical execution?
- Are sample volumes, batches and peak-release patterns representative?
- Which water, media and culture-management strategy is approved for the site?
- What turnaround is genuinely required, and which incubation or review steps
  constrain it?
- What happens if the primary incubator, autoclave or contract laboratory is
  unavailable?

The absence of an answer is not a defect in the model. It is a decision risk that
the model has made visible.

## A practical discovery packet

Before a Blueprint workshop, request a concise packet rather than asking every
team for everything:

1. Product list, dosage forms, markets and forecast batch volumes.
2. Current approved microbial specifications and methods, where shareable.
3. Sample plans, samples per batch and release timeline expectations.
4. Method-suitability, recovery and neutralization evidence status.
5. Existing or proposed execution model: in-house, outsourced or hybrid.
6. Existing equipment, usable capacity, downtime history and constraints.
7. Media, water, culture and waste-handling strategy.
8. Current floor plan or space constraints if they exist.
9. Growth, launch, transfer or export scenarios that could change demand.
10. The people who own QC, QA, production, engineering and procurement decisions.

This packet is usually enough to build an initial evidence-aware model and, more
importantly, to identify what cannot responsibly be sized yet.

## Key takeaway

The fastest way to make a weak laboratory plan is to start with equipment. The
stronger sequence is:

**portfolio → requirement → method architecture → physical execution → workload
→ capacity → operating model → reviewed project output.**

When the evidence is incomplete, preserve the uncertainty. It is better to show
the decision that still needs owner input than to convert it into a confident but
unsupported procurement number.
