# Pharmaceutical microbial identification application pack

Microbial identification is not one test with one universally correct resolution.
The useful question is:

> What level of characterization is required to support this specific product,
> process, contamination-control or investigation decision?

```text
isolate source and event
→ decision risk and required resolution
→ culture purity and preparation
→ identification platform and reference system
→ confidence, ambiguity and confirmation
→ investigation, trend or disposition action
```

## Application boundary

This pack structures identification demand and evidence. It does not decide that an
organism is objectionable, prescribe identification depth for every recovery,
validate a platform, interpret a score outside the approved procedure, or claim that
two matching names prove a common source.

USP General Chapter &lt;1113&gt; describes characterization, identification and
strain typing as different levels that may be appropriate for microorganisms from
drug materials, water, manufacturing environments, intermediates and products. It
also notes that basic characterization can be sufficient for many risk assessments.
See [USP &lt;1113&gt; Microbial Characterization, Identification, and Strain
Typing](https://doi.usp.org/USPNF/USPNF_M3688_01_01.html).

## Define the decision before the resolution

| Application | Decision supported | Possible resolution need |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Routine flora trending | Is the site's organism profile stable or shifting? | Characterization, genus or species according to program |
| Environmental excursion | What source/pathway and process risk are plausible? | Risk-based genus/species; confirmation for consequential findings |
| Water-system signal | Does ecology suggest sampling contamination, transient ingress or persistence? | Identification sufficient for ecology, recurrence and objectionability review |
| Product/material recovery | Does identity change specification, objectionability or disposition? | Often higher confidence and resolution with orthogonal confirmation as justified |
| Sterility-test positive | What does the isolate imply about laboratory, process and batch? | High-confidence identification; strain comparison may be needed for source hypotheses |
| Growth-promotion/control culture | Is the challenge or control organism authentic and pure? | Method-defined confirmation and culture genealogy |
| Recurring isolate investigation | Are recoveries plausibly related? | Identification alone may be insufficient; strain typing may be required |

Species-level output should not be requested merely because a platform can print it.
Conversely, a low count should not justify shallow characterization when identity is
material to patient or process risk.

## Build the source–decision–resolution matrix

For every application, capture:

- isolate identifier and parent sample/result;
- product, material, water point, environment, personnel or laboratory source;
- date, location, operation, batch/shift and event status;
- count, colony type and original medium/incubation;
- intended decision and consequence of error;
- minimum characterization or taxonomic resolution;
- primary platform and approved reference database;
- confidence/acceptance rule;
- confirmation or orthogonal method trigger;
- preservation and retention requirement;
- turnaround target and escalation owner;
- investigation, trend and disposition linkage.

This matrix converts “percent isolates identified” into a defensible workload model.

## Preserve isolate genealogy

The chain from original colony to reported identity should remain traceable:

```text
parent sample and plate
→ selected colony/morphotype
→ subculture and purity check
→ working isolate
→ preparation/extraction
→ instrument run and raw result
→ database/version interpretation
→ confirmed report
→ retained or discarded culture
```

Record split cultures, repeat subcultures, mixed growth, loss of viability, storage,
shipment to an external laboratory and every reassignment of identity. A result that
cannot be linked back to the original recovery has limited investigation value.

## Establish purity before identification

Identification systems generally assume a suitable, pure culture. Define:

- colony-selection rationale, including distinct morphotypes;
- subculture medium and incubation;
- colony and cellular morphology;
- Gram or other differential stain where applicable;
- purity acceptance and mixed-culture response;
- minimum biomass and culture age;
- handling of slow-growing, stressed, mucoid, filamentous or non-sporulating isolates;
- preservation before repeated manipulation changes the culture.

A confident platform score from the wrong colony or a mixed preparation does not
answer the original event.

## Match the method architecture to the application

### Phenotypic characterization

Morphology, staining, catalase/oxidase and biochemical reactions can support rapid
triage, purity assessment and risk classification. Define which observations are
reported, how they guide the next method and where they are insufficient.

### Biochemical or automated phenotypic identification

Control inoculum density, culture condition, panel/card selection, incubation,
quality controls, software/database version, probability or confidence rules and
atypical profile handling.

### MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry

Define target preparation, direct versus extraction workflow, calibration/control
requirements, replicate spots, spectrum acceptance, score thresholds, database
coverage, closely related taxa, repeat/extraction rules and confirmation triggers.

### Targeted sequencing

Define DNA extraction, target region, amplification controls, sequence quality,
contamination review, forward/reverse reconciliation, database/search method,
coverage and identity criteria, ambiguous taxa and reporting convention. 16S or ITS
does not guarantee species-level discrimination for every organism.

### Strain typing or higher-resolution comparison

Use only when the decision requires relatedness evidence. Define the method,
discrimination, reference isolates, quality criteria and interpretation by qualified
specialists. Matching species names do not demonstrate that isolates share a source.

## Control reference databases and libraries

The reference system is part of the method. Record:

- database/vendor and version;
- included organism scope and known gaps;
- nomenclature and taxonomy updates;
- local or user-added library governance;
- reference-strain traceability and spectrum/sequence quality;
- validation/verification set by organism group and application;
- version-change assessment and requalification;
- cybersecurity, backup and result-retention controls;
- external database access date and parameters.

Do not silently reinterpret historical results after a taxonomy or database update.
Preserve both the original reported identity and any controlled reassessment.

## Define confidence and discordance rules in advance

The procedure should specify:

- acceptable, tentative, ambiguous and no-identification categories;
- platform-specific score/probability/quality criteria;
- agreement required across replicates;
- when to repeat preparation versus select another colony;
- when an orthogonal platform is required;
- handling of genus-only or multiple-candidate results;
- conflict between morphology, Gram reaction and instrument output;
- conflict between two identification technologies;
- rules for reporting the nearest reliable taxonomic level;
- expert-review and external-laboratory triggers.

Never choose the more convenient of two discordant identities without a documented
scientific rationale.

## Verify the platform for intended use

Verification or validation evidence should cover the organism groups and decisions
actually claimed. Consider:

- representative bacteria, yeasts, molds, spores and difficult taxa;
- site/environmental and product-relevant isolates;
- inclusivity, exclusivity and closely related organisms;
- repeatability across operators, days and reagent lots;
- culture age, medium and preparation effects;
- mixed/contaminated cultures and no-identification behavior;
- database coverage and version;
- comparison with a qualified reference method;
- reportable resolution and confidence;
- failure, repeat and confirmation rates;
- outsourced provider method and data package.

Verification cannot guarantee correct identification of organisms absent from the
reference system.

## Connect identity to investigation evidence

Interpret the result with:

- organism ecology and likely reservoirs;
- survival, sporulation, biofilm or disinfectant implications;
- location, process and material movement;
- previous site recoveries and recurrence;
- related water, environment, personnel, laboratory or product isolates;
- product route, formulation and vulnerable patient population;
- count, timing and exposure;
- method limitations and confidence;
- alternative source hypotheses.

FDA's microbiology laboratory inspection reference emphasizes that the significance
of an organism depends on product use and potential hazard, and that identification
records linked to sample source support review of microbial problems. See [FDA
Microbiological Pharmaceutical Quality Control Labs](https://www.fda.gov/inspections-compliance-enforcement-and-criminal-investigations/inspection-guides/microbiological-pharmaceutical-quality-control-labs-793).

Identity informs a hypothesis; it does not prove root cause.

## Build the reporting and review decision

The controlled report should contain:

- isolate and parent-event identifiers;
- submitted and observed culture condition;
- method, instrument, reagent and database versions;
- quality controls and run validity;
- raw candidate(s), score and approved interpretation;
- reported taxonomic level and confidence category;
- morphology or other supporting/discordant evidence;
- repeats, extraction or confirmation performed;
- limitations and unresolved ambiguity;
- reviewer and approval timestamps;
- links to trend, investigation and disposition records.

Preserve raw spectra, sequences, chromatograms, images or panel outputs according to
the controlled data-retention strategy.

## Convert identification into resource demand

Model demand by source and required resolution, including:

- colony selection and subculture;
- purity checks and basic characterization;
- media, stains, reagents, targets/cards and extraction materials;
- incubation and culture-retention space;
- instrument preparation, calibration, run and cleaning;
- repeat, extraction and orthogonal confirmation rates;
- raw-data review and report approval;
- database/library maintenance and version assessment;
- reference-culture and quality-control demand;
- isolate freezer/archive capacity and genealogy administration;
- external testing shipment, queue and technical review;
- after-hours excursion and expedited turnaround;
- investigation meetings and cross-isolate comparison.

Use morphotype and escalation scenarios, not only sample counts. One environmental
plate may create several isolates, while many routine plates create none.

## Make-versus-buy and continuity decisions

For an in-house platform, evaluate utilization, specialist coverage, database scope,
qualification, service, backup and lifecycle cost. For outsourcing, evaluate:

- provider method, claimed resolution and accreditation/quality controls;
- culture shipment and viability;
- turnaround and expedited service;
- raw-data and database-version transparency;
- repeat/confirmation governance;
- confidentiality and data ownership;
- isolate return, retention and disposal;
- alternate-provider continuity;
- technical consultation and investigation support.

An attractive per-isolate price can hide delay, ambiguous-result rework and loss of
genealogy.

## Lifecycle and change control

Reassess the application when changing culture medium/condition, preparation,
instrument, reagent/target, software, score threshold, database, nomenclature,
reference library, report format, external provider, required resolution or isolate
retention.

Trace the change to verification, historical comparability, trend continuity,
training, BOM, capacity, electronic records and open investigations.
