# How to Prepare for a GMP Audit in 30 Days

A GMP inspection notice can make a site feel as if everything must be fixed at
once. That is the trap. Thirty days is not enough time to rebuild a weak quality
system, but it is enough time to find the highest-risk gaps, organize evidence,
prepare people, and stop avoidable inspection mistakes.

The goal is not to create a perfect-looking audit room. The goal is to make the
truth easy to understand: what is controlled, what is open, what risk is known,
what interim controls are in place, and what CAPA is driving the system forward.

Inspection readiness is daily discipline. A 30-day plan is just a focused sprint
to prove that discipline.

## The first rule: do not hide the mess

If a deviation is overdue, a CAPA effectiveness check failed, or an audit-trail
review is late, hiding it makes the risk worse. Inspectors are trained to connect
records. A batch record leads to an OOS. An OOS leads to a CAPA. A CAPA leads to
training, change control, validation, or supplier qualification. If the chain
breaks, the story breaks.

The better approach is:

- know your open risks;
- prioritize by patient, product, data integrity, and validated-state impact;
- close only what can be closed honestly;
- document rationale for anything still open;
- define interim controls where risk remains;
- prepare owners to explain the status clearly.

An open CAPA with strong risk assessment and management oversight is usually more
defensible than a rushed closure with weak evidence.

## Days 1-3: build the audit command center

Start by defining who owns the inspection response.

Set up:

- **Audit lead:** usually QA, accountable for inspection flow and decisions.
- **Front room:** hosts inspector interactions, interviews, request logging, and
  answer coordination.
- **Back room:** retrieves, reviews, and organizes requested records before they
  are provided.
- **SME list:** named owners for QC lab, manufacturing, warehouse, validation,
  engineering, microbiology, data systems, complaints, suppliers, and release.
- **Runner/document controller:** tracks request IDs, versions, timestamps, and
  exact copies provided.
- **Decision escalation path:** who approves sensitive records, disagreements,
  commitments, and potential 483 response strategy.

Create a simple request log with these fields:

| Field | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Request number | Keeps all requests traceable |
| Exact request wording | Prevents over-producing documents |
| Owner | Avoids orphaned requests |
| Records provided | Creates the official trail |
| Version/date | Prevents wrong or obsolete documents |
| Time received and fulfilled | Shows control and discipline |
| Notes/commitments | Captures follow-up promises |

This log becomes the spine of the inspection. Without it, teams forget what was
given, duplicate records, or contradict themselves.

## Days 4-7: triage the open quality system

Pull a live list of open and recently closed items:

- deviations;
- OOS and OOT investigations;
- CAPAs and effectiveness checks;
- change controls;
- audit findings and self-inspection actions;
- complaints and recalls;
- supplier quality issues;
- calibration, qualification, and maintenance exceptions;
- validation deviations;
- data integrity incidents;
- overdue SOP reviews or training;
- stability, APR/PQR, and trend commitments.

Rank them by inspection risk:

1. **Patient or product quality risk**
2. **Data integrity risk**
3. **Sterility assurance or contamination control risk**
4. **Batch-release impact**
5. **Repeat or systemic recurrence**
6. **Overdue action with weak justification**
7. **Known regulatory commitment**

For each high-risk item, ask:

- Is the problem statement clear?
- Is product impact assessed?
- Is root cause supported by evidence?
- Are interim controls defined?
- Are CAPA actions mapped to root cause?
- Is the effectiveness check objective and time-bound?
- Is the owner ready to explain the status?

Do not spend the first week polishing low-risk documents while a critical CAPA is
overdue. The inspection will follow risk, and so should you.

## Days 8-10: make critical documents retrievable

Inspectors often judge a quality system partly by how quickly and consistently it
can retrieve its own evidence. A document that exists but takes three hours to
find does not inspire confidence.

Prepare an evidence map for:

- quality manual or PQS overview;
- site master file or facility overview where applicable;
- organization chart and Quality Unit responsibilities;
- SOP index and document-control procedure;
- training matrix and role-based training evidence;
- deviation/OOS/CAPA procedures;
- change-control procedure and recent examples;
- batch-record review and release procedure;
- complaint and recall procedures;
- supplier qualification procedure;
- validation master plan;
- calibration and equipment qualification program;
- computerized systems inventory and data integrity controls;
- audit-trail review procedure and recent evidence;
- self-inspection/internal audit schedule and reports;
- APR/PQR examples;
- product list, batch list, and recent release history.

For each item, know the controlled location, current version, owner, and likely
SME. Avoid creating uncontrolled "audit copies" that drift from the source of
truth.

## Days 11-14: review the four evidence chains inspectors follow

A strong inspection preparation plan does not review documents one by one. It
walks evidence chains.

### Chain 1: Batch release

Pick a recent released batch and trace:

- batch record;
- deviations and interventions;
- in-process controls;
- QC test results and CoA;
- OOS/OOT history;
- audit-trail review;
- equipment/calibration status;
- material CoAs and supplier status;
- QA disposition decision.

The question is: **does the release decision match the total evidence?**

### Chain 2: OOS/deviation to CAPA

Pick a significant investigation and trace:

- event detection;
- immediate containment;
- product impact;
- laboratory or process hypothesis testing;
- root cause;
- CAPA;
- change control or training;
- effectiveness check;
- recurrence trend.

The question is: **did the site learn enough to reduce recurrence?**

### Chain 3: Change control to validated state

Pick a meaningful change and trace:

- change request;
- quality, validation, regulatory, and stability impact;
- risk assessment;
- required actions;
- training/SOP updates;
- qualification or validation impact;
- implementation evidence;
- effectiveness or post-change monitoring.

The question is: **was the change controlled before routine use?**

### Chain 4: Data integrity

Pick a release-critical computerized system and trace:

- user access;
- roles and privileges;
- audit trail settings;
- audit-trail review evidence;
- backup and retention;
- invalid or repeated runs;
- system validation status;
- incident history.

The question is: **can the data be trusted from creation through review?**

## Days 15-18: fix obvious gaps without inventing history

By mid-sprint, you will have a list of gaps. Separate them into three groups.

| Gap type | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| Administrative gap with objective evidence available | Correct it properly through the quality system |
| Real quality gap with risk controlled | Document risk, interim controls, owner, CAPA, and due date |
| Real quality gap with uncontrolled risk | Escalate immediately; consider batch, process, or distribution impact |

Do not backdate training. Do not recreate audit-trail review as if it happened on
time. Do not close CAPAs just because the inspection is coming. Inspection
preparation should strengthen the quality system, not create a second problem.

Good language for an open issue is calm and specific:

> We identified this gap during readiness review. Product impact was assessed as
> low because [evidence]. Interim control is [control]. CAPA [ID] is open to
> address the systemic cause, with effectiveness check [metric/window].

That answer is not perfect, but it is controlled.

## Days 19-21: rehearse SMEs

Subject matter experts should not memorize scripts. They should practice clear,
truthful, evidence-backed answers.

Coach SMEs to:

- answer the question asked;
- pause before responding;
- say "I will verify that" instead of guessing;
- use the approved procedure language;
- avoid volunteering unrelated problems;
- explain what they personally do, not what they think another department does;
- bring answers back to evidence;
- escalate if a request is unclear or outside their scope.

Run short interview drills:

- "Walk me through how you handle an OOS."
- "Show me how audit trails are reviewed for this system."
- "How do you know this analyst was trained?"
- "What happens when a CAPA effectiveness check fails?"
- "How do you prevent use of out-of-calibration equipment?"
- "How do you qualify a critical supplier?"
- "How do you know this batch was ready for release?"

The best SME answers are boring in a good way: procedure, evidence, example,
control.

## Days 22-24: run the mock audit

A mock audit should not be friendly theatre. It should test the system the way an
external inspector would.

Use a small team that is independent enough to challenge assumptions. Give them a
scope:

- one released batch;
- one OOS or deviation;
- one CAPA;
- one data system;
- one change control;
- one supplier or material;
- one equipment or calibration record;
- one training record;
- one self-inspection finding.

Require the mock audit team to:

- issue document requests through the request log;
- interview SMEs;
- time document retrieval;
- challenge inconsistencies;
- classify gaps by risk;
- identify repeat themes;
- recommend CAPA only where warranted.

A mock audit that finds nothing is usually a weak mock audit. The goal is to find
the painful issues before someone else does.

## Days 25-27: prepare for inspection logistics

Make the physical and digital flow simple:

- confirm visitor access, room booking, Wi-Fi, screen sharing, and printing rules;
- define whether records are shown electronically, printed, or exported;
- confirm who can provide copies and who approves them;
- prepare a secure area for back-room review;
- remove obsolete uncontrolled documents from shared folders and workspaces;
- verify that controlled documents are accessible;
- align shift coverage for key SMEs;
- prepare a daily inspection debrief rhythm;
- define who records commitments made to inspectors.

Also prepare the tone. The inspection team should be professional, calm, and
transparent. Defensiveness makes normal questions feel suspicious.

## Days 28-30: rehearse closeout and 483 response readiness

FDA explains that Form FDA 483 observations are inspectional observations, not a
final agency compliance determination. But they matter. FDA's pharmaceutical
inspection information states that companies generally have **15 business days**
to provide a voluntary response to a Form FDA 483. FDA's 2026 draft guidance on
responding to Form 483 observations also recommends submitting a response within
15 business days when a firm chooses to respond.

That means you should prepare before the closeout meeting.

For each potential observation:

- capture the exact wording;
- do not argue from memory;
- ask clarifying questions respectfully;
- identify immediate correction if possible;
- assign a response owner;
- start root-cause assessment;
- assess product and patient risk;
- define CAPA or interim controls;
- collect supporting documents;
- set realistic completion dates.

If an observation is accurate, acknowledge it and respond with evidence. If you
disagree, support the disagreement with objective records and clear rationale.
Either way, avoid vague promises like "we will retrain personnel" unless training
is truly the cause and you can verify changed performance.

## The 30-day checklist

Use this as a final pass:

- [ ] Inspection roles are assigned.
- [ ] Request log is ready.
- [ ] SME list and escalation path are current.
- [ ] Open deviations, OOS/OOT, CAPAs, and changes are triaged by risk.
- [ ] Critical overdue items have documented rationale or escalation.
- [ ] High-risk CAPAs have objective effectiveness checks.
- [ ] Recent batch-release evidence chains are reviewed.
- [ ] Data integrity controls are reviewed for release-critical systems.
- [ ] Training records match current SOPs and job roles.
- [ ] Calibration, qualification, and maintenance status is current or justified.
- [ ] Supplier and material controls are retrievable.
- [ ] Self-inspection findings feed CAPA where needed.
- [ ] Mock audit is complete and gaps are ranked.
- [ ] SMEs have practiced evidence-backed answers.
- [ ] Front-room/back-room logistics are tested.
- [ ] Potential 483 response owners are identified.

## What not to do

Avoid these last-minute moves:

- closing CAPAs without effectiveness evidence;
- backdating training or reviews;
- rewriting SOPs without training and implementation;
- creating uncontrolled "audit binders";
- hiding known gaps from SMEs;
- making verbal commitments that are not logged;
- giving more documents than requested;
- letting everyone answer every question;
- treating the mock audit as a morale exercise.

The inspection will test whether your quality system is real. Keep the preparation
real too.

## The QC/QA takeaway

Thirty days should produce four things: a clean view of risk, retrievable
evidence, prepared people, and controlled inspection flow.

You do not need every problem solved before the inspector arrives. You do need to
show that the site knows its risks, protects product quality, owns its gaps, and
uses CAPA to improve the system.
