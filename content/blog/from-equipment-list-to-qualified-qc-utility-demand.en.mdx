# From equipment list to qualified QC utility demand

An equipment list does not define the utilities a laboratory needs. A nameplate can
describe a maximum electrical load, but it does not explain operating schedules,
simultaneous starts, heat rejection, water quality, drainage, gases, exhaust,
networking or the consequence of an interruption.

The useful decision chain is:

```text
method and workflow demand
→ equipment operating states and schedule
→ point-of-use utility attributes
→ connected, coincident and peak demand
→ distribution, monitoring and resilience basis
→ engineering design and qualification evidence
```

Atlas can structure this basis and expose missing inputs. It does not size building
services, design distribution systems, approve hazardous exhaust, calculate HVAC
loads or qualify a utility.

## Start with the quality decision, not the connection schedule

For each equipment item or functional area, record what quality decision it supports
and what happens when the utility is unavailable or outside its approved condition.

| Supported activity | Utility question | Decision consequence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Incubation or controlled storage | Which temperature, alarm, power and backup conditions preserve the qualified state? | Delayed result, invalidated exposure or sample/material impact |
| Steam sterilization | What steam, water, drainage, power, ventilation and cycle-recovery basis applies? | Load cannot be processed or cycle evidence becomes unacceptable |
| Biosafety cabinet operation | Which room, airflow, exhaust, power and alarm interfaces are required by the selected configuration? | Work may stop or lose its approved containment/protection basis |
| Analytical instrument run | Which power quality, gases, cooling, vacuum, data and environmental conditions support a valid sequence? | Aborted sequence, sample stability risk or repeat demand |
| Media and reagent preparation | Which water grade, temperature, drainage and environmental controls support preparation? | Material cannot be prepared or may require investigation |

The same nominal connection can have a different criticality depending on the
method step, sample stability, turnaround commitment and qualified backup.

## Build a point-of-use utility register

Create one record per equipment configuration and installation point. Capture:

- equipment class, configuration, quantity and lifecycle stage;
- room, zone and intended use;
- utility type and point-of-use quality attributes;
- vendor reference, document version and data confidence;
- connected, normal, startup, standby, cleaning and shutdown demand;
- operating cycle, duration, frequency and expected coincidence;
- heat, moisture, vibration, noise and discharge released to the room or system;
- pressure, flow, temperature, purity, stability and recovery requirements;
- connection, isolation, backflow, drainage, exhaust and access assumptions;
- alarm, monitoring, data retention and escalation needs;
- maximum tolerable interruption and recovery requirement;
- qualified alternate, emergency supply or shutdown strategy;
- engineering owner, method owner, QA reviewer and unresolved evidence.

Do not copy one vendor datasheet across several possible models. Configuration,
accessories, local voltage, operating mode and installation option can materially
change demand.

## Separate utility identity from required quality

Labels such as “water,” “air,” “steam” or “power” are incomplete. Define the
attribute required at the point of use and why it matters.

Possible attributes include:

- electrical voltage, phase, frequency, power quality, earthing and ride-through;
- water grade, microbial/chemical condition, temperature, pressure and flow;
- steam quality and operating envelope;
- gas identity, purity, pressure, flow and cylinder/bulk supply arrangement;
- vacuum level, capacity and contamination protection;
- exhaust type, capture requirement and discharge restriction;
- drainage temperature, flow, chemistry, biological load and neutralization need;
- network segmentation, bandwidth, time synchronization and data availability;
- room temperature, humidity, pressure relationship and heat-removal capacity.

The project must determine which attributes are product-, method-, equipment-, EHS-
or facility-critical. Atlas should not infer a GMP classification from a utility name.

## Model operating states and schedules

For each resource, distinguish:

1. idle or standby;
2. startup, warm-up or pull-down;
3. normal processing;
4. peak or worst-case cycle;
5. cleaning, regeneration or sanitization;
6. maintenance and calibration;
7. controlled shutdown and restart;
8. failure recovery.

Link these states to the workload calendar. Monthly average consumption cannot show
whether two autoclaves start together, several chambers recover after door opening,
or a morning analytical sequence creates a short gas or cooling peak.

## Keep connected, coincident and consumed demand separate

Use explicit definitions:

```text
connected demand = sum of installed maximum or design connections
coincident demand = demand expected during the defined simultaneous scenario
consumption = utility quantity used over a stated period
```

The engineering basis should document diversity assumptions rather than hide them
inside a single factor. Test at least routine, peak production, cleaning/sanitization,
restart-after-outage and future-expansion scenarios.

An illustrative arithmetic total is not a design load until configuration, schedule,
diversity, margins and engineering criteria are reviewed.

## Reconcile room loads and interfaces

Equipment can affect more than its direct connection. Record:

- sensible and latent heat release;
- exhaust and make-up air interaction;
- door-opening and material-transfer patterns;
- floor loading, vibration and anchoring;
- service and removal clearances;
- hot, chemical or biologically contaminated discharge;
- cylinder storage and gas detection;
- condensate, leak and overflow pathways;
- alarms that must reach a monitored location;
- dependencies shared with adjacent production or laboratory areas.

This register gives qualified engineers an auditable starting point. It is not a room
heat-load calculation or an HVAC design.

## Design resilience around interruption consequence

For every critical utility–equipment pair, ask:

- How long can the activity tolerate an interruption?
- Does the equipment fail safe, hold state or lose the run?
- Which samples, media, cultures, standards or records are exposed?
- How is an excursion detected, time-stamped and escalated?
- Can work move to a qualified alternate?
- Is backup power enough, or are cooling, gases, network and exhaust also required?
- How long does recovery, cleaning, requalification or backlog clearance take?
- Can two apparent backups fail through one common distribution path?

Possible controls include local UPS, emergency power, independent alarms, stored
utility capacity, dual feeds, qualified shared equipment, contract-laboratory backup,
spares, service response and a controlled stop-work plan. Selection remains a
site risk and engineering decision.

## Connect utility evidence to qualification

EU GMP Annex 15 describes the user requirements specification as a reference point
through the validation lifecycle and expects qualification to consider equipment,
facilities, utilities and systems. See the European Commission's official [EudraLex
Volume 4 GMP guidelines](https://health.ec.europa.eu/medicinal-products/eudralex/eudralex-volume-4_en).

The evidence chain may include:

- approved user and functional requirements;
- vendor and engineering design data;
- design review and risk assessment;
- installation and connection verification;
- calibration and monitoring configuration;
- operating-range and alarm challenges;
- utility quality sampling or testing where applicable;
- performance under representative load and operating states;
- failure, recovery and backup challenges;
- preventive maintenance and periodic review.

The project must define what is commissioned, qualified, calibrated, monitored or
verified and which function owns each record.

## Evidence package for engineering handoff

Before utility design freeze, collect:

1. approved capability, method and equipment scope;
2. configuration-specific vendor data;
3. point-of-use utility attribute register;
4. operating-state and coincidence schedule;
5. room, exhaust, drainage and data interfaces;
6. routine, peak, cleaning and restart scenarios;
7. current facility capacity and measurement evidence;
8. expansion allowance and release triggers;
9. interruption consequence and resilience decision;
10. monitoring, alarm and record-retention requirements;
11. commissioning and qualification responsibility matrix;
12. assumptions, exclusions, owners and open decisions.

## What Atlas should block

Atlas should prevent a utility basis from appearing engineering-ready when:

- equipment configuration or quantity is unresolved;
- only nameplate maxima or generic allowances are available;
- point-of-use quality attributes are undefined;
- operating schedules and coincidence assumptions are missing;
- heat, exhaust, drainage, gas or network interfaces are omitted;
- current site capacity is asserted without measured evidence;
- interruption consequence and backup are not reviewed;
- utility monitoring and alarm ownership are unclear;
- future loads have no space, connection or release trigger;
- engineering and QA review boundaries are unnamed.
