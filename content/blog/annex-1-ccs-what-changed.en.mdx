# Annex 1 (2022): what actually changed

EU GMP Annex 1 (2022) did not simply add more sterile manufacturing rules. It
changed the inspection conversation. The old question was often, "Do you have the
procedure?" The new question is closer to, **"Show me how your contamination
control system works, and show me the data that proves it is still working."**

That shift matters because Annex 1 now expects sterile manufacturers to connect
facilities, people, process design, utilities, monitoring, deviations, and quality
risk management into one coherent control story.

## 1. CCS became the backbone

The Contamination Control Strategy (CCS) is the headline change. A CCS is not a
folder of SOPs or a one-time gap assessment. It is the documented logic that shows
how the site prevents microbial, particulate, and endotoxin contamination across
the full product lifecycle.

A useful CCS links:

- contamination sources;
- preventive controls;
- monitoring evidence;
- residual risk;
- escalation triggers;
- CAPA and continuous improvement.

The practical test is simple: if an environmental monitoring trend, media-fill
failure, cleanroom change, or intervention change does not feed back into the CCS,
the CCS is probably decorative rather than living.

## 2. QRM moved from support tool to operating logic

Quality risk management is no longer an occasional document used to justify an
exception. Annex 1 expects QRM to shape routine decisions: cleanroom grade, sampling
location, monitoring frequency, intervention design, barrier technology, hold time,
sterilization approach, and investigation depth.

"We have always done it this way" is weak evidence. A stronger answer is: "This is
the contamination risk, this is the control, this is the data showing the control is
effective, and this is when we escalate."

## 3. Barrier technology is harder to ignore

Annex 1 gives stronger emphasis to separating operators from critical sterile
operations through isolators, RABS, closed systems, and well-designed airflow
protection. This does not mean every legacy line must instantly become an isolator,
but it does mean a site should be able to explain why its current technology is
appropriate for the process risk.

For open aseptic operations, the burden of proof is higher: intervention control,
airflow visualization, aseptic behavior, gowning qualification, and environmental
monitoring need to support the same story.

## 4. Monitoring became feedback, not decoration

Annex 1 puts more weight on monitoring as a signal system. Viable and non-viable
monitoring, especially in Grade A/B operations, should be risk-based, trended, and
connected to contamination control decisions.

The weak pattern is pass/fail review: "No action limit was exceeded, so no issue."
The stronger pattern is trend interpretation: "Counts are still within limits, but
this recovery pattern is drifting near a material transfer route, so we are testing
a source hypothesis before it becomes a failure."

## 5. Sterile filtration decisions need stronger rationale

Sterilizing-grade filtration received sharper expectations, including the role of
pre-use post-sterilization integrity testing (PUPSIT), post-use integrity testing,
filter redundancy where appropriate, bioburden control before filtration, and
validated hold times. The point is not only whether a filter passed. It is whether
the filtration strategy is robust for the product, process, and contamination risk.

## 6. Media fills need to represent the real process

Aseptic process simulation is expected to challenge the routine and worst-case
process credibly. That includes interventions, line stoppages, shift patterns,
operator roles, setup, lyophilizer loading where relevant, and the actual
contamination vulnerabilities of the process.

The central inspection question is not "Did the last media fill pass?" It is:
"Would this media fill reveal the failures your real process is most likely to
create?"

## 7. Evidence has to stay current

Annex 1 raises the value of living evidence. Cleanroom classification, smoke
studies, environmental monitoring trends, APS outcomes, sterilization validation,
cleaning/disinfection qualification, and deviation history should be reviewed as a
connected system.

This creates a different audit posture. A site should be ready to walk from:

1. process step;
2. contamination risk;
3. preventive control;
4. monitoring evidence;
5. trend or deviation;
6. decision or CAPA.

If those links are hard to show, the Annex 1 problem is usually not a missing
paragraph. It is a weak control narrative.

## What to do first

If the 2022 revision still feels too large, start with four practical moves:

- Build a CCS map by process step, not by department.
- Review Grade A/B monitoring locations against actual intervention risk.
- Compare smoke studies and APS scripts with real routine and non-routine
  operations.
- Pick three recent EM, APS, or deviation signals and check whether they updated
  the CCS, SOPs, training, limits, or CAPA plan.
