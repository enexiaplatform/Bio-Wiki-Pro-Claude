# Extractables and leachables: what comes out of the container

A product spends its life in contact with materials — the container, the closure,
tubing, filters, single-use bags. Those materials are not perfectly inert, and over
time small amounts of chemicals can migrate **out of the material and into the
product.** Controlling that is the world of extractables and leachables (E&L).

## Extractables vs leachables

The two terms are related but distinct, and the difference matters:

- **Extractables** are compounds that *can* be pulled out of a material under
  **exaggerated** conditions — aggressive solvents, heat, time. They define the
  worst-case universe of what *could* migrate.
- **Leachables** are compounds that *actually* migrate into the product under
  **real** storage and use conditions, over the real shelf life.

You study extractables to know what to look for, then confirm which of those show up
as leachables in the actual product. Leachables are the real patient-exposure
question; extractables are the screening map.

## Why it's risk-based

Not every product needs the same depth. The risk rises with:

- **Route** — parenterals and inhalation products are far higher risk than a tablet
  in a blister
- **Contact** — duration, temperature, and the solvent power of the formulation
- **Material** — plastics, elastomers, and adhesives leach more than glass

So an oily injectable in a stoppered vial gets serious E&L attention; a dry powder in
foil, much less.

## Why biologics and single-use add urgency

Biologics are often parenteral, sensitive, and increasingly made in **single-use
systems** (plastic bags, tubing, connectors) where the product contacts a lot of
polymer. That combination has pushed E&L from a niche packaging topic to a mainstream
control-strategy concern.
