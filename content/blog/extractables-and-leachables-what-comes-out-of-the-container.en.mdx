# Extractables and leachables: what comes out of the container

A drug product spends its life touching materials: the container, closure, liner,
label adhesive, syringe barrel, plunger, tubing, filters, single-use bags,
connectors, gaskets, and sometimes process equipment surfaces. Those materials
are not perfectly inert. Over time, small amounts of chemicals can migrate out
of the material and into the product.

Controlling that risk is the world of extractables and leachables (E&L). It is
easy to dismiss E&L as a packaging science niche until a late-stage leachable
appears in a stability lot, a single-use assembly changes supplier, or a
biologic shows unexplained degradation after contact with a polymer. Then E&L
becomes a patient-safety, quality, regulatory, and supply-chain problem all at
once.

The practical purpose of E&L work is simple: identify what could migrate, confirm
what actually migrates, assess whether patient exposure is acceptable, and
control the materials so the product stays safe and fit for its intended use.

## Extractables vs leachables

The two terms are related, but they are not interchangeable.

- **Extractables** are compounds that can be pulled out of a material under
  exaggerated or aggressive laboratory conditions: strong solvents, heat, long
  contact time, high surface area, or other forcing conditions. They define the
  potential universe of what could migrate.
- **Leachables** are compounds that actually migrate into the drug product under
  normal or reasonably foreseeable storage and use conditions over the product's
  shelf life.

Extractables are the map. Leachables are the patient-exposure question.

That distinction matters because a long extractables list does not mean all
compounds will appear in the product, and a clean supplier extractables package
does not automatically prove the final drug product is safe. You use
extractables data to know what to look for, then confirm and assess the
leachables that matter for the actual product.

## Why E&L is risk-based

Not every product needs the same level of E&L work. Risk depends on how much
material contact exists, how powerful the formulation is at extracting chemicals,
how much product the patient receives, and how sensitive the route of
administration is.

In general, risk rises with:

- **Route of administration:** inhalation, ophthalmic, and parenteral products
  usually carry higher concern than oral solids.
- **Contact duration:** long shelf life in direct contact with elastomers,
  plastics, or adhesives increases the chance of migration.
- **Formulation chemistry:** surfactants, oils, organic solvents, extremes of
  pH, high salt, or protein formulations can increase extraction or interaction.
- **Dose and exposure:** a high daily dose can make a low concentration more
  relevant.
- **Material type:** elastomers, plastics, inks, adhesives, coatings, and
  single-use polymers generally need more scrutiny than well-characterized
  glass.
- **Temperature and processing:** sterilization, gamma irradiation, heat, and
  long hold times can create or increase potential migrants.

So an oily injectable in a rubber-stoppered vial or a biologic held in a
single-use bag deserves a much deeper E&L package than a dry tablet in a blister.

## The packaging question regulators care about

FDA's container-closure guidance frames packaging as part of the product's CMC
control strategy. The container closure system must be suitable for its intended
use, and the information submitted should support protection, compatibility,
safety, and performance.

For E&L, that means a reviewer is not only asking, "What is the container made
of?" They are asking:

- Can the materials protect the product through shelf life?
- Could the materials add substances to the product?
- Could the materials absorb, bind, or remove important product components?
- Could the materials interact with the formulation, preservative, protein,
  solvent, or closure system?
- Does the risk assessment match the route of administration and patient dose?
- Are the proposed controls strong enough for commercial life cycle changes?

The same logic applies to manufacturing contact materials. A single-use bag or
tubing set may not be the final package, but if the product sits in it during
processing, it can still introduce leachables.

## ICH Q3E: important, but still draft

ICH Q3E is intended to harmonize assessment and control of extractables and
leachables. As of July 4, 2026, the publicly listed ICH/EMA status is still draft
with consultation closed, not a final adopted guideline. That matters. Teams can
use it to anticipate the direction of expectations, but they should not cite it
as a final binding standard.

The useful direction of travel is clear: E&L should be science-based,
risk-based, tied to patient safety and product quality, and controlled through a
lifecycle approach. The practical mistake is treating Q3E as either irrelevant
because it is draft, or final because it is useful. The right posture is to track
it, learn from it, and align where appropriate while still following current
regional requirements, pharmacopoeial chapters, and approved procedures.

## The Analytical Evaluation Threshold in plain language

E&L studies can generate many unknown peaks. Without a threshold, teams can drown
in analytical noise. The Analytical Evaluation Threshold (AET) is a practical
reporting and identification level. Above the AET, a compound generally needs to
be identified and evaluated. Below it, the compound may not need the same level
of work, depending on the study design and risk.

Conceptually:

```text
AET = safety threshold / maximum daily product exposure
```

Then an uncertainty factor is often applied to account for variability in
analytical response factors, because unknown compounds do not all respond the
same way in a detector.

For example, if a safety threshold is expressed as micrograms per day, a product
with a larger daily dose has a lower concentration threshold. That is why dose
matters. A leachable concentration that looks tiny in a vial can become more
important if the patient receives a large daily volume.

The AET is not a license to ignore toxicology. It is a way to focus analytical
identification and safety assessment on the signals most likely to matter.

## What a practical E&L strategy includes

A useful E&L program usually has several layers.

1. **Material inventory:** list all direct and relevant indirect product-contact
   materials: primary package, closure, delivery device, single-use systems,
   filters, tubing, gaskets, process aids, and labels or inks where relevant.
2. **Risk ranking:** classify each material by route, contact time, temperature,
   surface area, formulation solvent power, sterilization, and patient exposure.
3. **Supplier data review:** collect composition, compliance declarations,
   extractables packages, change notification commitments, and quality
   agreements.
4. **Extractables assessment:** identify the potential migrants from materials
   using suitable solvents and conditions, or justify reliance on high-quality
   supplier data.
5. **Leachables study:** test the actual product, container, or process
   simulation under real or justified accelerated conditions to confirm what
   actually migrates.
6. **Toxicological assessment:** evaluate identified leachables against
   appropriate safety thresholds, route, dose, duration, and patient population.
7. **Product-quality assessment:** check whether leachables affect potency,
   purity, stability, appearance, particles, pH, protein aggregation,
   preservative content, or device performance.
8. **Lifecycle control:** manage supplier changes, material changes, sterilization
   changes, manufacturing site changes, and packaging changes through change
   control.

The strongest programs do not wait until validation or registration stability to
start this work. They begin at material selection.

## Supplier data helps, but it is not the product study

Good supplier extractables packages are valuable. They can prevent duplicate
testing, identify target compounds, support risk assessments, and help select
lower-risk materials. But supplier data has limits.

Before relying on it, ask:

- Was the tested material truly equivalent to the material you will use?
- Were the extraction solvents and conditions relevant to your formulation?
- Were the analytical methods broad enough for volatile, semi-volatile,
  non-volatile, organic, and inorganic species where relevant?
- Was the material sterilized or irradiated the same way?
- Does the data cover the same surface area, contact time, and temperature risk?
- Are unknowns identified or at least controlled through a justified threshold?
- Does the supplier provide change notification commitments?

Supplier data can define the possible universe. It usually cannot, by itself,
prove what migrates into your final product through its shelf life.

## Why biologics and single-use systems add urgency

Biologics often combine several E&L risk factors: parenteral route, sensitive
protein structure, long development timelines, cold storage, low impurity
tolerance, and extensive contact with single-use systems during manufacturing.

Leachables in biologics can matter even when they are not acutely toxic. They may
contribute to:

- protein oxidation, aggregation, or fragmentation;
- loss of potency;
- changes in visible or subvisible particles;
- interaction with surfactants or stabilizers;
- preservative loss or preservative interaction;
- analytical interference;
- immunogenicity concerns where product quality changes increase immune risk.

This is why E&L should connect to formulation development, stability, process
validation, container closure strategy, and biologics comparability. It is not
only a toxicology exercise.

## E&L is a stability and change-control issue

Leachables are often time-dependent. A compound may be absent at release and
detectable after months of storage. A product may look fine in early development
but show a new peak after a stopper, film, tubing, sterilization cycle, or supplier
site changes.

That makes E&L part of the stability strategy. It also makes it part of change
control.

Changes that should trigger an E&L impact assessment include:

- new container, closure, stopper, syringe, cartridge, or delivery-device
  component;
- new single-use bag, tubing, connector, filter, gasket, or assembly;
- supplier site, material grade, resin, additive, colorant, lubricant, adhesive,
  or coating change;
- sterilization method or dose change;
- process hold time, temperature, or contact surface-area change;
- formulation pH, surfactant, preservative, solvent, or protein concentration
  change;
- shelf-life or storage-condition change;
- unexplained stability trend, new impurity peak, particle trend, or potency
  loss.

The change-control question should be explicit: "Could this change alter what
the product contacts, what migrates, how much migrates, or how the product
responds?"

## Common E&L mistakes

E&L failures often come from timing and assumptions, not from lack of analytical
technology.

Common mistakes include:

- starting E&L only after the package or single-use system is already locked;
- assuming a material is safe because it is common in the industry;
- using supplier extractables data without checking relevance to the actual
  product;
- missing adhesives, inks, lubricants, processing aids, or device components;
- focusing only on toxicology and missing product-quality effects;
- treating an extractables study as a substitute for a leachables study in a
  high-risk product;
- ignoring sterilization or irradiation effects;
- failing to connect E&L to stability and change control;
- accepting unknown peaks above threshold without a defensible identification or
  toxicological rationale.

The best time to prevent these problems is during development and material
selection. The most expensive time is after registration batches or commercial
launch.

## Practical E&L review checklist

Use this checklist before approving a packaging system, single-use assembly, or
high-risk material change:

- **Scope:** Have all product-contact and relevant process-contact materials been
  listed?
- **Risk ranking:** Is the risk level justified by route, dose, contact duration,
  temperature, surface area, and formulation chemistry?
- **Supplier data:** Are extractables packages current, relevant, and traceable
  to the exact material and sterilization condition?
- **Study design:** Do extractables and leachables studies cover appropriate
  chemistries, methods, thresholds, and time points?
- **AET:** Is the AET calculated from a justified safety threshold and maximum
  daily exposure, with uncertainty handled?
- **Identification:** Are peaks above threshold identified or scientifically
  justified?
- **Toxicology:** Has patient exposure been assessed for route, dose, duration,
  and population?
- **Product quality:** Has impact on potency, purity, stability, appearance,
  aggregation, particles, pH, preservative, and device performance been assessed?
- **Stability:** Are leachables monitored or justified over shelf life where
  risk requires it?
- **Change control:** Are supplier and internal changes tied to E&L impact
  assessment?
- **Regulatory:** Is the E&L package aligned with the filing strategy and current
  regional expectations?

## Key takeaway

Extractables tell you what a material could give up. Leachables tell you what
the product actually receives. The control strategy is strongest when E&L is
built into material selection, packaging design, single-use qualification,
stability, toxicology, and change control from the start.

If the E&L story only appears at the end of development, it becomes a rescue
exercise. If it begins when materials are chosen, it becomes a design control.
