# Mock Audit Walkthrough — From the Auditor's Side

A full inspection narrated from the auditor's perspective, so nothing on the day
surprises you. Read it once before your audit; read it again as you finish the
gap-analysis sheet.

---

## Day 0 — Before I arrive
I've already read your previous inspection observations, your registered
processes, and any field alerts or recalls tied to your site. I arrive with a
mental list of where problems usually hide for a site like yours. My first
impression starts at reception: how long it takes you to assemble the room and
produce the first document tells me a lot.

## Opening meeting
I state scope and duration. I watch who's in the room and who's clearly nervous.
I'll ask for your quality manual / site master file, your org chart, and a list of
products and recent batches. **What I'm really testing:** can you orient me quickly
and confidently, or is everything a scramble?

## The document phase
I pick a recent batch and ask for its complete record. Then I pull threads:
- A deviation referenced in it → show me the deviation and its CAPA.
- An OOS in the period → walk me through the investigation.
- The audit trail for the test → show me it was reviewed.

**What I'm testing:** traceability and whether your records are complete,
contemporaneous, and reviewed. Gaps, late entries, and unreviewed audit trails are
where 483s are written. If a runner takes 40 minutes to find a superseded SOP, I
now wonder what else you can't retrieve.

## The floor walk
On the floor I watch behavior, not just paperwork. Are people following the SOP or
their memory? Is the logbook filled in now, or at the end of the shift? In a
cleanroom I watch gowning and technique. I'll ask an operator a simple question
about their task — and I'm listening for whether practice matches the procedure.

**What I'm testing:** do you do what you say? The fastest way to lose me is an
operator who does it one way while the SOP says another.

## The interviews
I ask open questions and let silence do the work. People who over-explain often
walk me straight into a problem they were trying to hide. The strong answer is
short, true, and backed by a record. "I don't know, I'll find out" is a perfectly
good answer; a confident guess that later proves wrong is not.

## When I find something
I'll probe to see if it's isolated or systemic. If you acknowledge it, show me the
related CAPA, and demonstrate you'd have caught it yourself, the finding may stay
minor. If you're defensive, or I sense the record was adjusted, I dig deeper — and
the scope of my concern grows.

## The data-integrity lens (always on)
Throughout, I'm asking: could this record have been altered without trace? Shared
logins, admin rights for analysts, audit trails off or unreviewed, "trial"
injections — any of these turns a routine inspection into a serious one, because
now I doubt **all** your data, not just one batch.

## Daily wrap-up
I tell you the day's concerns. Use the evening well: pull documents for tomorrow's
topics, prepare honest explanations, and don't try to paper over a real gap
overnight — I'll notice a record that appeared since yesterday.

## Closing meeting
I read out my observations. For each, I want to see you understand it precisely. I
don't need you to agree on the spot, and I don't want a scientific argument in the
room — that belongs in your written response. What I'm forming is an overall
judgment: **is this a quality system that finds and fixes its own problems, run by
people who tell the truth?** If yes, even several observations are recoverable. If
no, that's the finding that matters.

---

## What separates sites that pass calmly
1. They can retrieve any record in minutes.
2. Practice matches procedure on the floor.
3. Their investigations find real root causes and prove the fix worked.
4. Their data integrity is boringly solid — unique logins, reviewed audit trails.
5. Their people are calm and honest because they have nothing to hide.

Build toward those five and the audit stops being something that happens *to* you.
