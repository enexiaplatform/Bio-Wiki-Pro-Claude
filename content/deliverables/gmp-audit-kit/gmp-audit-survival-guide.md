# The GMP Audit Readiness Guide

A practical framework for QC/QA professionals facing an FDA, EU GMP, or
customer audit — from preparation to the closing meeting.

---

## Part 1 — The mindset

An inspection is not a trap; it is a test of whether your quality system works the
way your documents say it does. Inspectors look for three things:

1. **Do you do what you say?** (procedures match practice)
2. **Can you prove it?** (records, contemporaneous and complete)
3. **Do you find and fix your own problems?** (a self-correcting system)

Everything below serves those three questions.

---

## Part 2 — 30 days out: preparation

### 2.1 Know your scope
Confirm what the audit covers (product, process, system, for-cause vs routine).
Pull the last inspection's observations and confirm every commitment was closed.

### 2.2 Run a self-inspection
Use the **SOP Gap Analysis** sheet in this kit. Walk the areas as if you were the
auditor. Score honestly — a self-inspection that finds nothing is the finding.

### 2.3 Tier and close gaps
Classify gaps critical / major / minor. You will not close everything; close the
critical ones and have a **documented, in-progress CAPA** for the rest. An open
gap with a credible plan beats a hidden gap.

### 2.4 Ready the document rooms
- Front room (where the inspector sits) and back room (where requests are pulled).
- Confirm you can retrieve any batch record, SOP (current + superseded), training
  record, calibration, and audit trail **within minutes**.
- Designate runners and a scribe.

### 2.5 Brief the team
Everyone who may be interviewed rehearses the **Audit Interview Q&A** in this kit.
Core rule: answer the question asked, truthfully, then stop.

## Part 3 — Audit day

### 3.1 Roles
- **Host / lead** — manages the inspector, sets pace, owns the agenda.
- **Scribe** — logs every question, request, and document shown (this becomes your
  response record).
- **Runners** — fetch documents from the back room.
- **SMEs** — brought in for their area only, then leave.

### 3.2 The golden rules for anyone interviewed
1. Listen to the whole question. Pause. Then answer.
2. Answer **only** what was asked — do not volunteer tours of adjacent problems.
3. If you don't know, say "I don't know, I will find out" — never guess.
4. Never speculate, never blame, never argue.
5. Tell the truth. Always. A lie to an inspector is the one unrecoverable error.

### 3.3 Handling document requests
Every document leaves the back room through one controlled point, logged by the
scribe. Review it for completeness before it reaches the inspector. If a record
has a known issue, don't hide it — be ready to explain the deviation and its CAPA.

### 3.4 When the inspector finds something
- Acknowledge it; don't get defensive.
- Understand exactly what concerns them (ask clarifying questions).
- If it's a real gap, say so and note it for CAPA.
- If it's a misunderstanding, provide the evidence calmly — once.

---

## Part 4 — The daily wrap-up and closing meeting

### 4.1 Daily debrief
Each evening, the team reviews the scribe log: what was asked, what was shown,
what's outstanding, and what looks like a likely observation. Prepare overnight
for the next day's topics and pull any promised documents.

### 4.2 The closing meeting
The inspector reads out observations (e.g. FDA Form 483 items). For each:
- Make sure you **understand** it precisely (restate it back).
- Note any factual error politely, with evidence.
- Do **not** argue scientific judgment in the room — capture it for the written
  response.

---

## Part 5 — After the audit: the response

### 5.1 Timelines
FDA 483 responses are expected within **15 business days** to be considered before
any Warning Letter decision. EU/customer timelines vary — confirm and meet them.

### 5.2 Anatomy of a strong response
For each observation:
1. **Restate** the observation.
2. **Immediate correction** — what you did at once (contain, quarantine, fix).
3. **Root cause** — the verified systemic cause (use the CAPA templates).
4. **Corrective + preventive action** — with owners and dates.
5. **Effectiveness check** — how you'll prove it worked.
6. **Evidence** — attach the records, not just claims.

### 5.3 The cardinal sins of responses
- Treating a systemic problem as a one-off ("we retrained the analyst").
- Promising actions with no dates or owners.
- Closing CAPAs with no effectiveness evidence.
- Missing the response deadline.

## Part 6 — The 10 most common GMP observations (and how to avoid them)

1. **Data integrity** — shared logins, unreviewed audit trails. *Fix:* unique
   accounts, scheduled + recorded audit-trail review.
2. **Inadequate investigations / OOS** — superficial root cause. *Fix:* the
   two-phase OOS process; no "testing into compliance."
3. **CAPA ineffective** — recurrence. *Fix:* real root cause + effectiveness checks.
4. **Cleaning validation gaps** — no HBEL basis, no recovery study.
5. **Environmental monitoring** — excursions closed without ID or impact.
6. **Calibration / equipment** — overdue, or use after a failed check.
7. **Training** — records don't match who did the work.
8. **Change control** — changes made without assessment or before approval.
9. **Supplier control** — no quality agreement, no change notification.
10. **Documentation** — incomplete records, late entries, uncontrolled forms.

Score yourself against these in the gap-analysis sheet.

---

## Part 7 — The 24-hour and day-of checklist

**The week before**
- [ ] Self-inspection complete; critical gaps closed; CAPAs open for the rest.
- [ ] Document retrieval tested (batch records, SOPs, training, calibration).
- [ ] Front/back room set; roles assigned; team briefed and rehearsed.
- [ ] Previous-inspection commitments confirmed closed.

**The morning of**
- [ ] Scribe ready with the request-log template.
- [ ] Runners briefed; back room staffed.
- [ ] SMEs on call; reachable within minutes.
- [ ] Calm, slept, fed. A rushed, exhausted team makes avoidable errors.

You are ready. Work the framework, tell the truth, and let your system speak for
itself.
