# Objectionable Organisms in Non-Sterile Products

For non-sterile products, passing the USP 61/62 microbial limits is not the whole
story. An organism can be within total count limits yet still be
**objectionable** — unacceptable given the product, its use, and the patient.

## What makes an organism objectionable?

It depends on context, not just a list:

- **Route of administration** — an organism tolerable in a topical may be
  dangerous in an inhalation or pediatric oral product.
- **Patient population** — immunocompromised users raise the risk.
- **Product chemistry** — whether the formulation supports proliferation.

## Beyond the specified organisms

USP 62 names specific organisms to test for, but the absence of those does not
prove a product is safe. A documented **microbial risk assessment** is what
auditors expect to see justifying which organisms matter for your product.

## Route-of-administration risk matrix

| Route | Risk level | Organisms of particular concern |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Inhalation / nasal | High | *Pseudomonas*, *Burkholderia cepacia* complex, molds |
| Ophthalmic (non-sterile precursors) | High | *P. aeruginosa* |
| Oral liquid (pediatric/immunocompromised) | Medium–High | *E. coli*, *Salmonella*, *B. cepacia* |
| Oral solid | Medium | Enterobacteria, depending on water activity |
| Topical (intact skin) | Lower | *S. aureus*, *P. aeruginosa* on broken skin |

The same count of the same organism can be release-blocking in one product and
acceptable in another — context, not a universal list, decides.

## Objectionable-organism decision framework

When an isolate appears, ask in order:

1. **Identify** to species (not just "Gram-negative rod").
2. **Could it survive/proliferate** in this formulation (water activity,
   preservative, pH)?
3. **Is the route/patient** vulnerable to it?
4. **Is it on a regulatory watch** (e.g. *Burkholderia cepacia* complex for
   aqueous non-sterile)?
5. **Conclusion:** objectionable → reject + investigate source; not objectionable
   → document the rationale.

Capture this logic in the micro risk assessment *before* an excursion, so the
disposition decision is principled rather than improvised under pressure.

## Recovering from a micro warning letter

A common pattern: a site releases product on "meets USP 61 limits" while an
objectionable organism (often *B. cepacia* complex) is present. Recovery means:
expand identification capability (genotypic), build the product-specific risk
assessment, re-examine the water system and ingredient bioburden as the source,
re-train on objectionable-organism evaluation, and trend isolates over time. The
regulator wants evidence the *system* now reasons about objectionability — not
just that the one batch was handled.

> Practitioner tip: invest in identification. "Total count passed" with an
> unidentified Gram-negative recovered from an aqueous oral product is exactly the
> gap warning letters are written about. Speciate, then decide.
