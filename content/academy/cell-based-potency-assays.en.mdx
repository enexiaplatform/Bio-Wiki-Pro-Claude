# Cell-Based Potency Assays (Bioassays)

Potency is the QC attribute that answers the only question that ultimately
matters: **does the drug do what it's supposed to do?** For biologics, that has to
be measured biologically — a bioassay.

## Why biological measurement

A correctly sequenced, correctly sized antibody can still be inactive if it's
misfolded or its binding region is modified. Only a functional assay — the
molecule acting on cells or a target — confirms activity. Common formats:

- **Cell-based functional assays** — proliferation, reporter-gene, cytotoxicity,
  or signaling readouts in a responsive cell line.
- **Binding assays** (ELISA/SPR) — measure target binding; sometimes a surrogate
  for potency when justified.

## Relative potency

Bioassays are too variable for absolute numbers, so potency is reported as
**relative potency**: the test sample's dose-response curve compared against a
**reference standard** run in the same plate. Result ≈ 100% means "as potent as
the reference."

## The 4-parameter logistic (4PL)

Dose-response data are sigmoidal, fit with a **4PL** model (top, bottom, slope,
EC50). Relative potency comes from the horizontal shift between the test and
reference curves — valid only when the curves are **parallel** (a parallelism
test gates the result).

## Living with variability

Cell-based assays are inherently noisy (cell passage, plate position, operator).
QC controls this with: a reference on every plate, system-suitability limits,
replicate plates, and **trending** of the reference's own parameters. A potency
result outside spec triggers an investigation that always asks "assay or sample?"

## Why it matters

Potency is often the hardest biologics method to validate and transfer, and the
most common source of OOS. Analysts who understand parallelism, 4PL fits, and
assay control are valuable precisely because bioassays are unforgiving.

> Tip: half of "potency OOS" results are assay artifacts. Strong system
> suitability and reference trending let you tell a real product change from a bad
> plate — before you quarantine a good batch.
