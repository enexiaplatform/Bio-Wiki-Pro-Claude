# Container Closure Integrity Testing (CCIT)

Container Closure Integrity Testing demonstrates that a package maintains a
sterile barrier and prevents product loss throughout shelf life. USP 1207 shifts
the field toward **deterministic**, instrument-based methods over probabilistic
probe tests.

## Deterministic vs probabilistic

- **Deterministic** — physicochemical methods with quantitative, repeatable
  signals (vacuum decay, high-voltage leak detection, headspace analysis).
- **Probabilistic** — methods whose result depends on chance (dye ingress,
  microbial immersion); higher variability and harder to validate.

## The maximum allowable leakage limit (MALL)

CCIT methods are validated against the MALL — the leak size below which neither
sterility nor product quality is compromised. Method sensitivity must reliably
detect leaks at or near that limit.

## Method selection by container type

| Container | Strong CCIT options | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Glass vial, rubber stopper | Vacuum decay, headspace (laser) | Headspace tracks O2/moisture ingress over time |
| Pre-filled syringe | Vacuum decay, high-voltage leak (HVLD) | Watch the plunger and tip-cap as leak paths |
| Ampoule (glass) | HVLD, vacuum decay | Fully sealed; HVLD good for conductive liquids |
| Flexible bag / pouch | Pressure decay, vacuum decay | Seal integrity is the usual failure mode |
| Lyophilized vial | Headspace (vacuum/moisture) | Headspace confirms stopper seat + vacuum hold |

Match the method's physics to the product (conductivity for HVLD, gas content for
headspace) and to the leak path you most fear for that package.

## Validation and positive controls

Validate against the **MALL** using **positive controls** — containers with
known, certified leaks (laser-drilled micro-holes, e.g. 2–10 µm). Demonstrate the
method reliably detects leaks at/near the MALL and reliably passes intact
("negative") units. Establish detection limit, repeatability, and robustness.
Probabilistic methods need far more replicates to make a statistical case — a key
reason USP &lt;1207&gt; favors deterministic approaches.

## Replacing the sterility test on stability

The sterility test is destructive, low-sensitivity, and prone to false positives
from handling. A validated CCIT method can serve as the container-integrity
assurance at stability time points instead — non-destructive, quantitative, and
applicable to more units. Regulators accept this when the CCIT method is properly
validated to the MALL and justified in the stability protocol.

> Practitioner tip: decide CCIT strategy at development, not at stability. The
> headspace baseline you capture on day zero is what makes later ingress trends
> interpretable — you can't retrofit a baseline you never measured.
