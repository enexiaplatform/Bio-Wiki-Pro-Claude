# Karl Fischer Water Determination

Water content is a critical attribute for many APIs and products — it drives
**hydrolytic degradation**, affects potency calculations, and influences physical
stability. Karl Fischer (KF) titration is the specific, accurate way to measure it
(unlike loss-on-drying, which captures any volatile).

## Volumetric vs coulometric

- **Volumetric KF** — titrant containing iodine is dispensed; suited to samples
  with **higher** water content (roughly 100 ppm to 100%).
- **Coulometric KF** — iodine is generated electrochemically *in situ*; ideal for
  **trace** water (down to ~ppm). Use it for dry APIs and low-moisture solids.

Pick the method to match the expected water level — wrong method, poor precision.

## How it works (briefly)

The KF reaction consumes water stoichiometrically with iodine in the presence of
sulfur dioxide and a base. The endpoint is detected when free iodine appears
(bivoltametric/"dead-stop" indication).

## Common error sources

- **Atmospheric moisture** ingress — keep the cell sealed and dry.
- **Incomplete sample dissolution/extraction** — water trapped in the matrix isn't
  titrated; use oven (gas-phase) sampling for difficult solids.
- **Side reactions** with certain analytes (aldehydes, ketones) — use special
  reagents.

## Why it matters

A controlled, validated KF method gives a reliable moisture spec — protecting
shelf life and ensuring assay results are reported on the right basis (e.g.
anhydrous).

> Tip: for very dry materials, coulometric KF with oven sampling avoids the
> matrix-extraction problem that quietly biases volumetric results low.
