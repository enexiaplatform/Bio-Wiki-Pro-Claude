# Calibration & Metrology Programs

Every measurement that supports a quality decision — temperature, pressure,
weight, pH, time — is only as trustworthy as the instrument's calibration. A GMP
calibration program makes that trust provable.

## Traceability

Calibrations must be **traceable** to a national/international standard (e.g.
NIST, or an accredited lab) through an unbroken chain of comparisons. "Calibrated
against another uncalibrated device" breaks the chain.

## Tolerance must fit the use

The calibration **tolerance** must be **at least as tight as the process
requirement** the instrument controls. An incubator held to ±2 °C can't be
qualified with a thermometer calibrated to ±5 °C. Define the acceptance criteria
from the process, not the instrument's spec sheet.

## Intervals and labels

Each instrument has a defined calibration **interval** (risk- and history-based),
a unique ID, a calibration label showing due date, and a status (in/out of
service). Overdue calibration on a release-critical instrument is a finding.

## Out-of-tolerance (OOT) at calibration

If an instrument is **found out of tolerance**, you can't just recalibrate and
move on — you must assess the **impact on all data and product** generated since
the last successful calibration, and act (investigation, possible recall) if
quality was affected.

> Tip: the OOT impact assessment is the part teams forget. A drifting balance
> found at annual calibration may have mis-weighed months of batches.
