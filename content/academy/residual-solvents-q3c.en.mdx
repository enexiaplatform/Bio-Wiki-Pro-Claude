# Residual Solvents (ICH Q3C)

Residual solvents are organic volatile chemicals used or produced in manufacturing
that remain in the drug substance, excipients, or product. ICH Q3C classifies them
by toxicity and sets limits to protect patients without banning useful chemistry.

## The three classes

- **Class 1** — solvents to be avoided (e.g. benzene, carbon tetrachloride);
  known or strongly suspected human carcinogens.
- **Class 2** — solvents to be limited (e.g. methanol, acetonitrile); non-genotoxic
  but with inherent toxicity. Controlled by PDE.
- **Class 3** — low-toxicity solvents (e.g. ethanol, acetone); regarded as safe
  at typical levels, generally limited to 0.5 percent unless justified higher.

## PDE and the two options

For Class 2 solvents, ICH Q3C gives a Permitted Daily Exposure and two ways to
show compliance:

- **Option 1** — a simple concentration limit (ppm) assuming a maximum daily dose.
- **Option 2** — a limit based on the actual daily dose, often allowing higher
  concentrations when the dose is small.

## Measuring control

Gas chromatography with headspace sampling is the workhorse method. As with
elemental impurities, a sound risk assessment of which solvents are actually used
or formed often shows that routine testing of every solvent isn't required.

## Option 1 vs Option 2 — worked comparison

Take a Class 2 solvent with **PDE = 50 mg/day** (illustrative).

- **Option 1** assumes a max daily dose of **10 g** of product:
  concentration limit = PDE / daily dose = 50 mg / 10 g = **5,000 ppm**. Simple,
  conservative, dose-independent — good when several components contribute the
  same solvent.
- **Option 2** uses the *actual* daily dose. If the real max dose is only **2 g**:
  allowable concentration = 50 mg / 2 g = **25,000 ppm**. Far more permissive —
  worth using when Option 1 is too tight for a low-dose product.

When the solvent comes from several excipients/the API, sum the contributions: the
**total** intake must stay within the PDE regardless of which option you use.

## Solvent risk-assessment template

1. List every solvent **used or formed** across synthesis, excipients, and
   processing (including recovered solvents).
2. Classify each (1/2/3) and note its PDE.
3. Identify which can plausibly remain in the final product (volatility, process
   steps, drying).
4. Decide testing: routine, skip-lot, or "controlled upstream and not tested in
   the product" — each with documented justification.
5. Confirm Class 1 solvents are absent/avoided; justify any that are unavoidable.

A sound assessment usually shows you needn't test *every* solvent in the finished
product — control can sit upstream (drug-substance spec, supplier control).

## Solvents with no established PDE

For a solvent not in Q3C, derive a PDE from its toxicology using the same
NOAEL/adjustment-factor approach as HBELs, or apply the relevant class's default
where structurally justified. Document the derivation — an "other" solvent without
a justified limit is a gap inspectors will find.

> Practitioner tip: residual-solvent control is mostly a *drying and upstream*
> problem, not a testing problem. If a Class 2 solvent keeps appearing near its
> limit, fix the drying step or the route — chasing it with tighter release
> testing only catches failures after the fact.
