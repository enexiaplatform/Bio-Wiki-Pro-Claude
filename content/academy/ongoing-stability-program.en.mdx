# Ongoing Stability Program

Registration stability proves shelf life at approval; the **ongoing stability
program** confirms the product *stays* stable in routine production. EU GMP and
ICH Q1A expect it to continue throughout the marketed life of the product.

## What it covers

- **At least one batch per year** per product, strength, and container/closure
  (unless none is made that year), placed on long-term conditions.
- A written **protocol**: which attributes, test intervals (e.g. annually +
  end-of-shelf-life), conditions, and acceptance criteria.
- Inclusion triggers: significant **changes**, reprocessing, or deviations may
  require additional batches on stability.

## When a result drifts

An out-of-trend or out-of-specification stability result is serious — it can
affect product **already on the market**. The investigation must assess batches
in distribution and may lead to field actions (notification, recall) if shelf
life is implicated.

## Bracketing and matrixing (ICH Q1D)

To control testing burden without losing assurance:

- **Bracketing** — test only the **extremes** (e.g. lowest and highest strength,
  smallest and largest container) and infer the intermediates. Valid when the
  extremes truly bound the intermediates.
- **Matrixing** — test a **subset** of samples at each time point on a planned,
  rotating schedule (e.g. not every strength at every interval).

Both require a justified design documented in the protocol — you can't matrix
away a real stability risk.

## Sample ongoing-stability protocol

- **Scope** — products/strengths/packs and the one-batch-per-year rule.
- **Conditions** — long-term (e.g. 25 °C/60% RH or 30 °C/75% RH per climatic
  zone); accelerated/intermediate only if triggered.
- **Attributes** — assay, degradation products, dissolution, water, microbial
  (where relevant), appearance, container/closure.
- **Intervals** — e.g. 0, 12, 24, 36 months + end of shelf life.
- **Acceptance criteria** — the registered shelf-life specifications.
- **Triggers for extra batches** — significant changes, reprocessing, major
  deviations, complaints suggesting stability issues.

## Worked stability-excursion impact assessment

*Event:* a degradant exceeds spec at the 24-month point. **Steps:** confirm the
result (no lab error); determine which **distributed** batches share the same
process/formulation/pack and are at or beyond that age; project the degradation
to estimate when batches breach spec; assess patient risk (is the degradant
toxic?). **Outcome:** if marketed batches are implicated, this drives a field
action (shelf-life reduction, customer notification, or recall) — the ongoing
program is precisely the early-warning system that catches this before patients
are exposed.

> Practitioner tip: connect ongoing stability to the APR and to change control.
> The most common gap is a change (supplier, process, site) made without adding a
> stability batch — so the first sign of trouble arrives at 24 months, on product
> already in patients' hands.
