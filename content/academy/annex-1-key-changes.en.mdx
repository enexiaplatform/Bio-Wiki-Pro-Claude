# EU GMP Annex 1 (2022) — Key Changes

The 2022 revision of EU GMP Annex 1 was the biggest overhaul of sterile
manufacturing guidance in years. It moved from a checklist to a **principles- and
risk-based** framework. Here's what changed and why it matters.

## The Contamination Control Strategy (CCS)

The headline change: every sterile manufacturer must have a documented, holistic
**Contamination Control Strategy** that integrates all controls — facility, people,
process, utilities, monitoring — into one living document, justified by risk.

## QRM woven throughout

Quality Risk Management (ICH Q9) is no longer a separate exercise — it's the basis
for decisions across the annex: monitoring locations, intervention design,
limits, and the CCS itself.

## Barrier technology and contamination control

Stronger expectations for **RABS and isolators**, airflow visualization (smoke
studies) in Grade A, and a clear stance that technology should separate operators
from the critical zone wherever practical.

## PUPSIT, EM, and media fills

- **PUPSIT** (pre-use post-sterilization integrity testing) of sterilizing filters
  is addressed explicitly, with a risk-based rationale required.
- Environmental monitoring expectations are more detailed (continuous viable/
  non-viable in Grade A/B; trend-driven).
- Aseptic process simulations (media fills) move toward a **zero-contamination
  expectation** with rigorous intervention design.

## CCS gap-analysis checklist (mapped to Annex 1 §2)

Work through each and rate Compliant / Partial / Gap with evidence:

- [ ] A single, approved CCS document exists and names an owner.
- [ ] Facility/equipment design rationale (flows, pressure cascade, segregation).
- [ ] Barrier technology decision (RABS/isolator) justified by risk.
- [ ] Grade A airflow visualization (smoke studies) current and on file.
- [ ] PUPSIT decision documented with a risk rationale (do it, or justify not).
- [ ] EM program: continuous viable + non-viable in Grade A/B, trend-driven limits.
- [ ] Media-fill program designed to a zero-contamination expectation; worst-case
      interventions scripted.
- [ ] Utilities (WFI/PW, clean steam, gases) controls and monitoring linked in.
- [ ] Each control's effectiveness reviewed against real monitoring data.
- [ ] CCS feeds — and is updated by — deviations, excursions, and APS results.

## What inspectors now expect

The 2022 revision shifted the burden from "do you have the SOP" to "show me the
*rationale* and the *data*." Inspectors ask you to walk a contamination risk from
source to control to monitoring evidence, and to demonstrate the CCS is living —
that a recent excursion actually changed something. A binder assembled for the
audit, with no thread back to monitoring trends, is the classic finding.

> Practitioner tip: the transition trap is treating the CCS as a documentation
> deliverable. Build it as the index to your contamination-control reasoning, then
> let QRM and monitoring data keep it honest.
