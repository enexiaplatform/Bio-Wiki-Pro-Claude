# Investigating Microbial Excursions (EM Data)

An environmental-monitoring (EM) excursion is not automatically a disaster — but
how you investigate it tells an auditor whether your contamination control is
real. The goal: understand the event, assess product impact, and prevent
recurrence.

## Alert vs action response

- **Alert level** — an early-warning signal. Heighten scrutiny; investigate if it
  recurs or trends. Not necessarily a formal investigation on its own.
- **Action level** — exceeds the acceptable threshold. Triggers a **formal
  investigation** with root cause and CAPA, and a product-impact assessment.

## What a credible investigation covers

1. **Confirm the result** — rule out lab/sampling error (without "testing into
   compliance").
2. **Identify the organism** — to at least genus/species; the ID often points to
   the source (skin flora → gowning/technique; Gram-negative → water/spills;
   mould → construction/HVAC).
3. **Correlate with trends** — same location, same organism, same shift?
4. **Assess product impact** — what was exposed between the last clean result and
   the excursion, and is the batch's sterility/quality assured?

## The classic failure

Closing an action-level excursion as "isolated event, no impact" without an
organism ID, trend review, or impact rationale. Auditors read that as a system
that doesn't learn.

## Organism-to-source reference table

The isolate ID is the single best clue to where the contamination came from:

| Organism type | Typical source |
| --- | --- |
| Gram-positive cocci (*Staphylococcus*, *Micrococcus*) | People — skin, gowning gaps, technique |
| Gram-positive rods / spore-formers (*Bacillus*) | Dust, raw materials, inadequate sporicidal cleaning |
| Gram-negative rods (*Pseudomonas*, *Ralstonia*, *Burkholderia*) | Water, wet surfaces, spills, drains |
| Mould / fungi (*Aspergillus*, *Penicillium*) | Construction, HVAC, humidity, packaging/cardboard |
| Diphtheroids / *Corynebacterium* | Human skin flora |

A shift in the *flora* (e.g. new Gram-negatives in a normally Gram-positive room)
is itself a signal, even within limits.

## Excursion decision tree

1. **Confirm** the result and sampling (no lab artifact).
2. **Alert or action?** Alert → trend/heighten; action → formal investigation.
3. **Identify** the organism to genus/species.
4. **Correlate** — location, organism, shift, recent events (maintenance,
   construction, personnel).
5. **Assess product impact** — what was exposed in the window, sterility assurance.
6. **CAPA** for action-level/systemic; verify effectiveness; trend.

## Product-impact assessment template

- Batches/lots exposed in the room between the last acceptable result and the
  excursion.
- Proximity of the excursion location to the open product / critical zone.
- The organism's risk given the product and route.
- Other controls in the window (Grade A protection, airflow, intervention log).
- **Conclusion:** impact / no impact — with the evidence, signed. "No impact"
  must be argued, not asserted.

> Practitioner tip: maintain a site flora library. When you already know your
> "normal" organisms by location, an excursion's significance — and its likely
> source — becomes obvious far faster, and your impact arguments are far stronger.
