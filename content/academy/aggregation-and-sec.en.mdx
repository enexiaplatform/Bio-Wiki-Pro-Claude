# Protein Aggregation & Size-Exclusion (SEC)

Aggregation — protein molecules sticking together into dimers, higher-order
soluble species, and particles — is one of the most scrutinized biologic CQAs.
Aggregates are a leading **immunogenicity** risk and can reduce efficacy, so
controlling and trending them is central to biologics QC and stability.

## Why aggregates matter

Aggregates can be recognized by the immune system as "danger," driving
anti-drug-antibody responses that neutralize the drug or cause reactions. They
also form on stress (heat, shaking, freeze-thaw, interfaces), so they're a primary
**stability-indicating** attribute.

## SEC — the workhorse

**Size-exclusion chromatography** separates by hydrodynamic size and quantifies:

- **Monomer** (the main species)
- **HMW** (high-molecular-weight: dimers/aggregates)
- **LMW** (low-molecular-weight: fragments)

SEC is robust, quantitative, and the standard release/stability method for soluble
aggregate content (often reported as % monomer / % HMW).

## SEC's limitations

SEC isn't perfect: sample **dilution** on-column and **column interactions** can
*dissociate* weak aggregates (under-reporting) or cause non-specific binding. The
mobile phase can mask or alter species. This is why SEC is validated carefully and
backed by orthogonal methods.

## Orthogonal and particle methods

- **SEC-MALS** — adds multi-angle light scattering for absolute molar mass
  (confirms a peak really is a dimer, not a conformer).
- **AUC (analytical ultracentrifugation)** — orthogonal, dilution-free
  confirmation, used in characterization.
- **Sub-visible particles** — light obscuration (USP &lt;788&gt;) and imaging
  (MFI) cover the larger particle size ranges SEC can't see.

A full aggregation picture spans **soluble (SEC) → sub-micron/sub-visible
(MFI/light obscuration) → visible** particles.

## Investigating an aggregation trend

Rising HMW on stability or release prompts: confirm the method (column age,
standards), check the stress history (temperature excursions, agitation in
shipping), and assess formulation (surfactant, pH). Aggregation trends often point
to handling or formulation, not the molecule itself.

> Practitioner tip: validate SEC against an orthogonal method (SEC-MALS or AUC) so
> you can defend that your % HMW is real and not a column artifact — and so you
> know the method isn't *hiding* aggregates by dissociating them.
