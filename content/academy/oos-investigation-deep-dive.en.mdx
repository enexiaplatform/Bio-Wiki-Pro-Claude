# OOS Investigation — Deep Dive

An out-of-specification (OOS) result is any test result that falls outside the
specification or acceptance criteria in the approved application, the compendia,
or the manufacturer's own limits. How you investigate it is one of the most
scrutinised parts of a GMP quality system — because it sits directly on the line
between sound science and *testing into compliance*. The framework below follows
the **FDA Guidance for Industry: Investigating Out-of-Specification Test Results
(2006)** and the legal precedent set in **United States v. Barr Laboratories
(1993)**.

The single most important principle: an OOS result is presumed valid until an
investigation demonstrates otherwise. You do not get to make it disappear by
re-testing until you like the answer.

## Phase I — Laboratory investigation

Phase I is a factual assessment, led by the analyst with the supervisor, done
**before** any retesting. Its job is to determine whether an assignable
laboratory cause explains the result.

- **Confirm the data as reported.** Check the calculation, the reporting, the
  integration, dilutions, standard and reagent preparation, and system
  suitability. A transcription or integration error is an assignable cause; a
  result you simply dislike is not.
- **Interview and preserve.** Talk to the analyst promptly, keep the original
  preparations and the column/instrument where possible, and record everything
  contemporaneously.
- **Judge the hypothesis on evidence.** You may only invalidate the original
  result when there is a *scientifically sound, documented* assignable cause —
  a failed system suitability, a demonstrably bad standard, an instrument
  malfunction with an audit-trail signature. "Analyst error" with no supporting
  evidence is not acceptable.

If — and only if — a clear laboratory cause is found and documented, the result
may be invalidated and the test repeated by the original or a second analyst.
The invalidation, not the convenient number, is what closes Phase I.

### Retesting, resampling, and averaging

These are where investigations most often go wrong:

- **Retesting** (re-analysing the *original* sample) requires a predefined,
  scientifically justified plan — how many retests, and the decision rule —
  written **before** the retests are run. Open-ended "test until it passes" was
  exactly what the Barr ruling prohibited.
- **Resampling** (taking a *new* sample) is only justified when the original
  sample is shown to be non-representative or compromised — not as a second bite
  at the apple.
- **Averaging** an OOS result with passing results to obtain a passing mean is
  generally **not** acceptable. Averaging is only appropriate where the method
  defines it (for example, replicate determinations that are part of the
  reportable value), and even then a large spread is itself a signal to
  investigate, not to smooth over.
- **Outlier tests** may support a microbiological or biological assay where the
  method allows them, but a chemical assay outlier cannot be used to discard an
  OOS result. An outlier test explains variability; it does not invalidate a
  result.

## Phase II — Full-scale investigation

When Phase I finds no assignable laboratory cause, the OOS result stands and the
investigation expands beyond the lab, under **21 CFR 211.192**.

- **Manufacturing review.** Examine the batch record, process parameters,
  in-process data, raw materials, equipment, and any concurrent deviations.
- **Widen the scope.** Assess other batches, other products made on the same
  line, and any related finished or in-process lots that could share the cause.
- **Determine root cause.** Use a structured method (5-Whys, Ishikawa) to reach
  a cause the evidence actually supports — not the most convenient one.
- **Decide batch disposition.** The QA unit makes the release/reject decision on
  the full weight of evidence. A batch may not be released on the basis of a
  passing retest that merely overrides an unexplained OOS.

Phase II closes with a documented conclusion, a disposition decision, and a
**CAPA** whose effectiveness is verified later — a corrective action that is
never checked is not a corrective action.

## Data-integrity traps to avoid

- Re-injecting or re-testing without a pre-approved plan and an assignable cause.
- Averaging a failing result into a passing mean.
- Invalidating a result on an undocumented "analyst error."
- Reviewing only the results table, not the chromatograms, audit trail, and
  integration.
- Closing an investigation with a root cause the evidence doesn't support.
