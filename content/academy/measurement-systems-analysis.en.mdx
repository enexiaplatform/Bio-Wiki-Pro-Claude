# Measurement Systems Analysis (Gauge R&R)

Every measurement is the true value plus measurement error. Measurement Systems
Analysis (MSA) quantifies that error so you know whether the variation you see is
the **process** or the **measurement system**. Gauge R&R is the core tool.

## Repeatability vs reproducibility

- **Repeatability** — variation when the *same* operator measures the *same* part
  repeatedly with the *same* gauge (equipment variation).
- **Reproducibility** — variation *between operators* measuring the same parts
  (appraiser variation).

Gauge R&R combines both into a single estimate of measurement-system variation.

## Reading the result

- **%GRR** (measurement variation as a share of total): a common rule of thumb is
  under 10% = acceptable, 10–30% = conditionally acceptable, over 30% =
  unacceptable.
- **ndc** (number of distinct categories) — how many groups the system can
  reliably tell apart; 5 or more is the usual target.

A high %GRR means your data is dominated by measurement noise — you can't trust
pass/fail decisions made on it.

## Why it matters in pharma QC

Specifications are tight. If your measurement system can't distinguish in-spec
from out-of-spec reliably, you'll reject good lots and pass bad ones. MSA is also
the unsung prerequisite behind meaningful SPC and process capability.

## A worked Gauge R&R layout

The standard crossed study: **10 parts × 3 operators × 3 trials = 90
measurements**.

- Choose **10 parts** that span the real process range (not 10 identical good
  ones — you need spread to estimate part-to-part variation).
- **3 operators** who normally run the method.
- **3 trials** each, **randomized and blinded** so operators can't recall prior
  readings.
- Analyze with ANOVA to partition total variation into repeatability (equipment),
  reproducibility (operator + operator×part), and part-to-part.

## Interpreting the output

| Metric | Good | Marginal | Reject |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| %GRR (of total or tolerance) | &lt;10% | 10–30% | &gt;30% |
| ndc | ≥5 | — | &lt;5 |

If reproducibility dominates → the problem is **operators/method ambiguity**
(training, unclear procedure). If repeatability dominates → the problem is the
**instrument** (worn, low resolution, needs maintenance/calibration).

## When the system fails

1. **Don't trust borderline pass/fail data** made on it — quarantine the
   decisions, not just the gauge.
2. **Diagnose** repeatability vs reproducibility to target the fix.
3. **Improve** — better fixturing, clearer SOP/training, higher-resolution
   instrument, or averaging multiple readings.
4. **Re-run** the Gauge R&R to prove the fix worked before relying on the data.

For continuous lab data (assay, content), the analog is method **precision**
(repeatability + intermediate precision from ICH Q2) judged against the spec
width — same idea: can the method resolve in-spec from out-of-spec?

> Practitioner tip: run MSA *before* you set up SPC or compute Cpk. Capability and
> control charts built on a noisy measurement system are charts of your gauge's
> error, not your process — and they'll send you chasing phantom signals.
