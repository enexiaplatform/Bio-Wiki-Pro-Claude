# Dry-Heat Sterilization & Depyrogenation

Dry heat does two jobs for heat-stable items (glass vials, metal): it
**sterilizes** and, at higher temperatures, **depyrogenates** — destroying
bacterial endotoxin that steam can't reliably remove.

## Sterilization vs depyrogenation

- **Sterilization** by dry heat needs higher temperature / longer time than steam
  (e.g. 160–180 °C ranges) because dry air transfers heat less efficiently.
- **Depyrogenation** is the tougher bar: the recognized standard is a **≥3-log
  (1000-fold) reduction of endotoxin**, typically validated around **250 °C** for
  a defined hold.

## FH — the lethality concept

Analogous to steam's F0, **FH** expresses accumulated dry-heat lethality at a
reference temperature (170 °C). Cycle development maps temperature/time to the
required FH and the depyrogenation endpoint.

## What gets qualified

- **Heat distribution** (empty + loaded) to find the cold spot.
- **Endotoxin indicators (EIs)** — vials spiked with a known endotoxin load (e.g.
  ≥1000 EU) placed at the cold spot must show ≥3-log reduction.
- For continuous **tunnels**: belt speed, zone temperatures, and airflow/HEPA
  integrity over the sterile zone.

## Endotoxin-indicator placement plan

1. Run **heat distribution** (empty, then loaded) to locate the **cold spot(s)** —
   slowest-to-heat positions and load-shadowed areas.
2. Place **endotoxin indicators** (vials/discs spiked with ≥1000 EU of purified
   LPS) at those cold spots plus representative locations across the load.
3. Place **temperature probes** alongside to correlate measured lethality (FH)
   with the achieved endotoxin reduction.
4. Run three consecutive cycles; each EI at the worst case must show **≥3-log
   reduction**, and the recovered control EIs must confirm the spiked load.

## Sterilization-only vs depyrogenation cycles

A cycle can be designed for sterilization (lower temperature/longer time) or for
depyrogenation (higher temperature, e.g. ~250 °C). Depyrogenation *includes*
sterilization, but not vice versa — a 180 °C sterilization cycle does **not**
reliably destroy endotoxin. Define the cycle's purpose up front; glass for sterile
fill that may carry endotoxin needs the depyrogenation bar.

## Tunnels vs batch ovens

| Aspect | Batch oven | Continuous tunnel |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Mode | Static load, timed cycle | Conveyor through heating zones |
| Critical params | Temp distribution, hold, cold spot | Belt speed, zone temps, airflow/HEPA over sterile zone |
| Aseptic link | Loaded/unloaded as a step | Feeds the filling line directly (Grade A exit) |
| Validation focus | Heat distribution + EI at cold spot | Slowest belt speed = worst case; EIs on the belt |

For tunnels, the worst case is the **fastest throughput / slowest heat** the
process permits, and the HEPA over the cooling/exit zone is part of the sterile
boundary feeding the filler.

> Practitioner tip: prove the cycle's *intent*. If glass is depyrogenated, your
> EIs — not just temperature — are the evidence. A perfect temperature trace with
> no endotoxin-reduction data does not demonstrate depyrogenation.
