# Technology Transfer (Process)

Technology transfer moves a product and its process from development to
manufacturing, or between manufacturing sites, with the knowledge needed to
reproduce it reliably. ICH Q10 treats it as a core element of the pharmaceutical
quality system; WHO TRS 961 Annex 7 gives a practical framework.

## Sending and receiving units

- **Sending Unit (SU)** — owns the process knowledge: development reports,
  validated methods, specifications, and the control strategy.
- **Receiving Unit (RU)** — must demonstrate it can run the process within the
  defined parameters and consistently meet quality attributes.

Transfer is a knowledge-management exercise as much as a documentation one — tacit
know-how is where transfers most often fail.

## The transfer protocol

A formal protocol defines scope, responsibilities, the parameters and attributes
to be compared, acceptance criteria, and the number of qualification/verification
batches — agreed by both parties **before** execution.

## Proving comparability

The RU demonstrates equivalence through process performance and product quality
data against pre-defined criteria, feeding into process validation at the new
site. Gaps trigger investigation, not a quiet adjustment of limits.

## Transfer-readiness checklist

- [ ] Development/process knowledge package compiled (CQAs, CPPs, control strategy,
      DoE/robustness data).
- [ ] Validated analytical methods + reference standards available to the RU.
- [ ] RU facility, equipment, and utilities qualified for the process.
- [ ] Equipment differences (scale, geometry, mixing) assessed for impact.
- [ ] Materials/suppliers aligned; any substitution risk-assessed.
- [ ] Transfer protocol approved by both SU and RU with criteria and batch count.
- [ ] Training and "person-to-person" knowledge transfer planned (tacit know-how).
- [ ] Regulatory strategy clear (post-approval change category, variations).

## Gap-analysis dimensions

Compare SU vs RU across: equipment train, scale and hold times, utilities and
environment, analytical capability, operator skill, and material sources. Each
difference is a potential variable — document it, assess impact, and either
control it or include it in the qualification batches' scope.

## Transfer the methods too

A process transfer that forgets the **analytical method transfer** stalls at the
RU's QC lab. Coordinate both: comparative testing (RU vs SU on the same samples),
co-validation, or full re-validation depending on risk. The RU can't release
batches it can't reliably test.

## Proving comparability

Define equivalence criteria *before* running batches — typically process
performance (yield, in-process parameters) and product quality attributes against
the SU's established ranges, using statistical comparison where appropriate. A
result outside criteria triggers investigation; you never quietly widen a limit to
make the transfer "pass."

> Practitioner tip: budget time for tacit knowledge. The development scientist who
> knows the slurry "looks wrong at this viscosity" carries information no SOP
> captures — send people, not just paper.
