# Visual Inspection of Injectables

Visual inspection is the routine patient-safety screen that looks for **visible
particulates** and visible container/closure defects before injectable units are
released. USP 790 sets the compendial expectation that injectable products are
inspected for visible particulates; USP 1790 adds practical guidance for building
the inspection process.

The phrase "100% visual inspection" can be misleading. It means every unit is
presented to an inspection process. It does **not** mean the process can detect
every defect with perfect certainty. Visual inspection is probabilistic: detection
changes with particle size, shape, color, density, product clarity, container
geometry, fill volume, lighting, presentation time, inspector attention, and machine
recipe performance.

## What the inspection screens for

A good visual inspection program has a defined defect taxonomy. At minimum, it
separates defects that can affect patient safety from defects that are primarily
cosmetic.

| Area | Examples | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Visible particulates | Glass, metal, fibers, elastomer, precipitate, foreign matter | Particles may indicate direct patient risk or process loss of control |
| Container integrity indicators | Cracks, chips, broken syringes, compromised seals, missing closures | A visible container/closure defect can signal sterility or dose risk |
| Fill and appearance | Low fill, high fill, discoloration, haze, lyophilized cake defect | May indicate process, formulation, or handling problems |
| Label and presentation | Wrong label, missing label, unreadable code, cosmetic damage | Can create use error or market-quality risk |

Critical defects usually require a much tighter acceptance approach than major or
minor cosmetic defects. The exact categories, examples, and acceptance criteria must
come from the approved SOP and product quality risk assessment.

## The two-step release model

Most operations use two complementary controls:

1. **100% inspection.** Every accepted unit from filling, lyophilization, or final
   packaging is inspected by manual, semi-automated, or automated methods.
2. **AQL or statistical acceptance sampling.** A sample of the accepted population is
   inspected against predefined critical, major, and minor defect limits.

The second step does not replace the first. It verifies that the inspected batch
quality is consistent with expectations and that the inspection process did not
allow an unacceptable defect rate through.

## Manual inspection controls

Manual inspection depends on a controlled environment and a qualified inspector.
The SOP should define the practical details, including:

- Lighting intensity, background contrast, and viewing distance.
- How the unit is presented, swirled, inverted, and allowed to settle.
- Minimum or maximum viewing time per background or view.
- Rest breaks, inspection duration limits, and fatigue controls.
- Rejection criteria for each defect category.
- Line clearance, segregation of rejects, and reconciliation of inspected units.

Inspector qualification should include appropriate vision checks, training on the
defect library, and challenge-set performance. Requalification should be periodic
and triggered by relevant changes, such as a new product, new container, new defect
set, recurring inspection discrepancy, or long absence from inspection duties.

## Automated inspection controls

Automated visual inspection can improve consistency, but it still needs validation
and lifecycle control. A practical validation package normally demonstrates that
the recipe, machine setup, reject mechanism, and challenge set detect relevant
defects at an acceptable level for the product and container.

Key controls include:

- Product-specific recipes with change control.
- Representative challenge units for visible particle and container defects.
- Sensitivity settings that balance false rejects against missed defects.
- Reject-bin verification and reconciliation.
- Periodic performance checks before, during, and after inspection campaigns.
- Investigation rules for camera faults, reject spikes, or recurring false rejects.

For difficult-to-inspect products, such as dark, opaque, highly viscous, foaming,
or complex biologic formulations, the process may need additional studies because
standard clear-solution assumptions may not hold.

## Link to subvisible particles

Visual inspection addresses visible particles. It does not replace subvisible
particulate testing, which is handled by USP 788 methods such as light obscuration
or microscopy. The two controls should be trended together because a visible particle
increase and a subvisible particle increase can point to the same root cause:
component shedding, glass delamination, stopper interaction, formulation
instability, equipment wear, or cleaning failure.

## When to investigate

Do not treat rejects as routine waste only. Visual inspection data is a process
signal. Escalate when you see:

- Any confirmed critical defect in accepted or sampled units.
- AQL failure after 100% inspection.
- Reject rates above alert or action limits.
- A new particle type or recurring particle morphology.
- A cluster linked to one filling line, stopper lot, vial lot, operator, or time
  window.
- A mismatch between manual and automated inspection outcomes.
- Customer complaint, field defect, or retain sample defect matching the same trend.

The investigation should identify the particle or defect where possible, assess
whether the batch and related batches are affected, and connect the outcome to CAPA
or continued process verification.

## Release documentation checklist

Before release, the evidence package should make the inspection decision
traceable:

- Approved inspection SOP and product defect library.
- Inspector qualification or automated inspection validation status.
- Lighting or machine setup checks.
- Batch count reconciliation: inspected, accepted, rejected, sampled, destroyed, or
  reworked as allowed.
- Defect counts by category and trend review against limits.
- AQL sampling record and disposition.
- Deviation, investigation, and CAPA references where triggered.

## Practical takeaway

Visual inspection is not a final cosmetic step. It is a controlled detection system
inside the contamination control and batch release strategy. The best programs treat
inspection outcomes as data: they classify defects consistently, qualify people and
machines, verify accepted batches statistically, and investigate trends before a
particle reaches the patient.
