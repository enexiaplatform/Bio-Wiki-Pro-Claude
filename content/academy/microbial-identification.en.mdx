# Microbial Identification Methods

A colony count tells you **how many** organisms were recovered. Microbial
identification tells you **what** was recovered, and that is often what determines
the investigation path.

The same count can mean very different things:

- *Micrococcus* on a glove plate may point toward personnel or gowning.
- *Pseudomonas* in a water sample may point toward wet surfaces, biofilm, or system
  control.
- *Bacillus* in Grade C may point toward dust, materials, or weak sporicidal
  cleaning.
- A mold recovery near a transfer path may point toward HVAC, construction,
  cardboard, or humidity.

Identification turns a colony into contamination intelligence.

## Characterization, identification, and strain typing

USP &lt;1113&gt; separates related ideas that are often blended together:

| Level | What it does | Example use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Characterization | Describes basic features such as colony morphology, Gram reaction, cellular shape, spores, catalase/oxidase, or growth pattern | Early triage, trend grouping, deciding whether deeper ID is needed |
| Identification | Assigns the organism to a group, genus, or species | EM excursions, sterility positives, objectionable-organism assessment |
| Strain typing | Compares isolates below species level to assess relatedness | Recurring contamination, product impact, suspected common source |

Not every recovery needs the deepest level. The depth should match the risk and the
decision being made.

## Common ID approaches

| Method | Strength | Limitation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gram stain and morphology | Fast first clue; separates broad groups | Not enough for species-level decisions |
| Biochemical or phenotypic systems | Practical and familiar for routine isolates | Can be weak for unusual, stressed, or slow-growing organisms |
| MALDI-TOF | Fast species-level ID for many bacteria and fungi using a protein mass fingerprint | Depends on database quality, sample prep, and acceptance criteria |
| 16S rRNA sequencing | Strong genotypic approach for bacteria, useful for difficult IDs | May not separate closely related species; needs sequence quality and interpretation |
| ITS or fungal sequencing | Useful for yeasts and molds | Library coverage and taxonomy can be challenging |
| Strain typing methods | Helps show whether isolates are related | Usually reserved for high-impact or recurring events |

A robust lab defines when each method is appropriate and how uncertain or low-score
results are handled.

## When to identify

Use a risk-based rule set. Identification is usually expected or strongly justified
for:

- Grade A/B environmental or personnel excursions;
- sterility test positives;
- media fill positives;
- recurring action or alert trends;
- objectionable-organism assessment in non-sterile products;
- water system Gram-negative or biofilm-associated recoveries;
- new or unusual facility flora;
- isolates linked to complaints, deviations, or product impact decisions.

Lower-risk, low-count recoveries in less critical areas may only need
characterization or genus-level ID, depending on the SOP and trend strategy.

## How far to identify

The decision is not "ID everything to species" or "ID only when forced." A practical
approach is:

| Situation | Typical depth |
| --- | --- |
| Grade A recovery | Species-level ID, investigation, product-impact assessment |
| Grade B action-level recovery | Genus/species ID and trend correlation |
| Recurring same location or same morphology | Species-level ID; consider strain typing if recurrence matters |
| Non-sterile product isolate | Species-level ID when objectionability depends on organism identity |
| Routine low-risk background flora | Characterization or genus-level ID may be sufficient if justified |
| Ambiguous MALDI/phenotypic result | Confirm with sequencing or alternate method when decision impact is high |

The SOP should define both the default rule and the escalation rule.

## Source clues from organism type

The organism does not prove the source, but it gives the investigation a starting
hypothesis.

| Organism pattern | Common source hypothesis |
| --- | --- |
| Coagulase-negative staphylococci, *Micrococcus*, diphtheroids | Human skin, gowning, glove disinfection, intervention technique |
| Spore-formers such as *Bacillus* | Dust, raw materials, cardboard, poor sporicidal cleaning |
| Gram-negative rods such as *Pseudomonas*, *Ralstonia*, *Burkholderia* | Water, wet surfaces, drains, biofilm, cleaning residues |
| Molds such as *Aspergillus* or *Penicillium* | Air handling, construction, humidity, packaging materials |
| Yeasts | Personnel, wet niches, raw materials, formulation or process residues |

Use ID with location, timing, activity, EM trends, cleaning records, airflow, and
personnel history. ID alone is a clue; correlation makes it evidence.

## Building an isolate library

A site isolate library is one of the best tools for contamination control. It helps
the site answer:

- What organisms are normal for each area or utility?
- Which organisms are new or increasing?
- Which locations share the same flora?
- Which isolates recur after cleaning or maintenance?
- Which organisms should be included in disinfectant qualification or method
  suitability studies?
- Which isolates matter for the contamination control strategy?

The library should include organism ID, source, date, location, method, confidence
level, and investigation outcome. For high-impact recurring isolates, strain typing
may be useful to distinguish "same species, unrelated events" from "same source
returning."

## Reviewer checklist

When reviewing an ID result, ask:

- Was the isolate pure and viable before identification?
- Was the method qualified or verified for the expected organism range?
- Was the confidence score or sequence quality acceptable?
- Does the ID fit the colony morphology and Gram stain?
- Was an ambiguous result escalated appropriately?
- Does the organism change the product-impact or objectionability decision?
- Was the isolate added to the site library or trend report?
- Does the investigation use the ID to test a source hypothesis?

If the ID result is not used in the investigation, the site may be collecting data
without turning it into control.

## Practical takeaway

Microbial identification is not a lab curiosity. It is how QC and QA connect a
recovery to risk, source, trend, product impact, and CAPA. The right depth of ID is
risk-based: enough to support the decision, not so shallow that the investigation is
blind, and not so excessive that the lab spends effort where no decision changes.
