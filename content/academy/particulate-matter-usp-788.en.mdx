# Particulate Matter in Injections (USP 788)

USP 788 sets limits for **subvisible** particulate matter — particles too small
to see but large enough to pose a patient risk in parenteral products. It is a
core release test for injectables.

## The two methods

- **Method 1 — Light Obscuration** — the primary method; a sensor counts
  particles as they block a light beam. Fast and automated.
- **Method 2 — Microscopic** — used when light obscuration is unsuitable (e.g.
  for products that are viscous, colored, or form bubbles).

## Counting thresholds

Limits are defined at particle sizes of 10 µm and greater, and 25 µm and greater,
with separate acceptance criteria for large-volume and small-volume injections.
Test the right number of units and pool/average per the chapter.

## Common pitfalls

- **Bubbles counted as particles** — degas and prepare samples carefully.
- Using light obscuration on a product it cannot read accurately instead of
  switching to the microscopic method.

> Tip: subvisible particulate trends can be an early signal of a container,
> closure, or formulation problem — review them, don't just pass/fail.
