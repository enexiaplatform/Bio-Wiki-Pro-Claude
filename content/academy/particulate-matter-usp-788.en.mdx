# Particulate Matter in Injections - USP 788

USP 788 controls **subvisible particulate matter** in injections: mobile,
undissolved particles other than gas bubbles that are unintentionally present in
solutions. These particles are too small for routine visual inspection but large
enough to matter for parenteral product quality and patient risk.

The chapter provides two procedures: Method 1, light obscuration particle count,
and Method 2, microscopic particle count. Method 1 is generally preferred when the
product can be measured reliably. Method 2 is used when light obscuration is not
suitable, for example because of reduced clarity, high viscosity, strong color,
or other product characteristics that interfere with the instrument.

## Visible vs Subvisible Particles

Particle control needs both lenses.

| Control | Main question | Typical standard or program |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Visible inspection | Are visible particles or visible container/product defects present? | Visual inspection program, USP 790, FDA visible particulate guidance. |
| Subvisible particle count | How many particles at defined size thresholds are present below routine visual detectability? | USP 788 for injections, USP 787 for certain therapeutic protein injections. |
| Particle identification | What is the particle made of, and where did it come from? | FTIR, Raman, microscopy, SEM/EDS, root-cause investigation. |
| Trend review | Is particle burden increasing before a specification failure occurs? | APR/PQR, CPV, stability, complaint and reject-trend review. |

Visual inspection cannot replace USP 788 testing. USP 788 testing also does not
prove a batch is free of visible particles. They answer different questions and
should be reviewed together when particle signals appear.

## Where USP 788 Fits

USP 788 is usually part of the release and stability control strategy for
injectable products. It connects to:

- formulation and solubility control;
- container closure and component cleanliness;
- siliconized components or silicone oil where relevant;
- filtration, filling, stoppering, capping, and equipment wear;
- lyophilization and reconstitution behaviour;
- shipping, agitation, freeze-thaw, and storage stress;
- visible inspection, complaints, and particle identification;
- deviation, OOS/OOT, and CAPA systems.

A passing USP 788 result is important, but the better control question is: are
the particle sources understood and controlled across the product lifecycle?

## Method 1 - Light Obscuration

Light obscuration counts particles as they pass through a sensor and block light.
It is fast, automated, and preferred for many clear, low-viscosity injectable
solutions.

Control points include:

- instrument calibration and suitability;
- clean sample environment and low-particle diluent where used;
- correct container preparation and gentle mixing;
- avoidance of air bubbles and foam;
- appropriate sample volume and number of units;
- discard or treatment of initial aliquots where the procedure requires it;
- correct calculation as particles per mL or per container;
- review of raw counts, not only pass/fail summary.

Light obscuration can be misleading when the product itself interferes with the
optical measurement. Turbid, colored, viscous, highly opalescent, or bubble-prone
products may require additional method suitability assessment or Method 2.

## Method 2 - Microscopic Particle Count

The microscopic method collects particles on a membrane and counts them by
microscopy. It is used when light obscuration is unsuitable or when microscopy is
needed to evaluate particles more directly.

Control points include:

- clean filtration assembly and membrane handling;
- suitable membrane pore size and background contrast;
- rinsing strategy that does not remove or dissolve relevant particles;
- analyst training and counting consistency;
- microscope calibration;
- control of contamination from lab air, glassware, tools, and gloves;
- recognition that some soft or fragile particles may behave differently during
  filtration than in the original product.

Method 2 is not simply a way to rescue a poor Method 1 result. The reason for
method selection should be scientifically justified and documented.

## Size Thresholds and Limits

USP 788 evaluates particle counts at two size thresholds:

- particles equal to or greater than 10 micrometers;
- particles equal to or greater than 25 micrometers.

Acceptance limits are different for large-volume and small-volume injections.
Large-volume injections are typically evaluated as particles per mL. Small-volume
injections are typically evaluated as particles per container. Always use the
current official compendial chapter and the correct container-volume category for
the product.

The common trap is mixing the reporting basis: per mL and per container are not
interchangeable.

## Sample Handling Matters

Particle testing is sensitive to handling. Poor technique can create false
results or hide real ones.

Watch for:

- air bubbles from shaking, rapid pipetting, or poor degassing;
- glassware or tubing shedding particles;
- stopper coring or elastomer fragments from sampling;
- dirty laminar flow hood, bench, or containers;
- incomplete reconstitution;
- protein aggregates or precipitates formed by agitation or temperature shift;
- silicone oil droplets in prefilled syringes;
- static, fibers, or lab wipes near open samples;
- sample settling if mixing is inconsistent;
- delay between preparation and analysis.

The analyst should understand whether the sample needs gentle inversion, standing
time, degassing, dilution, reconstitution, or protection from agitation. Particle
methods are not just instrument methods; they are sample-handling methods.

## Method Suitability

Before relying on routine results, confirm that the method is suitable for the
specific product.

Ask:

- Is the product clear enough for light obscuration?
- Does viscosity affect sampling or flow through the instrument?
- Does color, opalescence, turbidity, or high protein concentration interfere?
- Are bubbles, silicone droplets, or aggregates counted in a way that reflects
  the intended control strategy?
- Does dilution change particle behaviour?
- Does filtration in Method 2 lose, dissolve, or deform particles?
- Are recovery and background controls acceptable?
- Are the sample volume and number of units feasible for the dosage form?

For therapeutic protein injections, USP 787 may be more appropriate than USP 788
in some cases because it addresses smaller available sample volumes and protein
product considerations. The chapter choice should be scientifically justified.

## Particle Sources

Particle investigations improve when the site classifies likely sources.

| Source category | Examples | Clues |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Formulation or product-related | Precipitate, protein aggregate, crystal, degradant. | Stability trend, temperature/agitation sensitivity, same product across lots. |
| Container closure | Glass lamellae, elastomer fragment, silicone oil, vial or syringe defect. | Same component lot, filling/stoppering association, visual inspection signal. |
| Process equipment | Metal, polymer, gasket, tubing, filter, pump wear. | Line-specific trend, maintenance link, particle chemistry. |
| Environment or handling | Fiber, dust, gowning material, lab contamination. | Sporadic events, open handling step, lab blank issue. |
| Administration or reconstitution | Stopper coring, diluent incompatibility, incomplete dissolution. | Complaint or simulated-use link. |

Do not stop at "extraneous particle." Identify material and likely entry point
when recurrence, patient risk, or batch impact is possible.

## Failure and OOT Investigation

A USP 788 failure, borderline result, or adverse trend should trigger structured
review:

1. Confirm calculation, sample identity, units, container category, and method.
2. Review instrument suitability, calibration, blank/background, and controls.
3. Check sample preparation, mixing, degassing, dilution, and analyst technique.
4. Review raw counts for outlier units and distribution pattern.
5. Compare visible inspection rejects and complaint data.
6. Review batch records, filtration, filling, stoppering, lyophilization, and
   component lots.
7. Check stability, shipping, agitation, and temperature exposure.
8. Identify particles where investigation risk justifies it.
9. Assess related batches, components, equipment trains, and markets.
10. Define CAPA and effectiveness checks based on root cause.

Avoid retesting until a passing average appears. Retesting must follow approved
OOS/OOT and laboratory investigation procedures.

## Trending and Control Strategy

Particle counts can be useful before they fail specifications. Trend:

- 10 micrometer and 25 micrometer counts separately;
- mean, range, and high-unit behaviour;
- container type, component lot, line, shift, and filling campaign;
- stability time point and storage condition;
- visual reject categories;
- complaint particle descriptions;
- formulation or process changes;
- method or instrument changes.

A rising 10 micrometer trend may point to early aggregation or component
interaction before visible particles appear. A 25 micrometer increase may carry
different clinical and investigation weight. Trend both.

## Decision Matrix

| Situation | Likely decision | Rationale |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Product is clear, low viscosity, low bubbles, and Method 1 is suitable. | Use light obscuration as routine method. | It is the preferred automated approach for many injections. |
| Product is turbid, highly colored, viscous, or optically unsuitable. | Justify Method 2 or alternative compendial strategy. | Method 1 may not produce reliable counts. |
| Method 1 fails and lab error is not demonstrated. | Open OOS investigation and assess batch impact. | Retesting to pass is not a control strategy. |
| Visible inspection rejects rise but USP 788 remains within limits. | Investigate visible particle source and compare trends. | Visible and subvisible controls are related but different. |
| Stability particle counts increase over time. | Assess formulation, container closure, storage, shipping, and shelf-life impact. | Particles can be stability-indicating. |
| New container, stopper, syringe, filter, or filling line is introduced. | Assess particle-control impact through change control. | Component/process changes can alter particle burden. |

## Documentation Checklist

Keep the particulate matter file inspection-ready with:

- approved test method and compendial reference;
- method selection rationale;
- method suitability assessment for product characteristics;
- instrument calibration and suitability records;
- sample handling and preparation instructions;
- blank, rinse, and background controls where applicable;
- raw particle counts and calculations;
- correct small-volume or large-volume acceptance basis;
- analyst training and qualification;
- OOS/OOT investigation procedure;
- particle identification strategy;
- link to visual inspection, complaints, stability, and change control;
- trend review in APR/PQR or product quality monitoring;
- CAPA and effectiveness checks for recurring particle sources.

## Common GMP Findings

Inspectors often challenge programs when:

- bubbles are not controlled during sample preparation;
- the wrong container-volume limit is applied;
- per mL and per container calculations are confused;
- light obscuration is used despite obvious product interference;
- Method 2 is used without scientific rationale;
- raw counts are not reviewed for outlier units;
- particle trends are not reviewed because all lots pass;
- visible and subvisible particle investigations are kept in separate silos;
- recurring particle types are not identified;
- container, closure, filter, or process changes do not assess particulate impact.

## Practical Takeaway

USP 788 is not only a release test. It is a window into formulation behaviour,
component cleanliness, filling process control, stability, and container closure
performance.

The best programs do three things:

1. Choose the right method for the product.
2. Control sample handling tightly enough to trust the count.
3. Trend particles as process knowledge, not just pass/fail data.
