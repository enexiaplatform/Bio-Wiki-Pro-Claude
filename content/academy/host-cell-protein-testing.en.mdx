# Host Cell Protein (HCP) Testing

Host cell proteins are **process-related impurities** — proteins from the
production cell line (CHO, E. coli, yeast) that co-purify with the product. Even
at trace levels they can be immunogenic or affect product stability, so HCP
clearance is a core part of demonstrating a purification process works.

## Why HCPs matter

HCPs can trigger immune responses in patients, act as adjuvants, or degrade the
product (some are proteases/lipases). Regulators expect HCPs reduced to low ppm
and *controlled and trended* batch to batch as evidence the process is consistent.

## The ELISA workhorse — and its blind spot

The routine method is an **HCP ELISA** using a polyclonal anti-HCP antibody raised
against a mock (null-cell) preparation. It's sensitive and high-throughput, but
its validity hinges on **antibody coverage**: the polyclonal must recognize the
many individual HCP species present. A commercial/generic antibody may miss
process-specific HCPs, under-reporting the true level.

Coverage is assessed (e.g. 2D gel/Western or antigen-downstream methods) and a
**process-specific** anti-HCP reagent is often developed for commercial control.

## Orthogonal and modern methods

- **2D-PAGE / 2D-DIGE** — visualizes individual species; used for coverage.
- **LC-MS/MS (proteomics)** — identifies and quantifies *specific* HCPs, catching
  individual high-risk proteins an ELISA total can hide. Increasingly expected for
  characterization and to justify the ELISA.

## Limits and control

HCP is reported as **ng HCP / mg product (ppm)**, with typical targets in the
low-double-digit to sub-ppm range depending on dose and route. The number alone
isn't enough — you also show *clearance* across purification and **trend** release
results.

## Investigating an HCP excursion

Confirm the assay (standard curve, dilutional linearity, coverage still valid),
then look upstream: a purification step underperforming, a column nearing
end-of-life, or a cell-culture shift raising the HCP load. Tie the finding to the
process, not just the plate.

> Practitioner tip: a "passing" total HCP ELISA can still hide a single
> high-risk, low-abundance protease. For high-dose or chronic products, back the
> ELISA with LC-MS/MS so you know *which* HCPs remain, not just how many ng.
