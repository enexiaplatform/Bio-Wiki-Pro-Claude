# Protein Characterization Methods

No single test defines a large protein, so biologics QC uses a panel of
**orthogonal** methods — each looking at a different attribute. Together they form
the identity and purity story for a monoclonal antibody or recombinant protein.

## Peptide mapping (primary structure)

The protein is enzymatically digested (e.g. trypsin) and the peptides separated by
LC, usually with MS detection. The resulting **map** is a fingerprint that
confirms the amino-acid **sequence** and can localize modifications (oxidation,
deamidation, clipping). It's the most definitive identity method and a key
comparability tool.

## Intact and reduced mass

**Intact mass** (by high-resolution MS) confirms the overall molecular weight and
reveals major glycoforms. **Reduced mass** breaks disulfides to weigh heavy and
light chains separately — useful for confirming chain identity and modifications.

## CE-SDS (size/purity)

Capillary electrophoresis–SDS is the modern replacement for SDS-PAGE: it sizes the
molecule and quantifies **size-based purity** — main peak vs fragments
(clips, free light chain) and, non-reduced, vs aggregates/incomplete species.

## Charge-variant analysis (icIEF / CEX)

Imaged capillary isoelectric focusing (**icIEF**) or **cation-exchange** HPLC
separates **charge variants** into acidic, main, and basic species. Deamidation,
sialylation, and C-terminal lysine all shift charge — so the charge profile is a
sensitive, routinely monitored CQA.

## How they work together

| Attribute | Method |
| --- | --- |
| Sequence / modifications | Peptide mapping (LC-MS) |
| Molecular weight / glycoforms | Intact & reduced mass |
| Size purity (fragments, aggregates) | CE-SDS (reduced & non-reduced) |
| Charge heterogeneity | icIEF / CEX |

Identity is concluded from the *combination* — e.g. peptide map + charge profile +
a binding assay — not any one result.

## Why it matters

These methods are the daily language of biologics QC and the backbone of every
comparability study. Fluency in what each method sees (and misses) is exactly the
skill that distinguishes a biologics analyst.

> Tip: when a process change is proposed, the characterization panel — not the
> release spec alone — is what proves the product is still "the same." Know which
> method would first reveal the change you're worried about.
