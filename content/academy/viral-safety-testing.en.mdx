# Viral Safety of Biologics

Biologics made in mammalian, avian, insect, or other human/animal-derived cell
systems can carry viral risk from the cell substrate, biological raw materials,
or production process. Because no single test can prove a product is free from
every possible virus, ICH Q5A(R2) builds viral safety from three complementary
layers.

## The three layers

1. **Control the source** - understand and qualify the cell line, master cell
   bank, working cell bank, and biological raw materials.
2. **Test at appropriate stages** - test cell banks, cells at the limit of in
   vitro cell age, and unprocessed bulk where the result can meaningfully control
   risk.
3. **Validate viral clearance** - demonstrate that purification removes or
   inactivates virus with a defensible log-reduction margin.

The result is a barrier strategy. Source controls reduce what could enter the
process. Testing looks for what may be present. Clearance provides margin for the
unknown.

## Source control

Source control asks where a virus could come from before manufacturing starts.
Reviewers look for a clear history of the cell substrate, bank genealogy,
adventitious-agent testing, raw-material controls, and change-control discipline.

Useful evidence includes:

- origin and development history of the cell line;
- master and working cell-bank qualification;
- testing for endogenous and adventitious viruses;
- control of animal- or human-derived raw materials;
- supplier qualification and raw-material change assessment.

## Testing

Testing should match the product, cell substrate, process, and risk. Depending on
the case, the panel may include in vitro adventitious-agent assays, retrovirus
testing, reverse transcriptase assays, electron microscopy, targeted PCR, or
broader molecular methods such as next-generation sequencing.

Testing is powerful, but its conclusion is limited by method scope, detection
limit, sample size, and representativeness. A negative result supports the
strategy; it does not replace the strategy.

## Viral clearance

Viral clearance studies show whether process steps remove or inactivate virus.
In a scaled-down model, a selected model virus is spiked into process material
before a step, the step is run under representative conditions, and the
difference between input and output is reported as a **log reduction value
(LRV)**.

Common clearance mechanisms include:

- **Inactivation** - low-pH hold, solvent/detergent treatment, heat, or other
  conditions that reduce infectivity.
- **Removal** - virus filtration, chromatography, precipitation, or partitioning
  steps that separate virus from product.

The strongest packages combine orthogonal steps. A low-pH hold and a virus
filter work by different mechanisms, so together they provide a more credible
margin than two steps that fail in the same way.

## What good looks like

- Cell banks and raw materials are characterized for viral risk.
- The testing panel is justified for the substrate and product.
- Unprocessed-bulk testing has clear acceptance and escalation rules.
- Viral clearance studies use representative scale-down models.
- Model-virus selection is justified.
- LRVs are supported by controls, assay suitability, and recovery data.
- Additive clearance claims are based on mechanistically different steps.
- Process and supplier changes trigger viral-safety impact assessment.

## Why it matters

Viral safety is a core biologics quality discipline because it connects cell-bank
science, QC testing, process validation, supplier quality, QA, and regulatory CMC.
The best answer to "how do you know this biologic is virus-safe?" is not "the
test was negative." It is "the source is controlled, the right stages are tested,
and the process has validated, orthogonal clearance margin."
