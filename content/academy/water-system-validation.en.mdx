# Pharmaceutical Water System Validation

Water is the most-used ingredient in pharma — and a prime contamination route.
Validating a Purified Water (PW) or Water for Injection (WFI) system proves it
**consistently** delivers water of the required chemical and microbial quality at
every point of use.

## The three-phase approach

- **Phase 1 (2–4 weeks)** — intensive daily sampling of **every** point of use to
  demonstrate the system can consistently produce quality water; finalize SOPs and
  ranges. Don't yet use the water for production.
- **Phase 2 (2–4 weeks)** — continue intensive sampling to confirm consistent
  performance with the established procedures; water may be released per
  procedure.
- **Phase 3 (≈1 year)** — sampling at the routine reduced frequency, across all
  seasons, to capture seasonal variation (feed-water temperature, microbial load).

## Why sample every point of use

Contamination is local — a dead leg or a poorly drained user point can fail while
the loop looks fine. Phase 1/2 sample **all** outlets; routine monitoring rotates
so every point is covered over time.

## Ongoing control

After validation: continuous TOC/conductivity (chemical) plus scheduled microbial
and endotoxin (WFI) monitoring against **alert and action levels**, with trending
and sanitization (thermal/chemical) on a defined cadence. Biofilm is the enemy.

## Three-phase protocol outline

| Phase | Duration | Sampling | Water use | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | 2–4 weeks | All points, daily | Not for production | Prove capability, finalize SOPs/limits |
| 2 | 2–4 weeks | All points, daily | Releasable per SOP | Confirm consistency under procedures |
| 3 | ~1 year | Routine reduced frequency, rotating | Routine | Capture seasonal variation |

Define up front: typical chemical attributes (conductivity and TOC per
pharmacopeia), microbial limits (e.g. PW action ~100 CFU/mL; WFI ~10 CFU/100 mL),
endotoxin for WFI (&lt;0.25 EU/mL), and the sanitization regime.

## Sampling-point rationale

- **Every use point** in Phases 1–2 — contamination is local; a single dead leg
  or poorly drained valve fails while the loop reads clean.
- **Source/generation/storage/return** sampled to localize where quality changes.
- **Worst-case points** (longest run, lowest flow, least-used outlets) prioritized
  in routine rotation.
- **Use-point as the user takes it** — sample the way production actually draws
  water (flush or not), so the data reflects real exposure.

## Excursion impact assessment

When water exceeds an action level, the question is: *what did this water touch?*

1. Identify the organism / measure the deviation magnitude.
2. List products and cleaning operations that used water from that point in the
   affected window.
3. Assess impact on each (was water an ingredient? final rinse? sterile process?).
4. Decide batch disposition; investigate the system cause (biofilm, sanitization
   failure, dead leg, breach).
5. CAPA + re-sanitize + re-sample to confirm return to control.

> Practitioner tip: water excursions are rarely "isolated." Biofilm sheds
> intermittently, so a single high count at one point often means the loop needs
> sanitization and trend review — not a quick resample-and-pass. Treat the system,
> not the sample.
