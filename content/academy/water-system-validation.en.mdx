# Pharmaceutical Water System Validation - Lifecycle Qualification

Validating a Purified Water (PW) or Water for Injection (WFI) system is the
documented proof that the system can consistently generate, store, distribute,
and deliver the required water quality at every relevant point of use. It is not
only a microbiology study and it is not finished when the first qualification
protocol is signed.

Water-system validation is lifecycle work: design it correctly, qualify the
installed system, challenge it under real operating conditions, prove consistent
water quality, then keep proving control through monitoring, maintenance,
sanitization, periodic review, and change control.

> Practice it: use the
> [Pharmaceutical Water System Monitoring workflow](/workflows/water-system-monitoring)
> to connect qualification evidence to routine sampling, trending, excursions,
> and product-impact decisions.

## Validation Lifecycle Map

| Stage | Core question | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| User Requirement Specification (URS) | What water quality, quantity, use points, and operating needs must the system meet? | Intended uses, grades, peak demand, critical points, regulatory commitments |
| Design Qualification (DQ) | Does the proposed design control the known risks? | P&ID, material of construction, slopes, dead-leg review, sanitization concept, automation design |
| Installation Qualification (IQ) | Was the system installed as designed? | As-built drawings, weld records, instruments, filters, valves, utilities, calibration |
| Operational Qualification (OQ) | Can the system operate within defined ranges? | Flow, temperature, pressure, conductivity, TOC, alarms, sanitization cycle, recovery tests |
| Performance Qualification (PQ) / validation phases | Does it consistently deliver acceptable water at use points? | Intensive sampling, all use points, chemical/microbial/endotoxin data, trend review |
| Continued process verification | Does control persist after routine use begins? | Routine monitoring, seasonal data, maintenance review, deviations, CAPA, periodic system review |

The most common validation weakness is treating the three-phase sampling protocol
as the whole validation. Sampling proves performance only when design, operation,
maintenance, and procedures are already controlled.

## Before Phase 1: Design Must Be Ready

Do not start performance sampling until the system is ready to be tested as a
controlled utility. Pre-phase readiness should confirm:

- approved URS and water-grade rationale for every use point;
- complete and verified P&ID/as-built drawings;
- sanitary design review for slopes, drainability, dead legs, vents, gaskets,
  hoses, sample valves, and low-use points;
- materials of construction and surface finish appropriate for the grade;
- instruments calibrated and alarms challenged;
- source/feedwater and pretreatment understood;
- cleaning/sanitization method defined;
- sampling SOP approved, including flush/no-flush logic;
- microbiology, endotoxin, TOC, conductivity, and sample hold-time methods ready;
- maintenance, filter change, and out-of-service procedures drafted.

If these are not in place, an intensive sampling phase may only document a system
that is not yet under procedural control.

## The Three-Phase Performance Approach

FDA's high-purity water inspection guide describes an initial phase with daily
sampling after each purification step and at each point of use for two to four
weeks. Industry practice commonly extends this into a three-phase performance
model.

| Phase | Typical duration | Sampling intensity | Water use | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Phase 1 - initial capability | 2-4 weeks | Daily after major treatment steps and at every point of use | Normally not for production | Establish operating ranges, sampling procedures, sanitization frequency, and initial control |
| Phase 2 - procedural consistency | 2-4 weeks | Similar intensive sampling under approved procedures | May be releasable if procedure allows and QA approves | Demonstrate the system performs consistently when operated by routine SOPs |
| Phase 3 - routine performance | About 1 year or other justified extended period | Approved routine schedule covering all points over time | Routine use | Capture seasonal feedwater variation and normal operating conditions |

The exact phase design should be justified for the system, water grade, product
risk, source-water variability, and regulatory expectations. A small purified
water loop and a WFI system serving aseptic processing should not be validated
with identical risk assumptions.

## What To Sample

Sampling should show both **where quality is created** and **where quality is
delivered**.

| Sample location | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Source/feedwater | Establishes incoming chemical and microbial challenge |
| After pretreatment steps | Localizes softener, carbon, RO, deionization, UV, or filter issues |
| Generation outlet | Confirms the purification train can meet the required grade |
| Storage tank | Challenges turnover, venting, spray ball coverage, and tank hygiene |
| Loop supply and return | Shows distribution control and thermal/flow stability |
| Every point of use | Proves delivered water quality at the place of actual use |
| Worst-case/low-use points | Challenges stagnation, long runs, hoses, and outlet hygiene |
| Post-sanitization and post-maintenance points | Confirms recovery after intervention |

Point-of-use sampling should reflect how water is actually drawn. If production
uses a hose, the validation should define whether the sample is taken with that
hose, after a defined flush, or under another justified routine condition. A
perfectly clean laboratory draw that production never uses is weak evidence.

## What To Test

| Test | Applies to | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Conductivity | PW and WFI | Ionic purity and chemical control |
| TOC | PW and WFI | Organic contamination and system cleanliness |
| Microbial count | PW and WFI | Bioburden and biofilm control |
| Bacterial endotoxins | WFI and endotoxin-sensitive uses | Pyrogen risk from Gram-negative contamination |
| Temperature / flow / pressure | Storage and distribution | Conditions that prevent stagnation and support control |
| Sanitization cycle parameters | Heat, chemical, ozone, or other cycle | Proof the recovery/control mechanism ran as intended |
| Organism identification | Excursions, trends, and high-risk points | Source tracing and product-impact assessment |

Specifications and action limits should be linked to the water grade and use.
Alert levels should be data-driven and periodically reviewed after enough
baseline data exist.

## Acceptance Criteria Should Be More Than "Pass"

A robust protocol defines:

- chemical and microbiological specifications;
- alert and action levels;
- minimum sample volumes and methods;
- hold times and transport conditions;
- valid/invalid sample rules;
- expected operating ranges for flow, temperature, pressure, and sanitization;
- allowed downtime or low-use conditions;
- number and pattern of excursions that trigger phase extension;
- recovery criteria after sanitization or corrective action;
- QA decision rules for moving from Phase 1 to Phase 2 and from Phase 2 to
  routine use.

Without these rules, teams often negotiate acceptance after seeing the data. That
is the opposite of a defensible validation.

## Interpreting Phase Data

Water data should be read as system behavior, not isolated sample events.

| Pattern | Interpretation | Validation response |
| --- | --- | --- |
| One point repeatedly higher than others | Local outlet, branch, hose, valve, or dead-leg issue | Investigate and correct before reducing sampling |
| Return loop drifts upward | Loop-level microbial control weakening | Review flow, temperature, sanitization, tank, and recent maintenance |
| Post-sanitization recovery is short-lived | Biofilm not controlled or sanitization not reaching the niche | Reassess design, contact time, heat distribution, or chemical concentration |
| TOC/conductivity stable but microbes rise | Chemical quality does not prove microbial control | Treat as microbiological system issue |
| Seasonal feedwater change affects pretreatment | Source-water challenge is variable | Add seasonal controls and Phase 3 trend review |
| Endotoxin appears without high counts | Dead Gram-negative biomass or biofilm shedding | Investigate WFI system and BET/product risk |

Phase failures do not automatically mean the project failed. They are useful if
they reveal design, operational, or procedural weaknesses before routine product
reliance.

## Moving Into Routine Use

Before declaring the system validated for routine use, the final report should
show:

1. the system was installed and operated as intended;
2. procedures were approved and used consistently;
3. all points of use were sampled as justified;
4. chemical, microbial, and endotoxin data met criteria;
5. excursions were investigated and resolved;
6. alert/action levels and routine monitoring frequencies were justified;
7. sanitization and maintenance programs were proven adequate;
8. open risks were accepted by QA with clear controls.

The final report should also identify routine monitoring locations and the
rotation that ensures every point remains covered over time.

## Continued Verification

A water system does not remain validated by memory. It remains validated because
the site keeps evidence that the system is still in control.

Continued verification should include:

- routine point-of-use sampling and loop trending;
- periodic organism review and site flora assessment;
- sanitization effectiveness review;
- maintenance, filter change, and calibration review;
- alarm and excursion review;
- seasonal trend review;
- annual or periodic product/use-point impact assessment;
- evaluation of low-use points, dead-leg risks, and temporary hoses;
- periodic review of whether alert/action levels still fit the data.

After Phase 3, a system review should confirm that routine monitoring is
adequate and that the system can remain under control across seasonal and
operational variation.

## Change Control Triggers

Requalification or targeted verification may be needed after:

- adding, removing, or relocating a point of use;
- changing product use or water grade expectation;
- modifying pretreatment, RO, distillation, ultrafiltration, UV, ozone, tank, or
  loop configuration;
- changing sanitization method, frequency, or parameters;
- replacing major instruments, valves, filters, hoses, or spray balls;
- repeated excursions or adverse trends;
- long shutdown, stagnant period, construction, or contamination event;
- changing sample method, incubation, microbial method, or endotoxin method.

The change-control question is simple: could this change affect water quality at
any use point or the evidence used to prove that quality? If yes, define the
verification before implementation.

## Product-Impact Logic For Validation Gaps

If validation data reveal a problem after water has been used, assess impact by
use.

| Water use | Impact question |
| --- | --- |
| Ingredient in product | Could the water attribute transfer directly to the batch? |
| Final rinse | Could residual water carry bioburden, endotoxin, TOC, or ionic contamination? |
| Cleaning solution make-up | Could cleaning effectiveness or residue profile be affected? |
| QC reagent/media prep | Could the result be invalid or biased? |
| Steam, humidification, or utility contact | Could condensate/contact route affect product or surfaces? |

For WFI and sterile product-contact uses, include endotoxin and sterility
assurance in the impact assessment, not only microbial count.

## Common GMP Findings

- Phase 1 started before SOPs, instruments, or sanitization procedures were
  ready.
- Sampling did not include all points of use or did not reflect actual use.
- Phase failures were averaged away instead of investigated.
- Routine sampling was reduced without a justified rotation and trend plan.
- Alert/action levels were copied from a template and never reassessed.
- Maintenance and sanitization changes were made without water-quality
  verification.
- Final validation report listed data but did not explain system behavior.
- Biofilm signals were treated as isolated lab events.

## Documentation Checklist

A defensible validation package should include:

- URS, DQ, IQ, OQ, PQ protocols and reports;
- current P&ID/as-built drawings and point-of-use list;
- water grade rationale by use point;
- sampling map and flush/no-flush rationale;
- test methods, sample volumes, containers, hold times, and incubation
  conditions;
- conductivity, TOC, microbial, endotoxin, and engineering parameter data;
- excursion investigations and CAPA;
- sanitization validation and routine sanitization records;
- routine monitoring plan and alert/action level rationale;
- Phase 3 or long-term routine review;
- change-control and requalification strategy.

## Practical Takeaway

Water-system validation is not a ceremonial three-phase exercise. It is the
proof that a designed utility can repeatedly deliver the right water, at the
right point, under real operating conditions, and that the site knows how to keep
it that way.

The best validation report reads like a system story: design risk, operating
control, point-of-use performance, seasonal behavior, excursions, recovery, and
the routine monitoring plan that will keep the story true.
