# Elemental Impurities (ICH Q3D)

ICH Q3D shifted control of metals in drug products from a single non-specific
colorimetric test to a **risk-based, toxicology-driven** framework. The old USP
heavy-metals limit test (USP 231) was retired because it was neither specific nor
sensitive enough.

## Permitted Daily Exposure (PDE)

Each element of concern has a **PDE** — the maximum acceptable daily intake,
derived from toxicology. Limits depend on the route of administration (oral,
parenteral, inhalation), since the same element carries different risk by route.

## Element classes

- **Class 1** — human toxicants (As, Cd, Hg, Pb); avoid or strictly limit.
- **Class 2** — route-dependent toxicants (2A more likely present, 2B less so).
- **Class 3** — lower toxicity by oral route, but relevant for parenteral and
  inhalation.

## The risk assessment

The deliverable auditors expect is a documented assessment of potential
elemental sources — drug substance, excipients, water, container/closure, and
equipment — concluding whether routine testing is needed or control is otherwise
assured.

> Tip: instrumental methods (ICP-OES, ICP-MS) per USP 233 are the modern way to
> measure. But measurement is the last resort — a sound risk assessment often
> shows testing isn't routinely required.
