# Nitrosamine Impurity Control

Since 2018, nitrosamine contamination has driven recalls across sartans,
metformin, ranitidine and more. Because many nitrosamines are probable human
carcinogens, acceptable intake (AI) limits sit in the nanogram-per-day range — a
serious analytical and control challenge.

## Where nitrosamines come from

- Secondary/tertiary amines reacting with nitrosating agents during synthesis.
- Contaminated raw materials, recovered solvents, and catalysts.
- Cross-contamination from shared equipment or supplier processes.
- Degradation during storage, sometimes driven by excipients.

## The regulatory three-step approach

1. **Risk assessment** — evaluate every product and process for nitrosamine
   potential, documented and product-specific.
2. **Confirmatory testing** — where risk is identified, test with validated,
   highly sensitive methods.
3. **Risk mitigation** — change the process, supplier, or controls; update the
   marketing authorisation as needed.

## Acceptable intake and the analytical bar

Limits are set from compound-specific AI values (or a class-specific limit where
data is sparse). Detecting at these levels typically demands LC-MS/MS or GC-MS/MS
with sample preparation designed to avoid *in-situ* nitrosamine formation during
analysis — a real, documented artifact.

## Root-cause source matrix

| Source category | Typical mechanism | Control lever |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Synthesis route | Amine + nitrosating agent (e.g. nitrite) | Redesign route; avoid nitrite; quench |
| Raw materials / reagents | Nitrite/nitrate contamination, recovered solvents | Supplier specs, limit nitrite |
| Recovered/recycled solvents & catalysts | Carry-over of amines/nitrosating species | Control recovery; fresh solvent |
| Shared equipment | Cross-contamination between products | Cleaning validation, dedication |
| Packaging / printing | Nitrocellulose lidding, certain inks | Material selection |
| Degradation in product | Amine API + nitrite from excipients | Excipient nitrite limits, antioxidants, pH |

## Nitrosamine risk-assessment template

1. **Inventory** every API, intermediate, reagent, solvent, excipient, and contact
   material for amine + nitrosating potential.
2. **Score likelihood** of formation/contamination per the matrix above.
3. **Identify** the specific nitrosamine(s) of concern (API-related NDSRIs vs
   small-molecule nitrosamines).
4. **Decide** — no risk (document) / potential risk (confirmatory test).
5. **Confirmatory test** with a validated, sensitive method where risk exists.
6. **Mitigate & update** the marketing authorization; re-assess on any change.

## Setting AI limits when data is incomplete

- **Compound-specific AI** from carcinogenic-potency data (TD50) when available.
- **Read-across** from a structurally similar nitrosamine with data.
- **Carcinogenic Potency Categorization Approach (CPCA)** — assigns an AI based on
  structural features (number/position of α-hydrogens) when no compound data
  exists; increasingly the default for novel NDSRIs.
- **Enhanced Ames test** can reclassify a non-mutagenic nitrosamine to an ICH
  Q3A/B-style limit, removing it from the ng/day regime entirely.

Document which approach you used and why — regulators expect a justified, tiered
rationale, not a single blanket limit.

> Practitioner tip: beware *in-situ* formation during analysis. Nitrosamines can
> be created in the sample vial from residual amine + nitrite under acidic
> conditions, producing false positives. Validate sample prep with an inhibitor
> (e.g. added amine-trap/antioxidant) to prove you're measuring the product, not
> your method.
