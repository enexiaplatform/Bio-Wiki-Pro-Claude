# Antimicrobial Effectiveness Testing (USP 51)

Multi-dose and many non-sterile aqueous products contain a **preservative system**
to stop microbes that get in during use. Antimicrobial Effectiveness Testing (AET,
USP &lt;51&gt;) proves that system actually works over the product's life.

## How the test works

The product is **inoculated** with a panel of challenge organisms (bacteria,
yeast, mould — e.g. *S. aureus*, *P. aeruginosa*, *E. coli*, *C. albicans*,
*A. brasiliensis*). At defined time points (e.g. 7, 14, 28 days) you enumerate
survivors and calculate the **log reduction**.

## Acceptance criteria by category

USP &lt;51&gt; defines product **categories** (e.g. injections, topicals, oral
liquids) each with its own required log reductions and "no increase" criteria at
set intervals. Stricter products (parenteral) demand faster, deeper kill.

## When it's needed

- During **development** to design the preservative system.
- On **stability** (the preservative can degrade — efficacy at end of shelf life
  matters).
- After **formulation changes** that could affect preservation.

## Why it matters

Under-preserved products can grow contaminants in the patient's hands; over-
preservation raises toxicity. AET finds the safe, effective minimum — and confirms
it lasts.

> Tip: pair AET with the lowest effective preservative level, and re-confirm
> efficacy at the end of shelf life, not just at release.
