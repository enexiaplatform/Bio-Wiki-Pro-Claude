# Health-Based Exposure Limits (HBEL / PDE)

Health-Based Exposure Limits put cleaning and cross-contamination control on a
toxicological footing. The EMA guideline made HBELs (expressed as a Permitted
Daily Exposure, PDE) the expected basis for shared-facility limits — superseding
the historical "1/1000 of the minimum dose" and "10 ppm" rules of thumb.

## Why HBEL replaced the old rules

The traditional defaults were arbitrary: they ignored a compound's actual
pharmacology and toxicology. An HBEL is derived from real data (NOAEL/LOAEL,
adjustment factors), giving a defensible, science-based daily exposure a patient
can tolerate with no adverse effect.

## From PDE to a cleaning limit (MACO)

The PDE feeds the **Maximum Allowable Carryover (MACO)** calculation, which —
combined with batch size, shared equipment surface area, and the smallest daily
dose of the next product — yields the surface residue limit your cleaning method
must reliably meet (e.g. µg/cm² on a swab).

## Where it matters most

- **Multi-product facilities** sharing equipment or air-handling.
- **Highly hazardous compounds** (potent, sensitizing, cytotoxic), where
  dedicated facilities may be required if the HBEL is very low.

## Deriving the PDE

The EMA/ICH Q3C-style formula:

```
PDE = (NOAEL × body weight) / (F1 × F2 × F3 × F4 × F5)
```

- **NOAEL** — no-observed-adverse-effect level (mg/kg/day) from the most relevant
  study.
- **F1** — interspecies extrapolation (e.g. 5 for rat→human).
- **F2** — interindividual variability (typically 10).
- **F3** — study duration adjustment.
- **F4** — severity of effect (e.g. genotoxicity, teratogenicity).
- **F5** — uncertainty if a LOAEL is used instead of a NOAEL.

A toxicologist (not QC) assigns these — but QC must understand the inputs to use
the limit sensibly.

## Worked PDE → MACO → swab limit

Given: PDE_A = 0.10 mg/day; next product B has min batch size 50 kg
(50,000,000 mg) and max daily dose 1,000 mg; shared product-contact area =
100,000 cm²; swab area = 25 cm²; recovery = 90%.

1. **MACO** = (0.10 × 50,000,000) / 1,000 = **5,000 mg** of A in a batch of B.
2. **Per-area limit** = 5,000 mg / 100,000 cm² = **0.05 mg/cm² = 50 µg/cm²**.
3. **Per-swab target** = 50 µg/cm² × 25 cm² = 1,250 µg, then **÷ 0.90 recovery**
   → the lab must reliably quantify ~**1,389 µg** per swab (the analytical method's
   LOQ must sit well below this).

## HBEL in the Contamination Control Strategy

The HBEL is the quantitative anchor for cross-contamination risk in a
multi-product CCS: it sets cleaning limits, justifies campaign/changeover
strategy, and flags when a compound is so hazardous that shared facilities can't
be justified at all. EMA also expects an HBEL even for products historically
considered "low risk."

## Compounds without enough toxicology data

When data are insufficient to derive a robust PDE, use a conservative default
(e.g. a low generic limit) **and** flag it for a proper toxicological assessment —
don't quietly fall back to the old 1/1000 rule. For genotoxic/highly potent
compounds, default thresholds (like the TTC) and specialist input apply.

> Practitioner tip: keep the toxicologist's HBEL monograph current and version-
> controlled. A cleaning limit is only as defensible as the PDE behind it — and
> inspectors increasingly ask to see that derivation, not just the swab result.
