# Data Integrity — Audit Trail Review (Deep Dive)

ALCOA+ defines the principles; the hard part is operationalising **audit trail
review** so data integrity is actually assured day to day.

## Risk-based review

Not every audit trail needs the same scrutiny. Tier systems by impact:
chromatography integration changes and result reprocessing are high-risk and
warrant routine review; low-risk metadata can be reviewed by exception.

## What reviewers look for

- Reprocessed or re-integrated results
- Aborted/incomplete runs and "test" injections
- Changes to methods, sequences, or clocks
- Disabled audit trails or shared logins

## Who and how often

Review by someone independent of the analyst, at a defined frequency tied to
risk (e.g. per batch for high-risk systems). Document the review — "we review
audit trails" without records is a finding.

## A risk matrix for common QC systems

| System | DI risk | Review frequency |
| --- | --- | --- |
| HPLC/GC (CDS) | **High** — integration, reprocessing, sequence edits | Every batch / every result |
| Dissolution, UV with reprocessing | **High–Medium** | Per batch |
| Balance with printout + LIMS | **Medium** | Periodic + per exception |
| pH meter, standalone instruments | **Low–Medium** | Periodic (e.g. monthly) |
| Infrastructure (OS clock, AD) | **Low** | By exception / IT change control |

Tier the *frequency* and *depth* of review to the risk — don't pretend a pH
meter deserves the same scrutiny as a CDS, and don't under-review the CDS.

## Audit-trail review SOP — the skeleton

1. **Scope & trigger** — which systems, tied to batch release vs periodic.
2. **Reviewer** — independent of the data generator; trained and named.
3. **What to examine** — result reprocessing, deleted/aborted runs, "test"
   injections, method/sequence/clock changes, failed logins, disabled trails.
4. **Decision** — accept / investigate; link any anomaly to a deviation.
5. **Record** — checklist signed and dated; retained with the batch record.
6. **Escalation** — what constitutes a data-integrity event and who is notified.

## What inspectors actually cite

Recurring FDA warning-letter themes (paraphrased): shared/generic logins so
actions can't be attributed; audit trails switched off or never reviewed;
"trial" injections used to dial in a passing result; analysts with admin rights
able to delete data; review limited to the result printout while the metadata
goes unexamined.

> Practitioner tip: the fastest credibility win in an inspection is producing a
> *signed, dated audit-trail review record* on demand. Many sites "do" review but
> can't prove it — and to a regulator, undocumented is undone.
