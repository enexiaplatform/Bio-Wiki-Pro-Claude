# Bioburden & Microbial Limits (USP &lt;61&gt;/&lt;62&gt;)

For non-sterile pharmaceutical products, "not sterile" does not mean
"microbiology does not matter." Microbial limits testing checks whether the
product carries an acceptable microbial population and whether specified
organisms are absent where the product, route, and patient population require it.

The practical goal is release confidence: can this product be used as intended
without an unacceptable microbiological risk?

## The two compendial tests

USP &lt;61&gt; and USP &lt;62&gt; answer different questions.

| Chapter | Question answered | Typical output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| USP &lt;61&gt; Microbial Enumeration | How many recoverable bacteria and fungi are present? | TAMC and TYMC as CFU/g or CFU/mL |
| USP &lt;62&gt; Tests for Specified Microorganisms | Are named organisms absent or present after enrichment/selective testing? | Present/absent for organisms such as *E. coli*, *S. aureus*, *P. aeruginosa*, *Salmonella*, bile-tolerant Gram-negative bacteria, *Candida albicans*, or *Clostridia* where applicable |
| USP &lt;1111&gt; Acceptance Criteria | What limits apply to this dosage form and route? | Count limits and specified-organism expectations |

Do not treat &lt;61&gt; and &lt;62&gt; as interchangeable. A product can meet the total
count limit and still fail because a relevant specified or objectionable
organism is present.

## What gets counted

USP &lt;61&gt; reports:

- **TAMC** - total aerobic microbial count, usually bacterial growth recovered
  under aerobic conditions.
- **TYMC** - total combined yeasts and moulds count.

Results are normally expressed per gram or per millilitre after accounting for
sample amount, dilution, plated volume, and the validated method. If duplicate
plates are used, the result should be calculated according to the approved
procedure rather than by a casual "looks about right" estimate.

## Choosing the test method

The method must fit the product matrix.

- **Membrane filtration** is useful when the product can be filtered and the
  filter can be rinsed to remove antimicrobial activity.
- **Plate count methods** are common for products that can be dispersed or
  diluted without excessive inhibition.
- **Most probable number (MPN)** may be used for low-count or difficult
  matrices, but it is less precise and should be justified.

The best method is not the easiest method. It is the method that recovers low
numbers of organisms reliably in the presence of the product.

## Method suitability

Method suitability is the proof that the product does not hide microorganisms
from the test. Preservatives, surfactants, low pH, high osmolarity, high alcohol,
viscosity, insoluble powders, and antimicrobial raw materials can all suppress
recovery.

A strong suitability package shows:

- the exact sample preparation, neutralizer, diluent, rinse, and incubation
  conditions;
- challenge organisms appropriate to the chapter;
- low inoculum recovery, commonly in the 10-100 CFU range;
- recovery from the product preparation, not only from clean diluent;
- negative controls showing no extrinsic laboratory contamination;
- clear actions if recovery is poor, such as more dilution, neutralization,
  additional rinsing, or a different method.

If suitability fails, the routine test result is not reliable. A "no growth"
result from an inhibitory product may only mean the method was neutralized by the
product.

## Reading results correctly

When reviewing a result, ask five questions:

1. Was the method suitable for this product and strength?
2. Were controls valid, including negative controls and media growth promotion?
3. Were colony counts in the countable range defined by the procedure?
4. Were dilution factors, units, and rounding rules applied correctly?
5. Does the result meet both the count limit and the specified-organism
   requirement?

Do not review TAMC/TYMC in isolation. Identity matters. A low total count may
still be unacceptable if the recovered organism is product-relevant or patient
relevant.

## Specified vs objectionable organisms

USP &lt;62&gt; names organisms to test for under defined conditions. "Objectionable"
is broader: it is a risk decision based on the organism, the product, the route
of administration, the population using it, and whether the organism can grow in
or degrade the product.

Examples:

- A Gram-negative water organism in an oral aqueous product may indicate a water
  system or preservative-control issue.
- *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* is especially concerning for products used near
  damaged skin, eyes, ears, or mucosa.
- *Burkholderia cepacia* complex may be objectionable for aqueous products and
  vulnerable patient populations even when not listed in a simple release
  checklist.

For a deeper decision framework, use the lesson
[Objectionable Organisms](/library/objectionable-organisms).

## Common failure patterns

- Reusing an old suitability study after a formula, preservative, supplier, or
  manufacturing change.
- Reporting "less than" values without confirming the detection limit and units.
- Treating a contaminated negative control as a minor issue instead of an invalid
  run.
- Ignoring repeated low-level recoveries because each individual result is below
  the limit.
- Failing to identify isolates when the organism could be objectionable.
- Assuming USP &lt;61&gt; counts replace USP &lt;62&gt; specified-organism testing.

## What good documentation looks like

A complete record should let QA reconstruct the test without guessing:

- product, lot, sample amount, dilution scheme, neutralizer, and method;
- media lots and growth-promotion status;
- incubation conditions and read dates;
- raw plate counts, calculation, units, and final result;
- controls and suitability reference;
- isolate identification or rationale when required;
- acceptance criteria from the product specification or USP &lt;1111&gt;;
- investigation reference for any excursion or invalid control.

## Why it matters

Microbial limits testing protects patients, but it also protects the site from
false confidence. A clean-looking result is only meaningful when the method can
recover organisms, the calculation is correct, and the organism significance is
understood.

The best microbiology review mindset is simple: count, identify where needed,
interpret by product risk, and trend the signal over time.
