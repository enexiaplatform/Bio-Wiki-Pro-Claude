# Sterility Testing - Foundations

Sterility testing is the compendial growth-based test used for products that are
required to be sterile. USP &lt;71&gt; and Ph. Eur. 2.6.1 describe the core approach:
expose a defined sample to growth media, incubate under defined conditions, and
observe for evidence of microbial growth.

The test is important, but it has limits. A passing result means no growth was
detected in the sample under the test conditions. It does not prove that every unit
in the batch is sterile. Sterility assurance comes from the manufacturing process:
sterilization, aseptic control, environmental monitoring, personnel control,
container closure integrity, and contamination control strategy. The test is a
release confirmation, not the source of sterility.

## The two compendial methods

| Method | When it fits | Key controls |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Membrane filtration | Filterable liquids, many injectables, ophthalmics, and products where the sample can pass through a microbial-retentive filter | Filter integrity, aseptic filtration setup, rinse strategy, recovery from filter |
| Direct inoculation | Products that cannot be filtered, small-volume products, viscous or device-related samples where filtration is unsuitable | Product-to-media ratio, neutralization of antimicrobial activity, adequate contact with media |

Membrane filtration is generally preferred when the product can be filtered because
it allows the product to be rinsed away from the membrane, reducing product
interference. Direct inoculation is useful when filtration is impractical, but the
product remains in the medium and may inhibit growth if suitability is not proven.

practice matching product scenarios to the likely method.

## Media and incubation

The compendial test uses two media to support a broad range of microorganisms.

| Medium | Main purpose | Typical incubation condition |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fluid thioglycollate medium (FTM) | Anaerobic and aerobic bacteria | 30-35 C |
| Soybean-casein digest medium (SCDM / TSB) | Fungi and aerobic bacteria | 20-25 C |

The minimum incubation period is **14 days**. During incubation, containers are
examined for visible growth such as turbidity, pellicle, sediment, or other signs
consistent with microbial proliferation.

## Method suitability

Method suitability, often called bacteriostasis/fungistasis testing, proves the
method can recover microorganisms if they are present. This is essential because a
product can inhibit microbial growth through preservative, antibiotic, pH, osmotic
strength, viscosity, formulation, or residual disinfectant effects.

A practical suitability package answers:

- Can low levels of challenge organisms grow in the presence of the product?
- Is the rinse or neutralization strategy adequate?
- Does the sample preparation damage or remove organisms?
- Are both media able to support growth after contact with the product?
- Does the method remain suitable after formulation, container, or process change?

Do not treat suitability as a one-time box. If product composition, test volume,
filtration setup, rinse fluid, closure system, or antimicrobial properties change,
the suitability rationale may need to be reassessed.

## Controls that protect the test

Sterility testing is performed under aseptic conditions, so the laboratory can
accidentally contaminate the test. Good controls include:

- qualified clean area or isolator for sterility testing;
- trained analysts and aseptic technique qualification;
- sterile, growth-supporting media with growth promotion evidence;
- negative controls to detect test contamination;
- controlled sample handling and decontamination of container exteriors;
- environmental monitoring of the test environment;
- documented reconciliation of units tested, media, filters, and observations;
- clear rules for reading, subculture if applicable, and result reporting.

The test should be designed so that a positive result is meaningful and a negative
result is trustworthy.

## Reading and interpretation

If no growth is observed after the required incubation, the sample passes the
compendial test under the conditions used. If growth is observed, the result is a
serious quality signal. It may indicate product contamination, testing
contamination, or another laboratory control failure, but it cannot be dismissed
without evidence.

Investigation should consider:

- organism identity and whether it matches facility, personnel, or product flora;
- growth pattern, timing, and which medium was positive;
- sterility test environment and analyst activities;
- negative controls and media controls;
- sample container condition and decontamination;
- batch manufacturing, filling, sterilization, and EM data;
- related batches, campaigns, and retain samples where relevant.

Invalidating a sterility test result requires strong evidence of laboratory error
or test contamination. A vague assumption that "it was probably the lab" is not
enough.

## Common pitfalls

- Running the compendial method before proving suitability for the product.
- Choosing direct inoculation when membrane filtration is feasible and better
  controls product inhibition.
- Treating a 14-day no-growth result as proof that the whole batch is sterile.
- Failing to identify organisms recovered from a positive test.
- Missing links between sterility test results, EM trends, media fills, and batch
  release decisions.
- Repeating the test to seek a passing result without a scientifically justified
  invalidation.

## Practical takeaway

Sterility testing is a high-stakes release test. To make it defensible, the site
needs the right compendial method, proven method suitability, controlled aseptic
execution, reliable media and incubation, and a disciplined investigation process
for any positive result.

The best reviewers do not ask only "did it pass?" They ask whether the method was
suitable, the test was protected from contamination, the sample was representative
for the approved procedure, and the result fits the broader sterility assurance
picture.
