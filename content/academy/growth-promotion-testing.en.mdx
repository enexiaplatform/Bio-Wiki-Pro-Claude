# Growth Promotion Testing (GPT)

Growth Promotion Testing proves that a culture media lot can recover
microorganisms before the lot is used for GMP microbiology results. Without GPT,
a "no growth" result is not fully interpretable. The sample may be clean, or the
medium may simply be unable to support growth.

## What GPT checks

GPT checks the media lot, not the product. The lab challenges the medium with a
small number of suitable organisms and confirms that growth occurs as expected.

Typical controls include:

- media lot identity, preparation, storage, and expiry;
- organism strain identity and passage control;
- low inoculum challenge, often not more than 100 CFU;
- incubation time, temperature, and atmosphere;
- reference or previously approved media lot where required;
- predefined acceptance criteria.

## Solid, liquid, and selective media

For **solid nonselective media**, recovery is commonly assessed by comparing
colony recovery with a reference or previously approved lot. A common compendial
concept is that recovery should be within a factor of two of the comparator or
calculated value, depending on the method.

For **liquid media**, the result is usually visible growth within the specified
time, comparable to a previously approved lot where applicable.

For **selective media**, GPT has two jobs:

- recover the target organism;
- inhibit organisms the medium is meant to suppress.

A selective medium that grows everything is not selective. A selective medium
that suppresses the target is not suitable.

## GPT vs method suitability

GPT asks whether the medium can support growth. Method suitability asks whether
the test can recover organisms in the presence of the product. A media lot can
pass GPT but still fail suitability if the product is antimicrobial or otherwise
inhibitory.

## Common pitfalls

- Using a media lot before GPT release.
- Challenging with too many organisms, which hides weak recovery.
- Forgetting selective-media inhibition testing.
- Losing strain traceability or passage control.
- Overheating media during preparation.
- Ignoring plate dehydration, expiry, or storage excursions.
- Retesting a failed GPT without investigating why it failed.

## What good looks like

A strong media-control program quarantines each media lot until GPT passes,
defines the organism panel and acceptance criteria per medium, traces media lots
to the tests that used them, and investigates GPT failures for product impact
when a lot was already used.
