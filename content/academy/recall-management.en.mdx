# Product Recall Management

A recall removes a defective or potentially harmful product from the market. A
mature quality system can execute one **fast and completely** — and proves it can
via periodic **mock recalls**.

## Recall classes (FDA framing)

- **Class I** — reasonable probability of serious harm or death.
- **Class II** — temporary or medically reversible harm; low probability of
  serious harm.
- **Class III** — unlikely to cause harm but violates regulations.

The class drives urgency, depth (how far down the distribution chain), and
communication.

## What a recall procedure needs

- **Traceability** — distribution records that let you identify every customer who
  received the affected batch(es), to the required level (wholesaler, pharmacy,
  patient where relevant).
- **Reconciliation** — quantity produced vs distributed vs returned/accounted for.
- **Regulator notification** within required timelines.
- **Defined roles** (recall coordinator, decision committee) and a communication
  plan.

## Mock recalls and effectiveness

Run **mock recalls** periodically to test traceability and speed (e.g. "can we
account for 100% of batch X within N hours?"). After a real recall, an
**effectiveness check** confirms the recalled product was actually retrieved.

## Recall decision tree

1. **Signal** — complaint, OOS, stability failure, deviation, or regulator query.
2. **Health-hazard evaluation** — assess risk to patients (severity ×
   probability × population). This drives the class.
3. **Decide scope** — which batches, which markets, how deep (wholesaler /
   pharmacy / patient).
4. **Classify** (I/II/III) and set timelines.
5. **Notify** — regulators within required windows; consignees with clear
   instructions.
6. **Retrieve & reconcile** — track returns against distributed quantity.
7. **Effectiveness check** — confirm retrieval; close with CAPA and trend.

The decision to recall (vs other field action) is a documented quality decision,
usually by a recall committee — not an individual under pressure.

## Mock-recall protocol template

- **Objective** — e.g. account for 100% of a chosen batch within a target time.
- **Scope** — pick a real batch with realistic multi-tier distribution.
- **Method** — trace from batch record → distribution records → consignees;
  reconcile quantities.
- **Acceptance** — % accounted for and time taken vs target (e.g. ≥99% within
  24–48 h).
- **Output** — gaps found (e.g. slow consignee data), CAPA, and a re-test date.

Run mock recalls at a defined frequency (often annually) and after major
distribution or system changes.

## How recalls connect to the QMS

A recall is rarely standalone: it usually originates in a **complaint**, an
**OOS/stability** failure, or a **deviation**, and it always ends in **CAPA** and
trending. The recall procedure should reference these processes so the root cause
is fixed — not just the market cleared. Recurring recalls of the same type are a
serious quality-system signal in management review.

> Practitioner tip: the weak link is almost always downstream distribution data,
> not your own records. Use mock recalls specifically to test how fast your
> distributors and 3PLs can give you consignee lists — that's where the real clock
> is lost in a Class I event.
