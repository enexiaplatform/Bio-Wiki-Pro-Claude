# Process Validation - Lifecycle Stages, PPQ, and CPV

Process validation is the documented evidence that a manufacturing process can
consistently deliver product meeting its predefined quality criteria. The modern
view is lifecycle-based: design the process, qualify it at commercial scale, and
keep verifying that it remains in control during routine production.

FDA's 2011 process validation guidance defines validation as the collection and
evaluation of data from process design through commercial production to establish
scientific evidence that the process can consistently deliver quality product. EU
GMP Annex 15 uses the same lifecycle logic: critical aspects of facilities,
equipment, utilities, and processes should be controlled through qualification
and validation over the life cycle of the product and process.

The practical message is simple: validation is not "three batches and done." It
is a data system that links development knowledge, PPQ evidence, routine process
monitoring, change control, and quality review.

## The Three-Stage Lifecycle

| Stage | Purpose | Main evidence | Common failure mode |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Stage 1 - Process Design | Define the commercial manufacturing process using development, scale-up, prior knowledge, and risk assessment. | CQAs, CPPs, CMAs, unit operation understanding, control strategy, process flow, batch record draft. | Treating development work as informal and not translating it into a control strategy. |
| Stage 2 - Process Qualification | Demonstrate that the designed process can reproducibly perform at commercial scale. | Qualified facility/equipment/utilities, PPQ protocol, PPQ batches, approved report. | Running three batches without proving why the design, acceptance criteria, sampling, and statistics are adequate. |
| Stage 3 - Continued Process Verification | Maintain ongoing assurance that the process remains in a state of control. | CPV plan, routine trend review, statistical process monitoring, APR/PQR links, deviation/CAPA/change review. | Filing the PPQ report and stopping active process learning. |

The stages overlap. Stage 1 knowledge feeds Stage 2 acceptance criteria. Stage 2
results define what must be monitored in Stage 3. Stage 3 trends can trigger
process improvement, revalidation, or a return to Stage 1 design work.

## Stage 1 - Process Design

Stage 1 is where the commercial process is defined. The strongest Stage 1
packages do not merely list parameters. They explain why those parameters matter
and how the process will be controlled.

Key outputs include:

- product quality target and critical quality attributes (CQAs);
- critical material attributes (CMAs) and supplier/material risks;
- process flow diagram and unit operation sequence;
- process parameter ranges and critical process parameters (CPPs);
- scale-up rationale and engineering batch knowledge;
- risk assessments linking CPPs/CMAs to CQAs;
- process control strategy and in-process controls;
- analytical method readiness for release and in-process testing;
- hold times, cleaning, equipment, utilities, and environmental requirements;
- draft master batch record and sampling strategy;
- known failure modes and response plans.

Stage 1 should make the commercial control strategy visible. If the team cannot
explain which inputs and parameters protect each CQA, Stage 2 PPQ becomes an
expensive demonstration with weak scientific meaning.

## From CQA to CPP to Control Strategy

The core logic is:

1. **CQA**: What product attribute must be controlled for safety, efficacy, or
   quality?
2. **Risk link**: Which material attributes or process parameters can affect it?
3. **CPP or key parameter**: Which variables need defined limits, controls, or
   heightened monitoring?
4. **Control**: How is the variable prevented, detected, corrected, or trended?
5. **Evidence**: What data show the control works?

Example:

| CQA | Possible drivers | Controls | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Blend uniformity | Mixing time, fill level, API particle size, equipment speed | Defined range, blend sampling, material controls | Development studies, scale-up data, PPQ blend results |
| Sterility assurance | Bioburden, filtration, aseptic interventions, hold time | Validated sterilization/filtration, APS, EM, intervention controls | Validation data, media fills, PPQ monitoring |
| Impurity profile | Reaction temperature, pH, reagent quality, hold time | Parameter limits, IPCs, material specifications | DoE, engineering batches, PPQ impurity trends |
| Dissolution | Granulation moisture, compression force, coating parameters | IPCs, equipment settings, release testing | Development link, PPQ and stability results |

This traceability is what separates lifecycle validation from paperwork.

## Stage 2 - Process Qualification

Stage 2 evaluates whether the process design can support reproducible commercial
manufacturing. It has two connected parts:

1. **Facility, utility, and equipment qualification**: the manufacturing system is
   fit for intended use.
2. **Process Performance Qualification (PPQ)**: the process, operated by trained
   personnel under routine controls, can produce conforming product.

Before PPQ, confirm readiness:

- equipment, utilities, automation, and computerized systems are qualified;
- analytical methods are validated or qualified for intended use;
- raw material and component controls are approved;
- cleaning validation or cleaning verification strategy is ready;
- operators are trained on the commercial batch record;
- process parameters and ranges are finalized;
- sampling locations, sample sizes, and statistical rationale are approved;
- deviations and unplanned events will be handled under GMP;
- quality unit approval is in place.

PPQ is not a ceremonial set of batches. It is the formal test of the commercial
control strategy.

## PPQ Protocol Design

A PPQ protocol should define, before execution:

- objective and scope;
- product, strength, site, line, equipment train, and batch size;
- number of batches and scientific rationale;
- process flow and unit operations included;
- CPPs, key process parameters, IPCs, and CQAs to monitor;
- sampling plan and rationale, including locations and timing;
- acceptance criteria and statistical methods;
- treatment of intra-batch and inter-batch variability;
- deviation handling and impact assessment rules;
- data integrity expectations and raw-data review;
- quality unit responsibilities and approval points;
- batch disposition strategy, including whether concurrent release is proposed.

The number of PPQ batches should be justified by process knowledge, risk,
variability, product complexity, and prior evidence. "Three" is common, but it is
not a scientific argument by itself.

## PPQ Execution and Report

During PPQ, execute the approved commercial process. Do not quietly tune the
process to make the batches pass. If a parameter range, sampling location, or
control strategy needs to change during PPQ, document and assess the change.

The final PPQ report should:

- reconcile all protocol requirements and raw data;
- summarize batch-by-batch and pooled results;
- evaluate deviations, interventions, alarms, and excursions;
- assess CPP/CQA trends and variability;
- compare results to prior development and engineering data;
- conclude whether the process is qualified;
- define any residual risks or CAPA;
- define the Stage 3 CPV monitoring plan or confirm it remains appropriate;
- include quality unit review and approval.

Approval should be based on the total evidence package, not only whether final
release tests pass.

## Stage 3 - Continued Process Verification

Stage 3 is the living part of validation. FDA describes the goal as continual
assurance that the process remains in a state of control during commercial
manufacture. A system for detecting unplanned departures from the designed
process is essential.

A CPV plan should define:

- which parameters and attributes are trended;
- data sources, frequency, and ownership;
- statistical tools and alert/action logic;
- intra-batch and inter-batch variability review;
- links to deviations, OOS/OOT, complaints, stability, and APR/PQR;
- review frequency for high-risk and lower-risk products;
- triggers for CAPA, change control, or revalidation;
- how learnings feed process improvement.

Examples of CPV signals:

- drift in a CPP even while release results remain in specification;
- rising IPC variability between operators, shifts, or equipment trains;
- one site or line trending differently from another;
- yield loss, rework, alarms, or manual intervention increases;
- stability or complaint data suggesting process-related quality change;
- repeated minor deviations around the same unit operation.

Stage 3 is where a process proves it is still validated, not where validation is
archived.

## Continued vs Continuous Process Verification

The terms are easy to mix up.

| Term | Practical meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Continued process verification | FDA Stage 3 ongoing assurance during routine commercial production. It applies after PPQ and throughout the product lifecycle. |
| Continuous process verification | An alternative or enhanced validation approach discussed in ICH Q8/Q9/Q10 contexts, using continuous monitoring and evaluation of process performance. |

Both rely on process understanding and data. The key is to define which approach
your site is using and how the data support the validated state.

## What Makes a Process "Validated"

A process is not validated because a protocol exists. It is validated when the
evidence shows that the defined process, operated within the approved control
strategy, can consistently produce product meeting its quality criteria.

The evidence should connect:

- product and process understanding;
- facility, utility, and equipment qualification;
- material controls and supplier qualification;
- validated or qualified analytical methods;
- batch record and operator controls;
- PPQ data and deviation conclusions;
- stability and microbiological evidence where relevant;
- routine CPV trends;
- change-control and CAPA effectiveness.

This connection matters because a weak link can undermine the whole validation
claim. A process with strong PPQ batches but uncontrolled raw material
variability is not robust. A process with validated equipment but no CPV is not
demonstrably maintained.

## Revalidation and Lifecycle Triggers

Revalidation should be based on risk and impact, not on a calendar alone.
Potential triggers include:

- formulation, strength, batch size, or manufacturing site change;
- equipment train, utility, automation, or software change;
- material supplier, grade, or specification change;
- process parameter range or control strategy change;
- cleaning, sterilization, filtration, or hold-time change;
- repeated deviations, OOS/OOT, complaints, or adverse CPV trend;
- major maintenance or facility modification;
- regulatory commitment or inspection finding;
- technology transfer or scale change.

Some changes require full PPQ. Others may require focused qualification,
engineering confirmation, enhanced CPV, or documented no-impact rationale. The
decision should be captured in change control.

## Decision Matrix

| Situation | Likely validation decision | Rationale |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Stage 1 knowledge clearly links CPPs/CMAs to CQAs and controls. | Proceed to PPQ readiness review. | The control strategy is scientifically defined. |
| PPQ batches pass release but show unexplained CPP drift. | Investigate before final qualification conclusion. | Final testing alone may hide process weakness. |
| PPQ deviation occurs but root cause and product impact are understood. | Continue, repeat, or add targeted evidence based on risk. | Not every deviation invalidates PPQ, but every deviation needs impact assessment. |
| Stage 3 shows a sustained trend toward an alert limit. | Open CPV review and CAPA before OOS occurs. | CPV should detect loss of control early. |
| Change affects a CPP range or CQA control. | Assess revalidation scope in change control. | Prior PPQ may no longer represent the new process. |
| Commercial batches remain stable for years with strong CPV evidence. | Maintain validated state and consider improvement opportunities. | Lifecycle validation supports continual improvement. |

## Documentation Checklist

Keep the validation file inspectable with:

- validation master plan or equivalent strategy;
- process flow diagram and unit operation map;
- CQA/CPP/CMA rationale and risk assessment;
- development, scale-up, and prior-knowledge summary;
- control strategy and IPC rationale;
- equipment, utility, facility, and automation qualification status;
- analytical method validation or qualification status;
- PPQ protocol, sampling plan, and statistical rationale;
- PPQ executed batch records and raw data;
- deviation, alarm, intervention, and excursion assessments;
- PPQ report and quality unit approval;
- CPV plan and routine trend reports;
- APR/PQR linkage;
- change-control and revalidation assessments;
- CAPA effectiveness checks where validation weaknesses were found.

## Common GMP Findings

Inspectors often challenge process validation when:

- the site treats PPQ as three successful release tests;
- Stage 1 development knowledge is not traceable to the control strategy;
- CPPs are listed without explaining their relationship to CQAs;
- sampling plans have no risk or statistical rationale;
- equipment or analytical readiness is incomplete before PPQ;
- PPQ deviations are closed without validation impact assessment;
- Stage 3 CPV monitors too few parameters or only final release tests;
- trends are reviewed annually but not acted on when early signals appear;
- change controls do not assess validation impact;
- revalidation decisions are based on habit rather than risk.

## Practical Takeaway

Process validation is a lifecycle promise:

1. We understand the process well enough to design it.
2. We can run it at commercial scale under routine controls.
3. We keep watching the right signals so the validated state remains true.

If the validation report cannot show those three things, it is not yet strong
evidence. It is only a document.
