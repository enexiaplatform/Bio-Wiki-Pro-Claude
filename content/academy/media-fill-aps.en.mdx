# Media Fill & Aseptic Process Simulation

An Aseptic Process Simulation (APS), or media fill, substitutes sterile growth
medium for product to demonstrate that the aseptic process can reliably produce
a sterile product. EU GMP Annex 1 (2022) treats it as a cornerstone of the
Contamination Control Strategy.

## Acceptance criteria (Annex 1)

- Fewer than 5,000 units: **no contaminated units** should be detected.
- 5,000 to 10,000 units: one contaminated unit triggers investigation and a
  consideration of a repeat; two triggers revalidation after investigation.
- More than 10,000 units: one contaminated unit triggers investigation; two
  trigger revalidation.

## Designing for worst case

A credible APS reproduces the worst-case conditions the process actually faces:
maximum interventions, shift changes, maximum number of operators, the longest
permitted run time, and the most challenging container/closure.

> to check worst-case design, intervention coverage, incubation, EM linkage, and disposition blockers.

## The intervention matrix

Every intervention the process *permits* must appear in the APS, classified and
counted:

- **Inherent (routine)** — done every batch (e.g. environmental monitoring,
  weight checks, component replenishment). Include at routine frequency.
- **Corrective (non-routine)** — performed to fix a problem (clearing a jam,
  replacing a stopper bowl, line stoppage). Include each at least once, designed
  to the worst-case posture and personnel.

List each, when it occurs, who performs it, and its aseptic risk. Any intervention
not simulated is, in effect, not qualified — and is the first thing an inspector
checks after a real-line intervention.

## Run duration and operator load

Simulate the **maximum permitted run time** (not a short demo) so operator
fatigue, line dynamics, and EM over a full shift are challenged. Include shift
changes and breaks, and the **maximum number of operators** allowed in the room.
Justify the filled-unit count statistically — enough to make "no growth" a
meaningful result against your acceptance table.

## Incubation and read

Fill, stopper, and incubate units (commonly two stages, e.g. ~20–25 °C then
~30–35 °C, ~7 days each / 14 total) to recover both molds/yeasts and bacteria.
Invert/agitate so medium contacts all closure surfaces. Pre-/post-incubation
counts confirm medium growth-promotion. Every unit is inspected; integrity-failed
units are reconciled, not silently discarded.

## Worked failure investigation

*Event:* 1 contaminated unit in a 9,500-unit fill → investigation + repeat (per
table). **Investigation:** identify the isolate (e.g. a *Micrococcus* skin flora);
review video and intervention log for that time window; check the gowning
qualification, EM, and personnel monitoring of operators present; map the unit's
position to a specific intervention. **Finding:** a corrective intervention done
with a near-touch over the open vial. **Action:** redesign the intervention,
retrain to the new method, and pass two consecutive repeat APS before resuming.

> Practitioner tip: film your media fills. When a unit grows, the intervention
> video is the single most powerful piece of evidence for finding — and proving —
> root cause. Memory and logs alone rarely settle it.
