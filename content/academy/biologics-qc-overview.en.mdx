# Biologics QC — What Makes It Different

Quality control for biologics (monoclonal antibodies, recombinant proteins,
vaccines, cell and gene therapies) is a different discipline from small-molecule
QC. The molecules are large, complex, and made in living systems — so the rules of
"test it against a spec" bend in important ways.

## Heterogeneity is normal

A small-molecule API is essentially one defined structure. A biologic is a
**population** of closely related variants — charge variants, glycoforms,
oxidized/deamidated species, aggregates. QC doesn't prove a single structure; it
proves the *distribution* of species stays within validated ranges.

## The process defines the product

Because the molecule is built by cells and folded/modified post-translationally,
the **manufacturing process shapes critical quality attributes** (CQAs). Change a
cell line, media, or purification step and you can change the glycosylation or
aggregate profile. This is why comparability studies after process changes are a
major regulatory exercise for biologics.

## Identity needs orthogonal methods

No single test captures a large protein's identity. QC uses **multiple orthogonal
methods** — peptide mapping (sequence), charge-based methods (icIEF/CEX), intact
mass, and a binding/potency assay — that together confirm the right molecule.

## Potency is biological

A small molecule's assay is a chemical measurement. A biologic's **potency** is
usually a **bioassay** — a cell-based or binding assay measuring the actual
biological activity, because structure alone doesn't guarantee function.

## Why it matters

Biologics QC analysts think in *profiles and trends*, not single pass/fail
numbers. The skills — orthogonal characterization, bioassays, aggregate and
glycan analysis, viral safety — are in high demand as the pipeline shifts toward
biologics and advanced therapies.
