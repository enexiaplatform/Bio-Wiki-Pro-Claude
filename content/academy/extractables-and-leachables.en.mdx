# Extractables & Leachables (E&L)

Extractables and leachables (E&L) studies assess chemical compounds that migrate
from packaging, container-closure systems, or single-use manufacturing equipment
into the drug product — a growing focus as single-use systems become standard.

## Extractables vs leachables

- **Extractables** — compounds forced out under exaggerated lab conditions
  (aggressive solvents, heat, time). They define the *potential* universe.
- **Leachables** — compounds that actually migrate into the product under real
  storage and use conditions. They are a subset of extractables that matters most
  for patient safety.

## The Analytical Evaluation Threshold (AET)

The AET is the concentration above which a compound must be identified and
toxicologically assessed. It is derived from a Safety Concern Threshold and the
product's dosing — so reporting below the AET is not required, which keeps the
study tractable.

## Designing the study

Risk scales with route of administration and contact: inhalation and parenteral
products with prolonged contact carry the highest risk. A defensible program ties
material selection, extractables screening, and a leachables study to that risk.

## E&L risk-ranking matrix

Risk scales with how the dosage form contacts the material and for how long:

| Dosage form | Contact risk | E&L expectation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Inhalation (MDI/DPI/nebulizer) | Highest | Full extractables + leachables |
| Injectable / large-volume parenteral | High | Full extractables + leachables |
| Ophthalmic, transdermal | Medium–High | Risk-based leachables |
| Oral liquid | Medium | Screening + targeted leachables |
| Oral solid (blister/HDPE) | Lower | Often supplier data + justification |

Layer on material factors (polymer type, additives, processing aids, surface area,
gamma-irradiation of single-use systems) and storage (time, temperature, solvent
power of the formulation).

## AET — the concept and a worked figure

The AET converts a safety threshold into an analytical reporting level:

```
AET (µg/mL) ≈ (SCT in µg/day) / (daily volume or dose intake)
```

*Example:* with a Safety Concern Threshold of 1.5 µg/day and a product where a
patient receives 10 mL/day, the AET ≈ 1.5 / 10 = **0.15 µg/mL**. Compounds below
the AET need not be identified/assessed; those above it must be. An *uncertainty
factor* (often ~50%) is applied to account for response-factor variability in the
screening method, lowering the practical reporting level.

## Aligning supplier data to avoid duplication

Single-use and packaging suppliers increasingly provide **extractables data
packages** (often to BPOG/USP &lt;665&gt;-style protocols). Use them to define the
*potential* compound list and to select your **target leachables**, then run a
product-specific **leachables** study under real conditions to confirm what
actually migrates. Don't re-run extractables the supplier already did to a
recognized protocol — but never substitute extractables data for the leachables
study where risk (inhalation/parenteral) demands it.

> Practitioner tip: start E&L at material selection, not at the stability stage.
> Choosing a well-characterized, low-additive polymer up front prevents the
> nightmare of discovering an unsafe leachable in a system you've already locked
> into your process.
